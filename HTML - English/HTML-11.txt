<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="A">A</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quotations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: A">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Poema_visual_transitable_en_tres_temps,_detall..JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Poema_visual_transitable_en_tres_temps%2C_detall..JPG/220px-Poema_visual_transitable_en_tres_temps%2C_detall..JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Poema_visual_transitable_en_tres_temps%2C_detall..JPG/330px-Poema_visual_transitable_en_tres_temps%2C_detall..JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Poema_visual_transitable_en_tres_temps%2C_detall..JPG/440px-Poema_visual_transitable_en_tres_temps%2C_detall..JPG 2x" data-file-width="3872" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Poema_visual_transitable_en_tres_temps,_detall..JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Like your body your mind also gets tired so refresh it by wise sayings. <a href="/wiki/Ali" title="Ali">Ali bin Abi Talib</a></div></div></div>
<ul><li>To appreciate and use correctly a valuable maxim requires a genius, a vital appropriating exercise of mind, closely allied to that which first created it.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/William_R._Alger" title="William R. Alger">William R. Alger</a>, "<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://books.google.com/books?id=n6xIAAAAcAAJ&amp;pg=PA180">The Utility and the futility of Aphorism</a>" in: <i>Atlantic Monthly: A Magazine of Literature, Art and Politics,</i> Vol. 11, Atlantic Monthly, 1863, p. 180.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>Like your body your mind also gets tired so refresh it by wise sayings.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ali" title="Ali">Ali bin Abi Talib</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nahjul_Balagha" class="mw-redirect" title="Nahjul Balagha">Nahjul Balagha</a>, Sayings:91</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>Books of quotations are an elemental model of how culture is perpetuated, the wisdom of the trite passed on to posterity, to be added to, edited, and modified by subsequent generations.
<ul><li>Robert Andrews, ed. <i>The Columbia dictionary of quotations.</i> Columbia University Press, 1993.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>Quotation is the highest compliment you can pay to an author. Perhaps the next highest is, when a writer of any kind is so considerable that you go to the labor and pains of endeavoring to refute him before the public, the very doing of which is an incidental admission of his talent and power.
<ul><li>Anonymous, quoted in: <a href="/wiki/Tryon_Edwards" title="Tryon Edwards">Tryon Edwards</a> (1853) <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://books.google.com/books?id=i-MsAAAAYAAJ&amp;pg=PA232">The World's Laconics: Or, The Best Thoughts of the Best Authors</a>.</i> p. 232.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>The man whose book is filled with quotations, has been said to creep along the shore of authors, as if he were afraid to trust himself to the free compass of reasoning. I would rather defend such authors by a different allusion, and ask whether honey is the worse for being gathered from many flowers.
<ul><li>Anonymous, quoted in: <a href="/wiki/Tryon_Edwards" title="Tryon Edwards">Tryon Edwards</a> (1853) <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://books.google.com/books?id=i-MsAAAAYAAJ&amp;pg=PA232">The World's Laconics: Or, The Best Thoughts of the Best Authors</a>.</i> p. 232.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>In general, when reading a scholarly critic, one profits more from his quotations than from his comments.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/W._H._Auden" title="W. H. Auden">W. H. Auden</a>, <i>The Dyer's Hand, and Other Essays</i> (1962), "Reading", p. 9.</li></ul></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="B">B</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quotations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: B">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Some books also may be read by deputy, and extracts made of them by others; but that would be only in less important arguments, and the meaner sort of books: else distilled books are, like common distilled waters, flashy things.  Reading maketh a full man; conference a ready man; and writing an exact man. And, therefore, if a man write little, he had need have a great memory; if he confer little, he had need have a present wit; and if he read little, he had need have much cunning, to seem to know that he doth not.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon" title="Francis Bacon">Francis Bacon</a>, I. Of studie, <i>Essaies</i> (Jan, 1597) as quoted by <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Abbott_Abbott" title="Edwin Abbott Abbott">E. A. Abbott</a>, <i>Bacon's essays</i> (1876) <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=2TUCAAAAQAAJ">Vol. 2</a> Essay L, Of Studies, p. 72. With Abbott's Notes related to study, pp. 247-248.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>There is not less wit nor invention in applying rightly a thought one finds in a book, than in being the first author of that thought. Cardinal du Perron has been heard to say that the happy application of a verse of Virgil has deserved a talent.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bayle" title="Pierre Bayle">Pierre Bayle</a>, <i>Works</i>, Volume II, p. 779; in <i>Hoyt's New Cyclopedia Of Practical Quotations</i> (1922), p. 653-54.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>It needs no dictionary of quotations to remind me that the eyes are the windows of the soul.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Max_Beerbohm" title="Max Beerbohm">Max Beerbohm</a>, <i>Zuleika Dobson</i> (1911).</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>The surest way to make a monkey of a man is to quote him. That remark in itself wouldn't make any sense if quoted as it stands.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Robert_Benchley" title="Robert Benchley">Robert Benchley</a>, in "Quick Quotations" in <i>My Ten Years in a Quandary and How They Grew</i> (1936).</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>Quotation, n. The act of repeating erroneously the words of another. The words erroneously repeated.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ambrose_Bierce" title="Ambrose Bierce">Ambrose Bierce</a>, <i>The Devil's Dictionary</i> (1911).</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>One whom it is easier to hate, but still easier to quote—Alexander Pope.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Augustine_Birrell" title="Augustine Birrell">Augustine Birrell</a>, <i>Alexander Pope</i>; in <i>Hoyt's New Cyclopedia Of Practical Quotations</i> (1922), p. 653-54.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>Its fall is entirely owing to itself, the seeds of death were in it from the commencement. The powers of satire and of derision, which it exercised with as little mercy as modesty, have proved, in the result, very humble powers; and after usurping an authority, the most dictatorial and audacious, a general doubt is now expressed as to the ability with which it was at one time supposed to have been conducted. Of this there certainly can be no dispute that it will be difficult to name as many volumes in the English language which afford so few quotable passages; and perhaps there can be no better proof of the original mediocrity of the contributors, whatever may have been the merit of a few occasional articles.
<ul><li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://books.google.com/books?id=UrkCAAAAIAAJ&amp;">Blackwood's Magazine</a>, "Historical View of the Rise, Progress, Decline and Fall of the Edinburgh Review" W. Blackwood, (1821), Vol.10, p. 680.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>Of all the many and (thanks to a free press) the ever-multiplying blessings attendant upon the "glorious constitution" of literature, not the least precious and profitable to a modern cultivator of systems and syllables, in pamphlets, magazines, and folios, is the right of Quotation.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Samuel_Laman_Blanchard" title="Samuel Laman Blanchard">Samuel Laman Blanchard</a>, <i>Sketches from life</i> (1846).</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>Shall we not rejoice then and revel in the glorious liberty of extract, and quote to the thousandth line? Shall we not have pages like the Pyramids?
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Samuel_Laman_Blanchard" title="Samuel Laman Blanchard">Samuel Laman Blanchard</a>, <i>Sketches from life</i> (1846).</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>I must stop to lament, that we cannot evince an admiring gratitude towards other excellent things by a like readiness of quotation: that we cannot, for instance, quote a star that we have been watching; or a hue of sunset; or a friend's voice, and his shake of the hand (I had almost said heart); or a beautiful picture...
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Samuel_Laman_Blanchard" title="Samuel Laman Blanchard">Samuel Laman Blanchard</a>, <i>Sketches from life</i> (1846).</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>Life itself is a quotation.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Jorge_Luis_Borges" title="Jorge Luis Borges">Jorge Luis Borges</a>, quoted in <i>Cool Memories</i> (1987) by Jean Baudrillard, (trans. 1990) Ch. 5.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>At all events, the next best thing to being <a href="/wiki/Witty" class="mw-redirect" title="Witty">witty</a> one's self, is to be able to quote another's wit.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Christian_Nestell_Bovee" title="Christian Nestell Bovee">Christian Nestell Bovee</a>, <i>Intuitions and Summaries of Thought</i> (1826), Volume II, p. 124.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>A great <a href="/wiki/Thought" title="Thought">thought</a> is a great boon, for which <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> is to be the first <a href="/wiki/Thanked" class="mw-redirect" title="Thanked">thanked</a>, then he who is the first to utter it, and then, in a lesser, but still in a considerable degree, the man who is the first to quote it to us.
<ul><li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_Nestell_Bovee" class="extiw" title="w:Christian Nestell Bovee">Christian Nestell Bovee</a>; as quoted in <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=JIQcAAAAMAAJ"><i>A Dictionary of Thoughts</i></a>, edited by Tryon Edwards, (1908).</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>Quotations can be valuable, like raisins in the rice pudding, for adding iron as well as eye appeal.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Peg_Bracken" title="Peg Bracken">Peg Bracken</a>, <i>I Didn't Come Here to Argue</i>.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Horace" title="Horace">Horace</a> ou <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolas_Boileau-Despr%C3%A9aux" class="extiw" title="w:Nicolas Boileau-Despréaux">Despréaux</a> l’a dit avant vous.—Je le crois sur votre parole; mais je l’ai dit comme mien. Ne puis-je pas penser après eux une chose vraie, et que d’autres encore penseront après moi?</i>
<ul><li>“<a href="/wiki/Horace" title="Horace">Horace</a> or <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolas_Boileau-Despr%C3%A9aux" class="extiw" title="w:Nicolas Boileau-Despréaux">Boileau</a> have said such a thing before you.”—”I take your word for it, but I have used it as my own. May I not have the same correct thought after them, as others may have after me?”</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jean_de_La_Bruy%C3%A8re" title="Jean de La Bruyère">Jean de La Bruyère</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Caract%C3%A8res" title="Les Caractères">Les Caractères</a></i> (1688), “Of Works of the Mind,” #69</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>At all times pseudoprofound aphorisms have been more popular than rigorous arguments.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Mario_Bunge" title="Mario Bunge">Mario Bunge</a>, <i>Evaluating Philosophies</i> (2012), p. xiv.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>All which he understood by rote,<br />And, as occasion serv'd, would quote.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Samuel_Butler_(poet)" title="Samuel Butler (poet)">Samuel Butler</a>, <i>Hudibras</i>, Part I (1663-64), Canto I, line 135.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>With just enough of learning to misquote.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Lord_Byron" title="Lord Byron">Lord Byron</a>, <i>English Bards and Scotch Reviewers</i> (1809), line 66.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>Perverts the Prophets, and purloins the Psalms.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Lord_Byron" title="Lord Byron">Lord Byron</a>, <i>English Bards and Scotch Reviewers</i> (1809), line 326.</li></ul></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="C">C</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quotations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: C">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Henry_David_Thoreau_quote_-_Library_Way_-_NY_City.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Henry_David_Thoreau_quote_-_Library_Way_-_NY_City.jpg/220px-Henry_David_Thoreau_quote_-_Library_Way_-_NY_City.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Henry_David_Thoreau_quote_-_Library_Way_-_NY_City.jpg/330px-Henry_David_Thoreau_quote_-_Library_Way_-_NY_City.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Henry_David_Thoreau_quote_-_Library_Way_-_NY_City.jpg/440px-Henry_David_Thoreau_quote_-_Library_Way_-_NY_City.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="1728" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Henry_David_Thoreau_quote_-_Library_Way_-_NY_City.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>There is nothing so ridiculous but some philosopher has said it. ~ <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a></div></div></div>
<ul><li>The great writers of aphorisms read as if they had all known each other very well.
<ul><li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elias_Canetti" class="extiw" title="w:Elias Canetti">Elias Canetti</a>, <i>The Human Province</i> (1942–1972).</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>You could compile the worst book in the world entirely out of selected passages from the best writers in the world.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Gilbert_K._Chesterton" class="mw-redirect" title="Gilbert K. Chesterton">Gilbert K. Chesterton</a>, in: Lilless McPherson Shilling, ‎Linda K. Fuller (1997) <i>Dictionary of Quotations in Communications.</i> p. xvi.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>To copy beauties, forfeits all pretence<br />To fame—to copy faults, is want of sense.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Charles_Churchill" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Churchill">Charles Churchill</a>, <i>The Rosciad</i> (1761), line 457.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>It is a good thing for an uneducated man to read books of quotations. <i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bartlett%27s_Familiar_Quotations" class="extiw" title="w:Bartlett&#39;s Familiar Quotations">Bartlett's Familiar Quotations</a></i> is an admirable work, and I studied it intently. The quotations when engraved upon the memory give you good thoughts. They also make you anxious to read the authors and look for more.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>, <i>Roving Commission: My Early Life</i> (1930) Chapter 9.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>There is nothing so ridiculous but some philosopher has said it.
