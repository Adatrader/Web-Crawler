<p>"<b>America the Beautiful</b>" is a <a href="/wiki/Patriotism" title="Patriotism">patriotic</a> song. The lyrics were written by <a href="/wiki/Katharine_Lee_Bates" title="Katharine Lee Bates">Katharine Lee Bates</a>, and the music was composed by church organist and choirmaster <a href="/wiki/Samuel_A._Ward" title="Samuel A. Ward">Samuel A. Ward</a> at <a href="/wiki/Grace_Church_(Newark)" title="Grace Church (Newark)">Grace Episcopal Church</a> in Newark, New Jersey.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The two never met.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Bates originally wrote the words as a poem, "<b>Pikes Peak</b>", first published in the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_of_July" class="mw-redirect" title="Fourth of July">Fourth of July</a> edition of the church periodical <i>The Congregationalist</i> in 1895. At that time, the poem was titled "America" for publication. Ward had originally written the music, "Materna", for the hymn "<a href="/wiki/O_Mother_dear,_Jerusalem" class="mw-redirect" title="O Mother dear, Jerusalem">O Mother dear, Jerusalem</a>" in 1882, though it was not first published until 1892.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Ward's music combined with the Bates poem was first published in 1910 and titled "America the Beautiful". The song is one of the most popular of the many U.S. patriotic songs.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=America_the_Beautiful&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Americathebeautiful.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Americathebeautiful.jpg/170px-Americathebeautiful.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="203" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Americathebeautiful.jpg/255px-Americathebeautiful.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Americathebeautiful.jpg/340px-Americathebeautiful.jpg 2x" data-file-width="636" data-file-height="760" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Americathebeautiful.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Commemoration plaque atop Pikes Peak in July 1999</div></div></div>
<p>In 1893, at the age of 33, Bates, an English professor at <a href="/wiki/Wellesley_College" title="Wellesley College">Wellesley College</a>, had taken a train trip to <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Springs,_Colorado" title="Colorado Springs, Colorado">Colorado Springs</a>, Colorado, to teach a short summer school session at <a href="/wiki/Colorado_College" title="Colorado College">Colorado College</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Several of the sights on her trip inspired her, and they found their way into her poem, including the <a href="/wiki/World%27s_Columbian_Exposition" title="World&#39;s Columbian Exposition">World's Columbian Exposition</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, the "White City" with its promise of the future contained within its gleaming white buildings;<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> the wheat fields of America's heartland <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>, through which her train was riding on July 16; and the majestic view of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> from high atop <a href="/wiki/Pikes_Peak" title="Pikes Peak">Pikes Peak</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FromSeatoShiningSea_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FromSeatoShiningSea-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On the pinnacle of that mountain, the words of the poem started to come to her, and she wrote them down upon returning to her hotel room at the original <a href="/wiki/Antlers_Hilton_Hotel" class="mw-redirect" title="Antlers Hilton Hotel">Antlers Hotel</a>. The poem was initially published two years later in <i>The Congregationalist</i> to commemorate <a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(United_States)" title="Independence Day (United States)">the Fourth of July</a>. It quickly caught the public's fancy. Amended versions were published in 1904 and 1911.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The first known melody written for the song was sent in by Silas Pratt when the poem was published in <i>The Congregationalist</i>. By 1900, at least 75 different melodies had been written.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins2009_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins2009-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Hymn_tune" title="Hymn tune">hymn tune</a> composed in 1882 by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_A._Ward" title="Samuel A. Ward">Samuel A. Ward</a>, the organist and choir director at <a href="/wiki/Grace_Church,_Newark" class="mw-redirect" title="Grace Church, Newark">Grace Church, Newark</a>, was generally considered the best music as early as 1910 and is still the popular tune today. Just as Bates had been inspired to write her poem, Ward, too, was inspired. The tune came to him while he was on a ferryboat trip from <a href="/wiki/Coney_Island" title="Coney Island">Coney Island</a> back to his home in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> after a leisurely summer day and he immediately wrote it down. Supposedly, he was so anxious to capture the tune in his head, he asked fellow passenger friend Harry Martin for his shirt cuff to write the tune on. He composed the tune for the old hymn "O Mother Dear, Jerusalem", retitling the work "Materna". Ward's music combined with Bates's poem were first published together in 1910 and titled "America the Beautiful".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ward died in 1903, not knowing the national stature his music would attain. Bates was more fortunate, since the song's popularity was well established by the time of her death in 1929.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins2009_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins2009-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At various times in the more than 100 years that have elapsed since the song was written, particularly during the <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> administration, there have been efforts to give "America the Beautiful" legal status either as a national hymn or as a national anthem equal to, or in place of, "<a href="/wiki/The_Star-Spangled_Banner" title="The Star-Spangled Banner">The Star-Spangled Banner</a>", but so far this has not yet succeeded. Proponents prefer "America the Beautiful" for various reasons, saying it is easier to sing, more melodic, and more adaptable to new orchestrations while still remaining as easily recognizable as "<a href="/wiki/The_Star-Spangled_Banner" title="The Star-Spangled Banner">The Star-Spangled Banner</a>". Some prefer "America the Beautiful" over "The Star-Spangled Banner" due to the latter's war-oriented imagery; others prefer "The Star-Spangled Banner" for the same reason. While that national dichotomy has stymied any effort at changing the tradition of the national anthem, "America the Beautiful" continues to be held in high esteem by a large number of Americans, and was even being considered <i>before</i> 1931, as a candidate to become the national anthem of the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This song was used as the background music of the television broadcast of the <a href="/wiki/Tiangong-1" title="Tiangong-1">Tiangong-1</a> launch.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The song is often included in songbooks in a wide variety of religious congregations in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<td><div class="poem">
<p>Original poem (1893)<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><br />
<br />
O beautiful for halcyon skies,<br />
For amber waves of grain,<br />
For purple mountain majesties<br />
Above the enameled plain!<br />
America! America!<br />
God shed His grace on thee,<br />
Till souls wax fair as earth and air<br />
And music-hearted sea!<br />
<br />
O beautiful for pilgrim feet<br />
Whose stern, impassioned stress<br />
A thoroughfare for freedom beat<br />
Across the wilderness!<br />
America! America!<br />
God shed His grace on thee<br />
Till paths be wrought through wilds of thought<br />
By pilgrim foot and knee!<br />
<br />
O beautiful for glory-tale<br />
Of liberating strife,<br />
When once or twice, for man's avail,<br />
Men lavished precious life!<br />
America! America!<br />
God shed His grace on thee<br />
Till selfish gain no longer stain,<br />
The banner of the free!<br />
<br />
O beautiful for patriot dream<br />
That sees beyond the years<br />
Thine alabaster cities gleam<br />
Undimmed by human tears!<br />
America! America!<br />
God shed His grace on thee<br />
Till nobler men keep once again<br />
Thy whiter jubilee!
</p>
</div>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_versions">Popular versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=America_the_Beautiful&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Popular versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bing_Crosby" title="Bing Crosby">Bing Crosby</a> included the song in a medley on his album <i><a href="/wiki/101_Gang_Songs" title="101 Gang Songs">101 Gang Songs</a></i> (1961).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Frank_Sinatra" title="Frank Sinatra">Frank Sinatra</a> recorded the song with <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Riddle" title="Nelson Riddle">Nelson Riddle</a> during the sessions for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Concert_Sinatra" title="The Concert Sinatra">The Concert Sinatra</a></i> in February 1963, for a projected 45 single release. The 45 was not commercially issued however, but the song was later added as a bonus track to the enhanced 2012 CD release of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Concert_Sinatra" title="The Concert Sinatra">The Concert Sinatra</a></i>.
</p><p>In 1976, while the United States celebrated its bicentennial, a soulful version popularized by <a href="/wiki/Ray_Charles" title="Ray Charles">Ray Charles</a> peaked at number 98 on the US R&amp;B chart.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Three different renditions of the song have entered the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Country_Songs" title="Hot Country Songs">Hot Country Songs</a> charts. The first was by <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Rich" title="Charlie Rich">Charlie Rich</a>, which went to number 22 in 1976.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> A second, by <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Newbury" title="Mickey Newbury">Mickey Newbury</a>, peaked at number 82 in 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/All-star" title="All-star">all-star</a> version of "America the Beautiful" performed by <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a> singers <a href="/wiki/Trace_Adkins" title="Trace Adkins">Trace Adkins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sherri%C3%A9_Austin" title="Sherrié Austin">Sherrié Austin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Billy_Dean" title="Billy Dean">Billy Dean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vince_Gill" title="Vince Gill">Vince Gill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carolyn_Dawn_Johnson" title="Carolyn Dawn Johnson">Carolyn Dawn Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toby_Keith" title="Toby Keith">Toby Keith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brenda_Lee" title="Brenda Lee">Brenda Lee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lonestar" title="Lonestar">Lonestar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lyle_Lovett" title="Lyle Lovett">Lyle Lovett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lila_McCann" title="Lila McCann">Lila McCann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lorrie_Morgan" title="Lorrie Morgan">Lorrie Morgan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jamie_O%27Neal" title="Jamie O&#39;Neal">Jamie O'Neal</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Oak_Ridge_Boys" title="The Oak Ridge Boys">The Oak Ridge Boys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Collin_Raye" title="Collin Raye">Collin Raye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Rogers" title="Kenny Rogers">Kenny Rogers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keith_Urban" title="Keith Urban">Keith Urban</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phil_Vassar" title="Phil Vassar">Phil Vassar</a> reached number 58 in July 2001. The song re-entered the chart following the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Popularity of the song increased greatly following the September 11 attacks; at some sporting events it was sung in addition to the traditional singing of the national anthem. During the first taping of the <i><a href="/wiki/Late_Show_with_David_Letterman" title="Late Show with David Letterman">Late Show with David Letterman</a></i> following the attacks, CBS newsman <a href="/wiki/Dan_Rather" title="Dan Rather">Dan Rather</a> cried briefly as he quoted the fourth verse.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XLVIII" title="Super Bowl XLVIII">Super Bowl XLVIII</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Coca-Cola_Company" title="The Coca-Cola Company">The Coca-Cola Company</a> aired a multilingual version of the song, sung in several different languages. The commercial received some criticism on social media sites, such as Twitter and Facebook, and from some conservatives, such as <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Beck" title="Glenn Beck">Glenn Beck</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Despite the controversies, Coca-Cola later reused the Super Bowl ad during <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_LI" title="Super Bowl LI">Super Bowl LI</a>, the opening ceremonies of the <a href="/wiki/2014_Winter_Olympics" title="2014 Winter Olympics">2014 Winter Olympics</a> and <a href="/wiki/2016_Summer_Olympics" title="2016 Summer Olympics">2016 Summer Olympics</a> and for patriotic holidays.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sbnation-cocacolaagain_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sbnation-cocacolaagain-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Idioms">Idioms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=America_the_Beautiful&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Idioms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"From sea to shining sea", originally used in the charters of some of the English Colonies in North America, is an American <a href="/wiki/Idiom" title="Idiom">idiom</a> meaning "from the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>" (or vice versa). Other songs that have used this phrase include the American patriotic song "<a href="/wiki/God_Bless_the_U.S.A." title="God Bless the U.S.A.">God Bless the U.S.A.</a>" and <a href="/wiki/Schoolhouse_Rock" class="mw-redirect" title="Schoolhouse Rock">Schoolhouse Rock</a>'s "Elbow Room". The phrase and the song are also the namesake of the <a href="/wiki/Shining_Sea_Bikeway" title="Shining Sea Bikeway">Shining Sea Bikeway</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Bike_path" title="Bike path">bike path</a> in Bates's hometown of <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Massachusetts" title="Falmouth, Massachusetts">Falmouth, Massachusetts</a>. The phrase is similar to the Latin phrase "<i><i lang="la" title="Latin language text"><a href="/wiki/A_Mari_Usque_Ad_Mare" title="A Mari Usque Ad Mare">A Mari Usque Ad Mare</a></i></i>" ("From sea to sea"), which serves as the official motto of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>"Purple mountain majesties" refers to the shade of the <a href="/wiki/Pikes_Peak" title="Pikes Peak">Pikes Peak</a> in <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Springs,_Colorado" title="Colorado Springs, Colorado">Colorado Springs, Colorado</a>, which inspired Bates to write the poem.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 2003 <a href="/wiki/Tori_Amos" title="Tori Amos">Tori Amos</a> song "Amber Waves," the "America the Beautiful" lyric "for amber waves of grain" is appropriated to create a <a href="/wiki/Personification" title="Personification">personification</a>; Amos imagines Amber Waves as an exotic dancer, like she by the same name, portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Julianne_Moore" title="Julianne Moore">Julianne Moore</a>, in <i><a href="/wiki/Boogie_Nights" title="Boogie Nights">Boogie Nights</a></i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Books">Books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=America_the_Beautiful&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lynn_Sherr" title="Lynn Sherr">Lynn Sherr</a>'s 2001 book <i>America the Beautiful</i> discusses the origins of the song and the backgrounds of its authors in depth. The book points out that the poem has <a href="/wiki/Common_meter" class="mw-redirect" title="Common meter">the same meter</a> as that of "<a href="/wiki/Auld_Lang_Syne" title="Auld Lang Syne">Auld Lang Syne</a>"; the songs can be sung interchangeably. Additionally, Sherr discusses the evolution of the lyrics, for instance, changes to the original third verse written by Bates.<sup id="cite_ref-Sherr2001_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sherr2001-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Melinda M. Ponder, in her 2017 biography <i>Katharine Lee Bates: From Sea to Shining Sea</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-FromSeatoShiningSea_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FromSeatoShiningSea-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> draws heavily on Bates's diaries and letters to trace the history of the poem and its place in American culture.
</p>
