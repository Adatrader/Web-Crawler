<p>The <b>Kennedy Space Center Visitor Complex</b> is the visitor center at <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">Kennedy Space Center</a> in Florida. It features exhibits and displays, historic spacecraft and memorabilia, shows, two <a href="/wiki/IMAX" title="IMAX">IMAX</a> theaters, and a range of bus tours of the spaceport. "Space Shuttle <i>Atlantis</i>" is home to the real <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Atlantis" title="Space Shuttle Atlantis">Space Shuttle <i>Atlantis</i></a> orbiter and the <a href="/wiki/Shuttle_Launch_Experience" class="mw-redirect" title="Shuttle Launch Experience">Shuttle Launch Experience</a>, a simulated ride into space. The center also provides astronaut training experiences, including a multi-axial chair and Mars Base simulator.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The visitor complex also has daily presentations from a veteran NASA astronaut.  A bus tour, included with admission, encompasses the separate <a href="/wiki/Apollo/Saturn_V_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Apollo/Saturn V Center">Apollo/Saturn V Center</a>. There were 1.7 million visitors to the visitor complex in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cape_Canaveral_Tourist_Information_Center.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Cape_Canaveral_Tourist_Information_Center.jpg/220px-Cape_Canaveral_Tourist_Information_Center.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="72" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Cape_Canaveral_Tourist_Information_Center.jpg/330px-Cape_Canaveral_Tourist_Information_Center.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Cape_Canaveral_Tourist_Information_Center.jpg/440px-Cape_Canaveral_Tourist_Information_Center.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1800" data-file-height="585" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cape_Canaveral_Tourist_Information_Center.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A small trailer served as the Visitors Information Center in the early 1960s</div></div></div>
<p>The complex had its beginning in 1963 when NASA Administrator <a href="/wiki/James_E._Webb" title="James E. Webb">James Webb</a> 1960s established self-guided tours the public could drive along a predetermined route through the Cape Canaveral Air Force Station and a small trailer containing simple displays on card tables. An estimated 100,000 visitors went through that first year. 
</p><p>
As the American space program's popularity grew with the Mercury Program and Alan Shepard's historic launch, large numbers of press and public flocked to the Cape Canaveral area to get a close up view. Webb was urged by <a href="/wiki/Olin_Teague" class="mw-redirect" title="Olin Teague">U.S. Rep. Olin Teague</a> of Texas to create a visitors' program.<sup id="cite_ref-kennedyspacecenter.com_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kennedyspacecenter.com-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> By 1964, more than 250,000 self-guided car tours, permitted between 1 and 4&#160;pm. ET on Sundays, were seen at Kennedy Space Center (KSC). </p><div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:243px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kennedy_Space_Center_Self-Drive_Tour_booklet.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f5/Kennedy_Space_Center_Self-Drive_Tour_booklet.png/241px-Kennedy_Space_Center_Self-Drive_Tour_booklet.png" decoding="async" width="241" height="189" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f5/Kennedy_Space_Center_Self-Drive_Tour_booklet.png/362px-Kennedy_Space_Center_Self-Drive_Tour_booklet.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f5/Kennedy_Space_Center_Self-Drive_Tour_booklet.png/482px-Kennedy_Space_Center_Self-Drive_Tour_booklet.png 2x" data-file-width="956" data-file-height="750" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kennedy_Space_Center_Self-Drive_Tour_booklet.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>self-guided tour from the 1960s</div></div></div><p>In 1965, KSC Director <a href="/wiki/Kurt_H._Debus" title="Kurt H. Debus">Kurt H. Debus</a> was authorized to spend $2&#160;million on a full-scale visitor center, covering 42 acres. Spaceport USA, as it was soon titled, hosted 500,000 visitors in 1967, its first year, and one million by 1969.  Ten-thousand visitors toured the center on December 24, 1968, following the Apollo 8 orbit of the Moon.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NASA_KSC_TWA_Tours.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/NASA_KSC_TWA_Tours.jpg/220px-NASA_KSC_TWA_Tours.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="101" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/NASA_KSC_TWA_Tours.jpg/330px-NASA_KSC_TWA_Tours.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/NASA_KSC_TWA_Tours.jpg/440px-NASA_KSC_TWA_Tours.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="469" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NASA_KSC_TWA_Tours.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bus tours were operated by TWA in the mid 1960s</div></div></div>
<p>Beginning July 22, 1966, public tours were offered on 40-passenger busses.  Operated by <a href="/wiki/TWA" class="mw-redirect" title="TWA">TWA</a>, a 1.5-hour tour that included the Vehicle Assemble Building and a 3-hour tour including launch facilities were available.  Tickets ranged from $0.50 for children 12 and under to $2.50 for adults for the longer tour. More than 1,500 people toured that first day and additional busses were quickly added to the fleet of former Greyhound busses.  TWA continued operating tours through at least the bicentennial celebrations in 1976.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Information_Complex_1969.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5a/Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Information_Complex_1969.jpg/220px-Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Information_Complex_1969.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="92" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5a/Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Information_Complex_1969.jpg/330px-Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Information_Complex_1969.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5a/Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Information_Complex_1969.jpg/440px-Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Information_Complex_1969.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1648" data-file-height="686" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Information_Complex_1969.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Visitor Information Complex, 1969</div></div></div>
<p>As NASA neared the Moon, popularity grew. By 1969, the visitor center was the second most visited Florida attraction, behind Tampa's Busch Gardens.<sup id="cite_ref-kennedyspacecenter.com_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kennedyspacecenter.com-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Even during the gap between the Apollo and Space Shuttle programs, attendance remained at over one million guests and it ranked as the fifth most popular tourist attraction in Florida.<sup id="cite_ref-KSC_History_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KSC_History-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When nearby <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_World" title="Walt Disney World">Walt Disney World</a> opened in 1971, visitor center attendance increased by 30%, but the public was often disappointed by the comparative lack of polish at KSC's tourist facilities. Existing displays were largely made up of trade show exhibits donated by NASA contractors. Later that year, a $2.3&#160;million upgrade of the visitor complex began with added focus on the benefits of space exploration along with the existing focus on human space exploration.<sup id="cite_ref-KSC_History_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KSC_History-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex_1998-08-06.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e7/Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex_1998-08-06.jpg/220px-Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex_1998-08-06.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e7/Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex_1998-08-06.jpg/330px-Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex_1998-08-06.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e7/Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex_1998-08-06.jpg/440px-Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex_1998-08-06.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2617" data-file-height="1740" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex_1998-08-06.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Visitor Complex in 1998</div></div></div>
<p>In 1995, <a href="/wiki/Delaware_North_Companies" class="mw-redirect" title="Delaware North Companies">Delaware North Companies</a> was selected to operate the visitor center. Between 1995 and 2007, the visitors center went through many changes, including the improvement of restaurants, retail shops, buses, and new exhibits. It is also when the visitor complex got its current name, Kennedy Space Center Visitor Complex. Since then, the facility has been entirely self-supporting and receives no taxpayer or government funding.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy Space Center Visitor Complex was voted the 8th best museum in the United States by Trip Advisor in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> NASA renewed the contract with Delaware North Companies through 2028.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attractions">Attractions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Attractions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex.jpg/220px-Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="139" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex.jpg/330px-Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex.jpg/440px-Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4743" data-file-height="3002" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Entrance to Kennedy Space Center, the John F. Kennedy memorial and a Space Shuttle stack in the background</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Space_shuttle_stack_at_KSC.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/25/Space_shuttle_stack_at_KSC.jpg/220px-Space_shuttle_stack_at_KSC.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/25/Space_shuttle_stack_at_KSC.jpg/330px-Space_shuttle_stack_at_KSC.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/25/Space_shuttle_stack_at_KSC.jpg/440px-Space_shuttle_stack_at_KSC.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Space_shuttle_stack_at_KSC.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Space Shuttle stack in front of the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Atlantis" title="Space Shuttle Atlantis">Space Shuttle Atlantis</a> Exhibit building</div></div></div>
<p>Included in the base admission is tour-bus transportation to <a href="/wiki/Launch_Complex_39" class="mw-redirect" title="Launch Complex 39">Launch Complex 39</a> and the surrounding KSC property, and the <a href="/wiki/Apollo/Saturn_V_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Apollo/Saturn V Center">Apollo/Saturn V Center</a>.  Previously, it used to include admission to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Astronaut_Hall_of_Fame" title="United States Astronaut Hall of Fame">Astronaut Hall of Fame</a>, 6 miles (9.7&#160;km) to the west. That building is now closed and the U.S. Astronaut Hall of Fame resides in a new exhibit at the visitor complex, Heroes &amp; Legends.
</p><p>
The Apollo/Saturn V Center, located 6 miles (9.7&#160;km) north inside NASA's gates, is a large museum built around its centerpiece exhibit, a restored <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> launch vehicle, and features other space related exhibits, including an Apollo capsule. Two theaters allow the visitor to relive parts of the Apollo program. One simulates the environment inside an Apollo firing room during an Apollo launch, and another simulates the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> Moon landing. The tour formerly included the Space Station Processing Facility (SSPF) where modules for the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> were tested.</p><div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mercury_Control_Debus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Mercury_Control_Debus.jpg/220px-Mercury_Control_Debus.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Mercury_Control_Debus.jpg/330px-Mercury_Control_Debus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Mercury_Control_Debus.jpg/440px-Mercury_Control_Debus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mercury_Control_Debus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Mercury</a> <a href="/wiki/Mission_Control_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Mission Control Center">Control</a> consoles in Heroes &amp; Legends.</div></div></div>
<p>The Visitor Complex includes two facilities run by the Astronauts Memorial Foundation. The most visible of these is the <a href="/wiki/Space_Mirror_Memorial" title="Space Mirror Memorial">Space Mirror Memorial</a>, also known as the Astronaut Memorial, a huge black <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> mirror through-engraved with the names of all <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronauts</a> who died in the line of duty. Elsewhere on the Visitor Complex grounds is the foundation's Center for Space Education, which includes a resource center for teachers, among other facilities; and the <a href="/wiki/Kurt_H._Debus" title="Kurt H. Debus">Kurt Debus</a> Conference Center.
</p><p>Heroes &amp; Legends, which replaced the previous Early Space Exploration exhibit, houses the U.S. Astronaut Hall of Fame and several displays of artifacts. Among them is the <a href="/wiki/Gemini_9A" title="Gemini 9A">Gemini 9A</a> spacecraft, as well as a recreation of the Mercury Control Center using consoles and furniture relocated from the original building at <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station">Cape Canaveral Air Force Station</a>. These were previously housed in the Mercury Mission Control facility, which was on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>, but it was demolished in May 2010 due to concerns about <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a> and the estimated $5-million cost to renovate the building after 40 years of exposure to salt air.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Atlantis_Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex_4.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Atlantis_Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex_4.jpg/220px-Atlantis_Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex_4.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="135" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Atlantis_Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex_4.jpg/330px-Atlantis_Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex_4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Atlantis_Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex_4.jpg/440px-Atlantis_Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex_4.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4830" data-file-height="2974" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Atlantis_Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex_4.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Atlantis</i> display</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_Shuttle_Atlantis">Space Shuttle <i>Atlantis</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Space Shuttle Atlantis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NASA_Space_Shuttle_Atlantis_Thrusters_Kennedy_Space_Center.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/NASA_Space_Shuttle_Atlantis_Thrusters_Kennedy_Space_Center.jpg/220px-NASA_Space_Shuttle_Atlantis_Thrusters_Kennedy_Space_Center.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/NASA_Space_Shuttle_Atlantis_Thrusters_Kennedy_Space_Center.jpg/330px-NASA_Space_Shuttle_Atlantis_Thrusters_Kennedy_Space_Center.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/NASA_Space_Shuttle_Atlantis_Thrusters_Kennedy_Space_Center.jpg/440px-NASA_Space_Shuttle_Atlantis_Thrusters_Kennedy_Space_Center.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2004" data-file-height="2004" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NASA_Space_Shuttle_Atlantis_Thrusters_Kennedy_Space_Center.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Space Shuttle <i>Atlantis</i> thrusters on display in 2016</div></div></div>
<p>In 2010, the center announced a $100&#160;million plan to house a retired <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> orbiter in a 10-story 64,000 square feet (5,900&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) facility.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> On April 12, 2011, the 30th anniversary of the launch of <a href="/wiki/STS-1" title="STS-1">STS-1</a>, NASA announced that <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Atlantis" title="Space Shuttle Atlantis">Space Shuttle <i>Atlantis</i></a> would be provided to the visitors center for display after its last flight on <a href="/wiki/STS-135" title="STS-135">STS-135</a> and subsequent decommissioning.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The exhibit officially opened on June 29, 2013, offering a nearly 360-degree view of the shuttle.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <i>Atlantis</i> is positioned at a 43.21 degrees angle with the payload bay doors open; a view only previously seen in space.
</p><p>The exhibit also includes a life sized replica of the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>, the Shuttle program's astrovan, Dr. <a href="/wiki/Maxime_Faget" title="Maxime Faget">Maxime Faget</a>’s Shuttle prototype from 1969, a large-scale slide mimicking the 22 degree slope of a Space Shuttle when landing, numerous astronaut training and Shuttle simulators, and other displays about life in space. On the ground level is the "Forever Remembered" exhibit, commemorating the 14 astronauts lost in both Space Shuttle <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Challenger_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Challenger disaster"><i>Challenger</i></a> and Space Shuttle <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Columbia disaster"><i>Columbia</i></a> disasters. Forever Remembered includes personal artifacts from the astronauts, two recovered pieces of the Shuttles, footage of the physical and emotional recovery, and the return to flight.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This exhibit replaced the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Explorer" class="mw-redirect" title="Space Shuttle Explorer">Space Shuttle Explorer</a> which was a full-scale, high-fidelity replica of the Space Shuttle which visitors were able to board. Explorer was removed from the KSC Visitor Center on December 11, 2011, and relocated to the Vehicle Assembly Building's turn basin dock adjacent to the Launch Complex 39 Press Site.<sup id="cite_ref-collect20111211_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collect20111211-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The vehicle remained at the turn basin until 2012, when it was moved to the <a href="/wiki/Space_Center_Houston" title="Space Center Houston">Space Center Houston</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-collect20120524_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collect20120524-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shuttle_Launch_Experience">Shuttle Launch Experience</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Shuttle Launch Experience">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shuttle_Launch_Simulation_Facility_exterior.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/92/Shuttle_Launch_Simulation_Facility_exterior.jpg/220px-Shuttle_Launch_Simulation_Facility_exterior.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/92/Shuttle_Launch_Simulation_Facility_exterior.jpg/330px-Shuttle_Launch_Simulation_Facility_exterior.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/92/Shuttle_Launch_Simulation_Facility_exterior.jpg/440px-Shuttle_Launch_Simulation_Facility_exterior.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shuttle_Launch_Simulation_Facility_exterior.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Space Shuttle Launch Simulation Facility building exterior</div></div></div>
<p>The Shuttle Launch Experience,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> designed by <a href="/wiki/Bob_Rogers_(designer)" title="Bob Rogers (designer)">Bob Rogers</a> and the design team BRC Imagination Arts, opened May 25, 2007. The attraction puts guests through a simulated Shuttle launch. <a href="/wiki/Delaware_North_Companies" class="mw-redirect" title="Delaware North Companies">Delaware North Companies</a> invested six years and $60&#160;million into the attraction. Astronauts, NASA experts and attraction-industry leaders were consulted during development. The attraction is housed in a 44,000-square-foot (4,100&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) building that holds four simulators, each accommodating 44 people.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Former Shuttle commander and then NASA Administrator <a href="/wiki/Charles_F._Bolden" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles F. Bolden">Charles F. Bolden</a> narrates the simulation and hosts the prerecorded pre-show.
In the attraction's early years, guests would enter from the outside and exit into a gift shop before heading back out to the Visitor Complex. In 2013, however, the attraction was later made part of the exhibit for the Space Shuttle <i>Atlantis</i>, with the former gift shop space being used for several simulators that allowed guests to try their hand at landing and docking the Space Shuttle.
</p>
<h3><span id="Heroes_.26_Legends"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Heroes_&amp;_Legends">Heroes &amp; Legends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Heroes &amp; Legends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gemini9a.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Gemini9a.jpg/220px-Gemini9a.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Gemini9a.jpg/330px-Gemini9a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Gemini9a.jpg/440px-Gemini9a.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gemini9a.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Gemini_9A" title="Gemini 9A">Gemini 9A</a> space capsule</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sigma7-1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Sigma7-1.jpg/220px-Sigma7-1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Sigma7-1.jpg/330px-Sigma7-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Sigma7-1.jpg/440px-Sigma7-1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sigma7-1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Mercury-Atlas_8" title="Mercury-Atlas 8">Sigma 7</a></i>, flown in 1962 by <a href="/wiki/Wally_Schirra" title="Wally Schirra">Wally Schirra</a> and shown on display in 2007, is now exhibited at Heroes &amp; Legends</div></div></div>
<p>The United States Astronaut Hall of Fame is now located in Heroes &amp; Legends, which replaced the previous Early Space Exploration exhibit inside the visitor complex's main entrance. The $20 million exhibit, which opened in 2016, focuses on America's first astronauts and nine characteristics of a hero. Each characteristic features astronaut artifacts and multimedia relevant to that characteristic. Key artifacts include the <a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">Gemini</a> <a href="/wiki/Gemini_9A" title="Gemini 9A">9A</a> capsule, the original NASA emblem from the <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Control_Center" title="Mercury Control Center">Mercury Control Center</a> (1959), the control center from <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Project Mercury</a> (remaining from Early Space Exploration), Mercury <a href="/wiki/Redstone_(rocket_family)" title="Redstone (rocket family)">Redstone Rocket</a> MR-6 (likely intended for <a href="/wiki/Deke_Slayton" title="Deke Slayton">Deke Slayton</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Wally_Schirra" title="Wally Schirra">Wally Schirra</a>'s  <i><a href="/wiki/Mercury-Atlas_8" title="Mercury-Atlas 8">Sigma 7</a></i> capsule. There are also artifacts from specific astronauts, such as <a href="/wiki/Gus_Grissom" title="Gus Grissom">Gus Grissom</a>’s suborbital flight suit from July 21, 1961.
</p><p>Heroes &amp; Legends also holds the U.S. Astronaut Hall of Fame, displaying the inducted men and women. In the center of the hall is an interactive kiosk with an inductee database, a mission index, and a virtual photo opportunity with the <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Seven" title="Mercury Seven">Mercury 7 astronauts</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing Company</a> is the title sponsor of Heroes &amp; Legends, which marked the first time the visitor complex entered an agreement with a corporate sponsor in more than 50 years.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Astronaut_Hall_of_Fame">U.S. Astronaut Hall of Fame</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: U.S. Astronaut Hall of Fame">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Astronaut_Hall_of_Fame" title="United States Astronaut Hall of Fame">United States Astronaut Hall of Fame</a></div>
<p>The United States Astronaut Hall of Fame, located inside the Heroes &amp; Legends building, is included with visitor complex daily admission.  The Hall of Fame was previously owned and operated by the U.S. Space Camp Foundation, but was purchased at auction by <a href="/wiki/Delaware_North_Companies" class="mw-redirect" title="Delaware North Companies">Delaware North Park Services</a> in September 2002 on behalf of NASA. The building was renamed the ATX Center and houses educational programs including Camp Kennedy Space Center and the Astronaut Training Experience.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

/></a></div><div class="thumbcaption">The rocket garden during Christmas</div></div></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rocket_Garden" class="mw-redirect" title="Rocket Garden">Rocket Garden</a> is located inside the front entrance, beyond Heroes &amp; Legends.It is an outdoor display of historic rockets that put Americans and satellites in space. Visitors can walk up to and around the base of the rockets. All of the rockets in the garden are legitimate rockets with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Mercury-Atlas" title="Mercury-Atlas">Mercury-Atlas</a>, which is a re-creation replica. The <a href="/wiki/Mercury-Redstone" class="mw-redirect" title="Mercury-Redstone">Mercury-Redstone</a>, Mercury-Atlas, and <a href="/wiki/Titan_II_GLV" title="Titan II GLV">Titan II</a> rockets launched astronauts and the <a href="/wiki/Juno_I" title="Juno I">Juno I</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juno_II" title="Juno II">Juno II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thor-Delta" title="Thor-Delta">Thor-Delta</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Atlas-Agena" title="Atlas-Agena">Atlas-Agena</a> rockets launched satellites from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station">Cape Canaveral</a>.  These are mounted upright whereas the largest rocket, a <a href="/wiki/Saturn_IB" title="Saturn IB">Saturn IB</a>, is mounted on its side. Saturn IB rockets launched <a href="/wiki/Project_Apollo" class="mw-redirect" title="Project Apollo">Apollo</a> Command/Service Modules into Earth orbit for Apollo, <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">Skylab</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Apollo%E2%80%93Soyuz_Test_Project" title="Apollo–Soyuz Test Project">Apollo–Soyuz Test Project</a>.  The Saturn IB is being restored in 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Juno I on display is painted with serial number "UE", a reference to the vehicle that launched the first US satellite, <i><a href="/wiki/Explorer_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Explorer I">Explorer I</a></i>.  A Juno II launched the first American probe to escape Earth's gravity and fly past the Moon.  Atlas-Agena rockets launched early probes to the Moon, <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Agena_target_vehicle" title="Agena target vehicle">Agena target vehicles</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Space_rendezvous" title="Space rendezvous">rendezvous</a> and docking by Gemini spacecraft—a necessary technique for the following Apollo missions.  The Thor-Delta was one of the most reliable and frequently used launch vehicles. The Titan II on display is a refurbished <a href="/wiki/LGM-25C_Titan_II" title="LGM-25C Titan II">Air Force ICBM</a> with a replica Gemini spacecraft, painted to resemble the <a href="/wiki/Gemini_3" title="Gemini 3">Gemini 3</a> booster. It was rescued from the <a href="/wiki/309th_Aerospace_Maintenance_and_Regeneration_Group" title="309th Aerospace Maintenance and Regeneration Group">Arizona Boneyard</a> and erected in 2010 to replace a deteriorating mockup composed of two first stages which had been on display for more than 20 years.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The Saturn IB on display is SA-209 which was designated for a possible <a href="/wiki/Skylab_Rescue" title="Skylab Rescue">Skylab Rescue</a> mission.
</p><p>The garden also features mock-ups of capsules from the <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">Gemini</a>, and Apollo programs that visitors can get in. An <a href="/wiki/F-1_(rocket_engine)" class="mw-redirect" title="F-1 (rocket engine)">F-1</a> rocket engine that powered the first stage of the Saturn V is also on display.  Free guided tours of the garden are available daily.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2019, visitor complex official Therrin Protze offered placement of a <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">Falcon 9</a> or <a href="/wiki/Falcon_Heavy" title="Falcon Heavy">Falcon Heavy</a> booster in the garden to <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a>: "We have the space available and the capability to make it happen."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> SpaceX chief executive <a href="/wiki/Elon_Musk" title="Elon Musk">Elon Musk</a> responded expressing interest in the offer.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span id="Apollo.2FSaturn_V_Center"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo/Saturn_V_Center">Apollo/Saturn V Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Apollo/Saturn V Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:KSC_Visitor_Complex_Saturn_V_Center_1998.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/31/KSC_Visitor_Complex_Saturn_V_Center_1998.jpg/220px-KSC_Visitor_Complex_Saturn_V_Center_1998.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/31/KSC_Visitor_Complex_Saturn_V_Center_1998.jpg/330px-KSC_Visitor_Complex_Saturn_V_Center_1998.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/31/KSC_Visitor_Complex_Saturn_V_Center_1998.jpg/440px-KSC_Visitor_Complex_Saturn_V_Center_1998.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2617" data-file-height="1722" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:KSC_Visitor_Complex_Saturn_V_Center_1998.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aerial view of the Apollo/Saturn V Center from 1998</div></div></div>

</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SaturnVcenter.JPG" class="image"><img alt="The Saturn V rocket is displayed horizontally, its engines at the left and the command module at its top on the right. <p>The Apollo/Saturn V Center <span class="nowrap">(<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex&amp;params=28.6055_N_80.6696_W_region:US-FL_type:landmark&amp;title=Apollo%2FSaturn+V+Center"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">28°36′20″N</span> <span class="longitude">80°40′11″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#xfeff; / &#xfeff;</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">28.6055°N 80.6696°W</span><span style="display:none">&#xfeff; / <span class="geo">28.6055; -80.6696</span></span><span style="display:none">&#xfeff; (<span class="fn org">Apollo/Saturn V Center</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>)</span> is located north-northwest of <a href="/wiki/Launch_Complex_39" class="mw-redirect" title="Launch Complex 39">Launch Complex 39</a> on the Kennedy Parkway N near the <a href="/wiki/Shuttle_Landing_Facility" title="Shuttle Landing Facility">Shuttle Landing Facility</a> and is only accessible to visitors by bus tours from the Visitors Complex. The center, which opened December 17, 1996,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> was designed by <a href="/wiki/Bob_Rogers_(designer)" title="Bob Rogers (designer)">Bob Rogers</a> and the design team BRC Imagination Arts,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> for NASA and Delaware North Companies. The opening of the exhibit was historic for NASA as it was the first large exhibit to be opened inside a restricted area, only accessible by Kennedy Space Center tour buses.<sup id="cite_ref-kennedyspacecenter.com_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kennedyspacecenter.com-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 100,000-square-foot<sup id="cite_ref-kennedyspacecenter.com_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kennedyspacecenter.com-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> facility was built to house a restored <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> launch vehicle and features other exhibits related to the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a>.  Until the structure was built, the Saturn V was displayed horizontally for many years outdoors just south of the <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_Assembly_Building" title="Vehicle Assembly Building">Vehicle Assembly Building</a> and tour buses brought visitors to it.
</p><p>Other large exhibits include the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_14" title="Apollo 14">Apollo 14</a> command module, which orbited the Moon 34 times in 1971,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> an unused <a href="/wiki/Apollo_command_and_service_module" title="Apollo command and service module">Apollo command and service module</a> <a href="/wiki/Skylab_Rescue" title="Skylab Rescue">(CSM-119)</a> and an unused <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Module" class="mw-redirect" title="Lunar Module">Lunar Module</a> (LM-9).  CSM-119 was designated for a possible <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">Skylab</a> rescue mission and as a backup for the <a href="/wiki/Apollo%E2%80%93Soyuz_Test_Project" title="Apollo–Soyuz Test Project">Apollo–Soyuz Test Project</a>. LM-9 was originally scheduled to be used on <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">Apollo 15</a>, but when missions after <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">Apollo 17</a> were <a href="/wiki/Canceled_Apollo_missions" title="Canceled Apollo missions">canceled</a>, a later LM (LM-10) was used instead.
</p><p>Also on display is a slice of <a href="/wiki/Moon_rock" title="Moon rock">Moon rock</a> that visitors can touch.  Other exhibits include a replica of the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Roving_Vehicle" title="Lunar Roving Vehicle">Lunar Roving Vehicle</a>, a variety of space suits including <a href="/wiki/Alan_Shepard" title="Alan Shepard">Alan Shepard</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Apollo_14" title="Apollo 14">Apollo 14</a> extravehicular suit, a piece of <a href="/wiki/Apollo_13" title="Apollo 13">Apollo 13</a>'s Lunar Module returned by the astronauts, lunar samples from <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">Apollo 15</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">Apollo 17</a>, and a large cut-away scale model of the Saturn V.  Two theaters allow visitors to relive parts of the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a> – one simulates the environment inside an Apollo-era firing room during the launch of <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a> and the other simulates the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> landing.
</p><p>In January 2017, "Ad Astra Per Aspera – A Rough Road Leads to the Stars" opened in the Apollo/Saturn V Center to commemorate the fallen astronauts of <a href="/wiki/Apollo_1" title="Apollo 1">Apollo 1</a>. The permanent tribute showcases personal memorabilia from the three astronauts, with photos and video from their professional and personal lives. The exhibit also displays the charred three-section Block I hatch from the fire, and a redesigned Block II hatch. The Block II hatch flew on all following Apollo missions that could open quicker in the event of an emergency.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Journey_to_Mars:_Explorers_Wanted">Journey to Mars: Explorers Wanted</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Journey to Mars: Explorers Wanted">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, the attraction Exploration Space; Explorers Wanted, also designed by BRC Imagination Arts, functioned as part immersive experience, and part futuristic recruitment center.  It is now known as Journey to Mars: Explorers Wanted with a focus on Mars exploration. The attraction, which employs large-scale video projections, dimensional exhibits and interactive experiences, is designed to immerse visitors into the adventure and unsolved challenges of future space exploration. The exhibit includes orbital docketing and lunar landing simulators, a full-sized development model of a crew vehicle, a model of a space exploration vehicle, and models of the Mars rover family: <i><a href="/wiki/Curiosity_(rover)" title="Curiosity (rover)">Curiosity</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Mars_Exploration_Rover" title="Mars Exploration Rover"><i>Spirit</i>, <i>Opportunity</i></a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Sojourner_(rover)" title="Sojourner (rover)">Sojourner</a></i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NASA_Now">NASA Now</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: NASA Now">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>NASA Now is an exhibit that has a revolving display of spacecraft from NASA and its commercial partners. NASA Now, as of summer 2017, has the space-flown <a href="/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)" title="Orion (spacecraft)">Orion</a> EFT-1 designed for NASA's future <a href="/wiki/Space_Launch_System" title="Space Launch System">Space Launch System (SLS)</a> and a scale model of an SLS rocket. There is also a full-scale model of the crew vehicle <a href="/wiki/CST-100_Starliner" class="mw-redirect" title="CST-100 Starliner">CST-100 Starliner</a> by <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a>. <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a>’s space-flown COTS-2 <a href="/wiki/SpaceX_Dragon" title="SpaceX Dragon">Dragon capsule</a>, which is the first commercial vehicle to dock with the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>, is also featured. A pressure vessel for CST-100 Starliner, a scale model of the <a href="/wiki/Dream_Chaser" title="Dream Chaser">Dream Chaser</a> cargo vehicle from <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_Corporation" title="Sierra Nevada Corporation">Sierra Nevada Corporation</a>, a scale model of <a href="/wiki/United_Launch_Alliance" title="United Launch Alliance">United Launch Alliance</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a> rocket, and a Vector-R rocket from <a href="/wiki/Vector_Space_Systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Vector Space Systems">Vector Space Systems</a> are also all in the exhibit.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> NASA Now is located inside the IMAX theater.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_Mirror_Memorial">Space Mirror Memorial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Space Mirror Memorial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Space_Mirror_Memorial" title="Space Mirror Memorial">Space Mirror Memorial</a></div>
<p>The Space Mirror Memorial, also known as the Astronaut Memorial, is maintained by the Astronauts Memorial Foundation and is located behind the IMAX theater on the grounds of the main Visitor Complex.  It honors NASA astronauts along with several military and civilian astronauts who have died in the line of duty.
</p>
<h3><span id="Robot_Scouts_.28closed.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Robot_Scouts_(closed)">Robot Scouts (closed)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Robot Scouts (closed)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1999, the attraction "Robot Scouts" opened as a walk-through exhibit highlighting NASA's unmanned planetary robot probes.<sup id="cite_ref-Robot_Scouts_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robot_Scouts-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The attraction, designed originally by award-winning experience designer <a href="/wiki/Bob_Rogers_(designer)" title="Bob Rogers (designer)">Bob Rogers (designer)</a> and the design team BRC Imagination Arts,<sup id="cite_ref-Robot_Scouts_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robot_Scouts-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> explores how robots help to pave the way for human spaceflight. During their visit, guests were guided through the attraction by a robot named StarQuester 2000, who explained to them the mission of the "Robot Scouts: Trailblazers for Human Exploration", with the help of the other then-active robots, including the Voyagers, the Vikings, Cassini, and even the Hubble Space Telescope. The experience culminated in a visit to a diorama of a Mars base, which even included a simulated Martian sunset.  The attraction is now closed, and replaced by a new more enhanced and activity-packed scout program.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Early_Space_Exploration_.28closed.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Space_Exploration_(closed)">Early Space Exploration (closed)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Early Space Exploration (closed)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1996, "Early Space Exploration" opened as an exhibit at the Kennedy Space Center in the far corner of the Rocket Garden near where the Visitor Complex's current entrance is now. As its name suggests, this exhibit celebrates the early years of American space exploration, with TVs displaying news broadcasts from the 1960s that described the events of certain flights as well as other memorabilia commemorating the time. The exhibit also featured the actual consoles from the Mercury Mission Control Center. This exhibit was closed in 2014 and transformed into Heroes &amp; Legends, with many of the items from the original Astronaut Hall of Fame relocated to here. The Mercury Mission Control Consoles were also kept from the aforementioned exhibit.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_events_and_programs">Special events and programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Special events and programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Visitors Complex also hosts special ticketed events run by Delaware North.  <a href="/wiki/Naturalization" title="Naturalization">Naturalization</a> ceremonies have been conducted in the Rocket Garden. Space shuttle launch viewing was offered and now rocket launch viewing, from the visitor complex, offering close views of the <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center_Launch_Complex_39" title="Kennedy Space Center Launch Complex 39">launch pads</a>. The U.S. Astronaut Hall of Fame hosts a yearly induction ceremony.<sup id="cite_ref-naturalization_ceremony_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-naturalization_ceremony-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The Astronaut Training Experience is also offered by Delaware North at the Astronaut Training Experience Center with several full-sized mock-ups of the space shuttle, Mission Control and training hardware.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The complex also runs week-long accredited day camps for children in grades 2–9.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The visitor complex offers events all year, ranging from astronaut presentations and signing opportunities, special guest appearances, and anniversary celebrations.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> for "<a href="/wiki/Walking_on_the_Moon" title="Walking on the Moon">Walking on the Moon</a>" by <a href="/wiki/The_Police" title="The Police">The Police</a> was shot at the Kennedy Space Center on 23 October 1979. It features the band members miming to the track amidst spacecraft displays, interspersed with NASA footage. Stewart Copeland strikes his drumsticks on a Saturn V Moon rocket. Also, the music video for the 1992 <a href="/wiki/Eurodance" title="Eurodance">Eurodance</a> song "<a href="/wiki/Rhythm_is_a_Dancer" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhythm is a Dancer">Rhythm is a Dancer</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Snap!" title="Snap!">Snap!</a> was filmed at Kennedy Space Center Visitor Complex's rocket garden.
</p>
