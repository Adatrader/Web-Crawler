	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">O Florida, Venereal Soil</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>"<b>O Florida, Venereal Soil</b>" is a poem from <a href="/wiki/Wallace_Stevens" title="Wallace Stevens">Wallace Stevens</a>'s first book of poetry, <i><a href="/wiki/Harmonium_(poetry_collection)" title="Harmonium (poetry collection)">Harmonium</a></i>. It was first published in the journal <i>Dial</i>, volume 73, July 1922,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and is therefore in the public domain.
</p>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="; background-color: lightyellow;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r887775652">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%;max-width:100%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-title" style="background-color: lightyellow;">O Florida, Venereal Soil</div>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style=""><div class="poem">
<p>A few things for themselves,<br />
Convolvulus and coral,<br />
Buzzards and live-moss,<br />
Tiestas from the keys,<br />
A few things for themselves,<br />
Florida, venereal soil,<br />
Disclose to the lover.<br />
<br />
The dreadful sundry of this world,<br />
The Cuban, Polodowsky,<br />
The Mexican women,<br />
The negro undertaker<br />
Killing the time between corpses<br />
Fishing for crayfish...<br />
Virgin of boorish births,<br />
<br />
Swiftly in the nights,<br />
In the porches of Key West,<br />
Behind the bougainvilleas,<br />
After the guitar is asleep,<br />
Lasciviously as the wind,<br />
You come tormenting,<br />
Insatiable,<br />
<br />
When you might sit,<br />
A scholar of darkness,<br />
Sequestered over the sea,<br />
Wearing a clear tiara<br />
Of red and blue and red,<br />
Sparkling, solitary, still,<br />
In the high sea-shadow.<br />
<br />
Donna, donna, dark,<br />
Stooping in indigo gown<br />
And cloudy constellations,<br />
Conceal yourself or disclose<br />
Fewest things to the lover ---<br />
A hand that bears a thick-leaved fruit,<br />
A pungent bloom against your shade.
</p>
</div></div>
</div>
<p>Buttel interprets the poem as continuous with <a href="/wiki/Baudelaire" class="mw-redirect" title="Baudelaire">Baudelaire</a>'s sense of the opposition between the corruptibility of the flesh and the perfection of an ideal world.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
The "few things for themselves" that Stevens lists in the first stanza are not philosophical generics like Aristotle's conception of what's worth doing for its own sake (philosophy) or Hobbes's list of appetitive desires that motivate all human beings. Stevens's list rather is highly specific and opaque to the reader whose imagination may not be piqued by buzzards, etc. One has to look beyond this opacity to whatever piques one's imagination, however quirky that may be to others. The same holds true for the "dreadful sundry of this world" listed in the second stanza. The poem is about Stevens's subjective response to <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, and he doesn't do any generalizing so as to share an abstract content with the reader.
</p><p>The word "venereal" refers to Venus, not to the sexual disease, as in <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">Milton</a>'s "venereal trains" (Milton, <i><a href="/wiki/Samson_Agonistes" title="Samson Agonistes">Samson Agonistes</a></i>, 533).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> She sometimes carries symbolically fertile fruit in her hand.
</p><p>However much the poet may be distracted by lascivious particulars, he does indeed want particulars: A hand that bears a thick-leaved fruit, A pungent bloom against donna's shade. This insistence on particularity is a familiar theme in Stevens. (See his treatment of beauty in "<a href="/wiki/Peter_Quince_at_the_Clavier" title="Peter Quince at the Clavier">Peter Quince at the Clavier</a>", for example.) Bates reads the poem as Stevens's wish that Florida "were less the harlot and more the sequestered inamorata".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> (His donna may be compared to the princess of the sea in "<a href="/wiki/Infanta_Marina" title="Infanta Marina">Infanta Marina</a>".)
</p><p>Buttel classifies it as one of the later poems in <i>Harmonium</i>, displaying "an extravagance of conception and an energy of language and tone that approach the violences of imagination that Stevens sought but had not found in the earlier poems."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He credits these later poems with establishing "an over-all development" in the book.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
