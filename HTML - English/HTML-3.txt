<p><b>Ybor City</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/iː/: &#39;ee&#39; in &#39;fleece&#39;">iː</span><span title="&#39;b&#39; in &#39;buy&#39;">b</span><span title="/ɔːr/: &#39;ar&#39; in &#39;war&#39;">ɔːr</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">EE</span>-bor</i></a>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is a historic neighborhood in <a href="/wiki/Tampa,_Florida" title="Tampa, Florida">Tampa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, located just northeast of <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Tampa" title="Downtown Tampa">downtown</a>. It was founded in the 1880s by <a href="/wiki/Vicente_Martinez-Ybor" class="mw-redirect" title="Vicente Martinez-Ybor">Vicente Martinez-Ybor</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Cigar" title="Cigar">cigar</a> manufacturers and was populated by thousands of immigrants, mainly from <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. For the next 50 years, workers in Ybor City's cigar factories rolled hundreds of millions of cigars annually.
</p><p>Ybor City was unique in the <a href="/wiki/American_South" class="mw-redirect" title="American South">American South</a> as a successful town almost entirely populated and owned by <a href="/wiki/Immigrant" class="mw-redirect" title="Immigrant">immigrants</a>. The neighborhood had features unusual among contemporary communities in the south, most notably its multi-ethnic and multi-racial population and their many <a href="/wiki/Mutual_aid_society" class="mw-redirect" title="Mutual aid society">mutual aid societies</a>. The cigar industry employed thousands of well-paid workers, helping Tampa grow from an economically depressed village to a bustling city in about 20 years and giving it the nickname "Cigar City".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ybor City grew and flourished from the 1890s until the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> of the 1930s, when a drop in demand for fine cigars reduced the number of cigar factories and <a href="/wiki/Mechanization" title="Mechanization">mechanization</a> in the cigar industry greatly reduced employment opportunities in the neighborhood. This process accelerated after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, and a steady exodus of residents and businesses continued until large areas of the formerly vibrant neighborhood were virtually abandoned by the late 1970s. Attempts at redevelopment failed until the 1980s, when an influx of artists began a slow process of <a href="/wiki/Gentrification" title="Gentrification">gentrification</a>. In the 1990s and early 2000s, a portion of the original neighborhood around 7th Avenue developed into a <a href="/wiki/Night_club" class="mw-redirect" title="Night club">night club</a> and entertainment district, and many old buildings were renovated for new uses. Since then, the area's economy has diversified with more offices and residences, and the population has shown notable growth for the first time in over half a century.
</p><p>Ybor City has been designated as a <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmark</a> <a href="/wiki/Historic_district_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Historic district (United States)">District</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Registered_Historic_Places_in_Hillsborough_County,_Florida" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Registered Historic Places in Hillsborough County, Florida">several structures</a> in the area are listed in the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>. In 2008, 7th Avenue, the main commercial thoroughfare in Ybor City, was recognized as one of the "10 Great Streets in America" by the <a href="/wiki/American_Planning_Association" title="American Planning Association">American Planning Association</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In 2010 <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Restaurant" title="Columbia Restaurant">Columbia Restaurant</a> was named a "Top 50 All-American icon" by <i><a href="/wiki/Nation%27s_Restaurant_News" title="Nation&#39;s Restaurant News">Nation's Restaurant News</a></i> magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment">Establishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ybor_City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Establishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the early 1880s, Tampa was an isolated village with a population of less than 1000 and a struggling economy.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> However, its combination of a good port, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Plant">Henry Plant's</a> new railroad line, and humid climate attracted the attention of <a href="/wiki/Vicente_Martinez_Ybor" title="Vicente Martinez Ybor">Vicente Martinez Ybor</a>, a prominent Spanish <a href="/wiki/Cigar" title="Cigar">cigar</a> manufacturer.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ybor had moved his cigar-making operation from <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> to <a href="/wiki/Key_West,_Florida" class="mw-redirect" title="Key West, Florida">Key West, Florida</a>, in 1869, due to political turmoil in the then-Spanish colony. But, labor unrest and the lack of room for expansion had him looking for another base of operations, preferably in his own <a href="/wiki/Company_town" title="Company town">company town</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ybormuseum.org_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ybormuseum.org-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jose_Marti_in_Ybor_City.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Jose_Marti_in_Ybor_City.jpg/220px-Jose_Marti_in_Ybor_City.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Jose_Marti_in_Ybor_City.jpg/330px-Jose_Marti_in_Ybor_City.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/89/Jose_Marti_in_Ybor_City.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="284" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jose_Marti_in_Ybor_City.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mart%C3%AD" title="José Martí">José Martí</a> and cigar workers on the steps of <a href="/wiki/Ybor_Factory_Building" title="Ybor Factory Building">V.M. Ybor's factory</a>, 1893</div></div></div>
<p>Ybor considered several communities in the southern United States and decided that an area of sandy scrubland just northeast of Tampa would be the best location. In 1885, the Tampa Board of Trade helped broker an initial purchase of 40 acres (160,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) of land, and Ybor quickly bought more.<sup id="cite_ref-ybormuseum.org_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ybormuseum.org-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> However, Ybor City very nearly didn't happen at all. Vicente Ybor initially failed to come to an agreement with the owner of the 40 acre parcel. The Tampa Board of Trade was horrified to find that the purchase had failed and hatched a plan to get the buyer and seller back together. Vicente Ybor was sitting in the train station on his way to Jacksonville to look at more property when the Board of Trade (a group of five, one of whom was Frederick Salomonson, future 3-time mayor of Tampa) arrived and persuaded Ybor to reconsider and the deal went forward from there, the birth of Ybor City.
</p><p>Cigar making was a specialized trade, and Tampa did not possess a workforce able to man the new factories. To attract employees, Ybor built hundreds of small houses for the coming influx of mainly <a href="/wiki/Cubans" title="Cubans">Cuban</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish people">Spanish</a> cigar workers, many of whom followed him from Key West and Cuba. Other cigar manufacturers, drawn by incentives provided by Ybor to further increase the labor pool, also moved in, quickly making Tampa a major cigar production center.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Italians were also among the early settlers of Ybor City. Most of them came from a few villages in southwestern Sicily. The villages were <a href="/wiki/Santo_Stefano_Quisquina" title="Santo Stefano Quisquina">Santo Stefano Quisquina</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alessandria_della_Rocca" title="Alessandria della Rocca">Alessandria della Rocca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bivona" title="Bivona">Bivona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cianciana" title="Cianciana">Cianciana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Contessa_Entellina" title="Contessa Entellina">Contessa Entellina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Sixty percent of them came from Santo Stefano Quisquina.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Before settling in Ybor City, many first worked in the sugar cane plantations in St. Cloud, central Florida. Some came by way of Louisiana.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> A number of families migrated from New Orleans after the lynching of eleven Italians in 1891 during the "Mafia Riot".<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Italians mostly brought their entire families with them, unlike other immigrants.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The foreign-born Italian population of Tampa grew from 56 in 1890 to 2,684 in 1940.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Once arriving in Ybor City, Italians settled mainly in the eastern and southern fringes of the city. The area was referred to as La Pachata, after a Cuban rent collector in that area. It was also called "Little Italy".<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike Cubans and Spaniards, the Italians arrived in the cigar town without cigar-making skills. When the early Italians entered the factories, it was at the bottom of the ladder, positions which did not involve handling tobacco. Working beside unskilled Cubans, mainly Afro-Cubans, they swept and hauled and were porters and doorkeepers. In time, many did become cigar workers, including Italian women. The majority of the Italian women worked as cigar strippers in 1900, an undesirable position mainly held by women who could find nothing else. However, eventually many of them became skilled cigar makers, earning more than the male Italian cigar makers. Other Italian immigrants started small businesses built around the cigar industry, such as cafés, food stores, restaurants, and boardinghouses.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The least known of the immigrants that came to Ybor City are the Germans,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> the Romanian Jews, and the Chinese. The Chinese and Jews were employed mainly in service trades and retail businesses.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The Germans arrived after the 1890s, and most were businessmen. In the cigar factories, they worked as managers, bookkeepers, and supervisors. Cigar boxes were made by German-owned factories. Several early cigar box labels were made by German lithographers. The Germans formed their own club, the Deutsch Amerikanischer Verein. The club building is still standing on <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.google.com/maps/@27.9633723,-82.451253,3a,65.9y,52.57h,95.64t/data=!3m6!1e1!3m4!1skltN9l9nfEjvSPnjEY6ZYQ!2e0!7i16384!8i8192">Nebraska and 11th Avenue</a>. It contained a restaurant open to the public that served German food. In 1919, because of anti-German feelings from World War I, they sold the building to the Young Men's Hebrew Association. The building is now used as offices for the City of Tampa.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1887, Tampa annexed the neighborhood. By 1900, the rough frontier settlement of wooden buildings and sandy streets had been transformed into a bustling town with brick buildings and streets, a streetcar line, and many social and cultural opportunities. Largely due to the growth of Ybor City, Tampa's population had jumped to almost 16,000.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ybor_Cigar_workers.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Ybor_Cigar_workers.jpg/220px-Ybor_Cigar_workers.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Ybor_Cigar_workers.jpg/330px-Ybor_Cigar_workers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Ybor_Cigar_workers.jpg/440px-Ybor_Cigar_workers.jpg 2x" data-file-width="872" data-file-height="695" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ybor_Cigar_workers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Inside an Ybor City cigar factory ca. 1920</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Golden_age">The Golden age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ybor_City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Golden age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
Ybor City grew and prospered during the first decades of the 20th Century. Thousands of residents built a community that combined Cuban, Spanish, Italian, and Jewish culture. "Ybor City is Tampa's Spanish India," observed a visitor to the area, "What a colorful, screaming, shrill, and turbulent world." <sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tampa_Circulo_Cubano01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Tampa_Circulo_Cubano01.jpg/220px-Tampa_Circulo_Cubano01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Tampa_Circulo_Cubano01.jpg/330px-Tampa_Circulo_Cubano01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Tampa_Circulo_Cubano01.jpg/440px-Tampa_Circulo_Cubano01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tampa_Circulo_Cubano01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Circulo_Cubano_de_Tampa" title="Circulo Cubano de Tampa">Circulo Cubano de Tampa</a>, one of Ybor City's social clubs</div></div></div>
<p>An aspect of life were the mutual aid societies built and sustained mainly by ordinary citizens. These clubs were founded in Ybor's early days (the first was the <a href="/wiki/El_Centro_Espanol_de_Tampa" class="mw-redirect" title="El Centro Espanol de Tampa">Centro Español</a>, established in 1891) and were run on dues collected from their members, usually 5% of a member's salary. In exchange, members and their whole family received services including free libraries, educational programs, sports teams, restaurants, numerous social functions like dances and picnics, and free medical services. Beyond the services, these clubs served as extended families and communal gathering places for generations of Ybor's citizens.
</p><p>There were clubs for each ethnic division in the community – the Deutscher-Americaner Club (for German and eastern Europeans), L'Unione Italiana (for Italians), <a href="/wiki/Circulo_Cubano_de_Tampa" title="Circulo Cubano de Tampa">El Circulo Cubano</a> (for light-skinned Cubans), La Union Marti-Maceo (for darker-skinned Cubans), El Centro Español (for Spaniards), and the largest, <a href="/wiki/Centro_Asturiano_de_Tampa" title="Centro Asturiano de Tampa">El Centro Asturiano</a>, which accepted members from any ethnic group<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although there was little racism in Ybor City, Tampa's <a href="/wiki/Jim_Crow_laws" title="Jim Crow laws">Jim Crow laws</a> at the time forbade <a href="/wiki/Afro-Cubans" title="Afro-Cubans">Afro-Cubans</a> from belonging to the same social organization as their lighter-skinned countrymen. Sometimes, differences in skin color within the same family made joining the same Cuban club impossible.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In general, the rivalries between all the clubs were friendly, and families were known to switch affiliations depending on which one offered preferred services and events.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cuban_Club_Ybor.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Cuban_Club_Ybor.jpg/220px-Cuban_Club_Ybor.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Cuban_Club_Ybor.jpg/330px-Cuban_Club_Ybor.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/17/Cuban_Club_Ybor.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="320" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cuban_Club_Ybor.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cuban Club in Ybor City</div></div></div>
<p>Cigar production reached its peak in 1929, when 500 million cigars were rolled in the factories of Ybor City.<sup id="cite_ref-Lastra_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lastra-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Not coincidentally, that was also the year that the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> began.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_and_rebirth">Decline and rebirth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ybor_City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Decline and rebirth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Depression was a major blow to cigar manufacturers. Worldwide demand plummeted as consumers sought to cut costs by switching to less-expensive cigarettes, and factories responded by laying off workers or shutting down. This trend continued throughout the 1930s as the remaining cigar factories gradually switched from traditional hand-rolled manufacturing to cheaper mechanized methods, further reducing the number of jobs and the salaries paid to workers.<sup id="cite_ref-nps.gov_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nps.gov-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, many returning veterans chose to leave Ybor City due to a lack of well-paying jobs and a US <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs" title="United States Department of Veterans Affairs">Veterans Administration</a> home loan program that was only applicable to new homes, of which there were few in the neighborhood. In fact, the home stock was aging poorly, as many of the structures built in the early days of Ybor City were still in use.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As the historic neighborhood continued to empty out and deteriorate through the 1950s and 1960s, the federal <a href="/wiki/Urban_Renewal" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban Renewal">Urban Renewal</a> program sought to revitalize the area by demolishing older structures and encouraging new residential and commercial development. The demolition took place, but due to a lack of funds, the redevelopment did not happen. The primary legacy of the program was blocks of vacant lots which remained empty for decades. The construction of <a href="/wiki/Interstate_4" title="Interstate 4">Interstate 4</a> through the center of the neighborhood during this period also resulted in the destruction of many buildings and cut most of the north-south routes through the area.<sup id="cite_ref-nps.gov_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nps.gov-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the early 1970s, very few businesses and residents remained, most notably the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Restaurant" title="Columbia Restaurant">Columbia Restaurant</a> and a few other businesses along 7th Avenue.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Recovery">Recovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ybor_City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Recovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the early 1980s, an influx of artists seeking interesting and inexpensive studio quarters started a slow recovery, followed by a period of commercial
<a href="/wiki/Gentrification" title="Gentrification">gentrification</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> By the early 1990s, many of the old long-empty brick buildings on 7th Avenue had been  converted into bars, restaurants, nightclubs, and other nightlife attractions.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>  Traffic grew so much that the city built parking garages and closed 7th Ave. to traffic to deal with the visitors.
</p><p>Since around 2000, the city of Tampa and the Ybor City Chamber of Commerce have encouraged a broader emphasis in development. With financial help from the city, Centro Ybor, a family-oriented shopping complex and movie theater, opened in the former home of the Centro Español social club. New apartments, condominiums and a hotel have been built on long-vacant lots, and old buildings have been restored and converted into residences and hotels. New residents began moving into Ybor City for the first time in many years.<sup id="cite_ref-theplanningcommission.org_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theplanningcommission.org-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The blocks surrounding 7th Avenue also thrive with restaurants, nightlife and shopping.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Reflecting the district's status as a party destination, Ybor City is referenced extensively in the lyrics of Brooklyn-based rock band <a href="/wiki/The_Hold_Steady" title="The Hold Steady">The Hold Steady</a>. The song "Killer Parties", for instance, contains the line "Ybor City is très <a href="/wiki/Amphetamine" title="Amphetamine">speedy</a>, but they throw such killer parties."<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> In May 2009 <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> super-retailer <a href="/wiki/IKEA" title="IKEA">IKEA</a> opened its long-awaited Tampa location in the southern edge of Ybor City.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Boundaries">Boundaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ybor_City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Boundaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, the boundaries of "Greater Ybor City" stretched from <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay" title="Tampa Bay">Tampa Bay</a> on the south to Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. Blvd. (formerly Buffalo Avenue) on the north, and from Nebraska Avenue on the west to 40th Street on the east.<sup id="cite_ref-Lastra_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lastra-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This eventually included all of today's neighborhoods of Historic Ybor, <a href="/wiki/East_Ybor,_Tampa,_Florida" class="mw-redirect" title="East Ybor, Tampa, Florida">East Ybor</a>, <a href="/wiki/V.M._Ybor,_Tampa,_Florida" class="mw-redirect" title="V.M. Ybor, Tampa, Florida">VM Ybor</a>, and Ybor Heights plus a portion of <a href="/wiki/East_Tampa,_Tampa,_Florida" class="mw-redirect" title="East Tampa, Tampa, Florida">East Tampa</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ybor_City_Historic_District" title="Ybor City Historic District">Ybor City Historic District</a> encompasses the central portion of that area, approximately straddling <a href="/wiki/Interstate_4" title="Interstate 4">Interstate 4</a>, which bisected the neighborhood in the 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The official boundaries of the Historic Ybor neighborhood are I-4 to the north, 22nd Street to the east, <a href="/wiki/Florida_State_Road_60" title="Florida State Road 60">Adamo Drive</a> to the south, and Nebraska Avenue to the west.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The area of this district is about 1 square mile (about 2.6&#160;km<sup>2</sup>). Though modern Ybor City also includes some of the surrounding area, its exact dimensions are loosely defined and subject to debate.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ybor_City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the height of its life as a thriving immigrant community, Ybor City's population was numbered in the tens of thousands. In the lowest point in the late 1970s, perhaps 1000 residents called the neighborhood home.<sup id="cite_ref-Lastra_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lastra-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In recent years, the numbers have begun to climb once more. Ybor City's population grew an estimated 42.5% between 2000 and 2003, mainly as a result of new condominium and apartment construction. As of 2003, approximately 2,900 residents lived in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-theplanningcommission.org_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theplanningcommission.org-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy_and_land_use">Economy and land use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ybor_City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Economy and land use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ybor City is one of the oldest sections of Tampa and is almost entirely an urban, built-up area. Commercial property comprises almost 50% of the land, institutional use (including the <a href="/wiki/Hillsborough_County,_Florida_Sheriff%27s_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="Hillsborough County, Florida Sheriff&#39;s Office">Hillsborough County Sheriff's Operations Center</a> and a satellite campus of <a href="/wiki/Hillsborough_Community_College" title="Hillsborough Community College">Hillsborough Community College</a>) 16%, residential use about 23%, and industrial use about 7%  <sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to a 2003 survey, the top five business types in the area were professional services (22.8%), retail (18.4%), manufacturing (14.0%), wholesale/distribution (13.2%), and restaurants &amp; bars (11.4%).<sup id="cite_ref-theplanningcommission.org_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theplanningcommission.org-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ybor_City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For the most part, Ybor City still uses the <a href="/wiki/Grid_plan" title="Grid plan">gridded street system</a> laid out by Gavino Guiterrez in 1885. Many roadways are now paved with modern materials, though a few <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">brick</a> streets remain.
</p><p>Because 21st and 22nd Streets, which cut north-south through the area, were once the main traffic routes between <a href="/wiki/Interstate_4" title="Interstate 4">Interstate 4</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Tampa_Bay" class="mw-redirect" title="Port of Tampa Bay">Port of Tampa Bay</a>, there was a large volume of <a href="/wiki/Semi-trailer_truck" title="Semi-trailer truck">semi-trailer truck</a> traffic funneling through the historic district for many years. This caused damage to narrow city roads, was a danger to pedestrians, and sometimes resulted in trucks colliding with historic buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In 2014, the elevated <a href="/wiki/I-4/Selmon_Expressway_Connector" title="I-4/Selmon Expressway Connector">I-4/Selmon Expressway Connector</a> was built to siphon heavy truck traffic away from the historic district.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_transit">Public transit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ybor_City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Public transit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ybor_City_Streetcar.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Ybor_City_Streetcar.jpg/220px-Ybor_City_Streetcar.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="182" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Ybor_City_Streetcar.jpg/330px-Ybor_City_Streetcar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/21/Ybor_City_Streetcar.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="331" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ybor_City_Streetcar.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Trolley in Ybor City</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/TECO_Line_Streetcar_System" title="TECO Line Streetcar System">TECO Line Streetcar System</a>, which links Ybor City, the <a href="/wiki/Channel_District,_Tampa,_Florida" class="mw-redirect" title="Channel District, Tampa, Florida">Channelside District</a> and <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Tampa" title="Downtown Tampa">Downtown Tampa</a>, began operating in October 2002. The Hillsborough Area Regional Transit Authority (<a href="/wiki/HARTline" class="mw-redirect" title="HARTline">HARTline</a>) operates the <a href="/wiki/Streetcar" class="mw-redirect" title="Streetcar">streetcars</a> as well as the bus system. Small startups have also begun utilizing <a href="/wiki/Neighborhood_Electric_Vehicle" title="Neighborhood Electric Vehicle">NEVs</a> to shuttle passengers between Tampa's core neighborhoods including Ybor.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ybor_City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ybor_City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Ybor City natives <a href="/wiki/Jose_Yglesias" title="Jose Yglesias">Jose Yglesias</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ferdie_Pacheco" title="Ferdie Pacheco">Ferdie Pacheco</a> have each written several books and stories set in their hometown. Most notably, Yglesias' debut novel is entitled <i>A Wake in Ybor City</i>, while Pacheco's memoir of his early life is called <i>Ybor City Chronicles</i>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Anna_in_the_Tropics" title="Anna in the Tropics">Anna in the Tropics</a></i>, the winner of the 2003 <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_for_Drama" title="Pulitzer Prize for Drama">Pulitzer Prize for Drama</a> written by <a href="/wiki/Nilo_Cruz" title="Nilo Cruz">Nilo Cruz</a>, is set in the cigar factories of Ybor City.</li>
<li>Ybor City and Tampa are prominently featured in the novels <i>Live by Night</i> and its sequel <i>World Gone By</i> by <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Lehane" title="Dennis Lehane">Dennis Lehane</a>.</li>
<li>Ybor City and the greater Tampa Bay area is the setting for the novel <i>Only Dangerous In The Breeding Season</i> by John Callaghan.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ybor_City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>The 2016 film <a href="/wiki/Live_by_Night_(film)" title="Live by Night (film)"><i>Live by Night</i></a> (based on the books of Dennis Lehane) is centered on an Ybor City <a href="/wiki/Rum-running" title="Rum-running">bootlegger</a> who becomes a notorious <a href="/wiki/Gangster" title="Gangster">gangster</a> during <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_in_the_United_States" title="Prohibition in the United States">Prohibition</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Crime_drama" class="mw-redirect" title="Crime drama">crime drama</a> was written, directed, and co-produced by <a href="/wiki/Ben_Affleck" title="Ben Affleck">Ben Affleck</a>, who also played the starring role. Although the movie is almost entirely set in Tampa during the early 1930s, much of it was shot in and around <a href="/wiki/Brunswick,_Georgia" title="Brunswick, Georgia">Brunswick, Georgia</a> due to movie production <a href="/wiki/Tax_incentive" title="Tax incentive">tax incentives</a> offered by the state of Georgia and the town's proximity to Affleck's home. Ybor City-inspired signage and decorations were added to buildings in downtown Brunswick, and several new buildings based on locations in Tampa were constructed on empty lots to add to the "fake Ybor City" setting.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ybor_City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>In 2016, the <a href="/wiki/Ad_Council" title="Ad Council">Ad Council</a> and the non-profit Love Has No Labels produced a <a href="/wiki/Public_service_announcement" title="Public service announcement">public service announcement</a> promoting diversity and inclusion starring <a href="/wiki/John_Cena" title="John Cena">John Cena</a> that used Ybor City as a backdrop for a commercial which premiered on July 4, 2016 to coincide with Pride Month. The PSA promotes the importance of acceptance of all communities, regardless of race, religion, gender, sexual orientation, age and ability.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
