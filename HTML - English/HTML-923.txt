<p><b>Click consonants</b>, or clicks, are speech sounds that occur as <a href="/wiki/Consonants" class="mw-redirect" title="Consonants">consonants</a> in many languages of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">Southern Africa</a> and in three languages of <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a>. Examples familiar to English-speakers are the <i><a href="/wiki/Tut-tut" class="mw-redirect" title="Tut-tut">Tut-tut</a></i> (British spelling) or <i>Tsk! Tsk!</i> (American spelling) used to express disapproval or pity, the <i><a href="/wiki/Lateral_click" title="Lateral click">tchick!</a></i> used to spur on a horse, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Alveolar_click" title="Alveolar click">clip-clop!</a></i> sound children make with their tongue to imitate a horse trotting.
</p><p>Technically, clicks are <a href="/wiki/Obstruent" title="Obstruent">obstruents</a> articulated with two closures (points of contact) in the mouth, one forward and one at the back. The enclosed pocket of air is <a href="/wiki/Rarefaction" title="Rarefaction">rarefied</a> by a sucking action of the tongue (in technical terminology, clicks have a <a href="/wiki/Airstream_mechanism" title="Airstream mechanism">lingual ingressive airstream mechanism</a>). The forward closure is then released,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup> producing what may be the loudest consonants in the language, but in some languages such as <a href="/wiki/Hadza_language" title="Hadza language">Hadza</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sandawe_language" title="Sandawe language">Sandawe</a>, clicks can be more subtle and may even be mistaken for <a href="/wiki/Ejective_consonant" title="Ejective consonant">ejectives</a>.
</p>

<p>Click consonants occur at six principal places of articulation. The <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet_(IPA)" class="mw-redirect" title="International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)</a> may represent a click by placing the assigned symbol for five of the places of articulation (there is as yet no dedicated symbol for the sixth) adjacent to a symbol for a non-click sound at the rear place of articulation, or the click symbol may be combined with diacritics for voicing, nasalization, etc. The IPA symbols are used in writing most <a href="/wiki/Khoisan_languages" title="Khoisan languages">Khoisan languages</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Bantu_languages" title="Bantu languages">Bantu languages</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Zulu_language" title="Zulu language">Zulu</a> typically use <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin</a> &#x27e8;c&#x27e9;, &#x27e8;x&#x27e9; and &#x27e8;q&#x27e9; for dental, lateral and alveolar clicks respectively.
</p>
<ul><li>The easiest clicks for English speakers are the <a href="/wiki/Dental_click" title="Dental click">dental clicks</a> written with a single pipe, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǀ</span>. They are all sharp (high-pitched) squeaky sounds made by sucking on the front teeth. A simple dental click is used in English to express pity or to shame someone, and sometimes to call an animal, and is written <i>tut!</i> in British English and <i>tsk!</i> in American English. In Italian this sound means "no" used as an answer to a direct question, while it is used to call cats when repeated several times.</li>
<li>Next most familiar to English speakers are the <a href="/wiki/Lateral_click" title="Lateral click">lateral clicks</a> written with a double pipe, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǁ</span>. They are also squeaky sounds, though less sharp than <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǀ</span>, made by sucking on the molars on either side (or both sides) of the mouth. A simple lateral click is made in English to get a horse moving, and is conventionally written <i>tchick!</i></li>
<li>Then there are the <a href="/wiki/Labial_click" class="mw-redirect" title="Labial click">labial clicks</a>, written with a bull's eye, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ʘ</span>. These are lip-smacking sounds, but without the pursing of the lips found in a kiss.</li></ul>
<p>The above clicks sound like <a href="/wiki/Affricate" class="mw-redirect" title="Affricate">affricates</a>, in that they involve a lot of friction. The other two families are more abrupt sounds that do not have this friction.
</p>
<ul><li>With the <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_click" title="Alveolar click">alveolar clicks</a>, written with an exclamation mark, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǃ</span>, the tip of the tongue is pulled down abruptly and forcefully from the roof of the mouth, sometimes using a lot of jaw motion, and making a hollow <i>pop!</i> like a cork being pulled from an empty bottle. These sounds can be quite loud.</li>
<li>Finally, for most languages, the <a href="/wiki/Palatal_click" title="Palatal click">palatal clicks</a>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǂ</span>, are made with a flat tongue, and are sharper popping sounds than the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǃ</span> clicks, like sharply snapped fingers.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages_with_clicks">Languages with clicks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Languages with clicks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Southern_Africa">Southern Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Southern Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Clicks occur in all three <a href="/wiki/Khoisan_languages" title="Khoisan languages">Khoisan language families</a> of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">southern Africa</a>, where they may be the most numerous consonants. To a lesser extent they occur in three neighbouring groups of <a href="/wiki/Bantu_languages" title="Bantu languages">Bantu languages</a>—which <a href="/wiki/Sprachbund" title="Sprachbund">borrowed them</a>, directly or indirectly, from Khoisan. In the southeast, in eastern <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swaziland" class="mw-redirect" title="Swaziland">Swaziland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lesotho" title="Lesotho">Lesotho</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>, they were adopted from a <a href="/wiki/Tuu_languages" title="Tuu languages">Tuu language</a> or languages by the languages of the Nguni cluster (especially <a href="/wiki/Zulu_language" title="Zulu language">Zulu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xhosa_language" title="Xhosa language">Xhosa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phuthi_language" title="Phuthi language">Phuthi</a>, but also to a lesser extent <a href="/wiki/Swati_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Swati language">Swazi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ndebele_language" title="Southern Ndebele language">Ndebele</a>), and spread from them in a reduced fashion to the Zulu-based <a href="/wiki/Pidgin" title="Pidgin">pidgin</a> <a href="/wiki/Fanagalo" title="Fanagalo">Fanagalo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sesotho_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Sesotho language">Sesotho</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tsonga_language" title="Tsonga language">Tsonga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ronga_language" title="Ronga language">Ronga</a>, the Mzimba dialect of <a href="/wiki/Tumbuka_language" title="Tumbuka language">Tumbuka</a> and more recently to <a href="/wiki/Ndau_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ndau language">Ndau</a> and urban varieties of <a href="/wiki/Pedi_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Pedi language">Pedi</a>, where the spread of clicks continues. The second point of transfer was near the <a href="/wiki/Caprivi_Strip" title="Caprivi Strip">Caprivi Strip</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Okavango_River" title="Okavango River">Okavango River</a> where, apparently, the <a href="/wiki/Yeyi_language" title="Yeyi language">Yeyi language</a> borrowed the clicks from a <a href="/wiki/Khoe_languages" title="Khoe languages">West Kalahari Khoe language</a>; a separate development led to a smaller click inventory in the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Mbukushu_language" title="Mbukushu language">Mbukushu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kwangali_language" title="Kwangali language">Kwangali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gciriku_language" title="Gciriku language">Gciriku</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuhane_language" title="Kuhane language">Kuhane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fwe_language" title="Fwe language">Fwe</a> languages in <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> These sounds occur not only in borrowed vocabulary, but have spread to native Bantu words as well, in the case of Nguni at least partially due to a type of word taboo called <a href="/wiki/Hlonipha" class="mw-redirect" title="Hlonipha">hlonipha</a>. Some <a href="/wiki/Creolised" class="mw-redirect" title="Creolised">creolised</a> varieties of Afrikaans, such as <a href="/wiki/Oorlams_Creole" title="Oorlams Creole">Oorlams</a>, retain clicks in Khoekhoe words.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Africa">East Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: East Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Three languages in <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a> use clicks: <a href="/wiki/Sandawe_language" title="Sandawe language">Sandawe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hadza_language" title="Hadza language">Hadza</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dahalo_language" title="Dahalo language">Dahalo</a>, an endangered South <a href="/wiki/Cushitic_languages" title="Cushitic languages">Cushitic language</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> that has clicks in only a few dozen words. It is thought the latter may remain from an episode of <a href="/wiki/Language_shift" title="Language shift">language shift</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Damin">Damin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Damin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The only non-African language known to have clicks as regular speech sounds is <a href="/wiki/Damin" title="Damin">Damin</a>, a ritual code once used by speakers of <a href="/wiki/Lardil_language" title="Lardil language">Lardil</a> in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. One of the clicks in Damin is actually an <a href="/wiki/Egressive_sound" title="Egressive sound">egressive</a> click, using the tongue to compress the air in the mouth for an outward (egressive) "spurt".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spread_of_clicks_from_loanwords">Spread of clicks from loanwords</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Spread of clicks from loanwords">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Once clicks are borrowed into a language as regular speech sounds, they may spread to native words, as has happened due to <i><a href="/wiki/Hlonipa" class="mw-redirect" title="Hlonipa">hlonipa</a></i> word-taboo in the <a href="/wiki/Nguni_languages" title="Nguni languages">Nguni languages</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Gciriku_language" title="Gciriku language">Gciriku</a>, for example, the European loanword <i>tomate</i> (tomato) appears as <i>cumáte</i> with a click <i>c</i>, though it begins with a <i>t</i> in all neighbouring languages.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marginal_usage_of_clicks">Marginal usage of clicks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Marginal usage of clicks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scattered clicks are found in <a href="/wiki/Ideophone" title="Ideophone">ideophones</a> and mimesis in other languages, such as <a href="/wiki/Kongo_language" title="Kongo language">Kongo</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ᵑǃ/</span>, <a href="/wiki/Mijikenda_language" title="Mijikenda language">Mijikenda</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ᵑǀ/</span> and Hadza <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʘ̃ʷ/</span> (Hadza does not otherwise have labial clicks). Ideophones often use phonemic distinctions not found in normal vocabulary.
</p><p>English and many other languages may use bare clicks in <a href="/wiki/Interjection" title="Interjection">interjections</a>, without the accompaniment of vowels, such as the dental "tsk-tsk" sound used to express disapproval, or the lateral <i>tchick</i> used with horses. In <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">Armenian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Levantine_Arabic" title="Levantine Arabic">Levantine Arabic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maltese_language" title="Maltese language">Maltese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>, occasionally in <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, as well as southern Italian languages such as <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">Sicilian</a>, in which a bare dental click accompanied by tipping the head upwards signifies "no". <a href="/wiki/Libyan_Arabic" title="Libyan Arabic">Libyan Arabic</a> apparently has three such sounds.
</p><p>Clicks occasionally turn up elsewhere, as in the special <a href="/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)" title="Register (sociolinguistics)">registers</a> twins sometimes develop with each other. In <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>, clicks have been reported allophonically, and similarly in French and German, faint clicks have been recorded in rapid speech where consonants such as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/t/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/k/</span> overlap between words.<sup id="cite_ref-germ_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-germ-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Rwanda_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Rwanda language">Rwanda</a>, the sequence <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/mŋ/</span> may be pronounced either with an epenthetic vowel, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[mᵊŋ]</span>, or with a light bilabial click, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[mʘ̃ŋ]</span>—often by the same speaker.
</p><p>Speakers of <a href="/wiki/Gan_Chinese" title="Gan Chinese">Gan Chinese</a> from <a href="/wiki/Ningdu" class="mw-redirect" title="Ningdu">Ningdu</a> county, as well as speakers of Mandarin from Beijing and <a href="/wiki/Jilin" title="Jilin">Jilin</a> and presumably people from other parts of the country, produce flapped nasal clicks in nursery rhymes with varying degrees of competence, in the words for 'goose' and 'duck', both of which begin with <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ŋ/</span> in Gan and until recently began with <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ŋ/</span> in Mandarin as well. In Gan, the nursery rhyme is,
</p>
<dl><dd><i>tʰien i tsʰak ᵑǃ¡o</i> 'a goose in the sky'</dd>
<dd><i>ti ha i tsʰak ᵑǃ¡a</i> 'a duck on the ground'</dd>
<dd><i>ᵑǃ¡o saŋ ᵑǃ¡o tʰan, ᵑǃ¡o pʰau ᵑǃ¡o</i> 'a goose lays a goose egg, a goose hatches a goose'</dd>
<dd><i>ᵑǃ¡a saŋ ᵑǃ¡a tʰan, ᵑǃ¡a pʰau ᵑǃ¡a</i> 'a duck lays a duck egg, a duck hatches a duck'</dd></dl>
<p>where the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ŋ/</span> onsets are all pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǃ̃¡]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Occasionally other languages are claimed to have click sounds in general vocabulary. This is usually a misnomer for <a href="/wiki/Ejective_consonant" title="Ejective consonant">ejective consonants</a>, which are found across much of the world.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Position_in_word">Position in word</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Position in word">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For the most part, the Southern African <a href="/wiki/Khoisan_languages" title="Khoisan languages">Khoisan languages</a> only use <a href="/wiki/Root_word" class="mw-redirect" title="Root word">root</a>-initial clicks.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;note 2&#93;</a></sup> Hadza, Sandawe and several <a href="/wiki/Bantu_languages" title="Bantu languages">Bantu languages</a> also allow <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">syllable</a>-initial clicks within roots. In no language does a click close a syllable or end a word, but then the languages that happen to have clicks have mostly CV syllables and allow at most only a limited set of consonants (such as a nasal or a glottal stop) to close a syllable or end a word, most consonants share the distribution of clicks in these languages.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_clicks_used">Types of clicks used</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Types of clicks used">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Khoesan families (Tuu, Kxʼa and Khoe) all have at least four click types, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">{ǀ ǁ ǃ ǂ</span>} or variants thereof. A few have five, with bilabial <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">{ʘ</span>} or retroflex <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">{‼</span>}. Hadza and Sandawe have three, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">{ǀ ǁ ǃ</span>}. Yeyi is the only Bantu language with four, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">{ǀ ǁ ǃ ǂ</span>}, while Xhosa and Zulu have three, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">{ǀ ǁ ǃ</span>}, and most Bantu languages with clicks have fewer than that.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_clicks">Types of clicks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Types of clicks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like other consonants, clicks can be described using four parameters: <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">place of articulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">manner of articulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phonation" title="Phonation">phonation</a> (including glottalisation) and <a href="/wiki/Airstream_mechanism" title="Airstream mechanism">airstream mechanism</a>. As noted above, clicks necessarily involve at least two closures, which in some cases operate partially independently: an anterior articulation traditionally represented by the special click symbol in the IPA—and a posterior articulation traditionally described as oral or <a href="/wiki/Nasal_consonant" title="Nasal consonant">nasal</a>, voiced or voiceless, etc. The literature also describes a contrast between <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">velar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uvular_consonant" title="Uvular consonant">uvular</a> rear articulations for some languages.
</p><p>However, recent work shows that in languages that make this distinction, all clicks have a uvular, or even pharyngeal, rear closure—and the clicks explicitly described as uvular are in fact clusters/contours of a click plus a pulmonic or ejective component, in which the cluster/contour has two release bursts, the forward (click) and then the rearward (uvular) component. "Velar" clicks in these languages have only a single release burst, that of the forward click release, and the release of the rear articulation isn't separately audible (Miller 2011).
</p><p>Nonetheless, in most of the literature the stated place of the click is the anterior articulation (called the <i>release</i> or <i>influx),</i> whereas the manner is ascribed to the posterior articulation (called the <i>accompaniment</i> or <i>efflux).</i> The anterior articulation defines the <i>click type</i> and is written with the IPA letter for the click (dental &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǀ</span>&#x27e9;, alveolar &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǃ</span>&#x27e9;, etc.), whereas the traditional term 'accompaniment' conflates the categories of manner (nasal, affricated), phonation (voiced, aspirated, breathy voiced, glottalised), as well as any change in the airstream with the release of the posterior articulation (pulmonic, ejective), all of which are transcribed with additional letters or diacritics, as in the <i>nasal alveolar click</i>, &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǃŋ</span>&#x27e9; or &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ᵑǃ</span>&#x27e9; or—to take an extreme example—the <i>voiced (uvular) ejective alveolar click</i>, &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ᶢǃ͡qʼ</span>&#x27e9;.
</p><p>The size of click inventories ranges from as few as three (in <a href="/wiki/Sesotho_phonology#Consonants" class="mw-redirect" title="Sesotho phonology">Sesotho</a>) or four (in <a href="/wiki/Dahalo_language" title="Dahalo language">Dahalo</a>), to dozens in the <a href="/wiki/Kx%CA%BCa_languages" title="Kxʼa languages">Kxʼa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuu_languages" title="Tuu languages">Tuu</a> (Northern and Southern Khoisan) languages. <a href="/wiki/Taa_language" title="Taa language">Taa</a>, the last vibrant language in the latter family, has 45 to 115 click phonemes, depending on analysis (clusters vs. contours), and over 70% of words in the dictionary of this language begin with a click.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Clicks appear more <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">stop</a>-like (sharp/abrupt) or <a href="/wiki/Affricate" class="mw-redirect" title="Affricate">affricate</a>-like (noisy) depending on their place of articulation: In southern Africa, clicks involving an <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">apical</a> <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_consonant" title="Alveolar consonant">alveolar</a> or <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">laminal</a> <a href="/wiki/Postalveolar_consonant" title="Postalveolar consonant">postalveolar</a> closure are acoustically abrupt and sharp, like stops, whereas <a href="/wiki/Labial_consonant" title="Labial consonant">labial</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">dental</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lateral_consonant" title="Lateral consonant">lateral</a> clicks typically have longer and acoustically noisier click types that are superficially more like affricates. In East Africa, however, the alveolar clicks tend to be <a href="/wiki/Flap_consonant" class="mw-redirect" title="Flap consonant">flapped</a>, whereas the lateral clicks tend to be more sharp.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transcription">Transcription</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Transcription">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Click_letter" title="Click letter">click letter</a></div>
<p>The five click places of articulation with dedicated symbols in the <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">International Phonetic Alphabet</a> (IPA) are <a href="/wiki/Labial_click" class="mw-redirect" title="Labial click">labial</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ʘ</span>, <a href="/wiki/Dental_click" title="Dental click">dental</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǀ</span>, <a href="/wiki/Palatal_click" title="Palatal click">palatal</a> ("palato-alveolar") <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǂ</span>, <a href="/wiki/Postalveolar_click" class="mw-redirect" title="Postalveolar click">(post)alveolar</a> ("retroflex") <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǃ</span> and <a href="/wiki/Lateral_click" title="Lateral click">lateral</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǁ</span>. In most languages, the alveolar and palatal types are abrupt; that is, they are sharp popping sounds with little frication (turbulent airflow). The labial, dental and lateral types, on the other hand, are typically noisy: they are longer, lip- or tooth-sucking sounds with turbulent airflow, and are sometimes called affricates. (This applies to the forward articulation; both may also have either an affricate or non-affricate rear articulation as well.) The <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">apical</a> places, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǃ</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǁ</span>, are sometimes called "grave", because their pitch is dominated by low frequencies; whereas the <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">laminal</a> places, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǀ</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǂ</span>, are sometimes called "acute", because they are dominated by high frequencies. (At least in the <a href="/wiki/N%C7%81ng_language" title="Nǁng language">Nǁng language</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ju%C7%80%CA%BChoan_language" title="Juǀʼhoan language">Juǀʼhoan</a>, this is associated with a difference in the placement of the rear articulation: "grave" clicks are <a href="/wiki/Uvular_consonant" title="Uvular consonant">uvular</a>, whereas "acute" clicks are <a href="/wiki/Pharyngeal_consonant" title="Pharyngeal consonant">pharyngeal</a>.) Thus the alveolar click <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ǃ/</span> sounds something like a cork pulled from a bottle (a low-pitch pop), at least in Xhosa; whereas the dental click <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ǀ/</span> is like English <i>tsk! tsk!,</i> a high-pitched sucking on the incisors. The lateral clicks are pronounced by sucking on the molars of one or both sides. The labial click <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʘ/</span> is different from what many people associate with a kiss: the lips are pressed more-or-less flat together, as they are for a <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[p]</span> or an <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[m]</span>, not rounded as they are for a <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[w]</span>.
</p><p>The most populous languages with clicks, Zulu and Xhosa, use the letters <i>c, q, x,</i> by themselves and in <a href="/wiki/Digraph_(orthography)" title="Digraph (orthography)">digraphs</a>, to write click consonants. Most Khoisan languages, on the other hand (with the notable exceptions of <a href="/wiki/Naro_language" title="Naro language">Naro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sandawe_language" title="Sandawe language">Sandawe</a>), use a more iconic system based on the <a href="/wiki/Vertical_bar" title="Vertical bar">pipe</a> &#x27e8;|&#x27e9;. (The exclamation point for the "retroflex" click was originally a pipe with a subscript dot, along the lines of <i>ṭ, ḍ, ṇ</i> used to transcribe the retroflex consonants of India.) There are also two main conventions for the second letter of the digraph as well: voicing may be written with <i>g</i> and uvular affrication with <i>x</i>, or voicing with <i>d</i> and affrication with <i>g</i> (a convention of Afrikaans). In two orthographies of Juǀʼhoan, for example, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ǃ̬/</span> is written <i>g!</i> or <i>dq</i>, and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ǃ͡χ/</span> <i>!x</i> or <i>qg</i>. In languages without <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ǃ͡χ/</span>, such as Zulu, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ǃ̬/</span> may be written <i>gq</i>.
</p>

<p>There are a few less-well-attested articulations.  A reported subapical retroflex articulation &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">‼</span>&#x27e9; in Grootfontein !Kung<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;note 4&#93;</a></sup> turns out to be alveolar with lateral release, &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǃ<sup>ǁ</sup></span>&#x27e9;; Ekoka !Kung has a fricated alveolar click with an s-like release, provisionally transcribed &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǃ͡s</span>&#x27e9;; and Sandawe has a "slapped" alveolar click, provisionally transcribed &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǃ¡</span>&#x27e9; (in turn, the lateral clicks in Sandawe are more abrupt and less noisy than in southern Africa). However, the Khoisan languages are poorly attested, and it is quite possible that, as they become better described, more click articulations will be found.
</p><p>Formerly when a click consonant was transcribed, two symbols were used, one for each articulation, and connected with a tie bar. This is because a click such as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ŋ͡ǂ]</span> was analysed as a nasal velar rear articulation <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ŋ]</span> pronounced simultaneously with the forward ingressive release <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǂ]</span>. The symbols may be written in either order, depending on the analysis: &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ŋ͡ǂ</span>&#x27e9; or &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǂ͡ŋ</span>&#x27e9;. However, a tie bar was not often used in practice, and when the manner is <a href="/wiki/Tenuis_consonant" title="Tenuis consonant">tenuis</a> (a simple <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[k]</span>), it was often omitted as well. That is, &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǂ</span>&#x27e9; = &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">kǂ</span>&#x27e9; = &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǂk</span>&#x27e9; = &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">k͡ǂ</span>&#x27e9; = &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǂ͡k</span>&#x27e9;. Regardless, elements that do not overlap with the forward release are usually written according to their temporal order: Prenasalisation is always written first (&#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ŋɡ͡ǂ</span>&#x27e9; = &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ŋǂ͡ɡ</span>&#x27e9; = &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ŋǂ̬</span>&#x27e9;), and the non-lingual part of a contour is always written second (&#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">k͡ǂʼqʼ</span>&#x27e9; = &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǂ͡kʼqʼ</span>&#x27e9; = &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǂ͡qʼ</span>&#x27e9;).
</p><p>However, it has become standard to analyse clicks as simplex segments, as research has shown that the front and rear articulations are not independent, and to use click symbols to cover the rear articulation as well, with diacritics rather than digraphs for the accompaniments. At first this tended to be &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǂ, ᶢǂ, ᵑǂ</span>&#x27e9; for &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">k͡ǂ, ɡ͡ǂ, ŋ͡ǂ</span>&#x27e9;, based on the belief that the rear articulation was velar; but as it has become clear that the rear articulation of both "velar" and "uvular" clicks is actually uvular or even pharyngeal, voicing and nasalisation diacritics more in keeping with the IPA have started to appear: &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǂ, ǂ̬, ǂ̃, ŋǂ̬</span>&#x27e9; for &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǂ, ᶢǂ, ᵑǂ, ŋᶢǂ</span>&#x27e9;.
</p>

<p>In practical orthography, the voicing or nasalisation is sometimes given the anterior place of articulation: <i>dc</i> for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ᶢǀ</span> and <i>mʘ</i> for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ᵑʘ</span>, for example.
</p><p>In the literature on Damin, the clicks are transcribed as &#x27e8;m!, nh!, n!, rn!&#x27e9;.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Places_of_articulation">Places of articulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Places of articulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Bilabial_click" title="Bilabial click">bilabial click</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dental_click" title="Dental click">dental click</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palatal_click" title="Palatal click">palatal click</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_click" title="Alveolar click">alveolar click</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lateral_click" title="Lateral click">lateral click</a>, <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_click" title="Retroflex click">retroflex click</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Velar_click" class="mw-redirect" title="Velar click">velar click</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">Places of articulation</a> are often called click <i>types, releases,</i> or <i>influxes,</i> though 'release' is also used for the accompaniment/efflux. There are seven or eight known places of articulation, not counting slapped or egressive clicks. These are <i>(bi)labial affricated</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ʘ</span>, or "(bi)labial"; <i>laminal denti-alveolar affricated</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǀ</span>, or "dental"; <i>apical (post)alveolar plosive</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǃ</span>, or "alveolar"; <i>laminal postalveolar (palato-alveolar) plosive</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǂ</span>, or "palatal"; <i>laminal postalveolar (palato-alveolar) affricated</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǂᶴ</span> (known only from <a href="/wiki/Ekoka_!Kung" title="Ekoka !Kung">Ekoka !Kung</a>); <i>subapical postalveolar (<a href="/wiki/Retroflex_click" title="Retroflex click">retroflex</a>)</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">‼</span> (only known from <a href="/wiki/Central_!Kung" title="Central !Kung">Central !Kung</a> and Damin); and <i>apical postalveolar lateral</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ǁ</span>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_back-vowel_constraint">The back-vowel constraint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: The back-vowel constraint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clicks_and_the_BVC.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/21/Clicks_and_the_BVC.svg/220px-Clicks_and_the_BVC.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="174" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/21/Clicks_and_the_BVC.svg/330px-Clicks_and_the_BVC.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/21/Clicks_and_the_BVC.svg/440px-Clicks_and_the_BVC.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="276" data-file-height="218" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clicks_and_the_BVC.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The shape of the tongue in Nama when articulating an alveolar click (blue) and a palatal click (red) [throat to the right]. The articulation of the vowel <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[i]</span> is slightly forward of the red line, with its peak coinciding with the dip of the blue line.</div></div></div>
<p>In several languages, including <a href="/wiki/Nama_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Nama language">Nama</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ju%C7%80%CA%BChoan_dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="Juǀʼhoan dialect">Juǀʼhoan</a>, the alveolar click types <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǃ]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǁ]</span> only occur, or preferentially occur, before <a href="/wiki/Back_vowel" title="Back vowel">back vowels</a>, whereas the dental and palatal clicks occur before any vowel. The effect is most noticeable with the high front vowel <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[i]</span>. In Nama, for example, the diphthong <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[əi]</span> is common but <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[i]</span> is rare after alveolar clicks, whereas the opposite is true after dental and palatal clicks. This is a common effect of <a href="/wiki/Uvular_consonant" title="Uvular consonant">uvular</a> or uvularised consonants on vowels in both click and non-click languages. In <a href="/wiki/Taa_language" title="Taa language">Taa</a>, for example, the back-vowel constraint is triggered by both alveolar clicks and uvular stops, but not by palatal clicks or velar stops: sequences such as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">*/ǃi/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">*/qi/</span> are rare to non-existent, whereas sequences such as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ǂi/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ki/</span> are common. It is also triggered by labial clicks, though not by labial stops. Clicks subject to this constraint involve a sharp <a href="/wiki/Retracted_tongue_root" class="mw-redirect" title="Retracted tongue root">retraction</a> of the tongue during release.
</p>

<p>Miller and colleagues (2003) used <a href="/wiki/Ultrasound_imaging" class="mw-redirect" title="Ultrasound imaging">ultrasound imaging</a> to show that the rear articulation of the alveolar clicks (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǃ]</span>) in Nama is substantially different from that of palatal and dental clicks. Specifically, the shape of the body of the tongue in palatal clicks is very similar to that of the vowel <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[i]</span>, and involves the same tongue muscles, so that sequences such as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǂi]</span> involved a simple and quick transition. The rear articulation of the alveolar clicks, however, is several centimetres further back, and involves a different set of muscles in the uvular region. The part of the tongue required to approach the palate for the vowel <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[i]</span> is deeply retracted in <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǃ]</span>, as it lies at the bottom of the air pocket used to create the vacuum required for click airstream. This makes the transition required for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǃi]</span> much more complex and the timing more difficult than the shallower and more forward tongue position of the palatal clicks. Consequently, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǃi]</span> takes 50 <a href="/wiki/Millisecond" title="Millisecond">ms</a> longer to pronounce than <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǂi]</span>, the same amount of time required to pronounce <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǃəi]</span>.
</p><p>Languages do not all behave alike. In <a href="/wiki/N%C7%81ng_language" title="Nǁng language">Nǀuu</a>, the simple clicks <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʘ, ǃ, ǁ/</span> trigger the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[əi]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[æ]</span> allophones of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/i/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/e/</span>, whereas <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ǀ, ǂ/</span> do not. All of the affricated contour clicks, such as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ǂ͡χ/</span>, do as well, as do the uvular stops <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/q, χ/</span>. However, the occlusive contour clicks pattern like the simple clicks, and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ǂ͡q/</span> does not trigger the back-vowel constraint. This is because they involve tongue-root raising rather than <a href="/wiki/Advanced_and_retracted_tongue_root" title="Advanced and retracted tongue root">tongue-root retraction</a> in the uvular-pharyngeal region. However, in <a href="/wiki/G%C7%80wi_dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="Gǀwi dialect">Gǀwi</a>, which is otherwise largely similar, both <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ǂ͡q/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ǂ͡χ/</span> trigger the back-vowel constraint (Miller 2009).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manners_of_articulation">Manners of articulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Manners of articulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Nasal_click" title="Nasal click">nasal click</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glottalised_click" class="mw-redirect" title="Glottalised click">glottalised click</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulmonic-contour_click" title="Pulmonic-contour click">pulmonic-contour click</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ejective-contour_click" title="Ejective-contour click">ejective-contour click</a></div>
<p>Click manners are often called click <i>accompaniments</i> or <i>effluxes</i>, but both terms have met with objections on theoretical grounds.
</p><p>There is a great variety of click manners, both simplex and complex, the latter variously analysed as consonant <a href="/wiki/Consonant_cluster" title="Consonant cluster">clusters</a> or <a href="/wiki/Contour_(linguistics)" title="Contour (linguistics)">contours</a>. With so few click languages, and so little study of them, it is also unclear to what extent clicks in different languages are equivalent. For example, the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǃkˀ]</span> of Khoekhoe, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǃkˀ ~ ŋˀǃk]</span> of Sandawe and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ŋ̊ǃˀ ~ ŋǃkˀ]</span> of Hadza may be essentially the same phone; no language distinguishes them, and the differences in transcription may have more to do with the approach of the linguist than with actual differences in the sounds. Such suspected allophones/allographs are listed on a common row in the table below.
</p><p>Some Khoisan languages are <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_typology" title="Linguistic typology">typologically</a> unusual in allowing mixed <a href="/wiki/Phonation" title="Phonation">voicing</a> in non-click consonant clusters/contours, such as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">dt͡sʼk͡xʼ</span>, so it is not surprising that they would allow mixed voicing in clicks as well. This may be an effect of epiglottalised voiced consonants, because voicing is incompatible with epiglottalisation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonation">Phonation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Phonation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As do other consonants, clicks vary in <a href="/wiki/Phonation" title="Phonation">phonation</a>. Oral clicks are attested with four phonations: <a href="/wiki/Tenuis_consonant" title="Tenuis consonant">tenuis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aspirated_consonant" title="Aspirated consonant">aspirated</a>, <a href="/wiki/Voiced_consonant" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiced consonant">voiced</a> and <a href="/wiki/Breathy_voice" title="Breathy voice">breathy voiced</a> (murmured). Nasal clicks may also vary, with plain voiced, breathy voiced / murmured nasal, aspirated and unaspirated voiceless clicks attested (the last only in Taa). The aspirated nasal clicks are often said to have 'delayed aspiration'; there is nasal airflow throughout the click, which may become voiced between vowels, though the aspiration itself is voiceless. A few languages also have pre-glottalised nasal clicks, which have very brief prenasalisation but have not been phonetically analysed to the extent that other types of clicks have.
</p><p>All languages have nasal clicks, and all but <a href="/wiki/Dahalo_language" title="Dahalo language">Dahalo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Damin" title="Damin">Damin</a> also have oral clicks. All languages but Damin have at least one phonation contrast as well.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Complex_clicks">Complex clicks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Complex clicks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Clicks may be pronounced with a third place of articulation, glottal. A <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">glottal stop</a> is made during the hold of the click; the (necessarily voiceless) click is released, and then the glottal hold is released into the vowel. Glottalised clicks are very common, and they are generally nasalised as well. The nasalisation cannot be heard during the click release, as there is no pulmonic airflow, and generally not at all when the click occurs at the beginning of an utterance, but it has the effect of nasalising preceding vowels, to the extent that the glottalised clicks of Sandawe and Hadza are often described as prenasalised when in medial position. Two languages, <a href="/wiki/G%C7%80wi_dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="Gǀwi dialect">Gǀwi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yeyi_language" title="Yeyi language">Yeyi</a>, contrast plain and nasal glottalised clicks, but in languages without such a contrast, the glottalised click is nasal. Miller (2011) analyses the glottalisation as phonation, and so considers these to be simple clicks.
</p><p>Various languages also have prenasalised clicks, which may be analysed as consonant sequences. <a href="/wiki/Sotho_language" title="Sotho language">Sotho</a>, for example, allows a syllabic nasal before its three clicks, as in <i>nnqane</i> 'the other side' (prenasalised nasal) and <i>seqhenqha</i> 'hunk'.
</p><p>There is ongoing discussion as to how the distinction between what were historically described as 'velar' and 'uvular' clicks is best described. The 'uvular' clicks are only found in some languages, and have an extended pronunciation that suggests that they are more complex than the simple ('velar') clicks, which are found in all. Nakagawa (1996) describes the extended clicks in <a href="/wiki/G%C7%80wi_dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="Gǀwi dialect">Gǀwi</a> as <a href="/wiki/Consonant_cluster" title="Consonant cluster">consonant clusters</a>, sequences equivalent to English <i>st</i> or <i>pl</i>, whereas Miller (2011) analyses similar sounds in several languages as click–non-click <a href="/wiki/Contour_(linguistics)" title="Contour (linguistics)">contours</a>, where a click transitions into a pulmonic or ejective articulation within a single segment, analogous to how English <i>ch</i> and <i>j</i> transition from occlusive to fricative but still behave as unitary sounds. With ejective clicks, for example, Miller finds that although the ejective release follows the click release, it is the rear closure of the click that is ejective, not an independently articulated consonant. That is, in a simple click, the release of the rear articulation is not audible, whereas in a contour click, the rear (uvular) articulation is audibly released after the front (click) articulation, resulting in a double release.
</p><p>These contour clicks may be <i>linguo-pulmonic</i>, that is, they may transition from a click (lingual) articulation to a normal pulmonic consonant like <span class="IPA nowrap" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_uvular_stop" title="Voiceless uvular stop">q</a>&#93;</span> (e.g. <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǂ͡q]</span>); or <i>linguo-glottalic</i> and transition from lingual to an ejective consonant like <span class="IPA nowrap" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Uvular_ejective" title="Uvular ejective">qʼ</a>&#93;</span> (e.g. <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǂ͡qʼ]</span>): that is, a sequence of ingressive (lingual) release + egressive (pulmonic or glottalic) release. In some cases there is a shift in place of articulation as well, and instead of a uvular release, the uvular click transitions to a velar or <a href="/wiki/Epiglottal_consonant" class="mw-redirect" title="Epiglottal consonant">epigottal</a> release (depending on the description, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǂ͡kxʼ]</span> or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǂᴴ]</span>). Although <a href="/wiki/Homorganic" class="mw-redirect" title="Homorganic">homorganic</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǂ͡χʼ]</span> does not contrast with heterorganic <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǂ͡kxʼ]</span> in any known language, they are phonetically quite distinct (Miller 2011).
</p><p>Apart from Dahalo, Damin and many of the Bantu languages (Yeyi and Xhosa being exceptions), 'click' languages have glottalised clicks. Contour clicks are restricted to southern Africa, but are very common there: they are found in all members of the Tuu, Kxʼa and Khoe families, as well as in the Bantu language Yeyi.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Variation_among_languages">Variation among languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Variation among languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a comparative study of clicks across various languages, using her own field work as well as phonetic descriptions and data by other field researchers, Miller (2011) posits 21 types of clicks that contrast in manner or airstream.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;note 6&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Homorganic" class="mw-redirect" title="Homorganic">homorganic</a> and heterorganic affricated ejective clicks do not contrast in any known language, but are judged dissimilar enough to keep separate. Miller's conclusions differ from those of the primary researcher of a language; see the individual languages for details.
</p>

<p>Yeyi also has prenasalised <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ŋᶢꞰ/</span>. </p><p>A DoBeS (2008) study of the Western ǃXoo dialect of Taa found several new manners: creaky voiced (the voiced equivalent of glottalised oral), breathy-voiced nasal, prenasalised glottalised (the voiced equivalent of glottalised) and a (pre)voiced ejective. These extra voiced clicks reflect Western ǃXoo morphology, where many nouns form their plural by voicing their initial consonant. DoBeS analyses most Taa clicks as clusters, leaving nine basic manners (marked with asterisks in the table). This comes close to Miller's distinction between simple and contour clicks, shaded light and medium grey in the table.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonotactics">Phonotactics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Phonotactics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Languages of the southern African Khoisan families only permit clicks at the beginning of a word root. However, they also restrict other classes of consonant, such as <a href="/wiki/Ejective_consonant" title="Ejective consonant">ejectives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Affricate" class="mw-redirect" title="Affricate">affricates</a>, to root-initial position. The Bantu languages, Hadza and Sandawe allow clicks within roots.
</p><p>In some languages, all click consonants within known roots are the same phoneme, as in Hadza <i>cikiringcingca</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ǀikiɺiN.ǀiN.ǀa/</span> 'pinkie finger', which has three <a href="/wiki/Tenuis_dental_click" title="Tenuis dental click">tenuis dental clicks</a>. Other languages are known to have the occasional root with different clicks, as in Xhosa <i>ugqwanxa</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/uᶢ̊ǃʱʷaᵑǁa/</span> '<a href="/wiki/Olea_capensis" title="Olea capensis">black ironwood</a>', which has a slack-<a href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_click" title="Voiced alveolar click">voiced alveolar click</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Nasal_lateral_click" title="Nasal lateral click">nasal lateral click</a>.
</p><p>Like other articulatorily complex consonants, clicks tend to be found in <a href="/wiki/Lexical_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Lexical words">lexical words</a> rather than in <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_word" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammatical word">grammatical words</a>, but this is only a tendency. In <a href="/wiki/N%C7%81ng_language" title="Nǁng language">Nǁng</a>, for example, there are two sets of <a href="/wiki/Personal_pronoun" title="Personal pronoun">personal pronouns</a>, a full one without clicks and a partial set with clicks (<i>ńg</i> 'I', <i>á</i> 'thou', <i>í</i> 'we all', <i>ú</i> 'you', vs. <i>nǀǹg</i> 'I', <i>gǀà</i> 'thou', <i>gǀì</i> 'we all', <i>gǀù</i> 'you'), as well as other grammatical words with clicks such as <i>ǁu</i> 'not' and <i>nǀa</i> 'with, and'.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Click_genesis_and_click_loss">Click genesis and click loss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Click genesis and click loss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One genetic study concluded that clicks, which occur in the languages of the genetically divergent populations Hadza and !Kung, may be an ancient element of human language.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> However, this conclusion relies on several dubious assumptions (see <a href="/wiki/Hadza_language" title="Hadza language">Hadza language</a>), and most linguists<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> assume that clicks, being quite complex consonants, arose relatively late in human history. How they arose is not known, but it is generally assumed that they developed from sequences of non-click consonants, as they are found allophonically for <a href="/wiki/Doubly_articulated_consonant" title="Doubly articulated consonant">doubly articulated consonants</a> in West Africa,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELadefoged1968_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELadefoged1968-24">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> where <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/tk/</span> sequences overlap at word boundaries in German,<sup id="cite_ref-germ_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-germ-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and for the sequence <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/mw/</span> in <a href="/wiki/Ndau_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ndau language">Ndau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tonga_language_(Zambia)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tonga language (Zambia)">Tonga</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;note 13&#93;</a></sup> Such developments have also been posited in historical reconstruction. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Sandawe_language" title="Sandawe language">Sandawe</a> word for 'horn', <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/tɬana/</span>, with a lateral affricate, may be a cognate with the root <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ᵑǁaː/</span> found throughout the <a href="/wiki/Khoe_languages" title="Khoe languages">Khoe family</a>, which has a lateral click. This and other words suggests that at least some Khoe clicks may have formed from consonant clusters when the first vowel of a word was lost; in this instance *<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[tɬana]</span> &gt; *<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[tɬna]</span> &gt; <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǁŋa] ~ [ᵑǁa]</span>.
</p><p>On the other side of the equation, several non-endangered languages in vigorous use demonstrate click loss. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Khoe_languages" title="Khoe languages">East Kalahari languages</a> have lost clicks from a large percentage of their vocabulary, presumably due to <a href="/wiki/Bantu_languages" title="Bantu languages">Bantu</a> influence. As a rule, a click is replaced by a consonant with close to the <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">manner of articulation</a> of the click and the <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">place of articulation</a> of the forward release: alveolar click releases (the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǃ]</span> family) tend to mutate into a velar stop or affricate, such as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[k], [ɡ], [ŋ], [k͡x]</span>; palatal clicks (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǂ]</span> <i>etc.</i>) tend to mutate into a palatal stop such as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[c], [&#8201;ɟ], [&#8201;ɲ], [cʼ]</span>, or a post-alveolar affricate <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[tʃ], [dʒ]</span>; and dental clicks (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǀ]</span> <i>etc.</i>) tend to mutate into an alveolar affricate <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ts]</span>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Difficulty">Difficulty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Click_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Difficulty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Clicks are often presented as difficult sounds to articulate within words. However, children acquire them readily; a two-year-old, for example, may be able to pronounce a word with a lateral click <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ǁ]</span> with no problem, but still be unable to pronounce <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[s]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Lloyd" title="Lucy Lloyd">Lucy Lloyd</a> reported that after long contact with the Khoi and San, it was difficult for her to refrain from using clicks when speaking English.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

</li>
<li id="cite_note-5"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-5">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Exceptions occurs in words borrowed from Bantu languages, which may have click in the middle.</span>

<li id="cite_note-9"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-9">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">&#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ɋ</span>&#x27e9;, &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ʇ</span>&#x27e9;, &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ʗ</span>&#x27e9; have descenders; &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ↆ</span>&#x27e9;, &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ʖ</span>&#x27e9; have ascenders.</span>

<li id="cite_note-10"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-10">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">&#x27e8;⦀&#x27e9; (a triple pipe) in Cole (1966) may have been the same thing. The Doke letter resembled &#x27e8;ψ&#x27e9;, or more precisely an inverted &#x27e8;⋔&#x27e9; (descender only).</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-13"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-13">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">A current exception is <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a>, which still calls the palatal clicks 'alveolar' and the alveolar clicks 'retroflex'.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-14"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-14">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Not counting the egressive "spurt" in Damin, and tree additional voiced manners in Western ǃXoo, which pair up with voiceless manners.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-15"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-15">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Ekoka ǃKung has an additional manner, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ˀᵑꞰ</span>. Grootfontein and Mangetti Dune ǃKung, on the other hand, have a substantially smaller inventory: <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">Ʞ, ᶢꞰ, Ʞʰ, ᵑꞰ, ᵑ̊Ʞʱ, ᵑꞰˀ, Ʞ͡χ, Ʞ͡kxʼ</span>.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-17"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-17">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Perhaps better described as <a href="/wiki/Slack_voice" title="Slack voice">slack voice</a>. Tone-depressor effect.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-18"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-18">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Tone-depressor effect. Sometimes a prenasalized click with a short, voiced oral occlusion, but usually without.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-20"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-20">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">not prenasalized</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-21"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-21">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">perhaps borrowed from Gǀui</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-22"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-22">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Not technically a click, but the only other attested sound with a lingual airstream mechanism.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-25"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-25">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Here the labial <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[m]</span> may have assimilated to the velar place of the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[w]</span>, as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[m͡ŋw]</span>, with the release of the labial before the velar later generating a click <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ᵐʘw]</span></span>
