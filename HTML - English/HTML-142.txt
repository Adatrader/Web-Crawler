<p><b>Warren</b> is a city in <a href="/wiki/Macomb_County,_Michigan" title="Macomb County, Michigan">Macomb County</a> in the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>. The <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 census</a> places the city's population at 134,056,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> making Warren the largest city in Macomb County, the <a href="/wiki/List_of_municipalities_in_Michigan_(by_population)" title="List of municipalities in Michigan (by population)">third largest city in Michigan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Metro_Detroit" title="Metro Detroit">Metro Detroit</a>'s largest suburb.
The city is home to a wide variety of businesses, including <a href="/wiki/General_Motors_Technical_Center" title="General Motors Technical Center">General Motors Technical Center</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> Detroit Arsenal, home of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_TACOM_Life_Cycle_Management_Command" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Army TACOM Life Cycle Management Command">United States Army TACOM Life Cycle Management Command</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tank_Automotive_Research,_Development_and_Engineering_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Tank Automotive Research, Development and Engineering Center">Tank Automotive Research, Development and Engineering Center</a> (TARDEC), the headquarters of <a href="/wiki/Big_Boy_Restaurants_International" class="mw-redirect" title="Big Boy Restaurants International">Big Boy Restaurants International</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Asset_Acceptance" title="Asset Acceptance">Asset Acceptance</a>. The current mayor is <a href="/wiki/James_R._Fouts" title="James R. Fouts">James R. Fouts</a>, who was elected to his first mayoral term in November 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beebe's Corners, the original settlement in what would become the city of Warren, was founded in 1830 at the corner of Mound Road and Chicago Road; its first resident was Charles Groesbeck.<sup id="cite_ref-WHC_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHC-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  Beebe's Corners was a carriage stop between <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Utica,_Michigan" title="Utica, Michigan">Utica</a>, and included a distillery, mill, tavern, and trading post.<sup id="cite_ref-WHC_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHC-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  It was not until 1837 that the now-defunct <a href="/wiki/Warren_Township,_Macomb_County,_Michigan" title="Warren Township, Macomb County, Michigan">Warren Township</a> was organized around the settlement, first under the name Hickory, then renamed Aba in April 1838, and finally renamed Warren shortly thereafter.<sup id="cite_ref-CLHistory_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CLHistory-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  It was named for <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a> veteran, and frontier cleric, Rev. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abel_Warren&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Abel Warren (page does not exist)">Abel Warren</a>.  However, when it was originally organized the township was named for Rev. Warren who was a <a href="/wiki/Methodist_Episcopal" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodist Episcopal">Methodist Episcopal</a> preacher who left his native <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> in 1824 for <a href="/wiki/Shelby_Township,_Michigan" title="Shelby Township, Michigan">Shelby Township</a>.  He went throughout the present-day <a href="/wiki/Macomb_County,_Michigan" title="Macomb County, Michigan">Macomb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lapeer_County,_Michigan" title="Lapeer County, Michigan">Lapeer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oakland_County,_Michigan" title="Oakland County, Michigan">Oakland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/St._Clair_County,_Michigan" title="St. Clair County, Michigan">St. Clair</a> Counties, baptizing, marrying, and burying pioneers of the area, as well as establishing congregations and preaching extensively.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  He was the first licensed preacher in the State of Michigan.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another version of the source of the city's name claims it was "named for General <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Warren" title="Joseph Warren">Joseph Warren</a> (1741–1775), who fell at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bunker_Hill" title="Battle of Bunker Hill">Battle of Bunker Hill</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Romig_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Romig-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The settlement was formally incorporated as the Village of Warren from Warren Township on April 28, 1893 out of one square mile bound by 14 Mile Road and 13 Mile Road to the north and south, and in half-a-mile east and west of Mound Road.<sup id="cite_ref-WHC_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHC-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The small village grew slowly, and had a population of 582 in 1940 and 727 in 1950, while the larger surrounding township grew at a much quicker pace.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Red Run and Bear Creek, just small creeks back in the 1800s, has blossomed into an open major inter-county stormdrain flowing through Warren, into the Clinton River, and onwards to Lake St. Clair.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Village of Warren and most of the surrounding Township of Warren, together with <a href="/wiki/Van_Dyke,_Michigan" title="Van Dyke, Michigan">Van Dyke</a>, incorporated as a city in 1957, less the city of <a href="/wiki/Center_Line,_Michigan" title="Center Line, Michigan">Center Line</a>, which had incorporated as a village from Warren Township in 1925 and as a city in 1936.<sup id="cite_ref-CLHistory_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CLHistory-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Between 1950 and 1960, Warren's population soared from 42,653 to 89,426. This population explosion was fueled by the post-WWII <a href="/wiki/Baby_Boom" class="mw-redirect" title="Baby Boom">Baby Boom</a> and later, by <a href="/wiki/White_flight" title="White flight">white flight</a> from its southern neighbor of <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a> in that decade. This change in population continued into the next decade when the city's population doubled again, ultimately reaching a high of 179,000 in 1970.
</p><p>The subsequent decades have seen Warren's population decline, while violent crime has increased.  Combined with collapsing housing prices, down -53% between 2011 and 2016, this has led Warren to a number 7 ranking in Forbes' Most Miserable Cities to Live in the US; joining two other Michigan cities, Detroit and Flint, in the Top 10.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the city has a total area of 34.46 square miles (89.25&#160;km<sup>2</sup>), of which 34.38 square miles (89.04&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) is land and 0.08 square miles (0.21&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) is water.<sup id="cite_ref-Gazetteer_files_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gazetteer_files-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The city covers a six-mile-by-six mile (10&#160;km x 10&#160;km) square in the southwest corner of <a href="/wiki/Macomb_County" class="mw-redirect" title="Macomb County">Macomb County</a> in suburban <a href="/wiki/Detroit,_Michigan" class="mw-redirect" title="Detroit, Michigan">Detroit</a> (minus <a href="/wiki/Center_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Center Line">Center Line</a>, which is a small city totally enclosed within Warren). Other cities bordering on Warren are <a href="/wiki/Detroit,_Michigan" class="mw-redirect" title="Detroit, Michigan">Detroit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hazel_Park,_Michigan" title="Hazel Park, Michigan">Hazel Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madison_Heights,_Michigan" title="Madison Heights, Michigan">Madison Heights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sterling_Heights,_Michigan" title="Sterling Heights, Michigan">Sterling Heights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fraser,_Michigan" title="Fraser, Michigan">Fraser</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roseville,_Michigan" title="Roseville, Michigan">Roseville</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eastpointe,_Michigan" title="Eastpointe, Michigan">Eastpointe</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Warren features a <a href="/wiki/Humid_continental_climate" title="Humid continental climate">humid continental climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen</a>: <i>Dfa</i>). Summers are somewhat hot with temperatures exceeding 90&#160;°F (32&#160;°C) on average 8.6 days. Winters are cold, with temperatures not rising above freezing on 39.1 days annually, while dropping to or below 0&#160;°F (−18&#160;°C) on average 1.2 days a year.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_highways">Main highways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Main highways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/I-696.svg/25px-I-696.svg.png" decoding="async" width="25" height="20" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/I-696.svg/38px-I-696.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/I-696.svg/50px-I-696.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="751" data-file-height="601" /> <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Interstate_696" title="Interstate 696">I-696</a></span> cuts east and west through the middle of Warren.</li>
<li><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/M-53.svg/20px-M-53.svg.png" decoding="async" width="20" height="20" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/M-53.svg/30px-M-53.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/M-53.svg/40px-M-53.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="600" /> <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/M-53_(Michigan_highway)" title="M-53 (Michigan highway)">M-53</a></span>, which is Van Dyke Avenue (also known as the <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Earle" title="Horatio Earle">Earle</a> Memorial Highway), leads into Van Dyke Freeway runs north and south and (roughly) bisects the city.</li>
<li><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/M-97.svg/20px-M-97.svg.png" decoding="async" width="20" height="20" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/M-97.svg/30px-M-97.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/M-97.svg/40px-M-97.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="600" /> <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/M-97_(Michigan_highway)" title="M-97 (Michigan highway)">M-97</a></span> also known as Groesbeck Highway named for former Governor <a href="/wiki/Alex_Groesbeck" class="mw-redirect" title="Alex Groesbeck">Alex Groesbeck</a> is near the eastern edge of Warren. It comes north from Detroit, and is a fast and wide diagonal connector to northern <a href="/wiki/Macomb_County,_Michigan" title="Macomb County, Michigan">Macomb County</a>.</li>
<li><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/M-102.svg/20px-M-102.svg.png" decoding="async" width="20" height="20" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/M-102.svg/30px-M-102.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/M-102.svg/40px-M-102.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="600" /> <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/M-102_(Michigan_highway)" title="M-102 (Michigan highway)">M-102</a></span> more commonly known as 8 Mile Road or more esoterically as <a href="/wiki/Baseline_(surveying)#&quot;Baseline_Road&quot;_in_the_United_States" title="Baseline (surveying)">Base Line Road</a> is the city's south border.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unnumbered_roads">Unnumbered roads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Unnumbered roads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mound Road is an important north-south artery in the city. East-west travel is mainly on the <a href="/wiki/Mile_Road_System_(Detroit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mile Road System (Detroit)">mile roads</a>. Most notable are <a href="/wiki/8_Mile_Road" class="mw-redirect" title="8 Mile Road">8 Mile Road</a>, which is on the southern border of Warren with <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>; <a href="/wiki/I-696" class="mw-redirect" title="I-696">11 Mile Road</a>, which serves as a service drive for <a href="/wiki/I-696" class="mw-redirect" title="I-696">I-696</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mile_Road_System_(Detroit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mile Road System (Detroit)">14 Mile Road</a>, which is on the northern border of Warren with <a href="/wiki/Sterling_Heights" class="mw-redirect" title="Sterling Heights">Sterling Heights</a>.

<p>The remaining figures are from the 2000 census except when otherwise stated.  The top six reported ancestries (people were allowed to report up to two ancestries, thus the figures will generally add to more than 100%) in Warren in 2000 were Polish (21.0%), German (20.4%), Irish (11.5%), Italian (10.6%), English (7.3%), and French (5.3%).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There were 55,551 households out of which 27.8% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 49.7% were married couples living together, 11.7% had a female householder with no husband present, and 33.9% were non-families. 28.8% of all households were made up of individuals and 12.0% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.47 and the average family size was 3.05.
</p><p>The city's age distribution was 22.9% under 18, 7.6% from 18 to 24, 30.8% from 25 to 44, 21.4% from 45 to 64, and 17.3% who were 65 or older. The median age was 38 years. For every 100 females, there were 95.6 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 92.1 males.
</p><p>The median income for a household in the city was $44,626, and the median income for a family was $52,444. Males had a median income of $41,454 versus $28,368 for females. The per capita income for the city was $21,407. 7.4% of the population and 5.2% of families were below the poverty line. Out of the total people living in poverty, 9.5% were under the age of 18 and 5.8% were 65 or older.
</p><p>There are a number of distinguishing characteristics about Warren which render it unique among American cities of its relative size. Warren was one of the fastest-growing municipalities in the country between 1940 and 1970, roughly doubling its population every 10 years. In 1940 the official population of Warren Township was 22,146; in 1950, it was 42,653; in 1960, after Warren Township had become the City of Warren, population had risen to 89,240; and by 1970 it had grown to 179,260.
</p><p>Since 1970, Warren has been consistently one of the faster-declining cities in population in the country. The population declined by 10% during each of the next two decades (1980: 161,060; 1990: 144,864), and dropped by 4.6% between 1990 and 2000.
</p><p>In 1970, whites made up 99.5% of the city's total population of 179,270; only 838 non-whites lived within the city limits. Racial integration came slowly to Warren in the ensuing two decades, with the white portion of the city dropping only gradually to 98.2% in 1980 and 97.3% as of 1990.  At that point integration started to accelerate, with the white population declining to 91.3% in 2000 and reaching 78.4% as of the 2010 census.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For 2000 the non-Hispanic white population of Warren was 90.4% of the total population. African-American were 2.7% of the population (which is the same as the total non-white population in 1990), Asians were 3.1% of the population, Native Americans 0.4%, other groups 0.3% and those reporting two or more races were 2.2% of the population. Hispanics or Latinos or any race were 1.4% of the population.<sup id="cite_ref-Bureau_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bureau-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Warren's population was as of 2000 one of the oldest among large cities in the United States. 16.1% of Warren's population was 65 or older at the last census, tied for fifth with <a href="/wiki/Hollywood,_Florida" title="Hollywood, Florida">Hollywood, Florida</a> among cities with 100,000+ population, and in fact the highest-ranking city by this measure outside of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Metzger_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metzger-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Warren is ranked 1st in the nation for longevity of residence. Residents of Warren on average have lived in that community 35.5 years, compared to the national average of eight years for communities of 100,000+ population.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Warren remains a population center for people of <a href="/wiki/Polish_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Polish American">Polish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lebanese_Americans" title="Lebanese Americans">Lebanese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Ukrainian American">Ukrainian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotch-Irish_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Scotch-Irish American">Scots-Irish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Filipino_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Filipino American">Filipino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maltese_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Maltese American">Maltese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assyrians_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Assyrians in the United States">Assyrian/Chaldean</a> descent.
</p><p>The post-1970 population change in Warren has been so pronounced that by 2000 there were 1,026 <a href="/wiki/Filipinos" title="Filipinos">Filipinos</a> in Warren as well as 1,145 Asian Indians in the city, and 1,559 American Indians. Many of the American Indians in Warren originated in the Southern United States with 429 <a href="/wiki/Cherokee" title="Cherokee">Cherokee</a> and 66 <a href="/wiki/Lumbee" title="Lumbee">Lumbee</a>. In fact the Lumbee were the third largest American Indian "tribe" in the city, with only the 193 <a href="/wiki/Chippewa" class="mw-redirect" title="Chippewa">Chippewa</a> outnumbering them.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_census">2010 census</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2010 census">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup id="cite_ref-FactFinder_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FactFinder-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> of 2010, there were 134,056 people, 53,442 households, and 34,185 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 3,899.2 inhabitants per square mile (1,505.5/km<sup>2</sup>). There were 57,938 housing units at an average density of 1,685.2 per square mile (650.7/km<sup>2</sup>). The racial makeup of the city was 78.4% <a href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 13.5% <a href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 0.4% <a href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 4.6% <a href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.4% from <a href="/wiki/Race_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (U.S. Census)">other races</a>, and 2.6% from two or more races. <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 2.1% of the population.
</p><p>There were 53,442 households of which 30.6% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 42.2% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 15.9% had a female householder with no husband present, 5.9% had a male householder with no wife present, and 36.0% were non-families. 30.4% of all households were made up of individuals and 12.6% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.49 and the average family size was 3.11.
</p><p>The median age in the city was 39.4 years. 22.7% of residents were under the age of 18; 9% were between the ages of 18 and 24; 26.1% were from 25 to 44; 26.1% were from 45 to 64; and 16.1% were 65 years of age or older. The gender makeup of the city was 48.4% male and 51.6% female.
</p><p>Between 2000 and 2010, the Asian population in Warren increased to almost 6,200, a 46% increase.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> This was a much slower growth rate than that of the African-American population that grew from 3,700 to over 18,000 or a more than 300% increase.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_and_infrastructure">Government and infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Government and infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Municipal_government">Municipal government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Municipal government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Warren municipal government is composed of a Mayor, City Council, Clerk, and different boards and commissions. Boards include the Zoning Board of Appeals, Board of Review, Employee Retirement Board of Trustees, and Construction Board of Appeals.  Commissions include Animal Welfare, Beautification, Compensation, Crime, Cultural, Disabilities, Historical, Housing, Library, Planning, Police &amp; Fire, and Village Historic District Commissions.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Amendment_lawsuit">First Amendment lawsuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: First Amendment lawsuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The City of Warren established a Christian prayer station at city hall that is operated by the Pentecostal Tabernacle Church of Warren. Douglas Marshall requested establishing a reason station. Mayor <a href="/wiki/James_R._Fouts" title="James R. Fouts">James R. Fouts</a> personally refused to grant Marshall's request in a letter based, in part, on the claim that the station would disrupt those using the prayer station. The <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_Liberties_Union" title="American Civil Liberties Union">American Civil Liberties Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/Americans_United_for_Separation_of_Church_and_State" title="Americans United for Separation of Church and State">Americans United for Separation of Church and State</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Freedom_from_Religion_Foundation" class="mw-redirect" title="Freedom from Religion Foundation">Freedom from Religion Foundation</a> jointly filed a complaint against the city. In 2015 there was a $100,000 judgment against the city government and mayor <a href="/wiki/James_R._Fouts" title="James R. Fouts">James R. Fouts</a> for denying Marshall the right to establish his atheist station.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neighborhoods">Neighborhoods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Neighborhoods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Southeast_Warren_.2848089.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Southeast_Warren_(48089)">Southeast Warren (48089)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Southeast Warren (48089)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Southeast Warren consists of the Belangers Garden, Berkshire Manor, Piper Van Dyke, Warrendale, and the southern portion of Warren Woods.<sup id="cite_ref-city-data.com_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-city-data.com-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The neighborhood population in 2009 was 33,031. The neighborhood's racial makeup was 70.14% White, 15.50% African-American, 2.27% Asian, 0.38% Native American, and 6.80% of other races. 1.84% were Hispanic or Latino of any race.
</p><p>The neighborhood's median household income in 2009 was $35,136. The per capita income was $15,301.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Much of Southeast Warren's residential architecture is based on the Bungalows built immediately after World War II. To the north of Stephens Road, many homes were built after 1960 in the brick ranch style. Besides the residential areas, Southeast Warren is also occupied by multiple industrial parks.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture_and_recreation">Culture and recreation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Culture and recreation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The City of Warren has a Department of Parks and Recreation which oversees the Aquatic, Community, and Fitness Centers along with a system of 24 parks. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_Symphony_Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Warren Symphony Orchestra (page does not exist)">Warren Symphony Orchestra</a> gives several concerts per season and changed its name to the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_Symphony_Orchestra&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Warren Symphony Orchestra (page does not exist)">Warren Symphony Orchestra</a> in 2016<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
In 2003 the city built a brand new Community Center where the old Warren High School was.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is also a nine member Cultural Commission.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Universal_Mall" title="Universal Mall">Universal Mall</a>, an enclosed shopping mall, was built in the city in 1965. In 2009, it was demolished for a new outdoor shopping center.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Italian American Cultural Society (IACS) had been located in Warren for a 20-year period. In 2004 it moved to its current location in <a href="/wiki/Clinton_Township,_Macomb_County,_Michigan" title="Clinton Township, Macomb County, Michigan">Clinton Township</a>. Its previous location was sold to a <a href="/wiki/Charter_school" title="Charter school">charter school</a> in July 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crime">Crime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Crime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Rising Violent Crime in Warren is coupled with decreasing population.
</p><p>In 2001 Warren had a population of 138,976 and reported 63 forcible rapes, 191 aggravated assaults and 559 burglaries.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> By 2012 the number of rapes had increased by 44% to 91, aggravated assaults had more than doubled to 476, as had burglaries to 999, while the population had declined by 4,600.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> 
The city has a high violent crime rate of 532 per 100,000 in 2012, 43% higher than the US national average of 371 per 100,000.
</p><p>Since 2000, there have been thirty-five reported murders; five in 2001, three in 2003, six in 2004, five in both 2005 and 2006, seven in 2007, and four in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Warren Police Department serves as the main law enforcement agency in the city.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_markers">Historical markers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren,_Michigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Historical markers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<li>Detroit Memorial Park Cemetery. Inventor <a href="/wiki/Elijah_McCoy" title="Elijah McCoy">Elijah McCoy</a> is buried there, as noted on the historical marker.<sup id="cite_ref-Markers_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Markers-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Former member of the band <a href="/wiki/The_Supremes" title="The Supremes">The Supremes</a> <a href="/wiki/Florence_Ballard" title="Florence Ballard">Florence Ballard</a> is also buried there.</li>
<li>Erin - Warren Fractional District No. 2 School</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/General_Motors_Technical_Center" title="General Motors Technical Center">General Motors Technical Center</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Warren_Truck_Assembly" title="Warren Truck Assembly">Warren Truck Assembly</a>, on location since 1938</li>
<li>Governor <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Joseph_Groesbeck" class="mw-redirect" title="Alexander Joseph Groesbeck">Alexander Joseph Groesbeck</a></li>
<li>John Theisen House</li>
<li>Village of Warren</li>
<li>Warren Township District No. 4 School</li>
<li>Warren Union Cemetery</li></ul>
