<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Update_related_to_previous_topic:_disinformation">Update related to previous topic: disinformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wikipedia:Wikipedia_Signpost/Newsroom/Suggestions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Update related to previous topic: disinformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>You had a previous article in June's "In the Media" topic: "<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Wikipedia_Signpost/2019-06-30/In_the_media" title="Wikipedia:Wikipedia Signpost/2019-06-30/In the media">The disinformation age</a>". An egregious example of disinformation has recently surfaced. The key and most worrisome nature of this is that it is apparently *not* the product of a nation-state actor nor any previously recognised organization. Rather, it has become <i>so easy</i> to construct a huge network of fake sites that <i>anyone can do it</i>. 
</p><p><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.bbc.com/news/world-asia-india-50749764">Pro-Indian 'fake websites targeted decision makers in Europe</a> (BBC) is one ref. That article links to <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.disinfo.eu/2019/12/16/fake-media-and-ngos-a-pro-indian-network-designed-to-influence-policymakers/">EU Disinfo Lab</a>. (Their <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://twitter.com/DisinfoEU/status/1194587360400494592?s=20">clarion call</a> on Twitter - MSNBC.UK&#160;!?)
</p><p>That NGO is apparently not associated directly with the EU's <a href="/wiki/East_StratCom_Task_Force" title="East StratCom Task Force">East StratCom Task Force</a> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://euvsdisinfo.eu/">EUvsDisinfo</a> project, though a previous BBC article referenced an article of theirs that discussed one of the newly identified 'newssites' in <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://euvsdisinfo.eu/how-to-get-the-european-parliament-to-read-russia-today/?highlight=ep%20today">October</a>. Passing off as an 'organ' of the EU is amazing, but it fooled MEPs! Consider also that <i>EUvsDisinfo missed the bigger picture</i>.
</p><p>(So far I have found only a small number of mentions of this here, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Fake_news_in_India#Fake_news_websites" title="Fake news in India">Fake news websites</a> , <a href="/wiki/The_Dublin_Gazette#Fake_news_controversy" title="The Dublin Gazette">Fake news controversy</a>)
</p><p><br />
The critical issue here is that we have reached a tipping point, where even a very small number of people with money and fervour can weave a massive network of deception that is unnoticed for years, and unrecognized even by groups looking for this evil. Fakery from any direction, for any purpose. 
</p><p>Your previous article gave some examples of disinformation. The above example reverses the emphasis. It is not enough to be aware of disinformation. Rather, one must now discern which few sources to trust. This escalation should be noted in the Signpost.
</p><p><br />

<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">Physicists</a> have determined that, <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(length)#Subatomic_scale" title="Orders of magnitude (length)">from the very small to the very large</a>, 99.9999999%<sup id="cite_ref-SA-20160923_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SA-20160923-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> of all <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">Universe</a> is <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">empty space</a>.</li></ul>

<p>I'm also noticing a some trend which Mandiberg does not explicitly observe. Namely, that there are several <a href="/wiki/Modern_liberalism_in_the_United_States" title="Modern liberalism in the United States">liberal</a>/<a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> leaning places in the south where there is a a dearth of Wikipedia activity, not just conservative/<a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> places (One of the hypotheses Mandiberg considers is that low-editing happens in conservative areas and may be due to conservative distaste for Wikipedia's historical left-lean and the existence of Conservapedia). These Democratic areas, such as north-eastern North Carolina are mostly rural and have significant black/African American populations. Historically, black Americans since the 1960s have voted mostly for liberal/Democratic candidates. The lack of editing in the "<a href="/wiki/Black_Belt_in_the_American_South" title="Black Belt in the American South">Black Belt</a>" thus can be used to demonstrate the hypothesis that the political leaning has less to with editing activity than low population density (one of Mandiberg's observations is that low activity correlates with low density, but he seems mostly concerned with the overwhelmingly-white Western US). This North Carolina map is also of some more interest to me because while the cluster of the worst counties for editing in the northeast are very rural and have significant black populations, they have also been economically weak for years and are shrinking. For the sake of considering <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Systemic_bias" title="Wikipedia:Systemic bias">Wikipedia:Systemic bias</a>, it may be important to consider that we might have problems on African-American figures or the places they live due to this low editing activity. -<a href="/wiki/User:Indy_beetle" title="User:Indy beetle">Indy beetle</a> (<a href="/wiki/User_talk:Indy_beetle" title="User talk:Indy beetle">talk</a>) 23:57, 26 February 2020 (UTC)
