<p><b>Robert Sheckley</b> (July 16, 1928 – December 9, 2005)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> was an American writer. First published in the <a href="/wiki/Science-fiction" class="mw-redirect" title="Science-fiction">science-fiction</a> magazines of the 1950s, his numerous quick-witted stories and novels were famously unpredictable, <a href="/wiki/Absurdism" title="Absurdism">absurdist</a>, and broadly comical.
</p><p>Nominated for <a href="/wiki/Hugo_award" class="mw-redirect" title="Hugo award">Hugo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nebula_award" class="mw-redirect" title="Nebula award">Nebula awards</a>, Sheckley was named <a href="/wiki/Author_Emeritus" title="Author Emeritus">Author Emeritus</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Science_Fiction_and_Fantasy_Writers_of_America" title="Science Fiction and Fantasy Writers of America">Science Fiction and Fantasy Writers of America</a> in 2001.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Sheckley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sheckley was born to an assimilated <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> family in <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>. In 1931 the family moved to <a href="/wiki/Maplewood,_New_Jersey" title="Maplewood, New Jersey">Maplewood, New Jersey</a>. Sheckley attended <a href="/wiki/Columbia_High_School_(New_Jersey)" title="Columbia High School (New Jersey)">Columbia High School</a>, where he discovered <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a>. He graduated in 1946<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and hitchhiked to <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> the same year, where he tried numerous jobs: landscape gardener, pretzel salesman, barman, milkman, warehouseman, and general laborer "board man" in a hand-painted necktie studio. Finally, still in 1946, he joined the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Army" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Army">U.S. Army</a> and was sent to Korea.<sup id="cite_ref-obit_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obit-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> During his time in the army he served as a guard, an army newspaper editor, a payroll clerk, and  guitarist in an army band. He left the service in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sheckley graduated from <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a> in 1951. The same year he married, for the first time, to Barbara Scadron. The couple had one son, Jason. Sheckley worked in an aircraft factory and as an assistant metallurgist for a short time, but his breakthrough came quickly: in late 1951 he sold his first story, <i>Final Examination</i>, to <i><a href="/wiki/Imagination_(magazine)" title="Imagination (magazine)">Imagination</a></i> magazine. He quickly gained prominence as a writer, publishing stories in <i>Imagination</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Galaxy_Science_Fiction" title="Galaxy Science Fiction">Galaxy</a></i>, and other science fiction magazines. The 1950s saw the publication of Sheckley's first four books: short story collections <i>Untouched by Human Hands</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ballantine_Books" title="Ballantine Books">Ballantine</a>, 1954), <i>Citizen in Space</i> (1955), and <i>Pilgrimage to Earth</i> (<a href="/wiki/Bantam_Books" title="Bantam Books">Bantam</a>, 1957), and a novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Immortality,_Inc." title="Immortality, Inc.">Immortality, Inc.</a></i> (first published as a serial in <i>Galaxy</i>, 1958).
</p><p>Sheckley and Scadron divorced in 1956. The writer married journalist Ziva  Kwitney in 1957. The newly married couple lived in Greenwich Village. Their daughter, <a href="/wiki/Alisa_Kwitney" title="Alisa Kwitney">Alisa Kwitney</a>, born in 1964, would herself become a successful writer.  Applauded by critic <a href="/wiki/Kingsley_Amis" title="Kingsley Amis">Kingsley Amis</a>, Sheckley was now  selling many of his deft, satiric stories to mainstream magazines such as <i><a href="/wiki/Playboy" title="Playboy">Playboy</a></i>. In addition to his science fiction stories, in the 1960s Sheckley started writing suspense fiction. More short story collections and novels appeared in the 1960s, and a film adaptation of an early story by Sheckley, <i><a href="/wiki/The_10th_Victim" title="The 10th Victim">The 10th Victim</a></i>, was released in 1965.
</p><p>Sheckley spent much of 1970s living on <a href="/wiki/Ibiza" title="Ibiza">Ibiza</a>. He and Kwitney divorced in 1972 and the same year Sheckley married Abby Schulman, whom he had met in Ibiza. The couple had two children, Anya and Jed. The couple separated while living in London. In 1980, the writer returned to the United States and became fiction editor of the newly established <i><a href="/wiki/Omni_(magazine)" title="Omni (magazine)">OMNI</a></i> magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-obit2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obit2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Sheckley  left <i>OMNI</i> in 1981 with his fourth wife, writer Jay Rothbell: they subsequently traveled widely in Europe, finally ending up in Portland, Oregon, where they separated.  He married Gail Dana of Portland in 1990. Sheckley continued publishing further science fiction and espionage or mystery stories, and collaborated with other writers such as <a href="/wiki/Roger_Zelazny" title="Roger Zelazny">Roger Zelazny</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harry_Harrison_(writer)" title="Harry Harrison (writer)">Harry Harrison</a>.
</p><p>During an April 2005 visit to <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> for the Ukrainian Sci-Fi Computer Week, an international event for <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> writers, Sheckley fell ill and had to be hospitalized in <a href="/wiki/Kiev" title="Kiev">Kiev</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> His condition was very serious for a week, but he appeared to be slowly recovering. Sheckley's official website ran a fundraising campaign to help cover his treatment and his return to the United States. He settled in Red Hook, in northern Dutchess County, New York, to be near his daughters Anya and Alisa. On November 20 he had surgery for a <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_aneurysm" class="mw-redirect" title="Cerebral aneurysm">brain aneurysm</a>; he died in a <a href="/wiki/Poughkeepsie_(city),_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Poughkeepsie (city), New York">Poughkeepsie</a> hospital on December 9, 2005.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Sheckley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sheckley was a prolific and versatile writer. His works include not only original short stories and novels, but also TV series episodes (<i><a href="/wiki/Captain_Video_and_His_Video_Rangers" title="Captain Video and His Video Rangers">Captain Video and His Video Rangers</a></i>), novelizations of works by others (<i><a href="/wiki/Babylon_5:_A_Call_to_Arms" title="Babylon 5: A Call to Arms">Babylon 5: A Call to Arms</a></i>, after the film,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>), stories in <a href="/wiki/Shared_universe" title="Shared universe">shared universes</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Heroes_in_Hell" title="Heroes in Hell">Heroes in Hell</a></i>, and collaborations with other writers. He was best known for his several hundred short stories,<sup id="cite_ref-obit_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obit-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> which he published in book form as well as individually. Typical Sheckley stories include "Bad Medicine" (in which a man is mistakenly treated by a psychotherapy machine intended for Martians), "Protection" (whose protagonist is warned of deadly danger unless he avoids the common activity of "lesnerizing", a word whose meaning is not explained), and "The Accountant" (in which a family of wizards learns that their son has been taken from them by a more sinister trade&#8212;accountancy). In many stories Sheckley speculates about alternative (and usually sinister) social orders, of which a good example is the story "A Ticket to Tranai" (which tells of a sort of <a href="/wiki/Utopia" title="Utopia">Utopia</a> designed for human nature as it actually is, which turns out to have terrible drawbacks).
</p><p>Sheckley's early stories include the far future AAA Ace detective agency series. In these tales, the two partners face unusual problems often related to human incompetence or laziness.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1990s, Sheckley wrote a series of three mystery novels featuring detective Hob Draconian, as well as novels set in the worlds of <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_Deep_Space_Nine" title="Star Trek: Deep Space Nine">Star Trek: Deep Space Nine</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Alien_(film)" title="Alien (film)">Alien</a></i>. Before his death Sheckley had been commissioned to write an original novel based on the TV series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prisoner" title="The Prisoner">The Prisoner</a></i> for <a href="/w/index.php?title=Powys_Media&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Powys Media (page does not exist)">Powys Media</a>, but died before completing the manuscript.
</p><p>His novel <i><a href="/wiki/Dimension_of_Miracles" title="Dimension of Miracles">Dimension of Miracles</a></i> is often cited as an influence on <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Adams" title="Douglas Adams">Douglas Adams</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hitchhiker%27s_Guide_to_the_Galaxy" title="The Hitchhiker&#39;s Guide to the Galaxy">The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy</a></i>, although in an interview for <a href="/wiki/Neil_Gaiman" title="Neil Gaiman">Neil Gaiman</a>'s book <i><a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Panic:_The_Official_Hitchhiker%27s_Guide_to_the_Galaxy_Companion" title="Don&#39;t Panic: The Official Hitchhiker&#39;s Guide to the Galaxy Companion">Don't Panic: The Official Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy Companion</a></i>, Adams said he had not read it until after writing the Guide.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Film.2C_TV_and_radio_adaptations"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Film,_TV_and_radio_adaptations">Film, TV and radio adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Sheckley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Film, TV and radio adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Robert_Sheckley_5405.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Robert_Sheckley_5405.jpg/220px-Robert_Sheckley_5405.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="257" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Robert_Sheckley_5405.jpg/330px-Robert_Sheckley_5405.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Robert_Sheckley_5405.jpg/440px-Robert_Sheckley_5405.jpg 2x" data-file-width="498" data-file-height="581" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Robert_Sheckley_5405.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Robert Sheckley c. 1954</div></div></div>
<p>One of Sheckley's early works, the 1953 <i><a href="/wiki/Galaxy_science_fiction" class="mw-redirect" title="Galaxy science fiction">Galaxy</a></i> short story "<a href="/wiki/Seventh_Victim" title="Seventh Victim">Seventh Victim</a>", was the basis for the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_10th_Victim" title="The 10th Victim">The 10th Victim</a></i>, also known by the original Italian title <i>La decima vittima</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-theguardian.com_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian.com-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The film starred <a href="/wiki/Marcello_Mastroianni" title="Marcello Mastroianni">Marcello Mastroianni</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ursula_Andress" title="Ursula Andress">Ursula Andress</a>. A novelization of the film, also written by Sheckley, was published in 1966.   The satirical premise  is that in the future killings are legal and televised, and that potential victims or hunters can get corporate sponsors and extra perks to assist them in succeeding as a professional, corporate-sponsored, celebrity killer.
</p><p>Sheckley's novel <i><a href="/wiki/Immortality,_Inc." title="Immortality, Inc.">Immortality, Inc.</a></i>&#8212;about a world in which the afterlife could be obtained via a scientific process&#8212;was very loosely adapted into a film, the 1992 <i><a href="/wiki/Freejack" title="Freejack">Freejack</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Mick_Jagger" title="Mick Jagger">Mick Jagger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Estevez" title="Emilio Estevez">Emilio Estevez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rene_Russo" title="Rene Russo">Rene Russo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Hopkins" title="Anthony Hopkins">Anthony Hopkins</a>. It was also adapted into the first episode of the third season of the British BBC series <i><a href="/wiki/Out_of_the_Unknown" title="Out of the Unknown">Out of the Unknown</a></i>. This episode is lost due to the then common practice of wiping the shows after broadcast.
</p><p>The 1954 story "Ghost V" and the 1955 story "The Lifeboat Mutiny" were adapted into two episodes of the <a href="/wiki/USSR" class="mw-redirect" title="USSR">USSR</a> science fiction TV series <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=This_Fantastic_World&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="This Fantastic World (page does not exist)">This Fantastic World</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> "Ghost V" was staged also by Estonian TV channel <a href="/wiki/Eesti_Televisioon" title="Eesti Televisioon">ETV</a> in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1958 short story "The Prize of Peril" was adapted in 1970 as the German TV movie <i><a href="/wiki/Das_Millionenspiel" title="Das Millionenspiel">Das Millionenspiel</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and again in 1983 as the French movie <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Prix_du_Danger" class="mw-redirect" title="Le Prix du Danger">Le Prix du Danger</a></i>. Written about a man who goes on a TV show in which he must evade people out to kill him for a week in order to win a large cash prize, it is perhaps the first-ever published work predicting the advent of <a href="/wiki/Reality_television" title="Reality television">reality television</a>. There are many similarities between Sheckley's story and Stephen King's novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Running_Man_(novel)" title="The Running Man (novel)">The Running Man</a></i>, published later in 1982, of which a film adaptation was later made.
</p><p>"<a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Game_of_X&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Game of X (page does not exist)">The Game of X</a>" (1965) was loosely adapted as the 1981 Disney film, <i><a href="/wiki/Condorman" title="Condorman">Condorman</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The short story "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Watchbird&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Watchbird (page does not exist)">Watchbird</a>" was adapted for the short-lived TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Masters_of_Science_Fiction" title="Masters of Science Fiction">Masters of Science Fiction</a></i>. It did not initially air in the US, but on February 12, 2012, the <a href="/wiki/Science_Channel" title="Science Channel">Science Channel</a> began airing the episodes, under the title <i>Stephen Hawking's Sci-Fi Masters</i>, beginning with the first domestic airing of the episode "Watchbirds".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> It was included on the DVD set for the series.
</p><p>The 1958 short story "The Store of the Worlds" from the collection "<a href="/wiki/Store_of_Infinity" title="Store of Infinity">The Store of Infinity</a>" was adapted to a short film titled "The Escape" by the filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Paul_Franklin_(visual_effects_supervisor)" title="Paul Franklin (visual effects supervisor)">Paul Franklin</a>, starring <a href="/wiki/Julian_Sands" title="Julian Sands">Julian Sands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Art_Malik" title="Art Malik">Art Malik</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olivia_Williams" title="Olivia Williams">Olivia Williams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ben_Miller" title="Ben Miller">Ben Miller</a>.  The film had its premiere at the 2017 <a href="/wiki/Tribeca_Film_Festival" title="Tribeca Film Festival">Tribeca Film Festival</a> in New York.
</p><p>A number of Sheckley's works, both as Sheckley and as Finn O'Donnevan, were also adapted for the radio show <i><a href="/wiki/X_Minus_One" title="X Minus One">X Minus One</a></i> in the late 1950s, including the above-mentioned "Seventh Victim", "Bad Medicine", and "Protection". The radio show <i><a href="/wiki/Tales_of_Tomorrow" title="Tales of Tomorrow">Tales of Tomorrow</a></i> also in the late 1950s did a version of "Watchbird" and <a href="/wiki/South_African_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="South African Broadcasting Corporation">South Africa radio</a> did their version of "Watchbird" on the series <i>SF68</i>.
</p><p>In 2007, Chris Larner and David Gilbert created the radio show "The Laxian Key" based on Sheckley's short stories. It was broadcast on BBC Radio 4 Extra.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
