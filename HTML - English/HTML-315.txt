<p>The <b>Internal Revenue Service</b> (<b>IRS</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Revenue_service" title="Revenue service">revenue service</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">United States federal government</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Government_agency" title="Government agency">government agency</a> is a bureau of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Department of the Treasury">Department of the Treasury</a>, and is under the immediate direction of the <a href="/wiki/Commissioner_of_Internal_Revenue" title="Commissioner of Internal Revenue">Commissioner of Internal Revenue</a>, who is appointed to a five-year term by the President of the United States. The IRS is responsible for <a href="/wiki/Revenue_service" title="Revenue service">collecting taxes</a> and administering the <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Code" title="Internal Revenue Code">Internal Revenue Code</a>, the main body of federal statutory tax law of the United States. The duties of the IRS include providing tax assistance to taxpayers and pursuing and resolving instances of erroneous or fraudulent tax filings. The IRS has also overseen various benefits programs, and enforces portions of the <a href="/wiki/Patient_Protection_and_Affordable_Care_Act" title="Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act">Affordable Care Act</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The IRS originated with the Commissioner of Internal Revenue, a federal office created in 1862 to assess the nation's first <a href="/wiki/Income_tax" title="Income tax">income tax</a>, which was to raise funds for the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>. The temporary measure provided over a fifth of the Union's war expenses and was allowed to expire a decade later. In 1913, the <a href="/wiki/Sixteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Sixteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Sixteenth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution</a> was ratified authorizing Congress to impose a tax on income, and the Bureau of Internal Revenue was established. In 1953, the agency was renamed the Internal Revenue Service.
</p><p>Though the IRS brings in most of the revenue needed to fund the federal government, its resources have been cut year after year. In 2016 the American College of Tax Counsel wrote to the Congressional leadership stating, "We have watched the agency struggle with significant decreases in funding that have caused staffing and morale issues. In our practices, we have seen the negative impact this has had on our clients, the taxpayers."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 2017 fiscal year, the IRS processed more than 245 million returns and collected more than $3.4 trillion in gross revenue, spending 34¢ for every $100 it collected.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 28, 2018, <a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_News" title="Bloomberg News">Bloomberg News</a> wrote, "The agency has been reeling from budget cuts. The current budget of $11.43 billion is less than in fiscal 2008, and the IRS pared about 15 percent of its workforce over the past five years. The enforcement staff has plunged by more than 25 percent since 2010."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 2018 fiscal year, the U.S. federal government spent $779 billion more than it brought in. It is estimated that in fiscal year 2019 the loss will be close to $1 trillion.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  In fiscal year 2019 the IRS plans to cut an additional 2,200 employees.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_Revenue_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Taxation_history_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Taxation history of the United States">Taxation history of the United States</a></div>
<h3><span id="American_Civil_War_.281861.E2.80.9365.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Civil_War_(1861–65)">American Civil War (1861–65)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_Revenue_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: American Civil War (1861–65)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a href="/wiki/File:George_S._Boutwell,_the_first_Commissioner_of_Internal_Revenue_Service.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/75/George_S._Boutwell%2C_the_first_Commissioner_of_Internal_Revenue_Service.jpg" decoding="async" width="190" height="225" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="190" data-file-height="225" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:George_S._Boutwell,_the_first_Commissioner_of_Internal_Revenue_Service.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/George_S._Boutwell" title="George S. Boutwell">George S. Boutwell</a> was the first <a href="/wiki/Commissioner_of_Internal_Revenue" title="Commissioner of Internal Revenue">Commissioner of Internal Revenue</a> under President <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In July 1862, during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> passed the <a href="/wiki/Revenue_Act_of_1862" title="Revenue Act of 1862">Revenue Act of 1862</a>, creating the office of Commissioner of Internal Revenue and enacting a temporary <a href="/wiki/Income_tax" title="Income tax">income tax</a> to pay war expenses.
</p><p>The Revenue Act of 1862 was passed as an emergency and temporary war-time tax. It copied a relatively new British system of income taxation, instead of trade and property taxation. The first income tax was passed in 1862:
</p>
<ul><li>The initial rate was 3% on income over $800, which exempted most wage-earners.</li>
<li>In 1862 the rate was 3% on income between $600 and $10,000, and 5% on income over $10,000.</li>
<li>In 1864 the rate was 5% on income between $600 and $5,000; 7.5% on income $5,000–10,000; and 10% on income $10,000 and above.</li></ul>
<p>By the end of the war, 10% of <a href="/wiki/Union_(American_Civil_War)" title="Union (American Civil War)">Union</a> households had paid some form of income tax, and the Union raised 21% of its war revenue through income taxes.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Post_Civil_War.2C_Reconstruction.2C_and_popular_tax_reform_.281866.E2.80.931913.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_Civil_War,_Reconstruction,_and_popular_tax_reform_(1866–1913)">Post Civil War, Reconstruction, and popular tax reform (1866–1913)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_Revenue_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Post Civil War, Reconstruction, and popular tax reform (1866–1913)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the Civil War, <a href="/wiki/Reconstruction_era" title="Reconstruction era">Reconstruction</a>, railroads, and transforming the North and South war machines towards peacetime required public funding. However, in 1872, seven years after the war, lawmakers allowed the temporary Civil War income tax to expire.
</p><p>Income taxes evolved, but in 1894 the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a> declared the Income Tax of 1894 unconstitutional in <i><a href="/wiki/Pollock_v._Farmers%27_Loan_%26_Trust_Co." title="Pollock v. Farmers&#39; Loan &amp; Trust Co.">Pollock v. Farmers' Loan &amp; Trust Co.</a></i>, a decision that contradicted <i><a href="/wiki/Hylton_v._United_States" title="Hylton v. United States">Hylton v. United States</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The federal government scrambled to raise money.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1906, with the election of President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a>, and later his successor <a href="/wiki/William_Howard_Taft" title="William Howard Taft">William Howard Taft</a>, the United States saw a <a href="/wiki/Populism" title="Populism">populist</a> movement for tax reform. This movement culminated during then candidate <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a>'s election of 1912 and in February 1913, the ratification of the <a href="/wiki/Sixteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Sixteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Sixteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution</a>:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Congress shall have power to lay and collect taxes on incomes, from whatever source derived, without apportionment among the several States, and without regard to any census or enumeration.
</p></blockquote>
<p>This granted Congress the specific power to impose an income tax without regard to apportionment among the states by population. By February 1913, 36 states had ratified the change to the Constitution. It was further ratified by six more states by March. Of the 48 states at the time, 42 ratified it. Connecticut, Rhode Island, and Utah rejected the amendment; Pennsylvania, Virginia, and Florida did not take up the issue.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Post_16th_Amendment_.281913.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_16th_Amendment_(1913–present)">Post 16th Amendment (1913–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_Revenue_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post 16th Amendment (1913–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though the constitutional amendment to allow the Federal government to collect income taxes was proposed by <a href="/wiki/William_Howard_Taft" title="William Howard Taft">President Taft</a> in 1909, the <a href="/wiki/Sixteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Sixteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">16th Amendment</a> was not ratified until 1913, just before the start of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>. In 1913 the first edition of the 1040 form was introduced. A copy of the very first IRS 1040 form, can be found at the IRS website<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> showing that only those with incomes of $3,000 (adjusted for inflation, the equivalent of $74,986 in 2017) or more were instructed to file.
</p><p>In the first year after ratification of the 16th Amendment, no taxes were collected. Instead, taxpayers simply completed the form and the IRS checked the form for accuracy. The IRS's workload jumped by ten-fold, triggering a massive restructuring. Professional tax collectors began to replace a system of "patronage" appointments. The IRS doubled its staff, but was still processing 1917 returns in 1919.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Income tax raised much of the money required to finance the war effort; in 1918 a new Revenue Act established a top tax rate of 77%.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1920_tax_forms_IRS.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/1920_tax_forms_IRS.jpg/220px-1920_tax_forms_IRS.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="168" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/1920_tax_forms_IRS.jpg/330px-1920_tax_forms_IRS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/1920_tax_forms_IRS.jpg/440px-1920_tax_forms_IRS.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="1175" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1920_tax_forms_IRS.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>People filing tax forms in 1920.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1919 the IRS was tasked with enforcement of laws relating to <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_in_the_United_States" title="Prohibition in the United States">prohibition of alcohol sales and manufacture</a>; this was transferred to the jurisdiction of the Department of Justice in 1930.  After repeal in 1933, the IRS resumed collection of taxes on beverage alcohol.<sup id="cite_ref-IRSHL_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IRSHL-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The alcohol, tobacco and firearms activities of the bureau were segregated into the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Alcohol,_Tobacco,_Firearms_and_Explosives" title="Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives">Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives</a> in 1972.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Revenue_Act_of_1942" title="Revenue Act of 1942">new tax act was passed in 1942</a> as the United States entered the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a>. This act included a special wartime surcharge. The number of American citizens who paid income tax increased from about 4 million in 1939 to over 42 million by 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1952, after a series of politically damaging incidents of tax evasion and bribery among its own employees, the Bureau of Internal Revenue was reorganized under a plan put forward by <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">President Truman</a>, with the approval of Congress. The reorganization decentralized many functions to new district offices which replaced the collector's offices. Civil service directors were appointed to replace the politically appointed collectors of the Bureau of Internal Revenue. Not long after, the Bureau was renamed the Internal Revenue Service.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1954 the filing deadline was moved from March 15 to April 15.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Tax_Reform_Act_of_1969" title="Tax Reform Act of 1969">Tax Reform Act of 1969</a> created the <a href="/wiki/Alternative_minimum_tax" title="Alternative minimum tax">Alternative Minimum Tax</a>.
</p><p>By 1986, limited electronic filing of tax returns was possible.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Service_Restructuring_and_Reform_Act_of_1998" title="Internal Revenue Service Restructuring and Reform Act of 1998">Internal Revenue Service Restructuring and Reform Act of 1998</a> ("RRA 98") changed the organization from geographically oriented to an organization based on four operating divisions.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> It added "10 deadly sins" that require immediate termination of IRS employees found to have committed certain misconduct.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Enforcement activities declined. The IRS Oversight Board noted that the decline in enforcement activities has "rais[ed] questions about tax compliance and fairness to the vast majority of citizens who pay all their taxes."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In June 2012, the IRS Oversight Board recommended to Treasury a fiscal year 2014 budget of $13.074 billion for the Internal Revenue Service.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On December 20, 2017, Congress passed the <a href="/wiki/Tax_Cuts_and_Jobs_Act_of_2017" title="Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017">Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017</a>. It was signed into law by President Trump on December 22, 2017.
</p><p>In the 2017 fiscal year, the IRS had 76,832 employees conducting its work, a decrease of 14.9 percent from 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-workforce_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-workforce-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Presidential_tax_returns_.281973.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidential_tax_returns_(1973)">Presidential tax returns (1973)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_Revenue_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Presidential tax returns (1973)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From the 1950s through the 1970s, the IRS began using technology such as microfilm to keep and organize records. Access to this information proved controversial, when <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">President Richard Nixon</a>'s tax returns were leaked to the public. His tax advisor, Edward L. Morgan, became the fourth law-enforcement official to be charged with a crime during <a href="/wiki/Watergate_scandal" title="Watergate scandal">Watergate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>John Requard, Jr., accused of leaking the Nixon tax returns, collected delinquent taxes in the slums of Washington. In his words: "We went after people for nickels and dimes, many of them poor and in many cases illiterate people who didn't know how to deal with a government agency." Requard admitted that he saw the returns, but denied that he leaked them.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Reporter <a href="/wiki/Jack_White_(reporter)" title="Jack White (reporter)">Jack White</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Providence_Journal" title="The Providence Journal">The Providence Journal</a></i> won the <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a> for reporting about Nixon's tax returns. Nixon, with a salary of $200,000, paid $792.81 in federal income tax in 1970 and $878.03 in 1971, with deductions of $571,000 for donating "vice-presidential papers".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> This was one of the reasons for his famous statement: "Well, I'm not a crook. I've earned everything I've got."
</p><p>So controversial was this leak, that most later US Presidents released their tax returns (though sometimes only partially). These returns can be found online at the Tax History Project.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Computerization_.281959.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Computerization_(1959–present)">Computerization (1959–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_Revenue_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Computerization (1959–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By the end of the Second World War, the IRS was handling sixty million tax returns each year, using a combination of mechanical desk calculators, <a href="/wiki/Accounting_machine" title="Accounting machine">accounting machines</a>, and pencil and paper forms. In 1948 <a href="/wiki/Punched_card" title="Punched card">punch card</a> equipment was used. The first trial of a computer system for income tax processing was in 1955, when an <a href="/wiki/IBM_650" title="IBM 650">IBM 650</a> installed at <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_metropolitan_area" title="Kansas City metropolitan area">Kansas City</a> processed 1.1 million returns. The IRS was authorized to proceed with computerization in 1959,  and purchased <a href="/wiki/IBM_1401" title="IBM 1401">IBM 1401</a> and <a href="/wiki/IBM_7070" title="IBM 7070">IBM 7070</a> systems for local and regional data processing centers. The <a href="/wiki/Social_Security_number" title="Social Security number">Social Security number</a> was used for taxpayer identification starting in 1965. By 1967, all returns were processed by computer and punched card data entry was phased out.<sup id="cite_ref-PC03_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PC03-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Information processing in the IRS systems of the late 1960s was in batch mode; microfilm records were updated weekly and distributed to regional centers for handling tax inquiries. A project to implement an interactive, realtime system, the "Tax Administration System", was launched, that would provide thousands of local interactive terminals at IRS offices. However, the <a href="/wiki/Government_Accountability_Office" title="Government Accountability Office">General Accounting Office</a> prepared a report critical of the lack of protection of privacy in TAS, and the project was abandoned in 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-PC03_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PC03-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1995, the IRS began to use the public Internet for electronic filing. Since the introduction of e-filing, self-paced online tax services have flourished, augmenting the work of tax accountants, who were sometimes replaced.
</p><p>In 2003, the IRS struck a deal with tax software vendors: The IRS would not develop online filing software and, in return, software vendors would provide free e-filing to most Americans.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> In 2009, 70% of filers qualified for free electronic filing of federal returns.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to an inspector general's report, released in November 2013, identity theft in the United States is blamed for US$4 billion worth of fraudulent 2012 tax refunds by the IRS. Fraudulent claims were made with the use of stolen taxpayer identification and Social Security numbers, with returns sent to addresses both in the US and internationally. Following the release of the findings, the IRS stated that it resolved most of the identity theft cases of 2013 within 120 days, while the average time to resolve cases from the 2011/2012 tax period was 312 days.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-foxnews.com_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-foxnews.com-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 2014, IRS Commissioner <a href="/wiki/John_Koskinen" title="John Koskinen">John Koskinen</a> expressed concern over the organization's ability to handle <a href="/wiki/Patient_Protection_and_Affordable_Care_Act" title="Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act">Obamacare</a> and administer <a href="/wiki/Premium_tax_credit" title="Premium tax credit">premium tax credits</a> that help people pay for health plans from the health law's insurance exchanges. It will also enforce the law's <a href="/wiki/Individual_shared_responsibility_provision" title="Individual shared responsibility provision">individual mandate</a>, which requires most Americans to hold health insurance.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>  In January 2015, <a href="/wiki/Fox_News" title="Fox News">Fox News</a> obtained an email which predicted a messy tax season on several fronts. The email was sent by IRS Commissioner Koskinen to workers. Koskinen predicted the IRS would shut down operations for two days later this year which would result in unpaid <a href="/wiki/Furlough" title="Furlough">furloughs</a> for employees and <a href="/wiki/Fan_service" title="Fan service">service cuts</a> for <a href="/wiki/Taxpayer" title="Taxpayer">taxpayers</a>. Koskinen also said delays to <a href="/wiki/IT" class="mw-redirect" title="IT">IT</a> investments of more than $200 million may delay new taxpayer protections against <a href="/wiki/Identity_theft_in_the_United_States" title="Identity theft in the United States">identity theft</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-foxnews.com_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-foxnews.com-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Also in January 2015, the editorial board of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> called the IRS budget cuts penny-wise-and-pound-foolish, where for every dollar of cuts in the budget, $6 were lost in  tax revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_IRS_name">History of the IRS name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_Revenue_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History of the IRS name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Department_of_Treasury_retirement_reception_of_Beth_Tucker_01.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Department_of_Treasury_retirement_reception_of_Beth_Tucker_01.JPG/220px-Department_of_Treasury_retirement_reception_of_Beth_Tucker_01.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Department_of_Treasury_retirement_reception_of_Beth_Tucker_01.JPG/330px-Department_of_Treasury_retirement_reception_of_Beth_Tucker_01.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Department_of_Treasury_retirement_reception_of_Beth_Tucker_01.JPG/440px-Department_of_Treasury_retirement_reception_of_Beth_Tucker_01.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2160" data-file-height="1440" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Department_of_Treasury_retirement_reception_of_Beth_Tucker_01.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>IRS and Department of the Treasury seal on lectern</div></div></div>
<p>As early as the year 1918, the Bureau of Internal Revenue began using the name "Internal Revenue Service" on at least one tax form.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In 1953, the name change to the "Internal Revenue Service" was formalized in Treasury Decision 6038.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_organization">Current organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_Revenue_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Current organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 1980s saw a reorganization of the IRS. A bipartisan commission was created with several mandates, among them to increase customer service and improve collections.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Congress later enacted the <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Service_Restructuring_and_Reform_Act_of_1998" title="Internal Revenue Service Restructuring and Reform Act of 1998">Internal Revenue Service Restructuring and Reform Act of 1998</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of that Act, the IRS now functions under four major operating divisions: Large Business and International (LB&amp;I), Small Business/Self-Employed (SB/SE), Wage and Investment (W&amp;I), and Tax Exempt &amp; Government Entities (TE/GE). Effective October 1, 2010, the name of the Large and Mid-Size Business division was changed to the Large Business &amp; International (LB&amp;I) division.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The IRS also includes a criminal law enforcement division: the <a href="/wiki/IRS_Criminal_Investigation_Division" title="IRS Criminal Investigation Division">IRS Criminal Investigation Division</a>. While there is some evidence that customer service has improved, lost tax revenues in 2001 were over $323 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The IRS is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, and does most of its <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">computer programming</a> in Maryland. It currently operates five submission processing centers which process <a href="/wiki/Tax_return_(United_States)" title="Tax return (United States)">returns</a> sent by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">mail</a> and returns filed electronically via E-file. Different types of returns are processed at the various centers with some centers processing individual returns and others processing business returns.
</p><p>Originally, there were ten submission processing centers across the country. In the early 2000s, the IRS closed five centers: Andover, MA; Holtsville, NY; Philadelphia, PA; Atlanta, GA; and Memphis, TN. This currently leaves five centers processing returns: Austin, TX; Covington, KY; Fresno, CA; Kansas City, MO; and Ogden, UT. In October 2016 the IRS announced that three more centers will close over a six-year period: Covington, KY in 2019; Fresno, CA in 2021; and Austin, TX in 2024. This will leave Kansas City, MO and Ogden, UT as the final two submission processing centers after 2024.
</p><p>The IRS also operates three computer centers around the country (in Detroit, Michigan; Martinsburg, West Virginia; and Memphis, Tennessee).<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commissioner">Commissioner</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_Revenue_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Commissioner">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Commissioner_of_Internal_Revenue" title="Commissioner of Internal Revenue">Commissioner of Internal Revenue</a></div>
<p>The current IRS commissioner is <a href="/wiki/Charles_Rettig" title="Charles Rettig">Charles P. Rettig</a> of California.
</p><p>There have been 48 previous commissioners of Internal Revenue and 28 acting commissioners since the agency's creation in 1862.<sup id="cite_ref-commish_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-commish-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From May 22, 2013 to December 23, 2013, senior official at the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Management_and_Budget" title="Office of Management and Budget">Office of Management and Budget</a> <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Werfel" title="Daniel Werfel">Daniel Werfel</a> was acting Commissioner of Internal Revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-WH_press_release_2013_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WH_press_release_2013-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Werfel, who attended law school at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina" title="University of North Carolina">University of North Carolina</a> and attained a master's degree from <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a>, prepared the government for a potential shutdown in 2011 by determining which services that would remain in existence.<sup id="cite_ref-WH_press_release_2013_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WH_press_release_2013-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>No IRS commissioner has served more than five years and one month since Guy Helvering, who served 10 years until 1943.<sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg1_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg1-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The most recent commissioner to serve the longest term was Doug Shulman, who was appointed by President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> and served for five years.<sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg1_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg1-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Office_of_the_Taxpayer_Advocate">Office of the Taxpayer Advocate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_Revenue_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Office of the Taxpayer Advocate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Office_of_the_Taxpayer_Advocate" title="Office of the Taxpayer Advocate">Office of the Taxpayer Advocate</a>, also called the Taxpayer Advocate Service, is an independent office within the IRS responsible for assisting taxpayers in resolving their problems with the IRS and identifying systemic problems that exist within the IRS.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> The acting <a href="/wiki/United_States_Taxpayer_Advocate" title="United States Taxpayer Advocate">United States Taxpayer Advocate</a>, also known as the acting National Taxpayer Advocate, is Bridget T. Roberts.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Independent_Office_of_Appeals">Independent Office of Appeals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_Revenue_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Independent Office of Appeals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Independent_Office_of_Appeals" title="Independent Office of Appeals">Independent Office of Appeals</a></div>
<p>The Independent Office of Appeals is an independent organization within the IRS that helps taxpayers resolve their tax disputes through an informal, administrative process. Its mission is to resolve tax controversies fairly and impartially, without litigation.<sup id="cite_ref-IRS_Appeals_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IRS_Appeals-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Resolution of a case in Appeals "could take anywhere from 90 days to a year."<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The current chief is Donna C. Hansberry.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Office_of_Professional_Responsibility_.28OPR.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Office_of_Professional_Responsibility_(OPR)">Office of Professional Responsibility (OPR)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_Revenue_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Office of Professional Responsibility (OPR)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Professional_Responsibility_(IRS)" title="Office of Professional Responsibility (IRS)">Office of Professional Responsibility (IRS)</a></div>
<p>OPR investigates suspected misconduct by attorneys, CPAs and enrolled agents ("tax practitioners") involving practice before the IRS and has the power to impose various penalties. OPR can also take action against tax practitioners for conviction of a crime or failure to file their own tax returns.
</p><p>According to former OPR director Karen Hawkins, "The focus has been on <a href="/wiki/Roadkill" title="Roadkill">roadkill</a> - the easy cases of tax practitioners who are non-filers."<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The current acting director is Elizabeth Kastenberg.<sup id="cite_ref-IRS_OPR_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IRS_OPR-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Programs">Programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_Revenue_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Volunteer Income Tax Assistance (VITA) and Tax Counseling for the Elderly (TCE) are volunteer programs that the IRS runs to train volunteers and provide tax assistance and counseling to taxpayers.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>  Volunteers can study e-course material, take tests, and practice using tax-preparation software. Link &amp; Learn Taxes (searchable by keyword on the IRS website), is the free e-learning portion of VITA/TCE program for training volunteers.
</p>

<p>For fiscal year 2009, the U.S. Congress appropriated spending of approximately $12.624 billion of "discretionary budget authority" to operate the Department of the Treasury, of which $11.522 billion was allocated to the IRS. The projected estimate of the budget for the IRS for fiscal year 2011 was $12.633 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> By contrast, during Fiscal Year (FY) 2006, the IRS collected more than $2.2 trillion in tax (net of refunds), about 44 percent of which was attributable to the individual income tax. This is partially due to the nature of the individual income tax category, containing taxes collected from working class, small business, self-employed, and capital gains. The top 5% of income earners pay 38.284% of the federal tax collected.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2007, the agency estimates that the United States Treasury is owed $354 billion more than the amount the IRS collects.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> This is known as the tax gap.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The gross tax gap is the amount of true tax liability that is not paid voluntarily and timely. For years 2008-2010, the estimated gross tax gap was $458 billion. The net tax gap is the gross tax gap less tax that will be subsequently collected, either paid voluntarily or as the result of IRS administrative and enforcement activities; it is the portion of the gross tax gap that will not be paid. It is estimated that $52 billion of the gross tax gap was eventually collected resulting in a net tax gap of $406 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, 234 million tax returns were filed allowing the IRS to collect $2.4 trillion out of which $384 billion were attributed to mistake or <a href="/wiki/Tax_evasion" title="Tax evasion">fraud</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Outsourcing_collection_and_tax-assistance">Outsourcing collection and tax-assistance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_Revenue_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Outsourcing collection and tax-assistance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 2006, the IRS started to outsource the collection of taxpayers debts to private debt collection agencies. Opponents to this change note that the IRS will be handing over personal information to these debt collection agencies, who are being paid between 29% and 39% of the amount collected. Opponents are also worried about the agencies' being paid on percent collected, because it will encourage the collectors to use pressure tactics to collect the maximum amount. IRS spokesman Terry Lemons responds to these critics saying the new system "is a sound, balanced program that respects taxpayers' rights and taxpayer privacy." Other state and local agencies also use private collection agencies.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2009, the IRS announced that it would no longer outsource the collection of taxpayers debts to private debt collection agencies. The IRS decided not to renew contracts to private debt collection agencies, and began a hiring program at its call sites and processing centers across the country to bring on more personnel to process collections internally from taxpayers.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>  As of October 2009, the IRS has ceased using private debt collection agencies.
</p><p>In September 2009, after <a href="/wiki/ACORN_2009_undercover_videos_controversy" title="ACORN 2009 undercover videos controversy">undercover exposé videos</a> of questionable activities by staff of one of the IRS's volunteer tax-assistance organizations were made public, the IRS removed <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Community_Organizations_for_Reform_Now" title="Association of Community Organizations for Reform Now">ACORN</a> from its volunteer tax-assistance program.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Administrative_functions">Administrative functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_Revenue_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Administrative functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Figure_1_IRS_Appropriations_for_Fiscal_Years_2000_to_2019_(49395060466).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Figure_1_IRS_Appropriations_for_Fiscal_Years_2000_to_2019_%2849395060466%29.jpg/310px-Figure_1_IRS_Appropriations_for_Fiscal_Years_2000_to_2019_%2849395060466%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="310" height="168" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Figure_1_IRS_Appropriations_for_Fiscal_Years_2000_to_2019_%2849395060466%29.jpg/465px-Figure_1_IRS_Appropriations_for_Fiscal_Years_2000_to_2019_%2849395060466%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Figure_1_IRS_Appropriations_for_Fiscal_Years_2000_to_2019_%2849395060466%29.jpg/620px-Figure_1_IRS_Appropriations_for_Fiscal_Years_2000_to_2019_%2849395060466%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2165" data-file-height="1175" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Figure_1_IRS_Appropriations_for_Fiscal_Years_2000_to_2019_(49395060466).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>IRS appropriations, 2000–2019<div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #870f18; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;Nominal appropriations</div><div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #0c4d8f; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;Adjusted for inflation</div></div></div></div>
<p>The IRS publishes <a href="/wiki/IRS_tax_forms" title="IRS tax forms">tax forms</a> which taxpayers are required to choose from and use for calculating and reporting their federal tax obligations. The IRS also publishes a number of forms for its own internal operations, such as Forms 3471 and 4228 (which are used during the initial processing of income tax returns).
</p><p>In addition to collection of revenue and pursuing tax cheaters, the IRS issues administrative rulings such as <a href="/wiki/Revenue_ruling" title="Revenue ruling">revenue rulings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Private_letter_ruling" title="Private letter ruling">private letter rulings</a>. In addition, the Service publishes the <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Bulletin" title="Internal Revenue Bulletin">Internal Revenue Bulletin</a> containing the various IRS pronouncements.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> The controlling authority of regulations and revenue rulings allows taxpayers to rely on them. A letter ruling is good for the taxpayer to whom it is issued, and gives some explanation of the Service's position on a particular tax issue.<sup id="cite_ref-irs.gov_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irs.gov-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>  Additionally, a letter ruling reasonably relied upon by a taxpayer allows for the waiver of penalties for underpayment of tax.
</p><p>As is the case with all administrative pronouncements, taxpayers sometimes litigate the validity of the pronouncements, and courts sometimes determine a particular rule to be invalid where the agency has exceeded its grant of authority. The IRS also issues formal pronouncements called Revenue Procedures, that among other things tell taxpayers how to correct prior tax errors. The IRS's own internal operations manual is the <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Manual" title="Internal Revenue Manual">Internal Revenue Manual</a>, which describes the clerical procedures for processing and auditing tax returns in excruciating detail. For example, the Internal Revenue Manual contains a special procedure for processing the tax returns of the President and <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President of the United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-irs.gov_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irs.gov-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>More formal rulemaking to give the Service's interpretation of a statute, or when the statute itself directs that the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Secretary of the Treasury">Secretary of the Treasury</a> shall provide, IRS undergoes the formal regulation process with a <a href="/wiki/Notice_of_proposed_rulemaking" title="Notice of proposed rulemaking">Notice of proposed rulemaking</a> (NPRM) published in the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Register" title="Federal Register">Federal Register</a> announcing the proposed regulation, the date of the in-person hearing, and the process for interested parties to have their views heard either in person at the hearing in Washington, D.C., or by mail. Following the statutory period provided in the <a href="/wiki/Administrative_Procedure_Act_(United_States)" title="Administrative Procedure Act (United States)">Administrative Procedure Act</a> the Service decides on the final regulations "as is," or as reflecting changes, or sometimes withdraws the proposed regulations. Generally, taxpayers may rely on proposed regulations until final regulations become effective. For example, human resource professionals are relying on the October 4, 2005 Proposed Regulations<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> (citation 70 <a href="/wiki/Federal_Register" title="Federal Register">F.R.</a> 57930-57984)<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> for the Section 409A on deferred compensation (the so-called <a href="/wiki/Enron" title="Enron">Enron</a> rules on deferred compensation to add teeth to the old rules) because regulations have not been finalized.
</p><p>The IRS oversaw the Homebuyer Credit and First Time Homebuyer Credit programs instituted by the federal government from 2008-2010. Those programs provided United States citizens with money toward the purchase of homes, regardless of income tax filings.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Labor_union">Labor union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_Revenue_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Labor union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Labor_unions_in_the_United_States" title="Labor unions in the United States">Labor unions in the United States</a></div>
<p>Most non-supervisory employees at the IRS are represented by a labor union. The exclusive labor union at the IRS is the <a href="/wiki/National_Treasury_Employees_Union" title="National Treasury Employees Union">National Treasury Employees Union</a> (NTEU). Employees aren't required to join the union or pay dues. The IRS and NTEU have a national <a href="/wiki/Collective_agreement" title="Collective agreement">collective bargaining agreement</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In pursuing administrative remedies against the IRS for certain unfair or illegal personnel actions, under federal law an IRS employee may choose only one of the three forums below:
</p>
<ul><li>NTEU, or</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/United_States_Merit_Systems_Protection_Board" title="United States Merit Systems Protection Board">United States Merit Systems Protection Board</a> (MSPB), or</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/United_States_Office_of_Special_Counsel" title="United States Office of Special Counsel">United States Office of Special Counsel</a> (OSC).<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Employees are also required to report certain misconduct to <a href="/wiki/Treasury_Inspector_General_for_Tax_Administration" title="Treasury Inspector General for Tax Administration">TIGTA</a>. Federal law prohibits reprisal or retaliation against an employee who reports wrongdoing.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_Revenue_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_allegations_of_misuse_of_the_Internal_Revenue_Service" title="List of allegations of misuse of the Internal Revenue Service">List of allegations of misuse of the Internal Revenue Service</a></div>
<p>The IRS has been accused of abusive behavior on multiple occasions.<sup id="cite_ref-witness_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-witness-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> Testimony was given before a Senate subcommittee that focused on cases of overly aggressive IRS collection tactics in considering a need for legislation to give taxpayers greater protection in disputes with the agency.
</p><p>Congress passed the <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Service_Restructuring_and_Reform_Act_of_1998" title="Internal Revenue Service Restructuring and Reform Act of 1998">Taxpayer Bill of Rights III</a> on July 22, 1998, which shifted the <a href="/wiki/Burden_of_proof_(law)" title="Burden of proof (law)">burden of proof</a> from the taxpayer to the IRS in certain limited situations. The IRS retains the legal authority to enforce liens and seize assets without obtaining judgment in court.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2002, the IRS accused James and Pamela Moran, as well as several others, of conspiracy, filing false tax returns and mail fraud as part of the Anderson Ark investment scheme. The Morans were eventually acquitted, and their attorney stated that the government should have realized that the couple was merely duped by those running the scheme.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2004, the law licenses of two former IRS lawyers were suspended after a federal court ruled that they defrauded the courts so the IRS could win a sum in tax shelter cases.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, the IRS became embroiled in a <a href="/wiki/IRS_targeting_controversy" title="IRS targeting controversy">political scandal</a> in which it was discovered that the agency subjected <a href="/wiki/Conservatism_in_the_United_States" title="Conservatism in the United States">conservative</a> or conservative-sounding groups filing for tax-exempt status to extra scrutiny.<sup id="cite_ref-washingtonpost1_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-washingtonpost1-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 5, 2014, 16 months after the scandal first erupted, a Senate Subcommittee released a report that confirmed that Internal Revenue Service used inappropriate criteria to target Tea Party groups, but found no evidence of political bias.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> The chairman of the Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations confirmed that while the actions were "inappropriate, intrusive, and burdensome," the Democrats have often experienced similar treatment.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> Republicans noted that 83% of the groups being held up by the IRS were right-leaning; and the Subcommittee Minority staff, which did not join the Majority staff report, filed a dissenting report entitled, "IRS Targeting Tea Party Groups".<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 25, 2015, the agency announced that criminals had illegally accessed the private tax information of over 100,000 taxpayers over a period of several months. By providing Social Security numbers and other information obtained from prior computer crimes, the criminals were able to use the IRS's online "Get Transcript" function to have the IRS provide them with the tax returns and other private information of American tax filers.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> On August 17, 2015, IRS disclosed that the breach had compromised an additional 220,000 taxpayer records.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> On February 27, 2016, the IRS disclosed that more than 700,000 Social Security numbers and other sensitive information had been stolen.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The IRS has been the subject of frequent criticism by many elected officials and candidates for political office, including some who have called to abolish the IRS. Among them were <a href="/wiki/Ted_Cruz" title="Ted Cruz">Ted Cruz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rand_Paul" title="Rand Paul">Rand Paul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Carson" title="Ben Carson">Ben Carson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Huckabee" title="Mike Huckabee">Mike Huckabee</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lugar" title="Richard Lugar">Richard Lugar</a><sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>.  In 1998 a Republican congressman introduced a bill to repeal the Internal Revenue Code by 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> In 2016, The <a href="/wiki/Republican_Study_Committee" title="Republican Study Committee">Republican Study Committee</a>, which counts over two-thirds of House of Representatives Republicans as its members, called for “the complete elimination of the IRS”, and Republican Representative <a href="/wiki/Rob_Woodall" title="Rob Woodall">Rob Woodall</a> of Georgia has introduced a bill every year since he entered Congress in 2011 to eliminate income taxes and abolish the IRS.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
