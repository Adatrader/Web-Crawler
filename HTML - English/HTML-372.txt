<p>A <b>Boston accent</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Regional_accent" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional accent">local accent</a> of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_New_England_English" title="Eastern New England English">Eastern New England English</a> native specifically to the city of <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> and its suburbs. Eastern New England English also traditionally includes New Hampshire, Maine, all of eastern Massachusetts, and arguably Rhode Island, though some uniquely local vocabulary appears only around Boston.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Some of the characteristics of traditional Boston accents may be retreating, particularly among younger residents. Linguist <a href="/wiki/William_Labov" title="William Labov">William Labov</a>, however, claims that, in the twenty-first century, there remains a relatively stable Boston accent.<sup id="cite_ref-Labov_2010_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Labov_2010-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Instead of merging the historical "short <i>o</i>" (as in <i>lot</i>) with the "broad <i>a</i>" (as in <i>father</i>), the Boston accent merges it with the "aw" as in "paw", so that <i>caught</i>, <i>cot</i>, <i>law</i>, <i>wand</i>, <i>rock</i>, <i>talk</i>, <i>doll</i>, and <i>wall</i> all are pronounced with the same open back rounded vowel <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɒ]</span> that contrasts with the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[a]</span> of <i>father</i> and <i>spa</i>, which means that even though <i>dark</i> has no <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɹ]</span> in many Boston accents, it remains distinct from <i>dock</i> because its vowel quality is different: <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[dak]</span> vs. <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[dɒk]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (July 2018)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> By contrast, New York accents and southern New England accents have <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[kʰɔətʔ]</span> for <i>caught</i> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[kʰäʔ]</span> for <i>cot</i> and the London accents have <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[kʰɔːt~kʰoːt]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[kʰɒt~kʰɔt]</span>, respectively.  In Boston and some other parts of New England, a few words ending in <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/t/</span>, e.g., <i>hot</i> and <i>got</i>, can be pronounced with an entirely different phoneme, however, sounding instead like <i>hut</i> and <i>gut</i>, respectively.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Eastern New England English has a so-called "<a href="/wiki/%C3%86-tensing" class="mw-redirect" title="Æ-tensing">nasal short-<i>a</i> system</a>". This means that the "short <i>a</i>" vowel <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[æ]</span> as in <i>cat</i> and <i>rat</i> becomes raised to a mid-high front diphthong <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[eə]</span> when it precedes a <a href="/wiki/Nasal_stop" class="mw-redirect" title="Nasal stop">nasal consonant</a> (but also, on a continuous scale in some other environments); thus, <i>man</i> is <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[meən]</span> and <i>planet</i> is <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈpʰleənəʔ]</span>. Boston shares this system with some of the Midwest and most of the West, though the raising in Boston tends to be more noticeable and extreme than elsewhere. This system is not shared with London or New York City accents. In addition to raising before nasals, Bostonians (unlike nearby New Hampshirites, for example) also tend to somewhat "raise" or "break" the "short <i>a</i>" sound the most before <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_fricative" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless fricative">voiceless fricatives</a> (followed by <a href="/wiki/Voiced_stop" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiced stop">voiced stops</a>, laterals, voiceless stops, and voiced fricatives), so that words like <i>half</i>, <i>bath</i>, and <i>glass</i> become <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[hɛəf]</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[bɛəθ]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɡlɛəs]</span>, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This trend began around the early-mid to mid-twentieth century, replacing the older Boston accent's London-like "broad <i>a</i>" system to an opener <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[a]</span> sound in those same example words (see "Declining characteristics" below).<sup id="cite_ref-Wood,_2010,_p._139_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood,_2010,_p._139-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Boston accents make a greater variety of distinctions between <a href="/wiki/English-language_vowel_changes_before_historic_r" class="mw-redirect" title="English-language vowel changes before historic r">short and long vowels before medial <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɹ]</span></a> than many other modern American accents do: <i>hurry</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈhʌɹi]</span> and <i>furry</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈfɝi]</span>; and <i>mirror</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈmɪɹə]</span> and <i>nearer</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈniəɹə]</span>, though some of these distinctions are somewhat endangered as people under 40 in neighboring New Hampshire and Maine have lost them. Boston shares these distinctions with both New York and Received Pronunciation, but the Midwest, for instance, has lost them entirely.
</p><p>The nuclei of the diphthongs <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/aɪ/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/aʊ/</span> may be raised to something like <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɐ]</span> before <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_consonant" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless consonant">voiceless consonants</a>: thus <i>write</i> has a higher vowel than <i>ride</i> and <i>lout</i> has a higher vowel than <i>loud</i>.  This effect is known usually as (one of the two phenomena of) <a href="/wiki/Canadian_raising" title="Canadian raising">Canadian raising</a>, though it is less extreme in New England than in most of <a href="/wiki/Canadian_English" title="Canadian English">Canada</a>. Furthermore, some Boston accents have a tendency to raise the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/aʊ/</span> diphthong in both voiced and voiceless environments and some Boston accents may raise the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/aɪ/</span> diphthong in certain voiced environments.
</p><p>The nuclei of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/oʊ/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/u/</span> are significantly less fronted than in many American accents. Both have <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/u/</span> may be diphthongized to approximately <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ʊu]</span> or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɵu]</span>.
</p><p>The more  deeply urban varieties of the Boston accent may use the <a href="/wiki/Dental_stop" title="Dental stop">dental stops</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[t̪, d̪]</span> to replace the normal English <a href="/wiki/Dental_fricative" title="Dental fricative">dental fricatives</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[θ, ð]</span>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-rhoticity">Non-rhoticity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston_accent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Non-rhoticity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The traditional Boston accent is non-rhotic, particularly in the early 1900s. Recent studies have shown that younger speakers use more of a <a href="/wiki/Rhotic_accent" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhotic accent">rhotic accent</a> than older speakers from the Boston region.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phoneme</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> does not appear in <a href="/wiki/Syllable_coda" class="mw-redirect" title="Syllable coda">coda position</a> (where in English <a href="/wiki/Phonotactics" title="Phonotactics">phonotactics</a> it must precede other consonants, see <a href="/wiki/English_phonology#Coda" title="English phonology">English phonology - coda</a>), as in most dialects of <a href="/wiki/English_language_in_England" title="English language in England">English in England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australian_English" title="Australian English">Australia</a>; <i>card</i> therefore becomes <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[kʰad]</span> "cahd". After high and mid-high vowels, the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> is replaced by <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ə]</span> or another neutral central vowel like <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɨ]</span>: <i>weird</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[wiɪd]</span>, <i>square</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈskweə]</span>. Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Lexical_stress" class="mw-redirect" title="Lexical stress">unstressed</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ər/</span> is replaced by <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ə]</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɐ]</span>, or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɨ]</span>, as in <i>color</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈkʰʌɫə]</span>.  A <a href="/wiki/Shibboleth" title="Shibboleth">famous example</a> is "Park the car in Harvard Yard", pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[pʰak ðə ˈkʰaɹ‿ɪn ˈhavəd ˈjad]</span>, or as if spelled "pahk the cah(r) in Hahvud Yahd".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Note that the <i>r</i> in <i>car</i> would usually be pronounced in this case, because the following word begins with a vowel. The Boston accent possesses both <a href="/wiki/Linking_R" class="mw-redirect" title="Linking R">linking R and intrusive R</a>: That is to say, an <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> will not be lost at the end of a word if the next word begins with a vowel, and indeed an <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> will be inserted after a word ending with a central or low vowel if the next word begins with a vowel: <i>the tuner is</i> and <i>the tuna is</i> are both <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ðə tʰunəɹɪz]</span>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Declining_features">Declining features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston_accent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Declining features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many characteristics of the Boston accent may be retreating, particularly among younger residents. In the most "old-fashioned" of Boston accents, there may be a lingering resistance to the <a href="/wiki/Horse%E2%80%93hoarse_merger" class="mw-redirect" title="Horse–hoarse merger">horse–hoarse merger</a>, so that <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɔr/</span> becomes a low back vowel <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɒ]</span>: <i>horse</i> is <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[hɒs]</span>, pronounced the same or almost the same as <i>hoss</i>, while the word <i>hoarse</i> is somewhat different <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[hoəs]</span>. Other words fall into these distinct classes too, like <i>for</i> vs. <i>four</i>: <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[fɒ]</span> vs. <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[foə]</span>. This distinction is rapidly fading out of currency, as it is in almost all regions of North America that still make it. For some Boston speakers, the words <i>tot</i>, <i>tort</i>, and <i>taught</i> may all be <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">homophones</a>.
</p><p>A feature that Boston speakers once shared with greater London, though now uncommon, is the <a href="/wiki/Broad_A" class="mw-redirect" title="Broad A">"broad <i>a</i>"</a> of the <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">BATH</span></span> <a href="/wiki/Lexical_set" title="Lexical set">lexical set</a> of words, making a distinction from the <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">TRAP</span></span> set (<span role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">see <a href="/wiki/Trap%E2%80%93bath_split" title="Trap–bath split">Trap–bath split</a></span>). In particular words that in other American accents have the "short <i>a</i>" pronounced as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[æ]</span>, that vowel was replaced in the nineteenth century (if not earlier and often sporadically by speakers as far back as the late eighteenth century)<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> with <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[a~ɑ]</span>: thus, <i>half</i> as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[haf]</span> and <i>bath</i> as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[baθ]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982523_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982523-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Fewer words have the broad <i>a</i> in Boston English than in the London accents, and fewer and fewer Boston speakers maintain the broad <i>a</i> system as time goes on, with its transition into a decline occurring in speakers born from about 1930 to 1950 (and first documented as a decline in 1977).<sup id="cite_ref-Wood,_2010,_p._139_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood,_2010,_p._139-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Boston speakers born before about 1930 used this broad <i>a</i> in the words <i>after</i>, <i>ask</i>, <i>aunt</i>, <i>bath</i>, <i>calf</i>, <i>can't</i>, <i>glass</i>, <i>half</i>, <i>laugh</i>, <i>pasture</i>, <i>path</i>, and perhaps other words, and born from about 1930 to 1950 use it only in <i>aunt</i>, <i>calf</i>, <i>half</i>, <i>laugh</i>, and <i>pass</i>. Speakers born since 1950 typically have no broad <i>a</i> whatsoever and, instead, slight <a href="/wiki/%C3%86-tensing" class="mw-redirect" title="Æ-tensing">/æ/ raising</a> (i.e. <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɛə]</span>), for example, in <i>craft</i>, <i>bad</i>, <i>math</i>, etc.)<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982523_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982523-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> with this same set of words and, variably, other instances of short <i>a</i> too.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982523_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982523-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Only <i>aunt</i> maintains the broad <i>a</i> sound in even the youngest speakers, though this one word is a common exception throughout all of the Northeastern U.S.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston_accent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although not all Boston-area speakers are non-rhotic, <a href="/wiki/Rhoticity_in_English" title="Rhoticity in English">non-rhoticity</a> remains the feature most widely associated with the region. As a result, it is frequently the subject of humor about Boston, as in comedian <a href="/wiki/Jon_Stewart" title="Jon Stewart">Jon Stewart</a> joking in his book <i><a href="/wiki/America_(The_Book)" title="America (The Book)">America</a></i> that, although <a href="/wiki/John_Adams" title="John Adams">John Adams</a> drafted the 1780 <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Massachusetts Constitution">Massachusetts Constitution</a>, "delegates from his state refused to ratify the letter 'R'."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Being conspicuous and easily identifiable as regional, Boston accents are routinely featured by actors in films set in Boston, particularly for working-class white characters, such as in <i><a href="/wiki/Good_Will_Hunting" title="Good Will Hunting">Good Will Hunting</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mystic_River_(film)" title="Mystic River (film)">Mystic River</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Departed" title="The Departed">The Departed</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Manchester_by_the_Sea_(film)" title="Manchester by the Sea (film)">Manchester by the Sea</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Town_(2010_film)" title="The Town (2010 film)">The Town</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ted_(film)" title="Ted (film)">Ted</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fighter" title="The Fighter">The Fighter</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Mass_(film)" title="Black Mass (film)">Black Mass</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gottlieb_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gottlieb-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Television series based within a Boston setting such as <i><a href="/wiki/Boston_Public" title="Boston Public">Boston Public</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Cheers" title="Cheers">Cheers</a></i> have featured the accent. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">Simpsons</a></i> character <a href="/wiki/Joe_Quimby" class="mw-redirect" title="Joe Quimby">Mayor Quimby</a> talks with an exaggerated Boston accent as reference to the former US Senator <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Ted Kennedy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Television <a href="/wiki/Comedy_sketch" class="mw-redirect" title="Comedy sketch">comedy sketches</a> have featured the accent, including "<a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Teens" title="The Boston Teens">The Boston Teens</a>" and "Dunkin Donuts" on <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i>, as well as "Boston Accent Trailer" on <i><a href="/wiki/Late_Night_with_Seth_Meyers" title="Late Night with Seth Meyers">Late Night with Seth Meyers</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gottlieb_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gottlieb-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Heat_(film)" title="The Heat (film)">The Heat</a></i>, the family of Shannon Mullins all speak with the Boston accent and confusion arises from the pronunciation of the word <i>narc</i> as <i>nahk</i>. <i><a href="/wiki/30_Rock" title="30 Rock">30 Rock</a></i> character Nancy Donovan speaks with a pronounced Boston accent. In the video game <i><a href="/wiki/Team_Fortress_2" title="Team Fortress 2">Team Fortress 2</a></i>, the character Scout, who is himself a Boston native, talks with a distinct Boston accent, although it sometimes lapses into a Brooklyn accent.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lexicon">Lexicon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston_accent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lexicon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some words most famously associated with the Boston area are:
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Bubbler" class="mw-redirect" title="Bubbler">Bubbler</a></i> or <i>water bubbler</i>: Drinking fountain.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>  This term is also used in <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a> and Australia.</li>
<li><i>Frappe</i> (pronounced "frap"): A beverage mixed with milk and ice cream, a.k.a. milkshake (in most other places), or if in Rhode Island (and especially if coffee flavored), called a "cabinet".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Hoodsie</i>: A small cup of ice cream, the kind that comes with a flat wooden spoon (from <a href="/wiki/HP_Hood" title="HP Hood">HP Hood</a>, the dairy that sells them.)<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Also (very offensive slang), a teenage girl.<sup id="cite_ref-Boston_To_English_Dictionary_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boston_To_English_Dictionary-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Elsewhere occasionally known as a <i>dixie cup</i>.</li>
<li><i>Jimmies</i>: <a href="/wiki/Sprinkles" title="Sprinkles">Sprinkles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Also common in the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_accent" class="mw-redirect" title="Philadelphia accent">Philadelphia</a> area.</li>
<li><i>Pissah</i>: "great" or "amazing" either realistically or sarcastically. Also spelled 'pissa'. This is from the word "pisser" with a Boston accent, but used as an adjective. Occasionally  combined with "wicked" to yield "wicked pissah".<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Spa</i>: A convenience store that has tonic (see below) on tap and (usually) sells sandwiches.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Tonic</i>: Any sweet, carbonated <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">soft drink</a> (known elsewhere as "soda" or "pop").<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (July 2018)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>
<li><i>Whiffle</i>: A crew cut or male haircut done with electric clippers.<sup id="cite_ref-Boston_To_English_Dictionary_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boston_To_English_Dictionary-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Wicked</i>: "Very" or "super", used as an adverb. "That hockey game was wicked awesome!" It can also be used to infer tones and moods, for example, "Ugh, that guy is wicked slow."</li></ul>
<p>Many words common to Boston are also common throughout the <a href="/wiki/Northern_American_English" title="Northern American English">New England dialects</a>: <i>blinkers</i> for "<a href="/wiki/Automotive_lighting#Turn_signals" title="Automotive lighting">automobile turn signals</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-Boston_To_English_Dictionary_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boston_To_English_Dictionary-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> (the <a href="/wiki/MassDOT" class="mw-redirect" title="MassDOT">Massachusetts Department of Transportation</a> even has signs reminding motorists, with Boston phonetic spelling, to "Use Yah Blinkah"),<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> <i>packie</i> (or <i>package store</i>) for "<a href="/wiki/Liquor_store" title="Liquor store">liquor store</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and <i>rotary</i> for "<a href="/wiki/Traffic_circle" class="mw-redirect" title="Traffic circle">traffic circle</a>" (these full-speed circular intersections being common in <a href="/wiki/Greater_Boston" title="Greater Boston">Greater Boston</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
