<p><b> Dr. Ashton Baldwin Carter</b> (born September 24, 1954) is an American <a href="/wiki/Public_policy" title="Public policy">public policy</a> professor who served as the 25th <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">secretary of defense</a> from February 2015 to January 2017. He is currently Director of the <a href="/wiki/Belfer_Center_for_Science_%26_International_Affairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Belfer Center for Science &amp; International Affairs">Belfer Center for Science &amp; International Affairs</a> at Harvard Kennedy School.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Carter began his career as a physicist. After a brief experience as an analyst for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congressional</a> <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Technology_Assessment" title="Office of Technology Assessment">Office of Technology Assessment</a>, he switched careers to public policy. He joined the <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_School_of_Government" title="John F. Kennedy School of Government">Kennedy School of Government</a> at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in 1984 and became chair of the International &amp; Global Affairs faculty.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Carter served as <a href="/wiki/Assistant_Secretary_of_Defense_for_Global_Strategic_Affairs" title="Assistant Secretary of Defense for Global Strategic Affairs">Assistant Secretary of Defense for International Security Policy</a> during <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Bill_Clinton" title="Presidency of Bill Clinton">President Clinton's first term</a>, from 1993 to 1996, responsible for policy regarding the former Soviet states, strategic affairs, and nuclear weapons. During <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Barack_Obama" title="Presidency of Barack Obama">President Obama's first term</a>, he served first as <a href="/wiki/Under_Secretary_of_Defense_for_Acquisition,_Technology_and_Logistics" class="mw-redirect" title="Under Secretary of Defense for Acquisition, Technology and Logistics">Under Secretary of Defense for Acquisition, Technology and Logistics</a> and then <a href="/wiki/United_States_Deputy_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Deputy Secretary of Defense">Deputy Secretary of Defense</a> until December 2013. In February 2015, he replaced <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Hagel" title="Chuck Hagel">Chuck Hagel</a> as Secretary of Defense and served until the end of the Obama administration.<sup id="cite_ref-Historical_Office_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Historical_Office-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For his service to national security, Carter has on five occasions been awarded the DOD <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Defense_Medal_for_Distinguished_Public_Service" title="Department of Defense Medal for Distinguished Public Service">Distinguished Public Service Medal</a>. He has also received the <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff" title="Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff">CJCS</a> <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff_Joint_Distinguished_Civilian_Service_Award" title="Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff Joint Distinguished Civilian Service Award">Joint Distinguished Civilian Service Award</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Defense_Intelligence_Medal" class="mw-redirect" title="Defense Intelligence Medal">Defense Intelligence Medal</a> for his contributions to intelligence. Carter is author or co-author of 11 books and more than 100 articles on physics, technology, national security, and management.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ash_Carter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ashton Baldwin Carter was born on September 24, 1954, in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia, Pennsylvania</a>. His father is William Stanley Carter, Jr., a <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> veteran, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">Navy</a> neurologist and psychiatrist, and department chairman at <a href="/wiki/Abington_Memorial_Hospital" class="mw-redirect" title="Abington Memorial Hospital">Abington Memorial Hospital</a> for 30 years. His mother is Anne Baldwin Carter, an English teacher.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-auto_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-philly.com_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-philly.com-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-english_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-english-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> He has three siblings, including <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_book" class="mw-redirect" title="Children&#39;s book">children's book</a> author <a href="/wiki/Cynthia_DeFelice" title="Cynthia DeFelice">Cynthia DeFelice</a>. As a child he was nicknamed Ash and Stoobie.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-english_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-english-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He was raised in <a href="/wiki/Abington_Township,_Montgomery_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Abington Township, Montgomery County, Pennsylvania">Abington, Pennsylvania</a>, on Wheatsheaf Lane.<sup id="cite_ref-philly.com_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-philly.com-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> At age 11, working at his first job at a Philadelphia car wash, he was fired for "wise-mouthing the owner."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-harvard.edu3_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard.edu3-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ash_Carter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carter was educated at Highland Elementary School (class of 1966) and at <a href="/wiki/Abington_Senior_High_School" title="Abington Senior High School">Abington Senior High School</a> (class of 1972) in Abington. In high school he was a wrestler, lacrosse player, cross-country runner, and president of the Honor Society.<sup id="cite_ref-philly.com_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-philly.com-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> He was inducted into Abington Senior High School's Hall of Fame in 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He attended the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> in the spring of 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-cnnlibrary_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnnlibrary-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In 1976 Carter received a B.A. in his double-major of Physics and Medieval History from <a href="/wiki/Yale_College" title="Yale College">Yale College</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Summa_cum_laude" class="mw-redirect" title="Summa cum laude">summa cum laude</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Phi_Beta_Kappa" title="Phi Beta Kappa">Phi Beta Kappa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cnnlibrary_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnnlibrary-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-timesofisraelrebecca_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timesofisraelrebecca-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> His senior thesis, “Quarks, Charm and the Psi Particle,” was published in <i><a href="/wiki/Yale_Scientific" class="mw-redirect" title="Yale Scientific">Yale Scientific</a></i> in 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> He was also an experimental research associate at <a href="/wiki/Fermi_National_Accelerator_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory">Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory</a> in 1975 (where he worked on <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quark</a> research) and at <a href="/wiki/Brookhaven_National_Laboratory" title="Brookhaven National Laboratory">Brookhaven National Laboratory</a> in 1976.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard.edu3_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard.edu3-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-harvard.edu1_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard.edu1-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Carter then became a <a href="/wiki/Rhodes_Scholarship" title="Rhodes Scholarship">Rhodes Scholar</a>, studying at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a>, from which he received his DPhil in <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physics" title="Theoretical physics">theoretical physics</a> in 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard.edu3_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard.edu3-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-timesofisraelrebecca_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timesofisraelrebecca-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He was subsequently a postdoctoral fellow research associate in theoretical physics at <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_University" title="Rockefeller University">Rockefeller University</a> from 1979 to 1980, studying <a href="/wiki/Time-reversal_invariance" class="mw-redirect" title="Time-reversal invariance">time-reversal invariance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_symmetry_breaking" class="mw-redirect" title="Dynamical symmetry breaking">dynamical symmetry breaking</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard.edu1_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard.edu1-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-auto1_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-harvard.edu4_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard.edu4-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> He was then a <a href="/wiki/Research_fellow" title="Research fellow">research fellow</a> at the <a href="/wiki/MIT_Center_for_International_Studies" title="MIT Center for International Studies">MIT Center for International Studies</a> from 1982 to 1984, during which time he wrote a public report assessing that the Reagan-proposed "<a href="/wiki/Strategic_Defense_Initiative" title="Strategic Defense Initiative">Star Wars</a>" initiative could not protect the US from a Soviet nuclear attack.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard.edu1_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard.edu1-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-auto1_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-harvard.edu4_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard.edu4-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_career">Academic career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ash_Carter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Academic career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carter taught at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, as an assistant professor from 1984 to 1986, associate professor from 1986 to 1988, professor and associate director of the <a href="/wiki/Belfer_Center_for_Science_and_International_Affairs" title="Belfer Center for Science and International Affairs">Center for Science and International Affairs</a> at Harvard University's <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_School_of_Government" title="John F. Kennedy School of Government">John F. Kennedy School of Government</a> from 1988 to 1990, and director of the Center from 1990 to 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard.edu1_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard.edu1-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> At the Kennedy School, he became chair of the International and Global Affairs faculty and Ford Foundation Professor of Science and International Affairs. He concurrently was co-director of the Preventive Defense Project of Harvard and <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford</a> Universities.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard.edu1_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard.edu1-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Department_of_Defense_career">Early Department of Defense career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ash_Carter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early Department of Defense career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>From 1993 to 1996, Carter served as <a href="/wiki/United_States_Assistant_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Assistant Secretary of Defense">assistant secretary of defense for international security policy</a> during President Clinton's first term.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard.edu2_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard.edu2-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-defense.gov1_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-defense.gov1-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> He was responsible for strategic affairs, including dealing with the threat of weapons of mass destruction elsewhere in the world, nuclear weapons policy (including overseeing the U.S. nuclear arsenal and missile defenses), the 1994 <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Posture_Review" title="Nuclear Posture Review">Nuclear Posture Review</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Agreed_Framework" title="Agreed Framework">Agreed Framework</a> signed in 1994 which froze North Korea's plutonium-producing <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactor</a> program,<sup id="cite_ref-frontline-2003_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-frontline-2003-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> the 1995 extension of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Non-Proliferation_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty">Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> the negotiation of the 1996 <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_Nuclear-Test-Ban_Treaty" title="Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty">Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty</a>, and the multibillion-dollar <a href="/wiki/Nunn%E2%80%93Lugar_Cooperative_Threat_Reduction" title="Nunn–Lugar Cooperative Threat Reduction">Nunn-Lugar Cooperative Threat Reduction</a> program and <a href="/wiki/Project_Sapphire" title="Project Sapphire">Project Sapphire</a> that removed all <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a> from <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-defense.gov1_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-defense.gov1-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-harvard.edu3_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard.edu3-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-house.gov_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-house.gov-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Carter directed military planning during the <a href="/wiki/Agreed_Framework" title="Agreed Framework">1994 crisis</a> over <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>'s nuclear weapons program.<sup id="cite_ref-frontline-2003_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-frontline-2003-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> He was also responsible for dealing with the establishment of defense and intelligence relationships with former Soviet countries in the wake of the collapse of the Soviet Union and its nuclear arsenal, and was chairman of <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>’s High Level Group.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard.edu3_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard.edu3-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-house.gov_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-house.gov-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>  He was also responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Counter_proliferation" class="mw-redirect" title="Counter proliferation">Counter proliferation</a> Initiative, control of sensitive US exports, and negotiations that led to the deployment of Russian troops as part of the <a href="/wiki/Dayton_Agreement" title="Dayton Agreement">Bosnia Peace Plan</a> <a href="/wiki/Implementation_Force" title="Implementation Force">Implementation Force</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-house.gov_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-house.gov-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From April 2009 to October 2011, Carter was <a href="/wiki/Under_Secretary_of_Defense_for_Acquisition,_Technology,_and_Logistics" class="mw-redirect" title="Under Secretary of Defense for Acquisition, Technology, and Logistics">Under Secretary of Defense for Acquisition, Technology, and Logistics</a>, with responsibility for DOD's procurement reform and innovation agenda and completion of procurements such as the <a href="/wiki/KC-46" class="mw-redirect" title="KC-46">KC-46</a> tanker.<sup id="cite_ref-defense.gov1_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-defense.gov1-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> He also led the development and production of thousands of mine-resistant ambush protected (<a href="/wiki/MRAP" title="MRAP">MRAP</a>) vehicles, and other acquisitions.<sup id="cite_ref-defense.gov1_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-defense.gov1-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> He instituted "Better Buying Power," seeking smarter and leaner purchasing.<sup id="cite_ref-defense.gov1_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-defense.gov1-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> From October 2011 to December 2013, Carter was <a href="/wiki/Deputy_Secretary_of_Defense" class="mw-redirect" title="Deputy Secretary of Defense">Deputy Secretary of Defense</a>, serving as the DOD’s <a href="/wiki/Chief_operating_officer" title="Chief operating officer">chief operating officer</a>, overseeing the department's annual budget and its three million civilian and military personnel, steering strategy and budget through sequester, and directing the reform of DOD's national security export controls.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard.edu2_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard.edu2-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-defense.gov1_23-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-defense.gov1-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> He was confirmed by Senate <a href="/wiki/Voice_vote" title="Voice vote">voice vote</a> for both positions.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In an April 4, 2013, speech, he affirmed that the 'Shift to Asia' initiative of President Obama was a priority that would not be affected by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_budget_sequestration_in_2013" title="United States budget sequestration in 2013">budget sequestration in 2013</a>. Carter noted that <i>The Shift to Asia</i> is principally an economic matter with new security implications. India, Australia, and New Zealand were mentioned as forthcoming security partners.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> His <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">Pentagon</a> arms-control responsibilities included matters involving the <a href="/wiki/START_II" title="START II">START II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-Ballistic_Missile_Treaty" title="Anti-Ballistic Missile Treaty">ABM</a>, CFE, and other arms-control treaties.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Secretary_of_Defense">Secretary of Defense</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ash_Carter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Secretary of Defense">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ash_Carter_official_portrait.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Ash_Carter_official_portrait.jpg/220px-Ash_Carter_official_portrait.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Ash_Carter_official_portrait.jpg/330px-Ash_Carter_official_portrait.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Ash_Carter_official_portrait.jpg/440px-Ash_Carter_official_portrait.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3788" data-file-height="4735" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ash_Carter_official_portrait.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Carter's official portrait</div></div></div>
<p>Carter was nominated by President Obama to be the 25th <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">United States Secretary of Defense</a> on December 5, 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SenateUP_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SenateUP-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In his nomination hearing before the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Armed_Services" title="United States Senate Committee on Armed Services">Senate Armed Services Committee</a>, he said he was "very much inclined" to increase U.S. military aid to Ukraine.<sup id="cite_ref-dionnissenbaum_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dionnissenbaum-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-losangelestimes_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-losangelestimes-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Speaking on the Middle East, he said the U.S. must militarily ensure a “lasting defeat” of <a href="/wiki/Islamic_State_of_Iraq_and_the_Levant" title="Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant">Islamic State</a> (ISIL) forces in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dionnissenbaum_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dionnissenbaum-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-losangelestimes_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-losangelestimes-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> He said he is not in favor of increasing the rate of prisoner releases from <a href="/wiki/Guantanamo_Bay_detention_camp" title="Guantanamo Bay detention camp">Guantanamo Bay</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> He also opined that the threats posed by <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> were as serious as those posed by the ISIL forces.<sup id="cite_ref-dionnissenbaum_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dionnissenbaum-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-losangelestimes_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-losangelestimes-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He was approved unanimously on February 1, 2015, by the Senate Armed Services Committee.  He was confirmed by the Senate on February 12 by a vote of 93–5<sup id="cite_ref-SenateUP_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SenateUP-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> and sworn in by Vice President <a href="/wiki/Joe_Biden" title="Joe Biden">Joe Biden</a> on February 17.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2015, Carter warned <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> to halt its rapid island-building in the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2015, Carter condemned <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> air strikes against ISIL and other rebel groups in Syria. On October 8, 2015, Carter, speaking at a meeting of NATO defence ministers in Brussels, said he believed Russia would soon start paying the price for its <a href="/wiki/Russian_military_intervention_in_the_Syrian_Civil_War" title="Russian military intervention in the Syrian Civil War">military intervention in Syria</a> in the form of reprisal attacks and casualties.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>A controversy arose in December 2015 when it was revealed that Carter had used a personal email account when conducting official business as Secretary of Defense.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2016, at Carter's direction, the Department of Defense opened all military roles to women, overriding a request by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">Marine Corps</a> to continue to exempt women from certain positions.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> In June 2016, Carter announced that <a href="/wiki/Transgender" title="Transgender">transgender</a> individuals would be allowed to join and openly serve in the military.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_roles">Other roles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ash_Carter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other roles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Defense.gov_photo_essay_120726-D-TT977-328.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Defense.gov_photo_essay_120726-D-TT977-328.jpg/220px-Defense.gov_photo_essay_120726-D-TT977-328.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Defense.gov_photo_essay_120726-D-TT977-328.jpg/330px-Defense.gov_photo_essay_120726-D-TT977-328.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Defense.gov_photo_essay_120726-D-TT977-328.jpg/440px-Defense.gov_photo_essay_120726-D-TT977-328.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2100" data-file-height="1398" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Defense.gov_photo_essay_120726-D-TT977-328.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Carter at the demilitarized zone separating North and South Korea</div></div></div>

<p>From 1990 to 1993, Carter was chairman of the Editorial Board of <i><a href="/wiki/International_Security" class="mw-redirect" title="International Security">International Security</a></i>. Previously, he held positions at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Technology_Assessment" title="Office of Technology Assessment">Congressional Office of Technology Assessment</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_University" title="Rockefeller University">Rockefeller University</a>.
</p><p>In 1997, Carter and former CIA Director <a href="/wiki/John_M._Deutch" title="John M. Deutch">John M. Deutch</a> co-chaired the Catastrophic Terrorism Study Group which urged greater attention to terrorism. In 1998 Carter, Deutch and <a href="/wiki/Philip_Zelikow" class="mw-redirect" title="Philip Zelikow">Philip Zelikow</a> (later executive director of the 9/11 Commission) published an article on “catastrophic terrorism” in Foreign Affairs.<sup id="cite_ref-fa-article_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fa-article-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> From 1998 to 2000, he was deputy to <a href="/wiki/William_Perry" title="William Perry">William J. Perry</a> in the North Korea Policy Review and traveled with him to <a href="/wiki/Pyongyang" title="Pyongyang">Pyongyang</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-frontline-2003_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-frontline-2003-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  In 2001–02, he served on the National Academy of Sciences Committee on Science and Technology for Countering Terrorism, and advised on the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Homeland_Security" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Homeland Security">Department of Homeland Security</a>.
</p><p>Carter was also co-director of the Preventive Defense Project,<sup id="cite_ref-OSDbio_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OSDbio-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> which designs and promotes security policies aimed at preventing the emergence of major new threats to the US.
</p><p>Carter had been a longtime member of the <a href="/wiki/Defense_Science_Board" title="Defense Science Board">Defense Science Board</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Defense_Policy_Board_Advisory_Committee" title="Defense Policy Board Advisory Committee">Defense Policy Board</a>, the principal advisory bodies to the Secretary of Defense.  During the Bush administration, he was also a member of Secretary of State <a href="/wiki/Condoleezza_Rice" title="Condoleezza Rice">Condoleezza Rice</a>'s International Security Advisory Board; co-chair of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee's Policy Advisory Group; a consultant to the Defense Science Board; a member of the National Missile Defense White Team, and a member of the National Academy of Sciences Committee on International Security and Arms Control.  He has testified frequently before the armed services, foreign relations, and homeland security committees of both houses of Congress.
</p><p>In addition to his public service, Carter was a senior partner at Global Technology Partners, focused on advising investment firms in technology and defense.  He has been a consultant to <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitretek_Systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitretek Systems">Mitretek Systems</a> on international affairs and technology matters, and speaks frequently to business and policy audiences.
</p><p>He was also a member of the boards of directors of the <a href="/wiki/Mitre_Corporation" title="Mitre Corporation">Mitre Corporation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitretek_Systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitretek Systems">Mitretek Systems</a> and the advisory boards of <a href="/wiki/MIT_Lincoln_Laboratory" title="MIT Lincoln Laboratory">MIT Lincoln Laboratory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Stark_Draper_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Stark Draper Laboratory">Draper Laboratory</a>.  Carter was also a member of the <a href="/wiki/Aspen_Strategy_Group" title="Aspen Strategy Group">Aspen Strategy Group</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Council_on_Foreign_Relations" title="Council on Foreign Relations">Council on Foreign Relations</a>, the <a href="/wiki/American_Physical_Society" title="American Physical Society">American Physical Society</a>, the <a href="/wiki/International_Institute_for_Strategic_Studies" title="International Institute for Strategic Studies">International Institute for Strategic Studies</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/National_Committee_on_U.S.-China_Relations" class="mw-redirect" title="National Committee on U.S.-China Relations">National Committee on U.S.-China Relations</a>. Carter was elected a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a>. He was named as a Fellow in the <a href="/wiki/American_Physical_Society" title="American Physical Society">American Physical Society</a> (Forum on Physics &amp; Society) in 2015.
</p><p>Carter also serves as an Honorary Director on the Board of Directors at the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/">Atlantic Council</a><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Positions">Positions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ash_Carter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Positions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Views_on_Iran">Views on Iran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ash_Carter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Views on Iran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carter's views on <a href="/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93United_States_relations" title="Iran–United States relations">Iran</a> have been perceived as hawkish.<sup id="cite_ref-timesofisraelrebecca_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timesofisraelrebecca-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In 2006, he authored a report for the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Endowment_for_International_Peace" title="Carnegie Endowment for International Peace">Carnegie Endowment for International Peace</a> advocating use or threat of force to prevent Iran from obtaining <a href="/wiki/Iranian_nuclear_program" class="mw-redirect" title="Iranian nuclear program">nuclear weapons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-timesofisraelrebecca_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timesofisraelrebecca-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Carter has supported diplomacy with Iran and written about methods of containing a nuclear-armed Tehran.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Support_for_military_interventions">Support for military interventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ash_Carter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Support for military interventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:160720-D-GO396-470_(28360310301).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/160720-D-GO396-470_%2828360310301%29.jpg/220px-160720-D-GO396-470_%2828360310301%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/160720-D-GO396-470_%2828360310301%29.jpg/330px-160720-D-GO396-470_%2828360310301%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/160720-D-GO396-470_%2828360310301%29.jpg/440px-160720-D-GO396-470_%2828360310301%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4120" data-file-height="2747" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:160720-D-GO396-470_(28360310301).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Carter meeting with <a href="/wiki/Mohammed_bin_Salman" class="mw-redirect" title="Mohammed bin Salman">Mohammed bin Salman</a> and his advisor <a href="/wiki/Ahmad_Asiri_(general)" title="Ahmad Asiri (general)">Ahmad Asiri</a> in 2016</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ashton_Carter_departs.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Ashton_Carter_departs.jpg/220px-Ashton_Carter_departs.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Ashton_Carter_departs.jpg/330px-Ashton_Carter_departs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Ashton_Carter_departs.jpg/440px-Ashton_Carter_departs.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4928" data-file-height="3280" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ashton_Carter_departs.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Carter departing from the Pentagon on his last day in office</div></div></div>
<p>Carter was a supporter of the <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">2003 invasion of Iraq</a>, as well as an advocate of "preventative" invasions of <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> In response to increase in tension in Ukraine, Carter considered proposing deployment of <a href="/wiki/Ground-launched_cruise_missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Ground-launched cruise missile">ground-launched cruise missiles</a> in Europe that could pre-emptively destroy Russian weapons.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>U.S. Representative <a href="/wiki/Ted_Lieu" title="Ted Lieu">Ted Lieu</a> has criticized Obama's administration for its continued support for <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabian-led_intervention_in_Yemen" title="Saudi Arabian-led intervention in Yemen">Saudi Arabian-led military intervention in Yemen</a>. On 2 March 2016, he sent a letter to Carter. Lieu, who served in the U.S. Air Force, wrote in the letter that the "apparent indiscriminate airstrikes on civilian targets in Yemen seem to suggest that either the coalition is grossly negligent in its targeting or is intentionally targeting innocent civilians."<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ash_Carter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carter is married to Stephanie (DeLeeuw) Carter.<sup id="cite_ref-cnnlibrary_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnnlibrary-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> He was previously married to the current and eighth president of <a href="/wiki/Bates_College" title="Bates College">Bates College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clayton_Spencer" title="Clayton Spencer">Clayton Spencer</a>, with whom he has two grown children, Ava and Will.<sup id="cite_ref-cnnlibrary_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnnlibrary-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ash_Carter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carter received the <a href="/wiki/Ten_Outstanding_Young_Americans" title="Ten Outstanding Young Americans">Ten Outstanding Young Americans</a> award from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Junior_Chamber" title="United States Junior Chamber">United States Junior Chamber</a> in 1987.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> For his service to national security, Carter has been awarded the DOD's highest civilian medal, the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Defense_Medal_for_Distinguished_Public_Service" title="Department of Defense Medal for Distinguished Public Service">Department of Defense Medal for Distinguished Public Service</a>, five times.<sup id="cite_ref-defense.gov1_23-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-defense.gov1-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> For critical liaison efforts with the chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff" title="Joint Chiefs of Staff">Joint Chiefs of Staff</a> and the geographic combatant commanders, he was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff_Joint_Distinguished_Civilian_Service_Award" title="Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff Joint Distinguished Civilian Service Award">Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff Joint Distinguished Civilian Service Award</a> in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-defense.gov1_23-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-defense.gov1-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> He also received the <a href="/wiki/Defense_Intelligence_Medal" class="mw-redirect" title="Defense Intelligence Medal">Defense Intelligence Medal</a> for his contributions to intelligence.<sup id="cite_ref-defense.gov1_23-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-defense.gov1-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
