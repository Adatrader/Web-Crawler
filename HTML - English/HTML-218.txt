<p><i><b>Newsweek</b></i> is an American weekly <a href="/wiki/News_magazine" title="News magazine">news magazine</a> founded in 1933. <i>Newsweek</i> was a widely distributed newsweekly through the 20th century, with many notable editors-in-chief throughout the years. <i>Newsweek</i> was acquired by <a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="The Washington Post Company">The Washington Post Company</a> in 1961, under whose ownership it remained until 2010.  Between 2008 and 2012, <i>Newsweek</i> experienced financial difficulties, leading to the cessation of print publication and a transition to all-digital format at the end of 2012. The print edition then relaunched in March 2014 under different ownership.
</p><p>Revenue declines prompted an August 2010 sale by owner <a href="/wiki/Graham_Holdings_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Graham Holdings Company">The Washington Post Company</a> to audio pioneer <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Harman" title="Sidney Harman">Sidney Harman</a>—for a purchase price of one dollar and an assumption of the magazine's liabilities.<sup id="cite_ref-nytsale_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytsale-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Later that year, <i>Newsweek</i> merged with the news and opinion website <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Beast" title="The Daily Beast">The Daily Beast</a></i>, forming <a href="/wiki/The_Newsweek_Daily_Beast_Company" title="The Newsweek Daily Beast Company">The Newsweek Daily Beast Company</a>. <i>Newsweek</i> was jointly owned by the estate of Harman and the diversified American media and Internet company <a href="/wiki/IAC_(company)" title="IAC (company)">IAC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-merger_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-merger-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-merger2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-merger2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 2013, IBT Media announced it had acquired <i>Newsweek</i> from IAC; the acquisition included the <i>Newsweek</i> brand and its online publication, but did not include <i>The Daily Beast</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ibtpr_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibtpr-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> IBT Media rebranded itself as <b>Newsweek Media Group</b> in 2017, but returned to IBT Media in 2018 after making <i>Newsweek</i> independent.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span id="Founding_and_early_years_.281933.E2.80.931961.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_and_early_years_(1933–1961)">Founding and early years (1933–1961)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newsweek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding and early years (1933–1961)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Newsweek_Jan_16_1939_Felix_Frankfurter.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Newsweek_Jan_16_1939_Felix_Frankfurter.jpg/220px-Newsweek_Jan_16_1939_Felix_Frankfurter.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="294" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Newsweek_Jan_16_1939_Felix_Frankfurter.jpg/330px-Newsweek_Jan_16_1939_Felix_Frankfurter.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/75/Newsweek_Jan_16_1939_Felix_Frankfurter.jpg 2x" data-file-width="420" data-file-height="561" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Newsweek_Jan_16_1939_Felix_Frankfurter.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>January 16, 1939, cover featuring <a href="/wiki/Felix_Frankfurter" title="Felix Frankfurter">Felix Frankfurter</a></div></div></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Newsweek_WWII_Armed_Forces_Overseas_Edition_1944.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Newsweek_WWII_Armed_Forces_Overseas_Edition_1944.jpg/220px-Newsweek_WWII_Armed_Forces_Overseas_Edition_1944.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="297" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Newsweek_WWII_Armed_Forces_Overseas_Edition_1944.jpg/330px-Newsweek_WWII_Armed_Forces_Overseas_Edition_1944.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Newsweek_WWII_Armed_Forces_Overseas_Edition_1944.jpg/440px-Newsweek_WWII_Armed_Forces_Overseas_Edition_1944.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1824" data-file-height="2463" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Newsweek_WWII_Armed_Forces_Overseas_Edition_1944.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>May 8, 1944 WWII "Armed Forces Overseas Edition"</div></div></div>
<p><i>News-Week</i> was launched in 1933 by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_J._C._Martyn" title="Thomas J. C. Martyn">Thomas J. C. Martyn</a>, a former foreign-news editor for <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i>. He obtained financial backing from a group of U.S. stockholders "which included <a href="/wiki/Ward_Cheney" title="Ward Cheney">Ward Cheney</a>, of the Cheney silk family, <a href="/wiki/John_Hay_Whitney" title="John Hay Whitney">John Hay Whitney</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Mellon" title="Paul Mellon">Paul Mellon</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Andrew_W._Mellon" class="mw-redirect" title="Andrew W. Mellon">Andrew W. Mellon</a>". Paul Mellon's ownership in <i>Newsweek</i> apparently represented "the first attempt of the Mellon family to function journalistically on a national scale".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The group of original owners invested around $2.5 million. Other large stockholders prior to 1946 were public utilities investment banker Stanley Childs and Wall Street corporate lawyer Wilton Lloyd-Smith.
</p><p>Journalist <a href="/wiki/Samuel_T._Williamson" title="Samuel T. Williamson">Samuel T. Williamson</a> served as the first editor-in-chief of <i>Newsweek</i>. The first issue of the magazine was dated February 17, 1933. Seven <a href="/wiki/Photograph" title="Photograph">photographs</a> from the week's news were printed on the first issue's cover.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1937 <i>News-Week</i> merged with the weekly journal <i>Today</i>, which had been founded in 1932 by future New York Governor and diplomat <a href="/wiki/W._Averell_Harriman" title="W. Averell Harriman">W. Averell Harriman</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Astor" title="Vincent Astor">Vincent Astor</a> of the prominent Astor family. As a result of the deal, Harriman and Astor provided $600,000 in venture capital funds and Vincent Astor became both the chairman of the board and its principal stockholder between 1937 and his death in 1959.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In 1937 <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Muir" title="Malcolm Muir">Malcolm Muir</a> took over as president and editor-in-chief. He changed the name to <i>Newsweek</i>, emphasized interpretive stories, introduced signed columns, and launched international editions. Over time the magazine developed a broad spectrum of material, from breaking stories and analysis to reviews and commentary.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><span id="Under_&#39;&#39;Post&#39;&#39;_ownership"></span>
</p>
<h3><span id="Under_Post_ownership_.281961.E2.80.932010.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Under_Post_ownership_(1961–2010)">Under <i>Post</i> ownership (1961–2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newsweek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Under Post ownership (1961–2010)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The magazine was purchased by <a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="The Washington Post Company">The Washington Post Company</a> in 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Osborn_Elliott" title="Osborn Elliott">Osborn Elliott</a> was named editor of <i>Newsweek</i> in 1961 and became the editor in chief in 1969.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:March_1,_1976_Newsweek_story_on_Bill_and_Emily_Harris.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/March_1%2C_1976_Newsweek_story_on_Bill_and_Emily_Harris.jpg/220px-March_1%2C_1976_Newsweek_story_on_Bill_and_Emily_Harris.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="280" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/March_1%2C_1976_Newsweek_story_on_Bill_and_Emily_Harris.jpg/330px-March_1%2C_1976_Newsweek_story_on_Bill_and_Emily_Harris.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/March_1%2C_1976_Newsweek_story_on_Bill_and_Emily_Harris.jpg/440px-March_1%2C_1976_Newsweek_story_on_Bill_and_Emily_Harris.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1356" data-file-height="1725" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:March_1,_1976_Newsweek_story_on_Bill_and_Emily_Harris.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>March 1, 1976, story about SLA members Bill and Emily Harris</div></div></div>
<p>In 1970, <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Holmes_Norton" title="Eleanor Holmes Norton">Eleanor Holmes Norton</a> represented sixty female employees of <i>Newsweek</i> who had filed a claim with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission that <i>Newsweek</i> had a policy of only allowing men to be reporters.<sup id="cite_ref-Newsweek_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newsweek-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The women won, and <i>Newsweek</i> agreed to allow women to be reporters.<sup id="cite_ref-Newsweek_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newsweek-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The day the claim was filed, <i>Newsweek'</i>s cover article was "Women in Revolt", covering the feminist movement; the article was written by a woman who had been hired on a freelance basis since there were no female reporters at the magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Edward_Kosner" title="Edward Kosner">Edward Kosner</a> became editor from 1975 to 1979 after directing the magazine's extensive coverage of the <a href="/wiki/Watergate_scandal" title="Watergate scandal">Watergate scandal</a> that led to the resignation of President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> in 1974.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Richard_Mills_Smith" title="Richard Mills Smith">Richard M. Smith</a> became chairman in 1998, the year that the magazine inaugurated its "Best High Schools in America" list,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> a ranking of public <a href="/wiki/Secondary_education" title="Secondary education">secondary schools</a> based on the <a href="/wiki/Challenge_Index" title="Challenge Index">Challenge Index</a>, which measures the ratio of <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Placement_Program" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced Placement Program">Advanced Placement</a> or <a href="/wiki/IB_Diploma_Programme" title="IB Diploma Programme">International Baccalaureate</a> exams taken by students to the number of graduating students that year, regardless of the scores earned by students or the difficulty in graduating. Schools with average <a href="/wiki/SAT" title="SAT">SAT</a> scores above 1300 or average <a href="/wiki/ACT_(examination)" class="mw-redirect" title="ACT (examination)">ACT</a> scores above 27 are excluded from the list; these are categorized instead as "Public Elite" High Schools. In 2008, there were 17 Public Elites.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Smith resigned as board chairman in December 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><span id="Restructuring_and_new_owner"></span>
</p>
<h4><span id="Restructuring_and_new_owner_.282008.E2.80.932010.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Restructuring_and_new_owner_(2008–2010)">Restructuring and new owner (2008–2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newsweek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Restructuring and new owner (2008–2010)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Newsweek_05_24_09.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/96/Newsweek_05_24_09.jpg/220px-Newsweek_05_24_09.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="321" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/9/96/Newsweek_05_24_09.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="240" data-file-height="350" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Newsweek_05_24_09.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The first issue released after the magazine switched to an opinion and commentary format.</div></div></div>
<p>During 2008–2009, <i>Newsweek</i> undertook a dramatic business restructuring.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Citing difficulties in competing with online news sources to provide unique news in a weekly publication, the magazine refocused its content on opinion and commentary beginning with its May 24, 2009, issue. It shrank its subscriber rate base, from 3.1 million to 2.6 million in early 2008, to 1.9 million in July 2009 and then to 1.5 million in January 2010—a decline of 50% in one year. Meacham described his strategy as "counterintuitive" as it involved discouraging renewals and nearly doubling subscription prices as it sought a more affluent subscriber base for its advertisers.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> During this period, the magazine also laid off staff. While advertising revenues were down almost 50% compared to the prior year, expenses were also diminished, whereby the publishers hoped <i>Newsweek</i> would return to profitability.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The financial results for 2009 as reported by The Washington Post Company showed that advertising revenue for <i>Newsweek</i> was down 37% in 2009 and the magazine division reported an operating loss for 2009 of $29.3 million compared to a loss of $16 million in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc020810_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc020810-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> During the first quarter of 2010, the magazine lost nearly $11 million.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By May 2010, <i>Newsweek</i> had been losing money for the past two years and was put up for sale.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The sale attracted international bidders. One bidder was Syrian entrepreneur <a href="/wiki/Abdulsalam_Haykal" title="Abdulsalam Haykal">Abdulsalam Haykal</a>, CEO of Syrian publishing company Haykal Media, who brought together a coalition of Middle Eastern investors with his company. Haykal later claimed his bid was ignored by <i>Newsweek</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;s</span> bankers, <a href="/wiki/Allen_%26_Co" class="mw-redirect" title="Allen &amp; Co">Allen &amp; Co</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The magazine was sold to audio pioneer <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Harman" title="Sidney Harman">Sidney Harman</a> on August 2, 2010, for $1 in exchange for assuming the magazine's financial liabilities.<sup id="cite_ref-nytsale_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytsale-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Harman's bid was accepted over three competitors.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Meacham left the magazine upon completion of the sale. Sidney Harman was the husband of <a href="/wiki/Jane_Harman" title="Jane Harman">Jane Harman</a>, at that time a member of Congress from California.
</p>
<h3><span id="Merger_with_The_Daily_Beast_.282010.E2.80.932013.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Merger_with_The_Daily_Beast_(2010–2013)">Merger with <i>The Daily Beast</i> (2010–2013)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newsweek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Merger with The Daily Beast (2010–2013)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Newsweek_Daily_Beast_Company" title="The Newsweek Daily Beast Company">The Newsweek Daily Beast Company</a></div>
<p>At the end of 2010, <i>Newsweek</i> merged with the online publication <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Beast" title="The Daily Beast">The Daily Beast</a></i>, following extensive negotiations between the respective proprietors. <a href="/wiki/Tina_Brown" title="Tina Brown">Tina Brown</a>, <i>The Daily Beast</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;s</span> editor-in-chief, became editor of both publications. The new entity, <a href="/wiki/The_Newsweek_Daily_Beast_Company" title="The Newsweek Daily Beast Company">The Newsweek Daily Beast Company</a>, was 50% owned by <a href="/wiki/IAC/InterActiveCorp" class="mw-redirect" title="IAC/InterActiveCorp">IAC/InterActiveCorp</a> and 50% by Harman.<sup id="cite_ref-merger_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-merger-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-merger2_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-merger2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wp1112_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wp1112-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><span id="2011_redesign"></span>
</p>
<h4><span id="Redesign_.282011.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Redesign_(2011)">Redesign (2011)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newsweek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Redesign (2011)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i>Newsweek</i> was redesigned in March 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-relaunch_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-relaunch-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The new <i>Newsweek</i> moved the "Perspectives" section to the front of the magazine, where it served essentially as a highlight reel of the past week on <i>The Daily Beast</i>. More room was made available in the front of the magazine for columnists, editors, and special guests. A new "News Gallery" section featured two-page spreads of photographs from the week with a brief article accompanying each one. The "NewsBeast" section featured short articles, a brief interview with a newsmaker, and several graphs and charts for quick reading in the style of <i>The Daily Beast</i>. This is where the <i>Newsweek</i> staple "Conventional Wisdom" was located. Brown retained <i>Newsweek</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;s</span> focus on in-depth, analytical features and original reporting on politics and world affairs, as well as a new focus on longer fashion and pop culture features. A larger culture section named "Omnivore" featured art, music, books, film, theater, food, travel, and television, including a weekly "Books" and "Want" section. The back page was reserved for a "My Favorite Mistake" column written by celebrity guest columnists about a mistake they made that helped shape who they are.<sup id="cite_ref-relaunch_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-relaunch-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Cessation_of_print_format_.282012.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Cessation_of_print_format_(2012)">Cessation of print format (2012)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newsweek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cessation of print format (2012)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Newsweek_final_issue.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/3c/Newsweek_final_issue.jpg/220px-Newsweek_final_issue.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="297" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/3/3c/Newsweek_final_issue.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="271" data-file-height="366" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Newsweek_final_issue.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The cover of <i>Newsweek</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;s</span> final print issue under <a href="/wiki/The_Newsweek_Daily_Beast_Company" title="The Newsweek Daily Beast Company">The Newsweek Daily Beast Company</a> ownership</div></div></div>
<p>On July 25, 2012, the company operating <i>Newsweek</i> indicated the publication was likely to go digital to cover its losses and could undergo other changes by the next year. <a href="/wiki/Barry_Diller" title="Barry Diller">Barry Diller</a>, chairman of the conglomerate IAC/InterActiveCorp, said his firm was looking at options since its partner in the <i>Newsweek</i>/<i>Daily Beast</i> operation had pulled out.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On October 18, 2012, the company announced that the American print edition would be discontinued at the end of 2012 after 80 years of publication, citing the increasing difficulty of maintaining a paper weekly magazine in the face of declining advertising and subscription revenues and increasing costs for print production and distribution.<sup id="cite_ref-pageturn_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pageturn-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The online edition is named "<i>Newsweek Global</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-economist-oct_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-economist-oct-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Spin-off_to_IBT_Media.2C_return_to_print_and_profitability_.282013.E2.80.932018.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Spin-off_to_IBT_Media,_return_to_print_and_profitability_(2013–2018)"><small>Spin-off to IBT Media, return to print and profitability (2013–2018)</small></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newsweek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Spin-off to IBT Media, return to print and profitability (2013–2018)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 2013, IAC chairman and founder Barry Diller stated at the <a href="/wiki/Milken_Institute" title="Milken Institute">Milken Global Conference</a> that he "wished he hadn't bought" <i>Newsweek</i> because his company had lost money on the magazine and called the purchase a "mistake" and a "<a href="/wiki/Snipe_hunt" title="Snipe hunt">fool's errand</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 3, 2013, <a href="/wiki/IBT_Media" title="IBT Media">IBT Media</a> acquired <i>Newsweek</i> from IAC on terms that were not disclosed; the acquisition included the <i>Newsweek</i> brand and its online publication, but did not include <i>The Daily Beast</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ibtpr_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibtpr-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 7, 2014, IBT Media relaunched a print edition of <i>Newsweek</i><sup id="cite_ref-resumeprint_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-resumeprint-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> with a cover story on the alleged creator of <a href="/wiki/Bitcoin" title="Bitcoin">Bitcoin</a>, which was widely criticized for its lack of substantive evidence. The magazine stood by its story.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Return_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Return-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>IBT Media returned the publication to profitability on October 8, 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-profitable_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-profitable-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2017, IBT Media appointed Matt McAllester, then Editor of Newsweek International, as Global Editor-in-chief of Newsweek.<sup id="cite_ref-Matt_McAllester_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Matt_McAllester-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>IBT Media became known as Newsweek Media Group.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, <i>Newsweek</i> journalists began reporting on their own management,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> after a raid by the Manhattan D.A. and the removal of servers from company offices. Columbia Journalism Review noted the probe "focused on loans the company took out to purchase the computer equipment",<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> and several reporters were fired after reporting on the issue.
</p>
<h3><span id="Spin-off_and_regaining_independence_.282018.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Spin-off_and_regaining_independence_(2018–present)"><small>Spin-off and regaining independence (2018–present)</small></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newsweek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Spin-off and regaining independence (2018–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In October 2018, after completing the strategic structural changes initially announced in March of the same year, IBT Media spun off <i>Newsweek</i>, making it an independent publication for the first time in almost 60 years.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Circulation_and_branches">Circulation and branches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newsweek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Circulation and branches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2003, worldwide circulation was more than 4 million, including 2.7 million in the U.S; by 2010 it reduced to 1.5 million (with newsstand sales declining to just over 40,000 copies per week). <i>Newsweek</i> publishes editions in Japanese, Korean, Polish, Romanian, Spanish, <a href="/wiki/Rioplatense_Spanish" title="Rioplatense Spanish">Rioplatense Spanish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>, Serbian, as well as an <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> <i>Newsweek International</i>. <i>Russian Newsweek</i>, published since 2004, was shut in October 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bulletin_(Australian_periodical)" title="The Bulletin (Australian periodical)">The Bulletin</a></i> (an Australian weekly until 2008) incorporated an international news section from <i>Newsweek</i>.
</p><p>Based in New York City, the magazine claimed 22 bureaus in 2011: nine in the U.S.: New York City, Los Angeles, Chicago/Detroit, Dallas, Miami, Washington, D.C., Boston and San Francisco, and others overseas in London, Paris, Berlin, Moscow, <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a>, Tokyo, Hong Kong, Beijing, <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>, Mexico City and <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>According to a 2015 column in the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Post" title="New York Post">New York Post</a></i>, <i>Newsweek</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;s</span> circulation had fallen to "just over 100,000" with staff at that time numbering "about 60 editorial staffers", up from a low of "less than 30 editorial staffers" in 2013, but with announced plans then to grow the number to "close to 100 in the next year".<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newsweek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Allegations_of_sexism">Allegations of sexism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newsweek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Allegations of sexism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1970, <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Holmes_Norton" title="Eleanor Holmes Norton">Eleanor Holmes Norton</a> represented sixty female employees of <i>Newsweek</i> who had filed a claim with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission that <i>Newsweek</i> had a policy of only allowing men to be reporters.<sup id="cite_ref-Newsweek_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newsweek-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The women won, and <i>Newsweek</i> agreed to allow women to be reporters.<sup id="cite_ref-Newsweek_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newsweek-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The day the claim was filed, <i>Newsweek'</i>s cover article was "Women in Revolt", covering the feminist movement; the article was written by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Helen_Dudar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Helen Dudar (page does not exist)">Helen Dudar</a>, a freelancer, on the belief that there were no female writers at the magazine capable of handling the assignment. Those passed over included <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Peer" title="Elizabeth Peer">Elizabeth Peer</a>, who had spent five years in Paris as a foreign correspondent.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1986_Cover_of_Newsweek.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e2/1986_Cover_of_Newsweek.jpg/150px-1986_Cover_of_Newsweek.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="201" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e2/1986_Cover_of_Newsweek.jpg/225px-1986_Cover_of_Newsweek.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/e2/1986_Cover_of_Newsweek.jpg 2x" data-file-width="273" data-file-height="365" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1986_Cover_of_Newsweek.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 1986 cover of <i>Newsweek</i> that discussed unmarried women in America.</div></div></div>
<p>The 1986 cover of <i>Newsweek</i> featured an article that said "women who weren't married by 40 had a better chance of being killed by a terrorist than of finding a husband".<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> <i>Newsweek</i> eventually apologized for the story and in 2010 launched a study that discovered 2 in 3 women who were 40 and single in 1986 had married since.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NWA_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NWA-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>  The story caused a "wave of anxiety" and some "skepticism" amongst professional and highly educated women in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_44-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NWA_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NWA-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The article was cited several times in the 1993 <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Sleepless_in_Seattle" title="Sleepless in Seattle">Sleepless in Seattle</a></i> starring <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hanks" title="Tom Hanks">Tom Hanks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meg_Ryan" title="Meg Ryan">Meg Ryan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_44-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Comparisons have been made with this article and the current rising issues surrounding the social stigma of unwed women in Asia called <i><a href="/wiki/Sheng_nu" title="Sheng nu">sheng nu</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_44-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:20091123_Newsweek_Palin_Cover.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/eb/20091123_Newsweek_Palin_Cover.png/150px-20091123_Newsweek_Palin_Cover.png" decoding="async" width="150" height="199" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/eb/20091123_Newsweek_Palin_Cover.png/225px-20091123_Newsweek_Palin_Cover.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/eb/20091123_Newsweek_Palin_Cover.png 2x" data-file-width="274" data-file-height="363" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:20091123_Newsweek_Palin_Cover.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Controversial <i>Newsweek</i> cover, November 23, 2009, issue</div></div></div>
<p>Former Alaska Governor and 2008 Republican Vice Presidential nominee <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Palin" title="Sarah Palin">Sarah Palin</a> was featured on the cover of the November 23, 2009, issue of <i>Newsweek</i>, with the caption "How do you Solve a Problem Like Sarah?" featuring an image of Palin in athletic attire and posing. Palin herself, the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> and other commentators accused <i>Newsweek</i> of <a href="/wiki/Sexism" title="Sexism">sexism</a> for their choice of cover in the November 23, 2009 issue discussing Palin's book, <i><a href="/wiki/Going_Rogue:_An_American_Life" class="mw-redirect" title="Going Rogue: An American Life">Going Rogue: An American Life</a></i>. "It's sexist as hell," wrote Lisa Richardson for the <i>Los Angeles Times</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Taylor_Marsh" title="Taylor Marsh">Taylor Marsh</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Huffington_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="The Huffington Post">The Huffington Post</a></i> called it "the worst case of pictorial sexism aimed at political character assassination ever done by a traditional media outlet".<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> David Brody of <a href="/wiki/CBN_News" class="mw-redirect" title="CBN News">CBN News</a> stated: "This cover should be insulting to women politicians."<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The cover includes a photo of Palin used in the August 2009 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/Runner%27s_World" title="Runner&#39;s World">Runner's World</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The photographer may have breached his contract with <i>Runner's World</i> when he permitted its use in <i>Newsweek</i>, as <i>Runner's World</i> maintained certain rights to the photo until August 2010. It is uncertain, however, whether this particular use of the photo was prohibited.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Minnesota Republican Congresswoman and presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/Michele_Bachmann" title="Michele Bachmann">Michele Bachmann</a> was featured on the cover of <i>Newsweek</i> magazine in August 2011, dubbed "the Queen of Rage".<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The photo of her was perceived as unflattering, as it portrayed her with a wide eyed expression some said made her look "crazy".<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Conservative commentator <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Malkin" title="Michelle Malkin">Michelle Malkin</a> called the depiction "sexist",<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> and Sarah Palin denounced the publication. <i>Newsweek</i> defended the cover's depiction of her, saying its other photos of Bachmann showed similar intensity.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Factual_errors">Factual errors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newsweek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Factual errors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unlike most large American magazines, <i>Newsweek</i> has not used fact-checkers since 1996. In 1997, the magazine was forced to recall several hundred thousand copies of a special issue called <i>Your Child</i>, which advised that infants as young as five months old could safely feed themselves <a href="/wiki/Zwieback" title="Zwieback">zwieback</a> toasts and chunks of raw carrot (to the contrary, both represent a choking hazard in children this young). The error was later attributed to a copy editor who was working on two stories at the same time.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2017, <i>Newsweek</i> published a story claiming that the First Lady of Poland refused to shake U.S. President Donald Trump's hand; <i>Snopes</i> described the assertion as "false".<sup id="cite_ref-:2_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> <i>Newsweek</i> corrected its story.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, <i>Newsweek</i> ran a story asserting that President Trump had wrongly colored the American flag while visiting a classroom; <i>Snopes</i> was unable to corroborate the photographic evidence.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In August 2018, <i>Newsweek</i> falsely reported that the Sweden Democrats, a far-right party, could win a majority in the 2018 Swedish parliamentary elections. Polls showed that the party was far away from winning a majority. By September 2018, <i>Newsweek's</i> inaccurate article was still up.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, former <i>Newsweek</i> journalist <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Alter" title="Jonathan Alter">Jonathan Alter</a> wrote in <i>The Atlantic</i> that since being sold to the <i>International Business Times</i> in 2013 that the magazine had "produced some strong journalism and plenty of clickbait before becoming a painful embarrassment to anyone who toiled there in its golden age".<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Former <i>Newsweek</i> writer Matthew Cooper criticized <i>Newsweek</i> for running multiple inaccurate stories in 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2018_investigation_and_firings">2018 investigation and firings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newsweek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 2018 investigation and firings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_District_Attorney" class="mw-redirect" title="Manhattan District Attorney">Manhattan District Attorney</a>'s office raided Newsweek's headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Manhattan" title="Lower Manhattan">Lower Manhattan</a> on January 18, 2018, and seized 18 computer servers as part of an investigation related to the company's finances.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> IBT, which owns Newsweek, had been under scrutiny for its ties to <a href="/wiki/David_Jang" title="David Jang">David Jang</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:3_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> a South Korean pastor and the leader of a Christian sect called "the Community".<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2018, two top editors and an undetermined number of reporters were fired without explanation.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> Celeste Katz, a reporter who was dismissed, had reported on the company's affairs, including the raid by the Manhattan district attorney's office; executive news editors Kenneth Li, who was also fired, had overseen the coverage. After the firings were announced, senior writer Matthew Cooper quit in protest. In a resignation letter that he posted on Twitter, Cooper denounced Newsweek Media Group both for the firings and for what he called a pattern of "reckless leadership".<sup id="cite_ref-:3_65-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newsweek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fareed_Zakaria" title="Fareed Zakaria">Fareed Zakaria</a>, a <i>Newsweek</i> columnist and editor of <i>Newsweek International</i>, attended a secret meeting on November 29, 2001, with a dozen policy makers, Middle East experts and members of influential policy research organizations that produced a report for President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> and his cabinet outlining a strategy for dealing with Afghanistan and the Middle East in the aftermath of <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11, 2001</a>. The meeting was held at the request of <a href="/wiki/Paul_Wolfowitz" title="Paul Wolfowitz">Paul D. Wolfowitz</a>, then the <a href="/wiki/Deputy_Secretary_of_Defense" class="mw-redirect" title="Deputy Secretary of Defense">Deputy Secretary of Defense</a>. The unusual presence of journalists, who also included <a href="/wiki/Robert_D._Kaplan" title="Robert D. Kaplan">Robert D. Kaplan</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Atlantic_Monthly" class="mw-redirect" title="The Atlantic Monthly">The Atlantic Monthly</a></i>, at such a strategy meeting was revealed in <a href="/wiki/Bob_Woodward" title="Bob Woodward">Bob Woodward</a>'s 2006 book <i><a href="/wiki/State_of_Denial:_Bush_at_War,_Part_III" class="mw-redirect" title="State of Denial: Bush at War, Part III">State of Denial: Bush at War, Part III</a></i>. Woodward reported in his book that, according to Kaplan, everyone at the meeting signed confidentiality agreements not to discuss what happened. Zakaria told <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> that he attended the meeting for several hours but did not recall being told that a report for the President would be produced.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Bosman_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Bosman-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> On October 21, 2006, after verification, the <i>Times</i> published a correction that stated:
</p>
<blockquote><p>An article in Business Day on Oct. 9 about journalists who attended a secret meeting in November 2001 called by Paul D. Wolfowitz, then the deputy secretary of defense, referred incorrectly to the participation of Fareed Zakaria, the editor of <i>Newsweek International</i> and a <i>Newsweek</i> columnist. Mr. Zakaria was not told that the meeting would produce a report for the Bush administration, nor did his name appear on the report.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Bosman_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Bosman-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The cover story of the January 15, 2015, issue, titled <i>What Silicon Valley Thinks of Women</i> proved controversial, due to both its illustration, described as "the cartoon of a faceless female in spiky red heels, having her dress lifted up by a <a href="/wiki/Cursor_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cursor (computing)">cursor</a> arrow", and its content, described as "a 5,000-word article on the creepy, sexist culture of the tech industry".<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-grove201501_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grove201501-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> Among those offended by the cover were <a href="/wiki/Today_(U.S._TV_program)" class="mw-redirect" title="Today (U.S. TV program)">Today Show</a> co-host <a href="/wiki/Tamron_Hall" title="Tamron Hall">Tamron Hall</a>, who commented "I think it's obscene and just despicable, honestly." <i>Newsweek</i> editor in chief James Impoco explained "We came up with an image that we felt represented what that story said about Silicon Valley ... If people get angry, they should be angry."<sup id="cite_ref-grove201501_70-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grove201501-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> The article's author, <a href="/wiki/Nina_Burleigh" title="Nina Burleigh">Nina Burleigh</a>, asked, "Where were all these offended people when women like <a href="/wiki/Heidi_Roizen" title="Heidi Roizen">Heidi Roizen</a> published accounts of having a venture capitalist stick her hand in his pants under a table while a deal was being discussed?"<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 1998, <i>Newsweek</i> reporter <a href="/wiki/Michael_Isikoff" title="Michael Isikoff">Michael Isikoff</a> was the first reporter to investigate allegations of a sexual relationship between U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monica_Lewinsky" title="Monica Lewinsky">Monica Lewinsky</a>, but the editors <a href="/wiki/Spike_(journalism)" title="Spike (journalism)">spiked</a> the story.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC19980125_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC19980125-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> The story soon surfaced online in the <a href="/wiki/Drudge_Report" title="Drudge Report">Drudge Report</a>.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/2008_U.S._presidential_election" class="mw-redirect" title="2008 U.S. presidential election">2008 U.S. presidential election</a>, the <a href="/wiki/John_McCain" title="John McCain">John McCain</a> campaign wrote a lengthy letter to the editor criticizing a cover story in May 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2019, journalist Tareq Haddad said he resigned from <i>Newsweek</i> when it refused to publish his story about documents published by <a href="/wiki/WikiLeaks" title="WikiLeaks">WikiLeaks</a> concerning the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_for_the_Prohibition_of_Chemical_Weapons" title="Organisation for the Prohibition of Chemical Weapons">Organisation for the Prohibition of Chemical Weapons</a>' report into the 2018 <a href="/wiki/Douma_chemical_attack" title="Douma chemical attack">Douma chemical attack</a>. Haddad said his information was inconvenient to the US government which had retaliated after the chemical attack. A Newsweek spokesperson responded that Haddad "pitched a conspiracy theory rather than an idea for objective reporting. Newsweek editors rejected the pitch."<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
