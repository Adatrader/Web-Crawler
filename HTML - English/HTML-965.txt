<p><b>Early Modern English</b> or <b>Early New English</b> (sometimes abbreviated <b>EModE</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <b>EMnE</b>, or <b>EME</b>) is the stage of the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> from the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Tudor_period" title="Tudor period">Tudor period</a> to the English <a href="/wiki/Interregnum_(England)" title="Interregnum (England)">Interregnum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(England)" class="mw-redirect" title="Restoration (England)">Restoration</a>, or from the transition from <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a>, in the late 15th century, to the transition to <a href="/wiki/Modern_English" title="Modern English">Modern English</a>, in the mid-to-late 17th century.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Before and after the accession of <a href="/wiki/James_VI_and_I" title="James VI and I">James I</a> to the English throne in 1603, the emerging English standard began to influence the spoken and written <a href="/wiki/Middle_Scots" title="Middle Scots">Middle Scots</a> of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.
</p><p>The grammatical and orthographical conventions of literary English in the late 16th century and the 17th century are still very influential on modern <a href="/wiki/Standard_English" title="Standard English">Standard English</a>. Most modern readers of English can understand texts written in the late phase of Early Modern English, such as the <a href="/wiki/King_James_Bible" class="mw-redirect" title="King James Bible">King James Bible</a> and the works of <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>, and they have greatly influenced Modern English.
</p><p>Texts from the earlier phase of Early Modern English, such as the late-15th century <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Morte_d%27Arthur" title="Le Morte d&#39;Arthur">Le Morte d'Arthur</a></i> (1485) and the mid-16th century  <i><a href="/wiki/Gorboduc_(play)" title="Gorboduc (play)">Gorboduc</a></i> (1561), may present more difficulties but are still obviously closer to Modern English grammar, lexicon, and phonology than are 14th-century Middle English texts, such as the works of <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English_Renaissance">English Renaissance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: English Renaissance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/English_Renaissance" title="English Renaissance">English Renaissance</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_from_Middle_English">Transition from Middle English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transition from Middle English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Middle English">Late Middle English</a></div>
<p>The change from <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> to Early Modern English was not just a matter of changes of vocabulary or pronunciation; a new era in the history of English was beginning.
</p><p>An era of linguistic change in a language with large variations in dialect was replaced by a new era of a more standardised language, with a richer lexicon and an established (and lasting) literature.
</p>
<ul><li>1476 ‚Äì <a href="/wiki/William_Caxton" title="William Caxton">William Caxton</a> starts printing in <a href="/wiki/Westminster" title="Westminster">Westminster</a>; however, the language that he uses reflects the variety of styles and dialects used by the authors who originally wrote the material.</li></ul>
<h5><span id="Tudor_period_.281485.E2.80.931603.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tudor_period_(1485‚Äì1603)">Tudor period (1485‚Äì1603)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tudor period (1485‚Äì1603)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Tudor_period" title="Tudor period">Tudor period</a> and <a href="/wiki/English_Renaissance" title="English Renaissance">English Renaissance</a></div>
<ul><li>1485 ‚Äì Caxton publishes <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Malory" title="Thomas Malory">Thomas Malory</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Morte_d%27Arthur" title="Le Morte d&#39;Arthur">Le Morte d'Arthur</a></i>, the first print bestseller in English. Malory's language, while archaic in some respects, is clearly Early Modern and is possibly a Yorkshire or Midlands dialect.</li>
<li>1491 or 1492 ‚Äì <a href="/wiki/Richard_Pynson" title="Richard Pynson">Richard Pynson</a> starts printing in London; his style tends to prefer <a href="/wiki/Chancery_Standard#Chancery_Standard" class="mw-redirect" title="Chancery Standard">Chancery Standard</a>, the form of English used by the government.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Henry_VIII">Henry VIII</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Henry VIII">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li><abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1509</span> ‚Äì Pynson becomes the King's official printer.</li>
<li>From 1525 ‚Äì Publication of <a href="/wiki/William_Tyndale" title="William Tyndale">William Tyndale</a>'s Bible translation, which was initially banned.</li>

Some have argued that since attendance at prayer book services was required by law for many years, the repetitive use of its language helped to standardise Modern English even more than the <i><a href="/wiki/King_James_Bible" class="mw-redirect" title="King James Bible">King James Bible</a></i> (1611) did.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Interregnum_and_Restoration">Interregnum and Restoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Interregnum and Restoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Interregnum_(England)" title="Interregnum (England)">Interregnum</a> were times of social and political upheaval and instability.
The dates for <a href="/wiki/Restoration_literature" title="Restoration literature">Restoration literature</a> are a matter of convention and differ markedly from genre to genre. In drama, the "Restoration" may last until 1700, but in poetry, it may last only until 1666, the <i><a href="/wiki/Annus_mirabilis#1666_‚Äì_The_year_of_wonders" title="Annus mirabilis">annus mirabilis</a></i> (year of wonders), and prose, it last until 1688, with the increasing tensions over succession and the corresponding rise in journalism and periodicals, or until 1700, when those periodicals grew more stabilised.
</p>
<ul><li>1651 ‚Äì Publication of <i><a href="/wiki/Leviathan_(Hobbes_book)" title="Leviathan (Hobbes book)">Leviathan</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes" title="Thomas Hobbes">Thomas Hobbes</a>.</li>
<li>1660‚Äì1669 ‚Äì <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Pepys" title="Samuel Pepys">Samuel Pepys</a> writes in his diary, which will become an important eyewitness account of the Restoration Era.</li>
<li>1662 ‚Äì New edition of the <i>Book of Common Prayer</i>, largely based on the 1549 and subsequent editions, which long remains a standard work in English.</li>
<li>1667 ‚Äì Publication of <i><a href="/wiki/Paradise_Lost" title="Paradise Lost">Paradise Lost</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">John Milton</a> and of <i><a href="/wiki/Annus_Mirabilis_(poem)" title="Annus Mirabilis (poem)">Annus Mirabilis</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/John_Dryden" title="John Dryden">John Dryden</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_to_Modern_English">Development to Modern English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Development to Modern English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Modern_English" title="Modern English">Modern English</a></div>
<p>The 17th-century <a href="/wiki/Port_town" class="mw-redirect" title="Port town">port towns</a> and their forms of speech gain influence over the old <a href="/wiki/County_town" title="County town">county towns</a>. From around the 1690s onwards, England experienced a new period of internal peace and relative stability, which encouraged the arts including literature.
</p><p>Modern English can be taken to have emerged fully by the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Georgian_era" title="Georgian era">Georgian era</a> in 1714, but <a href="/wiki/English_orthography" title="English orthography">English orthography</a> remained somewhat fluid until the publication of Johnson's <i><a href="/wiki/A_Dictionary_of_the_English_Language" title="A Dictionary of the English Language">A Dictionary of the English Language</a></i>, in 1755.
</p><p>The towering importance of <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> over the other Elizabethan authors was the result of his <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare%27s_influence" class="mw-redirect" title="William Shakespeare&#39;s influence">reception</a> during the 17th and the 18th centuries, which directly contributes to the development of <a href="/wiki/Standard_English" title="Standard English">Standard English</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Shakespeare%27s_plays" title="Shakespeare&#39;s plays">Shakespeare's plays</a> are therefore still familiar and comprehensible 400 years after they were written,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> but the works of <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Langland" title="William Langland">William Langland</a>, which had been written only 200 years earlier, are considerably more difficult for the average modern reader.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">Orthography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Orthography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shakespeare.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Shakespeare.jpg/200px-Shakespeare.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="256" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Shakespeare.jpg/300px-Shakespeare.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Shakespeare.jpg/400px-Shakespeare.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1943" data-file-height="2489" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shakespeare.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>'s writings are universally associated with Early Modern English</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a> of Early Modern English was fairly similar to that of today, but spelling was unstable. Early Modern English, as well as Modern English, inherited orthographical conventions predating the <a href="/wiki/Great_Vowel_Shift" title="Great Vowel Shift">Great Vowel Shift</a>.
</p><p>Early Modern English spelling was similar to <a href="/wiki/Middle_English_orthography" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle English orthography">that of Middle English</a>. Certain changes were made, however, sometimes for reasons of etymology (as with the <a href="/wiki/Silent_b" class="mw-redirect" title="Silent b">silent &#x27e8;b&#x27e9;</a> that was added to words like <i>debt</i>, <i>doubt</i> and <i>subtle</i>).
</p><p>Early Modern English orthography had a number of features of spelling that have not been retained:
</p>
<ul><li>The letter &#x27e8;S&#x27e9; had two distinct lowercase forms: &#x27e8;s&#x27e9; (short <i>s</i>), as is still used today, and &#x27e8;≈ø&#x27e9; (<a href="/wiki/Long_s" title="Long s">long <i>s</i></a>). The short <i>s</i> was always used at the end of a word and often elsewhere. The long <i>s</i>, if used, could appear anywhere except at the end of a word. The double lowercase <i>S</i> was written variously &#x27e8;≈ø≈ø&#x27e9;, &#x27e8;≈øs&#x27e9; or &#x27e8;√ü&#x27e9; (the last ligature is still used in German <a href="/wiki/%C3%9F" title="√ü">√ü</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> That is similar to the alternation between medial (œÉ) and final <a href="/wiki/Lower_case" class="mw-redirect" title="Lower case">lower case</a> <a href="/wiki/Sigma" title="Sigma">sigma</a> (œÇ) in <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/U" title="U">&#x27e8;u&#x27e9;</a> and <a href="/wiki/V" title="V">&#x27e8;v&#x27e9;</a> were then considered as not two distinct letters but as still different forms of the same letter. Typographically, &#x27e8;v&#x27e9; was frequent at the start of a word and &#x27e8;u&#x27e9; elsewhere:<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> hence <i>vnmoued</i> (for modern <i>unmoved</i>) and <i>loue</i> (for <i>love</i>). The modern convention of using &#x27e8;u&#x27e9; for the vowel sound(s) and &#x27e8;v&#x27e9; for the consonant appears to have been introduced in the 1630s.<sup id="cite_ref-Salmon_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salmon-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Also, &#x27e8;w&#x27e9; was frequently represented by &#x27e8;vv&#x27e9;.</li>
<li>Similarly, <a href="/wiki/I" title="I">&#x27e8;i&#x27e9;</a> and <a href="/wiki/J" title="J">&#x27e8;j&#x27e9;</a> were also still considered not as two distinct letters, but as different forms of the same letter: hence <i>ioy</i> for <i>joy</i> and <i>iust</i> for <i>just</i>. Again, the custom of using &#x27e8;i&#x27e9; as a vowel and &#x27e8;j&#x27e9; as a consonant began in the 1630s.<sup id="cite_ref-Salmon_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salmon-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The letter &#x27e8;√æ&#x27e9; (<a href="/wiki/Thorn_(letter)" title="Thorn (letter)">thorn</a>) was still in use during the Early Modern English period but was increasingly limited to handwritten texts. In Early Modern English printing, &#x27e8;√æ&#x27e9; was represented by the Latin &#x27e8;Y&#x27e9; (see <a href="/wiki/Ye_olde" title="Ye olde">Ye olde</a>), which appeared similar to thorn in blackletter typeface <big>&#x27e8;ùñû&#x27e9;</big>. Thorn had become nearly totally disused by the late Early Modern English period, the last vestiges of the letter being its <a href="/wiki/Typographic_ligature" class="mw-redirect" title="Typographic ligature">ligatures</a>, y<sup>e</sup> (thee), y<sup>t</sup> (that), y<sup>u</sup> (thou), which were still seen occasionally in the 1611 <a href="/wiki/King_James_Bible" class="mw-redirect" title="King James Bible">King James Bible</a> and in Shakespeare's Folios.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>A silent &#x27e8;e&#x27e9; was often appended to words, as in <i>≈øpeake</i> and <i>cowarde</i>. The last <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">consonant</a> was sometimes doubled when the &#x27e8;e&#x27e9; was added: hence <i>manne</i> (for <i>man</i>) and <i>runne</i> (for <i>run</i>).</li>
<li>The sound <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ å/</span> was often written &#x27e8;o&#x27e9; (as in <i>son</i>): hence <i>≈øommer</i>, <i>plombe</i> (for modern <i>summer</i>, <i>plumb</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The final syllable of words like <i>public</i> was variously spelt but came to be standardised as <i>-ick</i>. The modern spellings with <i>-ic</i> did not come into use until the mid-18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>&#x27e8;y&#x27e9; was often used instead of &#x27e8;i&#x27e9;.<sup id="cite_ref-oed_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oed-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The vowels represented by &#x27e8;ee&#x27e9; and &#x27e8;e_e&#x27e9; (for example in <i>meet</i> and <i>mete</i>) changed, and &#x27e8;ea&#x27e9; became an alternative.<sup id="cite_ref-oed_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oed-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Many spellings had still not been standardised, however. For example, <i>he</i> was spelled as both <i>he</i> and <i>hee</i> in the same sentence in Shakespeare's plays and elsewhere.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Phonology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consonants">Consonants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Consonants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most consonant sounds of Early Modern English have survived into present-day English; however, there are still a few notable differences in pronunciation:
</p>
<ul><li>Today's "silent" consonants found in the <a href="/wiki/English_consonant_clusters" class="mw-redirect" title="English consonant clusters">consonant clusters</a> of such words as <i>knot, gnat, sword</i> were still fully pronounced up until the mid-to-late 16th century and thus presumably by Shakespeare, though they were <a href="/wiki/Phonological_history_of_English_consonant_clusters" title="Phonological history of English consonant clusters">fully reduced</a> by the early 17th century.<sup id="cite_ref-OP_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OP-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Gh_(digraph)" title="Gh (digraph)">digraph &lt;ght&gt;</a>, in words like <i>night</i>, <i>thought</i>, and <i>daughter</i>, originally pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[xt]</span> in much older English, was probably reduced to simply <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[t]</span> (as it is today) or at least heavily reduced in sound to something like <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ht]</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[√ßt]</span>, or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ft]</span>. It seems likely that much variation existed for many of these words.</li>
<li>The now-silent <i>l</i> of <i>would</i> and <i>should</i> may have persisted in being pronounced as late as 1700 in Britain and perhaps several decades longer in the <a href="/wiki/British_American_colonies" class="mw-redirect" title="British American colonies">British American colonies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The <i>l</i> in <i>could</i>, however, first appearing in the early 16th century, was presumably never pronounced.</li>
<li>The modern phoneme <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ í/</span> was not documented as occurring until the second half of the 17th century. Likely, that phoneme in a word like <i>vision</i> was pronounced as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/zi/</span> and in <i>measure</i> as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/z/</span>.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhotic_vowels">Rhotic vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Rhotic vowels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>r</i> sound (the phoneme <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span></span>/</a></span></span>) was probably always pronounced with following vowel sounds (more in the style of today's <a href="/wiki/General_American" class="mw-redirect" title="General American">General American</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Country_Accent" class="mw-redirect" title="West Country Accent">West Country English</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-English" title="Hiberno-English">Irish</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Scottish_English" title="Scottish English">Scottish</a> accents, and less like today's <a href="/wiki/Estuary_English" title="Estuary English">typical London</a> or <a href="/wiki/Received_Pronunciation" title="Received Pronunciation">Received Pronunciation</a>). Furthermore, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/…õ/, /…™/ and / å/</span> were not necessarily merged before <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span>, as they are in most modern English dialects. The stressed modern phoneme <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/…úÀêr/: &#39;ur&#39; in &#39;fur&#39;">…úÀêr</span></span>/</a></span></span>, when it is spelled &#x27e8;er&#x27e9;, &#x27e8;ear&#x27e9; and perhaps &#x27e8;or&#x27e9; (as in <i>clerk</i>, <i>earth</i>, or <i>divert</i>), had a vowel sound with an <i>a</i>-like quality, perhaps about <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[…ê…π]</a></span> or <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[√§…π]</a></span>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sonnet_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sonnet-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> With the spelling &#x27e8;or&#x27e9;, the sound may have been backed, more toward <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[…í…π]</a></span> in words like <i>worth</i> and <i>word</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sonnet_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sonnet-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In some pronunciations, words like <i>fair</i> and <i>fear</i>, with the spellings &#x27e8;air&#x27e9; and &#x27e8;ear&#x27e9;, rhymed with each other, and words with the spelling &#x27e8;are&#x27e9;, such as <i>prepare</i> and <i>compare</i>, were sometimes pronounced with a more open vowel sound, like the verbs <i>are</i> and <i>scar</i>. See <a href="/wiki/Great_Vowel_Shift#Later_mergers" title="Great Vowel Shift">Great Vowel Shift ¬ß&#160;Later mergers</a> for more information.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Particular_words">Particular words</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Particular words">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Nature</i> was pronounced approximately as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[Ààn…õÀêt…ô…π]</span><sup id="cite_ref-Hark_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hark-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and may have rhymed with <i>letter</i> or, early on, even <i>latter</i>. <i>One</i> may have merged to the sound of <i>own</i>, with both <i>one</i> and <i>other</i> using the era's long <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r920966791">.mw-parser-output span.smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}.mw-parser-output span.smallcaps-smaller{font-size:85%}</style><span class="smallcaps smallcaps-smaller">GOAT</span> vowel, rather than today's <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r920966791"/><span class="smallcaps smallcaps-smaller">STRUT</span> vowels.<sup id="cite_ref-Hark_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hark-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <i>Tongue</i> merged to the sound of <i>tong</i> and rhymed with <i>song</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sonnet_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sonnet-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Grammar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pronouns">Pronouns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Pronouns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kjv-hebrews.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1d/Kjv-hebrews.png/250px-Kjv-hebrews.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="384" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1d/Kjv-hebrews.png/375px-Kjv-hebrews.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1d/Kjv-hebrews.png/500px-Kjv-hebrews.png 2x" data-file-width="1589" data-file-height="2443" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kjv-hebrews.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Epistle_to_the_Hebrews" title="Epistle to the Hebrews">Epistle to the Hebrews</a> in the 1611 <a href="/wiki/King_James_Bible" class="mw-redirect" title="King James Bible">King James Bible</a>. <small><i>God who at sundry times, and in divers manners, spake in times past unto the Fathers by the Prophets, Hath in these last dayes spoken unto us by his Sonne, whom he hath appointed heire of all things, by whom also he made the worlds, who being the brightnesse of his glory, and the expresse image of his person, and upholding all things by the word of his power, when hee had by himselfe purged our sinnes, sate down on <a href="/wiki/English_articles#Ye_form" title="English articles">ye</a> right hand of the Maiestie on high, Being made so much better then the Angels, as hee hath by inheritance obtained a more excellent Name then they.</i></small></div></div></div>
<p>Early Modern English had two second-person personal pronouns: <i><a href="/wiki/Thou" title="Thou">thou</a></i>, the informal singular pronoun, and <i>ye</i>, the plural (both formal and informal) pronoun and the formal singular pronoun.
</p><p>"Thou" and "ye" were both common in the early-16th century (they can be seen, for example, in the disputes over <a href="/wiki/Tyndale" class="mw-redirect" title="Tyndale">Tyndale</a>'s translation of the Bible in the 1520s and the 1530s) but by 1650,"thou" seems old-fashioned or literary.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It has effectively completely disappeared from Modern <a href="/wiki/Standard_English" title="Standard English">Standard English</a> but is still in use in some situations. In many churches in the United Kingdom and the United States, primarily those that use the King James Bible, "thou" is still used to address God in prayer and is felt to denote reverence. "Thou" also remains in regular use in particular regional <a href="/wiki/English_dialects" class="mw-redirect" title="English dialects">English dialects</a>, but its pronunciation is often reduced to "tha".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The translators of the <a href="/wiki/King_James_Bible" class="mw-redirect" title="King James Bible">King James Bible</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> (begun 1604 and published 1611, while Shakespeare was at the height of his popularity) had a particular reason for keeping the "thou/thee/thy/thine" forms that were slowly beginning to fall out of spoken use, as it enabled them to match the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew" class="mw-redirect" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> distinction between second person singular ("thou") and plural ("ye"). It was not to denote reverence (in the King James Bible, God addresses individual people and even Satan as "thou") but only to denote the singular. Over the centuries, however, the very fact that "thou" was dropping out of normal use gave it a special aura and so it gradually came to be used to express reverence in hymns and in prayers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Like other personal pronouns, <i>thou</i> and <i>ye</i> have different forms dependent on their <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">grammatical case</a>; specifically, the objective form of <i>thou</i> is <i>thee</i>, its possessive forms are <i>thy</i> and <i>thine</i>, and its reflexive or emphatic form is <i>thyself</i>.
</p><p>The objective form of <i>ye</i> was <i>you</i>, its possessive forms are <i>your</i> and <i>yours</i> and its reflexive or emphatic forms are <i>yourself</i> and <i>yourselves</i>.
</p><p>The older forms "mine" and "thine" had become "my" and "thy" before words beginning with a consonant other than <i>h</i>, and "mine" and "thine" were retained before words beginning with a vowel or an <i>h</i>, as in <i>mine eyes</i> or <i>thine hand</i>.
</p>

</li>
<li id="cite_note-its-23"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-its_23-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-its_23-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">From the early <a class="mw-selflink selflink">Early Modern English</a> period up until the 17th century, <i>his</i> was the possessive of the third-person neuter <i>it</i> as well as of the third-person masculine <i>he</i>. Genitive "it" appears once in the 1611 <a href="/wiki/King_James_Bible" class="mw-redirect" title="King James Bible">King James Bible</a> (Leviticus 25:5) as <i>groweth of it owne accord</i>.</span>
</li>
</ol></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Verbs">Verbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Verbs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tense_and_number">Tense and number</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Tense and number">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the Early Modern period, the verb inflections became simplified as they evolved towards their modern forms:
</p>
<ul><li>The third-person singular present lost its alternate inflections: <i>-(e)th</i> became obsolete, and <i>-s</i> survived. (Both forms can be seen together in Shakespeare: "With her, that <i>hateth</i> thee and <i>hates</i> us all".)<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The plural present form became uninflected. Present plurals had been marked with <i>-en</i> and singulars with <i>-th</i> or <i>-s</i> (<i>-th</i> and <i>-s</i> survived the longest, especially with the singular use of <i>is</i>, <i>hath</i> and <i>doth</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Marked present plurals were rare throughout the Early Modern period and <i>-en</i> was probably used only as a stylistic affectation to indicate rural or old-fashioned speech.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The second-person singular indicative was marked in both the present and past tenses with <i>-st</i> or <i>-est</i> (for example, in the past tense, <i>walkedst</i> or <i>gav'st</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Since the indicative past was not and still is not otherwise marked for person or number,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> the loss of <i><a href="/wiki/Thou" title="Thou">thou</a></i> made the past subjunctive indistinguishable from the indicative past for all verbs except <i>to be</i>.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Modal_auxiliaries">Modal auxiliaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Modal auxiliaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/English_modal_auxiliary_verb" class="mw-redirect" title="English modal auxiliary verb">modal auxiliaries</a> cemented their distinctive syntactical characteristics during the Early Modern period. Thus, the use of modals without an infinitive became rare (as in "I must to Coventry"; "I'll none of that"). The use of modals' present participles to indicate aspect (as in "Maeyinge suffer no more the loue &amp; deathe of Aurelio" from 1556), and of their preterite forms to indicate tense (as in "he follow'd Horace so very close, that of necessity he must fall with him") also became uncommon.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some verbs ceased to function as modals during the Early Modern period. The present form of <i>must</i>, <i>mot</i>, became obsolete. <i>Dare</i> also lost the syntactical characteristics of a modal auxiliary and evolved a new past form (<i>dared</i>), distinct from the modal <i>durst</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Perfect_and_progressive_forms">Perfect and progressive forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Perfect and progressive forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Perfect_(grammar)" title="Perfect (grammar)">perfect</a> of the verbs had not yet been standardised to use only the auxiliary verb "to have". Some took as their auxiliary verb "to be", such as this example from the King James Bible: "But which of you... will say unto him... when he <b>is</b> come from the field, Go and sit down..." [Luke XVII:7]. The rules for the auxiliaries for different verbs were similar to those that are still observed in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Unaccusative_verb" title="Unaccusative verb">unaccusative verb</a>).
</p><p>The modern syntax used for the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_aspect" class="mw-redirect" title="Progressive aspect">progressive aspect</a> ("I am walking") became dominant by the end of the Early Modern period, but other forms were also common such as the prefix <i>a-</i> ("I am a-walking") and the infinitive paired with "do" ("I do walk"). Moreover, the <i>to be</i> + -<i>ing</i> verb form could be used to express a passive meaning without any additional markers: "The house is building" could mean "The house is being built".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">Vocabulary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Vocabulary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of words that are still in common use in Modern English have undergone <a href="/wiki/Semantic_field" title="Semantic field">semantic narrowing</a>.
</p><p>The use of the verb "<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.etymonline.com/word/suffer#etymonline_v_22311">to suffer</a>" in the sense of "to allow" survived into Early Modern English, as in the phrase "suffer the little children" of the King James Bible, but it has mostly been lost in Modern English.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Also, this period reveals a curious case of one of the earliest Russian borrowings to English (which is historically a rare occasion itself<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>); at least as early as 1600, the word "<a class="external text" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/steppe">steppe</a>" (rus. <a class="external text" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D1%81%D1%82%D0%B5%D0%BF%D1%8C">—Å—Ç–µ–ø—å</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> first appeared in English in <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>'s comedy "<a href="/wiki/A_Midsummer_Night%27s_Dream" title="A Midsummer Night&#39;s Dream">A Midsummer Night's Dream</a>". It is believed that this is a possible indirect borrowing via either German or French.
</p>
