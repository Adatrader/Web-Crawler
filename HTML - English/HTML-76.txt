<p>The <b>Sermon on the Mount</b> (<a href="/wiki/Anglicized" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglicized">anglicized</a> from the Matthean <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a> Latin section title: <i>Sermo in monte</i>) is a collection of sayings and teachings of <a href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus Christ</a>, which emphasizes his <a href="/wiki/Christian_morality" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian morality">moral teaching</a> found in the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Gospel of Matthew</a> (chapters <a href="/wiki/Matthew_5" title="Matthew 5">5</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matthew_6" title="Matthew 6">6</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Matthew_7" title="Matthew 7">7</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-ODCC_self_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODCC_self-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is the first of the <a href="/wiki/Five_Discourses_of_Matthew" title="Five Discourses of Matthew">Five Discourses of Matthew</a> and takes place relatively early in the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Jesus" title="Ministry of Jesus">Ministry of Jesus</a> after he has been <a href="/wiki/Baptism_of_Jesus" title="Baptism of Jesus">baptized</a> by <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a>, finished his <a href="/wiki/Fasting" title="Fasting">fasting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meditation_retreat" class="mw-redirect" title="Meditation retreat">meditation retreat</a> in the desert, and begun to preach in <a href="/wiki/Galilee" title="Galilee">Galilee</a>. The name and location of the mountain is unstated; the <a href="/wiki/Mount_of_Beatitudes" title="Mount of Beatitudes">Mount of Beatitudes</a> is the traditional interpretation.
</p><p>The Sermon is the longest continuous discourse of Jesus found in the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, and has been one of the most widely quoted elements of the <a href="/wiki/Canonical_Gospels" class="mw-redirect" title="Canonical Gospels">Canonical Gospels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-VaughtPref_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VaughtPref-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  It includes some of the best known teachings of Jesus, such as the <a href="/wiki/Beatitudes" title="Beatitudes">Beatitudes</a>, and the widely recited <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s_Prayer" title="Lord&#39;s Prayer">Lord's Prayer</a>. The Sermon on the Mount is generally considered to contain the central tenets of <a href="/wiki/Christian_discipleship" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian discipleship">Christian discipleship</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-VaughtPref_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VaughtPref-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_setting">Background and setting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sermon_on_the_Mount&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and setting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Jesus" title="Ministry of Jesus">Ministry of Jesus</a></div>
<p>The Sermon on the Mount occupies chapters <a href="/wiki/Matthew_5" title="Matthew 5">5</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matthew_6" title="Matthew 6">6</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matthew_7" title="Matthew 7">7</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Gospel of Matthew</a>. The Sermon has been one of the most widely quoted elements of the <a href="/wiki/Canonical_Gospels" class="mw-redirect" title="Canonical Gospels">Canonical Gospels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-VaughtPref_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VaughtPref-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This is the first of the <a href="/wiki/Five_Discourses_of_Matthew" title="Five Discourses of Matthew">Five Discourses of Matthew</a>, the other four being <a href="/wiki/Matthew_10" title="Matthew 10">Matthew 10</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matthew_13" title="Matthew 13">Matthew 13</a> (1–53), <a href="/wiki/Matthew_18" title="Matthew 18">Matthew 18</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Olivet_discourse" class="mw-redirect" title="Olivet discourse">Olivet discourse</a> in Matthew 24.<sup id="cite_ref-Kellum194_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kellum194-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Sermon is set early in the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Jesus" title="Ministry of Jesus">Ministry of Jesus</a>, after he has been <a href="/wiki/Baptism_of_Jesus" title="Baptism of Jesus">baptized</a> by <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a> in <a href="/wiki/Matthew_3" title="Matthew 3">chapter 3</a> of Matthew's Gospel, gathered his first disciples in <a href="/wiki/Matthew_4" title="Matthew 4">chapter 4</a>, and had returned from a long fast and contemplation in the <a href="/wiki/Judaean_Desert" title="Judaean Desert">Judaean Desert</a> where he had been <a href="/wiki/Temptation_of_Jesus" class="mw-redirect" title="Temptation of Jesus">tempted by Satan</a> to renounce his spiritual mission and gain worldly riches.
</p><p>Before this episode, Jesus had been "all about <a href="/wiki/Galilee" title="Galilee">Galilee</a>" preaching, as in <a href="/wiki/Matthew_4:23" title="Matthew 4:23">Matthew 4:23</a>, and "great crowds followed him" from all around the area. The setting for the sermon is given in <a href="/wiki/Matthew_5:1" class="mw-redirect" title="Matthew 5:1">Matthew 5:1</a>-<a href="/wiki/Matthew_5:2" class="mw-redirect" title="Matthew 5:2">2</a>. Jesus sees the multitudes, goes up into the mountain, is followed by his disciples, and begins to preach. The Sermon is brought to its close by <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew%208:1–8:1&amp;version=nkjv">Matthew 8:1</a>, which reports that Jesus "came down from the mountain followed by great multitudes".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Components">Components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sermon_on_the_Mount&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Beatitudes" title="Beatitudes">Beatitudes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salt_and_Light" class="mw-redirect" title="Salt and Light">Salt and Light</a>, <a href="/wiki/Light_of_the_World" title="Light of the World">Light of the World</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lamp_under_a_bushel" title="Lamp under a bushel">Lamp under a bushel</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Mote_and_the_Beam" title="The Mote and the Beam">The Mote and the Beam</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wise_and_Foolish_Builders" class="mw-redirect" title="Wise and Foolish Builders">Wise and Foolish Builders</a></div>
<p>While the issue of the exact theological structure and composition of the Sermon on the Mount is subject to debate among scholars, specific components within it, each associated with particular teachings, can be identified.<sup id="cite_ref-Talbert21_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talbert21-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WCarter_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WCarter-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Meister_des_Lehrbuchs_Kaiser_Maximilians_I._001.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Meister_des_Lehrbuchs_Kaiser_Maximilians_I._001.jpg/170px-Meister_des_Lehrbuchs_Kaiser_Maximilians_I._001.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="232" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Meister_des_Lehrbuchs_Kaiser_Maximilians_I._001.jpg/255px-Meister_des_Lehrbuchs_Kaiser_Maximilians_I._001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Meister_des_Lehrbuchs_Kaiser_Maximilians_I._001.jpg/340px-Meister_des_Lehrbuchs_Kaiser_Maximilians_I._001.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2024" data-file-height="2765" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Meister_des_Lehrbuchs_Kaiser_Maximilians_I._001.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s_Prayer" title="Lord&#39;s Prayer">Lord's Prayer</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Matthew_6:9" title="Matthew 6:9">Matthew 6:9</a>, 1500, Vienna</div></div></div>
<p><span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew+5%3A3-12&amp;version=9">Matthew 5:3–12</a></span> discusses the <a href="/wiki/Beatitudes" title="Beatitudes">Beatitudes</a>. These describe the character of the people of the Kingdom of Heaven, expressed as "blessings".<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The Greek word most versions of the Gospel render as "blessed," can also be translated "happy" (<span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew+5%3A3-12&amp;version=YLT">Matthew 5:3–12</a></span> of <a href="/wiki/Young%27s_Literal_Translation" title="Young&#39;s Literal Translation">Young's Literal Translation</a> for an example).  In Matthew, there are eight (or nine) blessings, while in Luke there are four, followed by four woes.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In almost all cases the phrases used in the Beatitudes are familiar from an <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> context, but in the sermon Jesus gives them new meaning.<sup id="cite_ref-Haste_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haste-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Together, the Beatitudes present a new set of ideals that focus on love and humility rather than force and mastery; they echo the highest ideals of Jesus' teachings on spirituality and compassion.<sup id="cite_ref-Haste_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haste-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Christian teachings, the <a href="/wiki/Works_of_Mercy" class="mw-redirect" title="Works of Mercy">Works of Mercy</a>, which have corporal and spiritual components, have resonated with the theme of the Beatitude for mercy.<sup id="cite_ref-Peace_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peace-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> These teachings emphasize that these acts of mercy provide both temporal and spiritual benefits.<sup id="cite_ref-Synop_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Synop-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew+5%3A13-16&amp;version=9">Matthew 5:13–16</a></span> presents the metaphors of <a href="/wiki/Salt_and_light" title="Salt and light">salt and light</a>. This completes the profile of God's people presented in the beatitudes, and acts as the introduction to the next section.
</p><p>There are two parts in this section, using the terms "salt of the earth" and <a href="/wiki/Light_of_the_World" title="Light of the World">Light of the World</a> to refer to the disciples&#160;&#8211;&#32; implying their value. Elsewhere, in <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/John#8:12" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/John">John 8:12</a>, Jesus applies <i>Light of the World</i> to himself.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Jesus preaches about <a href="/wiki/Hell" title="Hell">hell</a> and what hell is like: "But I say unto you, That whosoever is angry with his brother without a cause shall be in danger of the judgment: and whosoever shall say to his brother "<i><a href="/wiki/Language_of_Jesus#Raca_.28.CE.A1.CE.B1.CE.BA.CE.B1.29" title="Language of Jesus">Raca</a></i> (fool)" shall be in danger of the council: but whosoever shall say, Thou fool, shall be in danger of hell fire." (Matthew 5:22 KJV<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>)
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Papyrus_1_-_recto.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Papyrus_1_-_recto.jpg/170px-Papyrus_1_-_recto.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Papyrus_1_-_recto.jpg/255px-Papyrus_1_-_recto.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Papyrus_1_-_recto.jpg/340px-Papyrus_1_-_recto.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1836" data-file-height="2160" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Papyrus_1_-_recto.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A page from <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Matthew</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Papyrus_1" title="Papyrus 1">Papyrus 1</a>, c. 250 AD</div></div></div>
<p>The longest discourse in the Sermon is <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew+5%3A17-48&amp;version=9">Matthew 5:17–48</a></span>, traditionally referred to as <i>the <a href="/wiki/Matthew_5#Antitheses" title="Matthew 5">Antitheses</a></i> or <i>Matthew's Antitheses</i>. In the discourse, Jesus fulfills and reinterprets the <a href="/wiki/Old_Covenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Covenant">Old Covenant</a> and in particular its <a href="/wiki/Ten_Commandments#Christianity" title="Ten Commandments">Ten Commandments</a>, contrasting with what "you have heard" from others. For example, he advises <a href="/wiki/Turning_the_other_cheek" title="Turning the other cheek">turning the other cheek</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Matthew_5:44" title="Matthew 5:44">to love your  enemies</a>, in contrast to taking <a href="/wiki/An_eye_for_an_eye" class="mw-redirect" title="An eye for an eye">an eye for an eye</a>. According to most interpretations of <a href="/wiki/Matthew_5:17" title="Matthew 5:17">Matthew 5:17</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matthew_5:18" title="Matthew 5:18">18</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matthew_5:19" title="Matthew 5:19">19</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Matthew_5:20" title="Matthew 5:20">20</a>, and most <a href="/wiki/Christian_views_on_the_old_covenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian views on the old covenant">Christian views of the Old Covenant</a>, these new interpretations of the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neviim" class="mw-redirect" title="Neviim">Prophets</a> are not <a href="/wiki/Antinomianism" title="Antinomianism">opposed to the Old Testament</a>, which was the position of <a href="/wiki/Marcion" class="mw-redirect" title="Marcion">Marcion</a>, but form Jesus' new teachings which bring about <a href="/wiki/Salvation_(Christianity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Salvation (Christianity)">salvation</a>, and hence must be adhered to, as emphasized in <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew+7%3A24-27&amp;version=9">Matthew 7:24–27</a></span> towards the end of the sermon.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Matthew_6" title="Matthew 6">Matthew 6</a> Jesus condemns doing what would normally be "<a href="/wiki/Good_works" title="Good works">good works</a>" simply for recognition and not from the heart, such as those of <a href="/wiki/Alms" title="Alms">alms</a> (6:1–4), <a href="/wiki/Prayer" title="Prayer">prayer</a> (6:5–15), and <a href="/wiki/Fasting" title="Fasting">fasting</a> (6:16–18). The discourse goes on to condemn the superficiality of <a href="/wiki/Economic_materialism" title="Economic materialism">materialism</a> and call the disciples not to worry about material needs, but to "seek" <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_God" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of God">God's kingdom</a> first. Within the discourse on ostentation, Matthew presents an example of correct prayer. Luke places this in a different context. The <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s_prayer" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord&#39;s prayer">Lord's prayer</a> (6:9–13) contains parallels to <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://bible.oremus.org/?passage=1+Chronicles+29:10–29:18&amp;version=nrsv">1 Chronicles 29:10–18</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first part of <a href="/wiki/Matthew_7" title="Matthew 7">Matthew 7</a>, i.e. <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew+7%3A1-6&amp;version=9">Matthew 7:1–6</a></span> deals with judging.  Jesus condemns those who judge others before first judging themselves: "Judge not, that ye be not judged."
</p><p>In the last part in <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew+7%3A17-29&amp;version=9">Matthew 7:17–29</a></span> Jesus concludes the sermon by warning against <a href="/wiki/False_prophet" title="False prophet">false prophets</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Teachings_and_theology">Teachings and theology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sermon_on_the_Mount&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Teachings and theology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Saint_Cajetan_Church,_Gustavo_A._Madero,_Federal_District,_Mexico11.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Saint_Cajetan_Church%2C_Gustavo_A._Madero%2C_Federal_District%2C_Mexico11.jpg/170px-Saint_Cajetan_Church%2C_Gustavo_A._Madero%2C_Federal_District%2C_Mexico11.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Saint_Cajetan_Church%2C_Gustavo_A._Madero%2C_Federal_District%2C_Mexico11.jpg/255px-Saint_Cajetan_Church%2C_Gustavo_A._Madero%2C_Federal_District%2C_Mexico11.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Saint_Cajetan_Church%2C_Gustavo_A._Madero%2C_Federal_District%2C_Mexico11.jpg/340px-Saint_Cajetan_Church%2C_Gustavo_A._Madero%2C_Federal_District%2C_Mexico11.jpg 2x" data-file-width="979" data-file-height="1306" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Saint_Cajetan_Church,_Gustavo_A._Madero,_Federal_District,_Mexico11.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Plaque of the 8 <i><a href="/wiki/Beatitudes" title="Beatitudes">Beatitudes</a></i>, St. Cajetan Church, <a href="/wiki/Lindavista" title="Lindavista">Lindavista</a>, Mexico</div></div></div>
<p>The teachings of the Sermon on the Mount have been a key element of <a href="/wiki/Christian_ethics" title="Christian ethics">Christian ethics</a>, and for centuries the sermon has acted as a fundamental recipe for the conduct of the followers of Jesus.<sup id="cite_ref-v_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-v-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Various religious and moral thinkers (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Leo_Tolstoy" title="Leo Tolstoy">Tolstoy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mahatma_Gandhi" title="Mahatma Gandhi">Gandhi</a>) have admired its message, and it has been one of the main sources of <a href="/wiki/Christian_pacifism" title="Christian pacifism">Christian pacifism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODCC_self_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODCC_self-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 5th century, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Augustine" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Augustine">Saint Augustine</a> began his book <i><a href="/wiki/Our_Lord%27s_Sermon_on_the_Mount_(Augustine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Our Lord&#39;s Sermon on the Mount (Augustine)">Our Lord's Sermon on the Mount</a></i> by stating:
</p>
<dl><dd>If any one will piously and soberly consider the sermon which our Lord Jesus Christ spoke on the mount, as we read it in the Gospel according to Matthew, I think that he will find in it, so far as regards the highest morals, a perfect standard of the Christian life.</dd></dl>
<p>The last verse of chapter 5 of Matthew (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Matthew#5:48" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Matthew">5:48</a>) is a focal point of the sermon that summarizes its teachings by advising the disciples to seek perfection."<sup id="cite_ref-Vaught7_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vaught7-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The Greek word <i>telios</i> used to refer to perfection also implies an end, or destination, advising the disciples to seek the path towards perfection and the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_God" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of God">Kingdom of God</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Vaught7_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vaught7-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> It teaches that God's children are those who act like God.<sup id="cite_ref-Talbert78_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talbert78-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The teachings of the sermon are often referred to as the <i>Ethics of the Kingdom</i>: they place a high level of emphasis on "purity of the heart" and embody the basic standard of Christian <a href="/wiki/Righteousness" title="Righteousness">righteousness</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theological_structure">Theological structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sermon_on_the_Mount&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Theological structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The issue of the theological structure and composition of the Sermon on the Mount remains unresolved.<sup id="cite_ref-Talbert21_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talbert21-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WCarter_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WCarter-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DAllison_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAllison-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> One group of theologians ranging from <a href="/wiki/Saint_Augustine" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Augustine">Saint Augustine</a> in the 5th century to <a href="/wiki/Michael_Goulder" title="Michael Goulder">Michael Goulder</a> in the 20th century, see the <a href="/wiki/Beatitudes" title="Beatitudes">Beatitudes</a> as the central element of the Sermon.<sup id="cite_ref-Talbert21_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talbert21-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Others such as <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCnther_Bornkamm" title="Günther Bornkamm">Bornkamm</a> see the Sermon arranged around the <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s_prayer" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord&#39;s prayer">Lord's prayer</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Patte" title="Daniel Patte">Daniel Patte</a>, closely followed by <a href="/wiki/Ulrich_Luz" title="Ulrich Luz">Ulrich Luz</a>, see a <a href="/wiki/Chiastic_structure" title="Chiastic structure">chiastic structure</a> in the sermon.<sup id="cite_ref-Talbert21_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talbert21-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WCarter_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WCarter-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dale_Allison" title="Dale Allison">Dale Allison</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glen_Stassen" title="Glen Stassen">Glen Stassen</a> have proposed a structure based on triads.<sup id="cite_ref-WCarter_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WCarter-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DAllison_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAllison-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kingsbury" class="mw-redirect" title="Jack Kingsbury">Jack Kingsbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hans_Dieter_Betz" title="Hans Dieter Betz">Hans Dieter Betz</a> see the sermon as composed of theological themes, e.g. righteousness or way of life.<sup id="cite_ref-Talbert21_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talbert21-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analysis_and_interpretation">Analysis and interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sermon_on_the_Mount&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Analysis and interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Biblical_literalism" title="Biblical literalism">Biblical literalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biblical_law_in_Christianity" class="mw-redirect" title="Biblical law in Christianity">Biblical law in Christianity</a></div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretations">Interpretations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sermon_on_the_Mount&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Interpretations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The high ethical standards of the Sermon have been interpreted in a wide variety of ways by different Christian groups and <a href="/wiki/Craig_S._Keener" title="Craig S. Keener">Craig S. Keener</a> states that at least 36 different interpretations regarding the message of the Sermon exist, which he divides into 8 categories of views:<sup id="cite_ref-Keener160_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keener160-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>The predominant medieval view, "reserving a higher ethic for <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a>, especially in monastic orders"<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Theology_of_Martin_Luther" title="Theology of Martin Luther">Luther's view</a> that it represents an impossible demand like the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Moses" title="Law of Moses">Law of Moses</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Anabaptist" class="mw-redirect" title="Anabaptist">Anabaptist</a> literal view which directly applies the teachings<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Social_Gospel#A_Theology_for_the_Social_Gospel_(1917)" title="Social Gospel">Social Gospel view</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Christian_existentialism" title="Christian existentialism">Christian existentialism</a> view</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Quest_of_the_Historical_Jesus" title="The Quest of the Historical Jesus">Schweitzer's view of an imminent eschatology</a> referring to an interim ethic</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dispensationalism#Eschatology" title="Dispensationalism">Dispensational eschatology</a> which refers to a future Kingdom of God</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Inaugurated_eschatology" title="Inaugurated eschatology">Inaugurated eschatology</a> in which the Sermon's ethics remain a goal to be approached, yet realized later</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_the_Sermon_on_the_Plain">Comparison with the Sermon on the Plain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sermon_on_the_Mount&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Comparison with the Sermon on the Plain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While Matthew groups Jesus' teachings into sets of similar material, the same material is scattered when found in Luke.<sup id="cite_ref-ODCC_self_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODCC_self-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The Sermon on the Mount may be compared with the similar but more succinct <a href="/wiki/Sermon_on_the_Plain" title="Sermon on the Plain">Sermon on the Plain</a> as recounted by the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">Gospel of Luke</a> (6:17–49), which occurs at the same moment in Luke's narrative, and also features Jesus heading up a mountain, but giving the sermon on the way down at a level spot. Some scholars believe that they are the same sermon, while others hold that Jesus frequently preached similar themes in different places.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_parallels_with_Buddhist_teachings">Modern parallels with Buddhist teachings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sermon_on_the_Mount&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Modern parallels with Buddhist teachings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although modern parallels between the teachings of Jesus and <a href="/wiki/Buddhist" class="mw-redirect" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> philosophy have been drawn (by the <a href="/wiki/14th_Dalai_Lama" title="14th Dalai Lama">14th Dalai Lama</a><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> for example), these comparisons emerged after missionary contacts in the 19th century, and there is no historically reliable evidence of contacts between Buddhism and Jesus during his life.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Modern scholarship has almost unanimously agreed that claims of the travels of Jesus to Tibet, Kashmir or India (see <a href="/wiki/Unknown_years_of_Jesus" title="Unknown years of Jesus">Unknown years of Jesus</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_influences_on_Christianity" title="Buddhist influences on Christianity">influence of Buddhism on his teachings</a> are without historical basis.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
The similarities between the teachings of Buddha and Jesus have been noted.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="According_to_the_Perennial_Philosophy">According to the Perennial Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sermon_on_the_Mount&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: According to the Perennial Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Perennial_philosophy" title="Perennial philosophy">perennialist</a> author <a href="/wiki/Frithjof_Schuon" title="Frithjof Schuon">Frithjof Schuon</a>, the message of the Sermon is a perfect synthesis of the whole Christian tradition. The text has the largest number of perennial and universal doctrines, and spiritual advice of all Scripture. Much of what  Bible readers remember from Scripture derives from the Sermon. Source of spiritual and moral instructions, the Sermon on the Mount is regarded by the Perennial Philosophy "as the quintessence itself of religion".<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Perennialism considers the injunctions of the Sermon on the Mount as belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Esoteric_Christianity" title="Esoteric Christianity">esoteric</a> dimension of Christianity.
</p>
