	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Michael Breen (author)</h1>

<p><b>Michael Breen</b> (born 31 July 1952) is an English <a href="/wiki/Author" title="Author">author</a>, consultant and <a href="/wiki/Journalist" title="Journalist">journalist</a> covering <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>.
</p><p>Breen writes occasional opinion columns for international and South Korean media. Since 2000, he has written a featured column for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Korea_Times" title="The Korea Times">The Korea Times</a></i>, an English-language daily in South Korea, where he comments on South Korean society, culture, and political issues.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Breen_(author)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Breen is a graduate of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a> and first began living in South Korea in 1982.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He was the correspondent in Korea for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Washington_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington Times">Washington Times</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In 1987, he became the first non-Korean president of the Seoul Foreign Correspondents Club. In 1994, he became a <a href="/wiki/Management_consultant" class="mw-redirect" title="Management consultant">management consultant</a> specializing in North Korea, with clients such as <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Coca-Cola</a>. He entered the <a href="/wiki/Public_relations" title="Public relations">public relations</a> field in 1999 as the managing director of Merit/Burson-Marsteller, where he remained until 2004. He is the founder and CEO of Insight Communications Consultants, a Seoul-based public relations firm. Breen was made an <a href="/wiki/Honorary_citizen" class="mw-redirect" title="Honorary citizen">honorary citizen</a> of <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a> in 2001.
</p><p>A former follower and biographer of the controversial Unification Church leader <a href="/wiki/Sun_Myung_Moon" title="Sun Myung Moon">Sun Myung Moon</a>, Breen was described in a 2005 <i><a href="/wiki/American_Prospect" class="mw-redirect" title="American Prospect">American Prospect</a></i> story as having brokered talks in the early 1990s between <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> and the North Korean leadership, laying groundwork for a visit by the staff of Moon's <i><a href="/wiki/Washington_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington Times">Washington Times</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Breen has also authored an <a href="/wiki/Unauthorized_biography" title="Unauthorized biography">unauthorized biography</a> of Moon, <i><a href="/wiki/Sun_Myung_Moon:_The_Early_Years" class="mw-redirect" title="Sun Myung Moon: The Early Years">Sun Myung Moon: The Early Years</a>.</i><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuit">Lawsuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Breen_(author)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lawsuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 25 December 2009, Breen wrote a satirical column in the paper which lampooned various South Korean public figures, including president <a href="/wiki/Lee_Myung-bak" title="Lee Myung-bak">Lee Myung-bak</a>, singer <a href="/wiki/Rain_(entertainer)" title="Rain (entertainer)">Rain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Samsung" title="Samsung">Samsung</a>. Displeased with Breen's allusions to their corruption and arrogance, Samsung filed civil and criminal suits against him and the paper for libel. After an apology and after Breen told prosecutors during interrogation that the column was his own idea, the paper was dropped as a respondent, but the suit against Breen himself remained.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> One South Korean media outlet claimed that the entire column as an insult to the country of South Korea itself.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Samsung dropped the civil suit after an apology by Breen. The criminal case went to trial but was thrown out by the judge on the grounds that there was "no victim."
</p>
