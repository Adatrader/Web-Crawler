<p><i><b>Rabbit at Rest</b></i> is a 1990 novel by <a href="/wiki/John_Updike" title="John Updike">John Updike</a>.  It is the fourth and final novel in a tetralogy, succeeding <i><a href="/wiki/Rabbit,_Run" title="Rabbit, Run">Rabbit, Run</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Rabbit_Redux" title="Rabbit Redux">Rabbit Redux</a></i>;  and <i><a href="/wiki/Rabbit_Is_Rich" title="Rabbit Is Rich">Rabbit Is Rich</a>.</i><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> A related novella, <i><a href="/wiki/Rabbit_Remembered" title="Rabbit Remembered">Rabbit Remembered</a></i>, was published in 2001.  <i>Rabbit at Rest</i> won the <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_for_Fiction" title="Pulitzer Prize for Fiction">Pulitzer Prize for Fiction</a> in 1991, the second "Rabbit" novel to garner that award.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rabbit_at_Rest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This novel is part of the series that follows the life of Harry "Rabbit" Angstrom from 1960 to 1990. <i>Rabbit at Rest</i> focuses on the years 1988–89. Harry, nearly 40 years after his glory days as a high school basketball star in a mid-sized <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> city, has retired with Janice, his wife of 33 years, to sunny Florida during the cold months, where Harry is depressed,  dangerously overweight and desperate for reasons to keep on living.
</p><p>Unable to stop nibbling corn chips, macadamia nuts and other junk food, Rabbit nearly dies after a heart attack while sunfishing with his nine-year-old granddaughter, Judy. In a "redemption" of the drowning death of his infant daughter Rebecca in the earlier novel <i><a href="/wiki/Rabbit,_Run" title="Rabbit, Run">Rabbit, Run</a></i>, he saves Judy from drowning during their sunfishing afternoon.
</p><p>He is distracted from his own existential worries by the acts of his drug-addicted son, Nelson, to whom Janice (the actual owner of the Angstroms' wealth) has given control of the family's thriving business, a Pennsylvania <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">Toyota</a> dealership. The discovery that Nelson has been stealing from the company to support his drug habit causes Harry to lose the family business. Despite his multiplying difficulties, Rabbit manages to take solace in the presence of Judy, who has matured into a beautiful and charming young lady, reminding him of his high-school glory days. He is less attached to his four-year-old grandson Roy, who seems wary and fearful of Rabbit, much like Nelson.
</p><p>While recuperating from heart surgery, Rabbit recognizes a nurse, Annabelle Byer, as his illegitimate daughter by his old girlfriend, Ruth. He spends time with her without identifying himself as her likely father. Then, his long-term mistress, Thelma Harrison (wife of his high-school nemesis Ron), dies of <a href="/wiki/Lupus" class="mw-redirect" title="Lupus">lupus</a>. Ron confronts Harry at Thelma's funeral, but the men later reconcile over golf.  Harry also encounters Cindy Murkett at the funeral, a woman he had once been obsessed with, and is saddened to see she has become an obese and bitter divorcee.
</p><p>After Nelson comes back from a treatment program, and Janice begins work as a real estate agent, the family finds out that Harry has had a one-night stand with Pru, Nelson's wife, on the night after he was released from the hospital. Janice's anger over this betrayal prompts Harry to escape to Florida. While in hiding, Harry has a heart attack shortly after winning a one-on-one basketball game with a local youth (echoing the opening of <i><a href="/wiki/Rabbit,_Run" title="Rabbit, Run">Rabbit, Run</a></i> in which Harry impulsively joins a group of teenagers playing basketball). Nelson and Janice reach Harry's bedside while he is still alive. Janice forgives him for his infidelities and he reconciles with his son. His personal business now largely resolved, Rabbit dies.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">Themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rabbit_at_Rest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The novel deals with the passage of time, and generational change. Harry's decline mirrors the decline of the United States which, in the period the novel is set, lacks what Harry saw as its animating purpose: the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
