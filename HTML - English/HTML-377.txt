<p><b>Jillian Anne Ellis</b> (born 6 September 1966) is an English-American soccer coach. She coached the <a href="/wiki/United_States_women%27s_national_soccer_team" title="United States women&#39;s national soccer team">United States women's national soccer team</a> for 5 years, starting in 2014 and stepping down on 6 October 2019. She also won 2 FIFA Women's World Cups with the USWNT and is the development director of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Soccer_Federation" title="United States Soccer Federation">United States Soccer Federation</a>, overseeing the national youth teams development program.
</p><p>In 2015 and 2019, the United States Women's National Soccer Team won the <a href="/wiki/2015_FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup" title="2015 FIFA Women&#39;s World Cup">World Cup</a> under her leadership, making her the second coach to win a World Cup back-to-back. Ellis was appointed head coach on 16 May 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-uss_headcoach2014_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uss_headcoach2014-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Prior to this appointment, she served as interim head coach following the removal of <a href="/wiki/Tom_Sermanni" title="Tom Sermanni">Tom Sermanni</a> on 6 April 2014,<sup id="cite_ref-uss_interim2014_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uss_interim2014-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> having previously held the position upon <a href="/wiki/Pia_Sundhage" title="Pia Sundhage">Pia Sundhage's</a> early departure in October 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-yah_ap_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yah_ap-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> She has also served as head coach for various college and United States national youth teams over the years.<sup id="cite_ref-uss_je_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uss_je-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_playing_career">Early life and playing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jill_Ellis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and playing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ellis did not play organized soccer until her family moved to the United States in 1981, as there was no organized soccer for girls in the UK in the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-WaPo_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WaPo-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> She captained the <a href="/wiki/Robinson_Secondary_School" title="Robinson Secondary School">Robinson Secondary School</a> team in <a href="/wiki/Fairfax,_Virginia" title="Fairfax, Virginia">Fairfax, Virginia</a> to the 1984 state championship and won the under-19 national title with the Braddock Road Bluebelles the same summer.<sup id="cite_ref-WaPo_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WaPo-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>She went on to play as a <a href="/wiki/Forward_(association_football)" title="Forward (association football)">forward</a> at <a href="/wiki/William_%26_Mary_Tribe_women%27s_soccer,_1981%E2%80%9389" title="William &amp; Mary Tribe women&#39;s soccer, 1981–89">William &amp; Mary</a> from 1984 to 1987, when she was named third-team All-American.<sup id="cite_ref-uss_je_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uss_je-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coaching_and_administrative_career">Coaching and administrative career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jill_Ellis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Coaching and administrative career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ellis has a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Soccer_Federation" title="United States Soccer Federation">USSF</a> Pro coaching licence; with coaching experiences that includes multiple stints for the under-20 and under-21 national teams, and an impressive record as a <a href="/wiki/UCLA_Bruins" title="UCLA Bruins">UCLA Bruins</a> coach.<sup id="cite_ref-yah_ap_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yah_ap-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-uss_je_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uss_je-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ucla_oas_je_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ucla_oas_je-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-soctms_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soctms-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-uss_je_u20_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uss_je_u20-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
She is the Development Director of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Soccer_Federation" title="United States Soccer Federation">United States Soccer Federation</a>. She served as assistant coach of the women's national team, head coach of a number of women youth teams, and was the interim coach of the senior women's national team in 2012,<sup id="cite_ref-uss_je_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uss_je-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and for 2 matches in 2014. She was appointed the permanent head coach of United States women in May 2014.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="College_coaching">College coaching</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jill_Ellis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: College coaching">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ellis served as an assistant coach for three universities: at <a href="/wiki/Maryland_Terrapins_women%27s_soccer" class="mw-redirect" title="Maryland Terrapins women&#39;s soccer">Maryland</a> for three years, 1994–96; at <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Cavaliers_women%27s_soccer" title="Virginia Cavaliers women&#39;s soccer">Virginia</a> for one year, 1996–97; and at <a href="/wiki/NC_State_Wolfpack_women%27s_soccer" title="NC State Wolfpack women&#39;s soccer">NC State</a> for another three years, 1988–90.
As an assistant coach at N.C. State, Ellis helped the NC State secure the 1988 <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Coast_Conference" title="Atlantic Coast Conference">Atlantic Coast Conference</a> title and a <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Division_I_Women%27s_Soccer_Championship" title="NCAA Division I Women&#39;s Soccer Championship">NCAA</a> Final Four appearance.<sup id="cite_ref-uss_je_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uss_je-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ellis headed the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Fighting_Illini_women%27s_soccer" class="mw-redirect" title="Illinois Fighting Illini women&#39;s soccer">Illinois</a> women's soccer program for two years, 1997–1999. In 1998, she brought the Fighting Illini to a 12–8 record and a first-ever <a href="/wiki/Big_Ten" class="mw-redirect" title="Big Ten">Big Ten</a> Tournament berth.<sup id="cite_ref-uss_je_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uss_je-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ellis led <a href="/wiki/UCLA_Bruins_women%27s_soccer" title="UCLA Bruins women&#39;s soccer">UCLA</a> to eight NCAA Final Fours, including seven in a row from 2003–2009, and won six straight <a href="/wiki/Pac-12_Conference" title="Pac-12 Conference">Pacific-10 Conference</a> titles from 2003–2008. She finished her time in Westwood with a record of 229 wins, 45 losses and 14 draws (229–45–14).
She was the 2000 <a href="/wiki/NSCAA" class="mw-redirect" title="NSCAA">NSCAA</a> National Coach of the Year after leading the Bruins to the NCAA Final in just her second season as head coach.<sup id="cite_ref-uss_je_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uss_je-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-espn_ucla_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-espn_ucla-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ellis has an all-time collegiate coaching record of 248 wins, 63 losses and 14 draws (248–63–14), compiled over 14 years with the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Fighting_Illini" title="Illinois Fighting Illini">Illinois Fighting Illini</a> and <a href="/wiki/UCLA_Bruins" title="UCLA Bruins">UCLA Bruins</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-uss_je_6-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uss_je-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ucla_bjs_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ucla_bjs-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_youth_teams_manager">National youth teams manager</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jill_Ellis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: National youth teams manager">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ellis was the head coach of the United States under-21 women's national football teams, coaching a team to win the Nordic Cup title at Germany 2000 and to Sweden's 2005 Nordic Cup. In another stint as youth team's head coach, she guided the U.S. under-20 women’s national team to the CONCACAF title in 2010 and to the <a href="/wiki/FIFA_U-20_Women%27s_World_Cup" title="FIFA U-20 Women&#39;s World Cup">FIFA U-20 Women's World Cup</a> in Germany.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Development_Director">National Development Director</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jill_Ellis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: National Development Director">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ellis was appointed by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Soccer_Federation" title="United States Soccer Federation">U.S. Soccer</a> as Development Director for the U.S. women's national teams in January 2011. The appointment, along with <a href="/wiki/April_Heinrichs" title="April Heinrichs">April Heinrichs</a> as Technical Director, marked the first time U.S. Soccer had appointed full-time positions to oversee the programs and development of national women's youth teams.<sup id="cite_ref-socam_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-socam-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As Development Director, Ellis interacts directly with coaches within the youth club leagues and guides the U.S. under-17, under-15 and under-14 teams.<sup id="cite_ref-uss_je_6-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uss_je-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-socam_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-socam-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_team_manager">National team manager</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jill_Ellis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: National team manager">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ellis was a scout for the U.S. women's national team at the <a href="/wiki/Football_at_the_2000_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Women%27s_tournament" title="Football at the 2000 Summer Olympics – Women&#39;s tournament">Sydney 2000 Olympics</a>, and while coach of the UCLA Bruins Ellis also served as an assistant coach under Pia Sundhage for the gold medal-winning U.S. women’s national team at the <a href="/wiki/Football_at_the_2008_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Women%27s_tournament" title="Football at the 2008 Summer Olympics – Women&#39;s tournament">Beijing 2008 Olympics</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2012_interim_coach">2012 interim coach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jill_Ellis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2012 interim coach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Following Sundhage's departure on 1 September 2012, for <a href="/wiki/Sweden_women%27s_national_football_team" title="Sweden women&#39;s national football team">Sweden</a>, Ellis (as women's national team program development director) served as the interim head coach until U.S. Soccer hired <a href="/wiki/Tom_Sermanni" title="Tom Sermanni">Tom Sermanni</a> as the full-time head coach on 1 January 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-yah_ap_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yah_ap-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-uss_je_6-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uss_je-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ucla_nws_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ucla_nws-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ellis's first appearances as head coach of <a href="/wiki/United_States_women%27s_national_soccer_team" title="United States women&#39;s national soccer team">United States women's national soccer team</a> was against <a href="/wiki/Germany_women%27s_national_football_team" title="Germany women&#39;s national football team">Germany</a>, on 20 October 2012, at <a href="/wiki/Bridgeview,_Illinois" title="Bridgeview, Illinois">Bridgeview, Illinois</a>, and on 23 October at <a href="/wiki/Hartford,_Connecticut" title="Hartford, Connecticut">Hartford, Connecticut</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Exhibition_game" title="Exhibition game">international friendly</a> matches were part of a series organized to celebrate the winning of the gold medal at the <a href="/wiki/Football_at_the_2012_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Women%27s_tournament" title="Football at the 2012 Summer Olympics – Women&#39;s tournament">2012 Olympics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-yah_ap_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yah_ap-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The first match finished at 1–1 and the second at 2–2.<sup id="cite_ref-mat001_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mat001-17">&#91;m 1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mat002_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mat002-18">&#91;m 2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With a match against <a href="/wiki/China_women%27s_national_football_team" title="China women&#39;s national football team">China</a> on 15 December 2012, Ellis completed her first stint as interim head coach of U.S. women's national team with 5 wins, 2 draws, and no loss.<sup id="cite_ref-mat001_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mat001-17">&#91;m 1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mat002_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mat002-18">&#91;m 2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mat003_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mat003-19">&#91;m 3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mat004_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mat004-20">&#91;m 4&#93;</a></sup>
<sup id="cite_ref-mat005_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mat005-21">&#91;m 5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mat006_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mat006-22">&#91;m 6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mat007_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mat007-23">&#91;m 7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2014_interim_coach">2014 interim coach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jill_Ellis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2014 interim coach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 6 April 2014, U.S. Soccer announced the firing of <a href="/wiki/Tom_Sermanni" title="Tom Sermanni">Tom Sermanni</a> and re-appointment of Ellis as interim head coach of the United States women's national soccer team.<sup id="cite_ref-uss_interim2014_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uss_interim2014-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> As interim head coach in 2014, Ellis had a 3–0 win against China and a 1–1 draw with Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-mat008_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mat008-24">&#91;m 8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mat009_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mat009-25">&#91;m 9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2014_appointment_as_head_coach">2014 appointment as head coach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jill_Ellis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2014 appointment as head coach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 16 May 2014, U.S. Soccer announced that Ellis had been appointed as the national team's head coach on a permanent basis.<sup id="cite_ref-uss_headcoach2014_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uss_headcoach2014-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Ellis's job as head coach was to qualify for the 2015 Women's World Cup and win the championship.<sup id="cite_ref-uss_headcoach2014_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uss_headcoach2014-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> On 5 July 2015, she coached the United States to a 5–2 victory over <a href="/wiki/Japan_women%27s_national_football_team" title="Japan women&#39;s national football team">Japan</a> to win the World Cup.<sup id="cite_ref-wcfin2015_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wcfin2015-26">&#91;m 10&#93;</a></sup>
Ellis was honored as <a href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Coach_of_the_Year#FIFA_World_Coach_of_the_Year_for_Women&#39;s_Football" title="FIFA World Coach of the Year">2015 FIFA World Coach of the Year for Women's Football</a> on 11 January 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-coty2015_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-coty2015-27">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, the U.S. women's national team recorded five shutout wins to secure the <a href="/wiki/2016_CONCACAF_Women%27s_Olympic_Qualifying_Championship" title="2016 CONCACAF Women&#39;s Olympic Qualifying Championship">2016 CONCACAF Women's Olympic Qualifying Championship</a>. However, the team struggled during the <a href="/wiki/2016_Summer_Olympics" title="2016 Summer Olympics">2016 Summer Olympics</a>, drawing against <a href="/wiki/Colombia_women%27s_national_football_team" title="Colombia women&#39;s national football team">Colombia</a> in the group stage and eliminated to eventual silver medal winners <a href="/wiki/Sweden_women%27s_national_football_team" title="Sweden women&#39;s national football team">Sweden</a> — a team led by former national team coach <a href="/wiki/Pia_Sundhage" title="Pia Sundhage">Pia Sundhage</a> — 4–3 on penalty kicks after drawing in regulation and extra time. The loss marked the first time that the U.S. women's national team did not advance to the gold medal game of the Olympics, as well as the first time that the team failed to advance to the semifinal round of a major tournament.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The effort was further marred when U.S. goalkeeper <a href="/wiki/Hope_Solo" title="Hope Solo">Hope Solo</a> called the Swedish team "cowards" for their defensive tactics. Ellis would later cite those comments as part of the reason why U.S. Soccer terminated Solo's contract and suspended her from the team.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The U.S. women's national team won four subsequent international friendly matches (9–0 against Thailand, 3–1 against the Netherlands, and 4–0 and 5–1 in two matches against Switzerland). The latter two friendlies featured the largest squad turnover of Ellis's tenure as she brought in 11 uncapped players, started or played 6 of them, and left several regular players out of camp.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-kneel_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kneel-32">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jill_Ellis,_USWNT_coach.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Jill_Ellis%2C_USWNT_coach.jpg/220px-Jill_Ellis%2C_USWNT_coach.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Jill_Ellis%2C_USWNT_coach.jpg/330px-Jill_Ellis%2C_USWNT_coach.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Jill_Ellis%2C_USWNT_coach.jpg/440px-Jill_Ellis%2C_USWNT_coach.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5667" data-file-height="3778" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jill_Ellis,_USWNT_coach.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ellis at a friendly against Portugal during the 2019 Victory Tour</div></div></div><p>Before the friendly against Thailand, Ellis asked U.S. women's national team midfielder <a href="/wiki/Megan_Rapinoe" title="Megan Rapinoe">Megan Rapinoe</a> not to kneel during the pre-game performance of the <a href="/wiki/The_Star-Spangled_Banner" title="The Star-Spangled Banner">United States national anthem</a>. Rapinoe had done so before matches with her <a href="/wiki/National_Women%27s_Soccer_League" title="National Women&#39;s Soccer League">National Women's Soccer League</a> team, <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Reign_FC" class="mw-redirect" title="Seattle Reign FC">Seattle Reign FC</a>, prior to camp in support of other professional athletes' <a href="/wiki/2016_U.S._national_anthem_protests" class="mw-redirect" title="2016 U.S. national anthem protests">similar protests</a>. Despite Ellis's request, she did not punish Rapinoe after the game.<sup id="cite_ref-kneel_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kneel-32">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 7 July 2019 she led the United States Women's National Team to win its fourth World Cup and its second consecutive.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-34">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In the final match against Netherlands in Lyon, France, the team won 2–0. The 2019 World Cup Champion Team beat the world record for most goals in a tournament with 26 goals.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-34">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> She became not only the first manager to win two Women's World Cup titles in history, but also the first national team coach, men's or women's, to have won two consecutive <a href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Cup" title="FIFA World Cup">FIFA World Cup</a> titles since <a href="/wiki/Vittorio_Pozzo" title="Vittorio Pozzo">Vittorio Pozzo</a> guided <a href="/wiki/Italy_national_football_team" title="Italy national football team">Italy's men's national football team</a> to two consecutive titles in both <a href="/wiki/1934_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1934 FIFA World Cup">1934</a> and <a href="/wiki/1938_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1938 FIFA World Cup">1938</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 30 July 2019, <a href="/wiki/U.S._Soccer" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Soccer">U.S. Soccer</a> announced that Ellis would be stepping down as coach of the Women's National Team. She will remain with the team through the World Cup victory tour. Following her departure, she will continue to work with U.S. Soccer in the role of Ambassador.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jill_Ellis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ellis grew up in <a href="/wiki/Cowplain" title="Cowplain">Cowplain</a>, a small village near <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a> on the southern coast of England and was a supporter of <a href="/wiki/Manchester_United_Football_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchester United Football Club">Manchester United</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-espnw_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-espnw-38">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>  Naturally athletic, she ran track and field, and played field hockey and <a href="/wiki/Netball" title="Netball">netball</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-espnw_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-espnw-38">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> but did not play organized soccer as it was considered "unladylike" in 1970s United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-WaPo_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WaPo-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> She would, however, tag along with her brother Paul and play with the boys whenever they needed an extra player.<sup id="cite_ref-WaPo_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WaPo-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Her father, John Ellis, a former <a href="/wiki/Royal_Marines" title="Royal Marines">Royal Marines</a> commando, was a longtime soccer ambassador for the British government, assigned to help create football programs worldwide, including in Trinidad and Tobago and in Singapore. In 1981, the Ellis family moved to <a href="/wiki/Northern_Virginia" title="Northern Virginia">Northern Virginia</a>, where he founded Soccer Academy in <a href="/wiki/Manassas,_Virginia" title="Manassas, Virginia">Manassas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WaPo_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WaPo-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Her brother Paul also became a soccer coach, coaching area high school teams and serving as an assistant coach at <a href="/wiki/George_Mason_Patriots_women%27s_soccer" class="mw-redirect" title="George Mason Patriots women&#39;s soccer">George Mason</a> before taking a full-time position at Soccer Academy Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-uss_je_6-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uss_je-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ellis earned a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">Bachelor of Arts</a> degree in English Literature and Composition at the <a href="/wiki/College_of_William_and_Mary" class="mw-redirect" title="College of William and Mary">College of William and Mary</a> in 1988, and was awarded an honorary Doctor of Humane Letters degree in 2016. She also did work towards a master's degree in technical writing at <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_State_University" title="North Carolina State University">North Carolina State University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WaPo_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WaPo-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Her mother Margaret "was horrified", on learning of her plan to give up her lucrative job as a technical writer at <a href="/wiki/Northern_Telecom" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Telecom">Northern Telecom</a> to work as an assistant coach with subsistence pay,<sup id="cite_ref-mh_20140904_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mh_20140904-39">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> while John advised her to "do something substantial" instead.<sup id="cite_ref-WaPo_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WaPo-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ellis lives in <a href="/wiki/Palmetto_Bay,_Florida" title="Palmetto Bay, Florida">Palmetto Bay, Florida</a>, a suburb of <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>, with her wife Betsy Stephenson, whom she married in 2013, and their adopted daughter Lily Stephenson - Ellis  .<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>  Ellis, along with her parents and brother, are <a href="/wiki/Naturalized_citizen_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Naturalized citizen of the United States">naturalized American citizens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WaPo_7-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WaPo-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors">Honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jill_Ellis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
