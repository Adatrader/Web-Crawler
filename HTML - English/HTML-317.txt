<p><b>Radley Balko</b> (born April 19, 1975)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is an American journalist, author, blogger, and speaker who lives in <a href="/wiki/Nashville" class="mw-redirect" title="Nashville">Nashville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>, and writes about criminal justice, the <a href="/wiki/War_on_Drugs" class="mw-redirect" title="War on Drugs">drug war</a>, and civil liberties for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>. Balko has written and published several books including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rise_of_the_Warrior_Cop" title="The Rise of the Warrior Cop">The Rise of the Warrior Cop</a></i> and <i>The Cadaver King and the Country Dentist</i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life_and_education">Personal life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radley_Balko&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Personal life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Balko is an <a href="/wiki/Atheist" class="mw-redirect" title="Atheist">atheist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He earned a B.A. in journalism and <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a> in 1997 from <a href="/wiki/Indiana_University_Bloomington" title="Indiana University Bloomington">Indiana University Bloomington</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Employment_and_publications">Employment and publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radley_Balko&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Employment and publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Balko blogs about criminal justice, the drug war and civil liberties for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>. Previously, he was a senior writer and investigative reporter for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Huffington_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="The Huffington Post">The Huffington Post</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-huffingtonbio_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-huffingtonbio-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> a senior editor at <i><a href="/wiki/Reason_(magazine)" title="Reason (magazine)">Reason</a></i> magazine, and a policy analyst for the <a href="/wiki/Cato_Institute" title="Cato Institute">Cato Institute</a>, specializing in vice and civil liberties issues. He writes on <a href="/wiki/War_on_Drugs" class="mw-redirect" title="War on Drugs">drug policy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Police_misconduct" title="Police misconduct">police misconduct</a>, <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a>, alcohol, tobacco, and <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">civil liberties</a>. He also writes on trade and <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalization</a> issues and more generally on politics and culture. He was also a biweekly columnist for <a href="/wiki/Fox_News" title="Fox News">Fox News</a> from 2002 until 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-reasonbio_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reasonbio-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> His work has been published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Playboy" title="Playboy">Playboy</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/TIME_magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="TIME magazine">TIME magazine</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Slate_(magazine)" title="Slate (magazine)">Slate</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Reason_(magazine)" title="Reason (magazine)">Reason</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Worth_(magazine)" title="Worth (magazine)">Worth</a></i> magazine, Canada's <i><a href="/wiki/National_Post" title="National Post">National Post</a></i>, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i>. He has appeared on <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a>, <a href="/wiki/CNBC" title="CNBC">CNBC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fox_News_Channel" class="mw-redirect" title="Fox News Channel">Fox News</a>, <a href="/wiki/MSNBC" title="MSNBC">MSNBC</a>, and <a href="/wiki/National_Public_Radio" class="mw-redirect" title="National Public Radio">National Public Radio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He began writing an opinion blog at <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> in January 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Balko's work on "<a href="/wiki/No_knock_warrant" class="mw-redirect" title="No knock warrant">no-knock</a>" drug raids was profiled in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, and cited by U.S. Supreme Court Justice <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Breyer" title="Stephen Breyer">Stephen Breyer</a> in his dissent in <i><a href="/wiki/Hudson_v._Michigan" title="Hudson v. Michigan">Hudson v. Michigan</a></i>.  He is credited with breaking and reporting the <a href="/wiki/Cory_Maye" title="Cory Maye">Cory Maye</a> case; his work on the Maye case was cited by the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="Mississippi Supreme Court">Mississippi Supreme Court</a>. He has also written extensively about the <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Frederick" title="Ryan Frederick">Ryan Frederick</a> case and the raid on <a href="/wiki/Cheye_Calvo" class="mw-redirect" title="Cheye Calvo">Cheye Calvo</a>'s home.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Balko has advocated the abolition of laws criminalizing <a href="/wiki/Drunk_driving" class="mw-redirect" title="Drunk driving">drunk driving</a>, arguing that the "punishable act should be violating road rules or causing an accident, not the factors that led to those offenses. Singling out alcohol impairment for extra punishment isn't about making the roads safer".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He has expressed his position against the judicial policy of civil asset forfeiture, arguing that it is a "practice contrary to a basic sense of justice and fairness".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Balko has also authored two books on the topic of increasing <a href="/wiki/Militarization_of_police" title="Militarization of police">militarization in police forces</a>:
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Rise_of_the_Warrior_Cop" title="The Rise of the Warrior Cop">Rise of the Warrior Cop: The Militarization of America's Police Forces</a></i> (PublicAffairs), 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Overkill: The Rise of Paramilitary Police Raids in America</i> (Cato Institute), 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> According to Balko, during the 1970s, there were about 300 <a href="/wiki/SWAT" title="SWAT">SWAT</a> raids a year; however, "As of 2005...100 to 150 per day".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radley_Balko&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2009, Balko's investigative report on expert witness fraud in a <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment_in_Louisiana" title="Capital punishment in Louisiana">Louisiana death penalty</a> case won the Western Publication Association's Maggie Award for reporting.<sup id="cite_ref-huffingtonbio_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-huffingtonbio-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Week" title="The Week">The Week</a></i> named Balko a finalist for Opinion Columnist of the Year.<sup id="cite_ref-huffingtonbio_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-huffingtonbio-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Also in 2011, the Los Angeles Press Club named Balko Best of Show Journalist of the Year, the judges saying:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Radley Balko is one of those throw-back journalists that understands the power of groundbreaking reporting and how to make a significant impact through his work. Time and time again, his stories cause readers to stop, think, and most significantly, take action.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
