<p><b>Hubert</b> "<b>Cubby</b>" <b>Selby Jr.</b> (July 27, 1928 – April 26, 2004) was an American writer. His best-known novels are <i><a href="/wiki/Last_Exit_to_Brooklyn" title="Last Exit to Brooklyn">Last Exit to Brooklyn</a></i> (1964) and <i><a href="/wiki/Requiem_for_a_Dream_(novel)" title="Requiem for a Dream (novel)">Requiem for a Dream</a></i> (1978), exploring worlds in the New York area. Both novels were adapted later as films, and he appeared in small roles in each.
</p><p>Selby wrote about a harsh underworld seldom portrayed in literature. His first novel was prosecuted for obscenity in the United Kingdom and banned in Italy, prompting defences from many leading authors such as <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Burgess" title="Anthony Burgess">Anthony Burgess</a>. He was highly influential to more than a generation of writers; for more than 20 years, he taught creative writing at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a> in Los Angeles, where he lived full-time after 1983.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hubert_Selby_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hubert Selby was born in 1928 in <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, to Adalin and Hubert Selby Sr., a merchant <a href="/wiki/Sailor" title="Sailor">seaman</a> and former <a href="/wiki/Coal_miner" class="mw-redirect" title="Coal miner">coal miner</a> from <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>. Selby and his wife Adalin had settled in <a href="/wiki/Bay_Ridge,_Brooklyn" title="Bay Ridge, Brooklyn">Bay Ridge</a>. The boy attended public schools, including the competitive <a href="/wiki/Stuyvesant_High_School" title="Stuyvesant High School">Stuyvesant High School</a>. His childhood nickname, "Cubby", accompanied him through his life.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Selby Jr. dropped out of school at the age of 15, to work in the city docks, before becoming a merchant seaman in 1947.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> That year, while at sea, Selby Jr. was diagnosed with advanced <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> ("TB") due to the cows on board, which had bovine tuberculosis; doctors predicted that he would live less than a year<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>.
</p><p>He was taken off the ship in <a href="/wiki/Bremen_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bremen (city)">Bremen</a>, Germany, and sent back to the United States. For the next three and a half years, Selby was in and out of the U.S. Public Health Hospital (part of a system of hospitals originally established to care for merchant seamen)<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> in New York for treatment. Antibiotics had not been available and TB was widespread.
</p><p>Selby went through an experimental drug treatment, <a href="/wiki/Streptomycin" title="Streptomycin">streptomycin</a>, that later caused some severe complications. During an operation, surgeons removed several of Selby's ribs in order to reach his lungs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> One of his <a href="/wiki/Lungs_collapsed" class="mw-redirect" title="Lungs collapsed">lungs collapsed</a>, and doctors removed part of the other. While the surgery saved Selby's life, he had a year-long recuperation and chronic <a href="/wiki/Human_lung" class="mw-redirect" title="Human lung">pulmonary</a> problems for the rest of his life. Selby was given <a href="/wiki/Painkillers" class="mw-redirect" title="Painkillers">painkillers</a>, among them <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a>, because of the severity of the surgery, and he became addicted. He struggled with substance abuse for decades.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Becoming_a_writer">Becoming a writer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hubert_Selby_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Becoming a writer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With no qualifications, no work experience aside from the Merchant Marine, and his poor health, Selby had trouble finding a job. He had married his wife Inez V. "Tiny" Taylor and they had two children, whom he raised while his wife worked in a department store.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>For the next ten years, Selby was mostly bedridden; he was frequently hospitalized with a variety of lung-related ailments. The doctors offered a bleak prognosis, suggesting he was unlikely to survive long because he "just didn't have enough lung capacity". <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Sorrentino" title="Gilbert Sorrentino">Gilbert Sorrentino</a>, a childhood friend who had become a writer, encouraged Selby to write fiction. Unable to have regular work because of his health, Selby decided, "I know the alphabet. Maybe I could be a writer."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He later wrote:
</p>
<blockquote><p>I was sitting at home and had a profound experience. I experienced, in all of my Being, that someday I was going to die, and it wouldn't be like it had been happening, almost dying but somehow staying alive, but I would just die! And two things would happen right before I died: I would regret my entire life; I would want to live it over again. This terrified me. The thought that I would live my entire life, look at it and realize I blew it forced me to do something with my life.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>With no formal training, Selby used a raw language to portray the bleak and violent world that was part of his youth. He has said, "I write, in part, by ear. I hear, as well as feel and see, what I am writing. I have always been enamoured with the music of the speech in New York."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Little concerned with proper <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Punctuation" title="Punctuation">punctuation</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Diction" title="Diction">diction</a>, Selby used unorthodox techniques in most of his works. He <a href="/wiki/Indentation_(typesetting)" title="Indentation (typesetting)">indented</a> his <a href="/wiki/Paragraph" title="Paragraph">paragraphs</a> with alternating lengths, often by simply dropping down one line when finished with a paragraph. Like <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kerouac" title="Jack Kerouac">Jack Kerouac</a> in his "spontaneous prose", Selby often completed his writing in a fast, <a href="/wiki/Stream_of_consciousness_writing" class="mw-redirect" title="Stream of consciousness writing">stream-of-consciousness</a> style. He replaced <a href="/wiki/Apostrophe" title="Apostrophe">apostrophes</a> with forward slashes "/," which were closer on the typewriter, to avoid interrupting his flow of writing. He did not use <a href="/wiki/Quotation_mark" title="Quotation mark">quotation marks</a>. He might present a dialogue as a complete paragraph, with no denotation among alternating speakers. His prose was stripped down, bare and blunt.
</p><p>Aspects of his experiences with <a href="/wiki/Longshoreman" class="mw-redirect" title="Longshoreman">longshoremen</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Homeless" class="mw-redirect" title="Homeless">homeless</a>, thugs, <a href="/wiki/Pimp" class="mw-redirect" title="Pimp">pimps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transvestism" title="Transvestism">transvestites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prostitute" class="mw-redirect" title="Prostitute">prostitutes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">homosexuals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Substance_dependence" title="Substance dependence">addicts</a> and the overall poverty-stricken community, is expressed in <i><a href="/wiki/Last_Exit_to_Brooklyn" title="Last Exit to Brooklyn">Last Exit to Brooklyn</a></i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_works">Early works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hubert_Selby_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Selby started working on his first short story, "The Queen Is Dead," in 1958. At the time, he had a succession of day jobs, but he wrote every night. During the day, he worked as a secretary, a gas station attendant, and a freelance copywriter. The short story developed slowly for the next six years before he published it.
</p><p>In 1961, his short story "Tralala" was published in the literary journal <i>The Provincetown Review</i>. It also appeared in <i>Black Mountain Review</i> and <i>New Directions</i>. His unstructured style and coarse descriptions supported his portrayal of the seedy life (ridden with violence, theft and mediocre con-artistry) and the gang rape of a <a href="/wiki/Prostitute" class="mw-redirect" title="Prostitute">prostitute</a>. A number of critics attacked the subjects and harshness of the story. The journal editor was arrested for selling <a href="/wiki/Pornographic" class="mw-redirect" title="Pornographic">pornographic</a> <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Minor_(law)" title="Minor (law)">minor</a>. The journal was used as evidence in an <a href="/wiki/Obscenity" title="Obscenity">obscenity</a> trial, but the case was later dismissed on appeal.
</p><p>On 24 October 1964, Selby married Judith Lumino, but the marriage soon fell apart. As he continued to write, his longtime friend LeRoi Jones (later <a href="/wiki/Amiri_Baraka" title="Amiri Baraka">Amiri Baraka</a>), the poet and playwright, encouraged him to contact <a href="/wiki/Sterling_Lord" title="Sterling Lord">Sterling Lord</a>, then Kerouac's agent. Selby combined "Tralala", "The Queen Is Dead" and four other loosely linked short stories as part of his first novel, <i>Last Exit to Brooklyn</i> (1964). The novel was accepted and published by <a href="/wiki/Grove_Press" title="Grove Press">Grove Press</a>, which had already published works by <a href="/wiki/William_S._Burroughs" title="William S. Burroughs">William S. Burroughs</a>.  In November 1964, <a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a> literary critic Eliot Fremont-Smith described the novel as "a brutal book," concluding that it "is not a book one 'recommends'--except perhaps to writers. From them, those who wish to read it, it deserves attention."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The novel was praised by many, including the poet <a href="/wiki/Allen_Ginsberg" title="Allen Ginsberg">Allen Ginsberg</a>, who predicted that it would "explode like a rusty hellish bombshell over America and still be eagerly read in a hundred years." But, at a time when literature excluded much that was harsh, not everyone wanted to read his detailed depictions of <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">homosexuality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drug_addiction" class="mw-redirect" title="Drug addiction">drug addiction</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Gang_rape" title="Gang rape">gang rape</a> and other forms of human <a href="/wiki/Battery_(crime)" title="Battery (crime)">brutality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cruelty" title="Cruelty">cruelty</a>. In 1967, the novel was <a href="/wiki/Last_Exit_to_Brooklyn#Trial" title="Last Exit to Brooklyn">prosecuted for obscenity</a> in the United Kingdom. The notable British writer <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Burgess" title="Anthony Burgess">Anthony Burgess</a> was among a number of writers who appeared as witnesses in its defense. The jury's conviction was later reversed on <a href="/wiki/Appeal" title="Appeal">appeal</a>. The novel was <a href="/wiki/Ban_(law)" title="Ban (law)">banned</a> in Italy.
</p><p>Although he wrote all his work while sober, Selby continued to battle drug addiction. In 1967 he was arrested for heroin possession and served two months in the Los Angeles County jail. After his release, he moved from New York to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> to try to escape his addictions and finally kicked the habit. He stayed clean of drugs but continued to battle alcohol abuse for the next two years. Also that year, Selby met his future wife, Suzanne Victoria Shaw, at a bar in <a href="/wiki/West_Hollywood" class="mw-redirect" title="West Hollywood">West Hollywood</a>. The couple moved in together two days after they met. They married in 1969, after Selby and his second wife, Judith, had finalized their divorce.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> For the next decade, Suzanne and Selby traveled back and forth between their home in <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">Southern California</a> and the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a>, settling permanently in the Los Angeles area in 1983. They had two children, daughter Rachel and son William.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_after_Last_Exit_to_Brooklyn">Life after <i>Last Exit to Brooklyn</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hubert_Selby_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Life after Last Exit to Brooklyn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1971, Selby published his second novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Room_(Selby_novel)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Room (Selby novel)">The Room</a>,</i> which received positive reviews. It featured a <a href="/wiki/Criminally_insane" class="mw-redirect" title="Criminally insane">criminally insane</a> man, locked in a room in a prison, who reminisces about his disturbing past. Selby described <i>The Room</i> as "the most disturbing book ever written." He said he could not read it for decades after writing it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Selby continued to write <a href="/wiki/Short_fiction" class="mw-redirect" title="Short fiction">short fiction</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">screenplays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Teleplay" title="Teleplay">teleplays</a> at his apartment in <a href="/wiki/West_Hollywood" class="mw-redirect" title="West Hollywood">West Hollywood</a>. His work was published in many magazines, including <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Mountain_Review" class="mw-redirect" title="Black Mountain Review">Black Mountain Review</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Evergreen_Review" title="Evergreen Review">Evergreen Review</a></i>, <i>Provincetown Review</i>, <i>Kulchur</i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Directions_Annual&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="New Directions Annual (page does not exist)">New Directions Annual</a></i>, <i>Yugen</i>, <i>Swank</i> and <i>Open City</i>.
</p><p>In the 1980s, Selby met <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a> singer <a href="/wiki/Henry_Rollins" title="Henry Rollins">Henry Rollins</a>, who had long admired the writer's works and publicly championed them.<sup id="cite_ref-Henry_and_Heidi_Podcast_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Henry_and_Heidi_Podcast-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Rollins helped broaden Selby's readership, and also arranged recording sessions and reading tours for Selby. Rollins issued original recordings through his own <a href="/wiki/2.13.61" title="2.13.61">2.13.61</a> publications, and distributed Selby's other works.<sup id="cite_ref-Henry_and_Heidi_Podcast_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Henry_and_Heidi_Podcast-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For the last 20 years of his life, Selby also taught <a href="/wiki/Creative_writing" title="Creative writing">creative writing</a> as an adjunct professor in the Master of Professional Writing program at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a>.
</p><p>A film adaptation of <i>Last Exit to Brooklyn</i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Uli_Edel" title="Uli Edel">Uli Edel</a>, was made in 1989. Selby appeared in <i>Brooklyn</i> in a brief cameo as a taxi driver. <i><a href="/wiki/Requiem_for_a_Dream_(novel)" title="Requiem for a Dream (novel)">Requiem for a Dream</a></i> (1978) was adapted as a film of the same name released in 2000. He had a small role as a prison guard taunting <a href="/wiki/Marlon_Wayans" title="Marlon Wayans">Marlon Wayans</a>, suffering through forced labor while withdrawing.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The last month of his life, Selby spent in and out of the hospital and died in <a href="/wiki/Highland_Park,_Los_Angeles" title="Highland Park, Los Angeles">Highland Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, on April 26, 2004 of <a href="/wiki/Chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease" title="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease">chronic obstructive pulmonary disease</a>. He refused <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a> on his deathbed, although he was in pain.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <i>The New York Times</i> published his obituary the next day.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Selby was the subject of the 2005 documentary, <i><a href="/wiki/Hubert_Selby_Jr:_It/ll_Be_Better_Tomorrow" class="mw-redirect" title="Hubert Selby Jr: It/ll Be Better Tomorrow">Hubert Selby Jr: It/ll Be Better Tomorrow</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unfinished_and_unpublished">Unfinished and unpublished</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hubert_Selby_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Unfinished and unpublished">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At least one work-in-progress remained unfinished and unpublished at the time of Selby's death: <i>The Seeds of Pain and the Seeds of Love</i>. Excerpts from this work are heard on the <i>Live in Europe 1989</i> CD.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hubert_Selby_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>In 1972, <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a> said that two novels influenced him greatly: <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kerouac" title="Jack Kerouac">Jack Kerouac</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Road" title="On the Road">On the Road</a></i> and Selby's <i>Last Exit to Brooklyn</i>. According to an article in the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a> published in 1973,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Bowie had "confessed that he had formed a desperate identification with" the latter novel.</li>
<li>Selby's first work, "The Queen Is Dead" (appearing as a chapter in <i>Last Exit to Brooklyn</i>), inspired the name of an album by <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> alternative rock group <a href="/wiki/The_Smiths" title="The Smiths">The Smiths</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In the book <i>Was This Man a Genius?</i> by Julie Hecht, the comedian <a href="/wiki/Andy_Kaufman" title="Andy Kaufman">Andy Kaufman</a> is quoted saying that his favourite book is <i>The Demon</i> by Hubert Selby (p.&#160;159).</li>
<li><i>Last Exit to Brooklyn</i> inspired the name of <a href="/wiki/Sting_(musician)" title="Sting (musician)">Sting</a>'s first band, Last Exit.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Manic_Street_Preachers" title="Manic Street Preachers">Manic Street Preachers</a> song, "Of Walking Abortion", from the album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Holy_Bible_(album)" title="The Holy Bible (album)">The Holy Bible</a></i>, begins with a quote from Selby: "I knew that someday I was gonna die. And I knew that before I died, two things would happen to me, that number one: I would regret my entire life; and number two: I would want to live my life over again."</li>
<li>British band <a href="/wiki/Alt-J" title="Alt-J">Alt-J</a> composed a song entitled "Fitzpleasure", inspired by the short story "Tralala" from <i>Last Exit to Brooklyn</i>.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Winding_Refn" title="Nicolas Winding Refn">Nicolas Winding Refn</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Bleeder_(film)" title="Bleeder (film)">Bleeder</a></i>, a character enters a book store asking for a Hubert Selby Jr. work.</li>
<li>The block of East 10th Street between Second and Third Avenues in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> (where Selby lived in 1964 with his second wife, Judith, and her son, James) is mentioned at Chapter 23 of <a href="/wiki/Tom_Robbins" title="Tom Robbins">Tom Robbins</a>'s 1976 novel, <a href="/wiki/Even_Cowgirls_Get_the_Blues_(novel)" title="Even Cowgirls Get the Blues (novel)"><i>Even Cowgirls Get the Blues</i></a>, being described specifically as the place where "Hubert Selby, Jr., wrote <i>Last Exit to Brooklyn</i>."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
