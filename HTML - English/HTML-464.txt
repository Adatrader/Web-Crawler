<p>In <a href="/wiki/Phonetics" title="Phonetics">phonetics</a>, <b>ejective consonants</b> are usually <a href="/wiki/Voiceless" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless">voiceless</a> <a href="/wiki/Consonants" class="mw-redirect" title="Consonants">consonants</a> that are pronounced with a <a href="/wiki/Airstream_mechanism#Glottalic_initiation" title="Airstream mechanism">glottalic egressive airstream</a>. In the phonology of a particular language, ejectives may contrast with <a href="/wiki/Aspiration_(phonetics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Aspiration (phonetics)">aspirated</a>, voiced and <a href="/wiki/Tenuis_consonant" title="Tenuis consonant">tenuis consonants</a>. Some languages have glottalized <a href="/wiki/Sonorants" class="mw-redirect" title="Sonorants">sonorants</a> with <a href="/wiki/Creaky_voice" title="Creaky voice">creaky voice</a> that pattern with ejectives phonologically, and other languages have ejectives that pattern with <a href="/wiki/Implosive_consonant" title="Implosive consonant">implosives</a>, which has led to phonologists positing a phonological class of <a href="/wiki/Glottalic_consonant" title="Glottalic consonant">glottalic consonants</a>, which includes ejectives.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ejective_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In producing an ejective, the <a href="/wiki/Stylohyoid_muscle" title="Stylohyoid muscle">stylohyoid muscle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digastric_muscle" title="Digastric muscle">digastric muscle</a> contract, causing the <a href="/wiki/Hyoid_bone" title="Hyoid bone">hyoid bone</a> and the connected glottis to raise, and the forward articulation (at the velum in the case of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[kʼ]</span>) is held, raising air pressure greatly in the mouth so when the oral articulators separate, there is a dramatic burst of air.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Adam%27s_apple" title="Adam&#39;s apple">Adam's apple</a> may be seen moving when the sound is pronounced. In the languages in which they are more obvious, ejectives are often described as sounding like “spat” consonants, but ejectives are often quite weak. In some contexts and in some languages, they are easy to mistake for tenuis or even voiced stops.<sup id="cite_ref-Fallon_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fallon-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> These weakly ejective articulations are sometimes called <i>intermediates</i> in older American linguistic literature and are notated with different phonetic symbols: &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">C!</span>&#x27e9; = strongly ejective, &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">Cʼ</span>&#x27e9; = weakly ejective. Strong and weak ejectives have not been found to be contrastive in any natural language.
</p><p>In strict, technical terms, ejectives are <a href="/wiki/Airstream_mechanism#Glottalic_initiation" title="Airstream mechanism">glottalic egressive</a> consonants. The most common ejective is <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[kʼ]</span> even if it is more difficult to produce than other ejectives like <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[tʼ]</span> or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[pʼ]</span> because the auditory distinction between <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[kʼ]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[k]</span> is greater than with other ejectives and voiceless consonants of the same place of articulation.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In proportion to the frequency of <a href="/wiki/Uvular_consonant" title="Uvular consonant">uvular consonants</a>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[qʼ]</span> is even more common, as would be expected from the very small oral cavity used to pronounce a <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_uvular_stop" title="Voiceless uvular stop">voiceless uvular stop</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[pʼ]</span>, on the other hand, is quite rare. That is the opposite pattern to what is found in the <a href="/wiki/Implosive_consonant" title="Implosive consonant">implosive consonants</a>, in which the bilabial is common and the velar is rare.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ejective fricatives are rare for presumably the same reason: with the air escaping from the mouth while the pressure is being raised, like inflating a leaky bicycle tire, it is harder to distinguish the resulting sound as salient as a <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[kʼ]</span>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ejective_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ejectives occur in about 20% of the world's languages.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Ejectives that phonemically contrast with pulmonic consonants occur in about 15% of languages around the world. The occurrence of ejectives often correlates to languages in mountainous regions such as the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Cordillera" title="North American Cordillera">North American Cordillera</a> where ejectives are extremely common. They frequently occur throughout the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maya_Mountains" title="Maya Mountains">Maya Mountains</a>. They are also common in <a href="/wiki/East_African_Rift" title="East African Rift">East African Rift</a> and the South African Plateau, see <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Africa" title="Geography of Africa">Geography of Africa</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> they are extremely common in the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> which forms an island of ejective languages. Elsewhere, they are rare. <a href="#CITEREFEverett2013">Everett (2013)</a> argues that the geographic correlation is because of decreased air pressure making ejectives easier to produce, as well as the way ejectives help to reduce water vapor loss.
</p><p>Language families that distinguish ejective consonants include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ethiosemitic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethiosemitic">Ethiosemitic</a>&#160;: Languages such as  <a href="/wiki/Amharic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Amharic language">Amharic</a> and  <a href="/wiki/Tigrinya_language" title="Tigrinya language">Tigrinya</a></li>
<li>All three families of the Caucasus&#160;: the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Caucasian_languages" title="Northwest Caucasian languages">Northwest Caucasian languages</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Abkhaz_language" title="Abkhaz language">Abkhaz</a> and languages of the Cherkess family&#160;; the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Caucasian_languages" title="Northeast Caucasian languages">Northeast Caucasian languages</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Chechen_language" title="Chechen language">Chechen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avar_language" title="Avar language">Avar</a>&#160;; and the <a href="/wiki/Kartvelian_languages" title="Kartvelian languages">Kartvelian languages</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Georgian_language" title="Georgian language">Georgian</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Athabaskan_languages" title="Athabaskan languages">Athabaskan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siouan_languages" title="Siouan languages">Siouan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salishan_languages" title="Salishan languages">Salishan</a> families of North America along with the many diverse families of the Pacific Northwest from central <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> to <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">Mayan family</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aymaran_languages" title="Aymaran languages">Aymaran family</a></li>
<li>the southern varieties of <a href="/wiki/Quechua_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Quechua languages">Quechua</a> (<a href="/wiki/Qusqu-Qullaw" class="mw-redirect" title="Qusqu-Qullaw">Qusqu-Qullaw</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Puelche_language" title="Puelche language">Puelche</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tehuelche_language" title="Tehuelche language">Tehuelche</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Chonan_languages" title="Chonan languages">Chonan languages</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alacalufan_languages" title="Alacalufan languages">Alacalufan family</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cushitic" class="mw-redirect" title="Cushitic">Cushitic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Omotic" class="mw-redirect" title="Omotic">Omotic</a> languages, <a href="/wiki/Hausa_language" title="Hausa language">Hausa</a></li>
<li>a few <a href="/wiki/Nilo-Saharan_languages" title="Nilo-Saharan languages">Nilo-Saharan languages</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sandawe_language" title="Sandawe language">Sandawe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hadza_language" title="Hadza language">Hadza</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Khoisan_languages" title="Khoisan languages">Khoisan</a> families of southern Africa</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Itelmen_language" title="Itelmen language">Itelmen</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Chukotko-Kamchatkan_languages" title="Chukotko-Kamchatkan languages">Chukotko-Kamchatkan languages</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yapese_language" title="Yapese language">Yapese</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">Austronesian family</a></li></ul>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Glottalic_theory" title="Glottalic theory">glottalic theory</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European language</a> had a series of ejectives (or, in some versions, <a href="/wiki/Implosive_consonant" title="Implosive consonant">implosives</a>), but no extant Indo-European language has retained them<sup class="reference" id="ref_Sindhia"><a href="#endnote_Sindhia">[a]</a></sup>. Ejectives are found today in <a href="/wiki/Ossetian_language" title="Ossetian language">Ossetian</a> only because of influence of the nearby <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Caucasian_languages" title="Northeast Caucasian languages">Northeast Caucasian</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Kartvelian_languages" title="Kartvelian languages">Kartvelian language</a> families.
</p><p>It had once been predicted that ejectives and implosives would not be found in the same language<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> but both have been found phonemically at several points of articulation in <a href="/wiki/Nilo-Saharan_languages" title="Nilo-Saharan languages">Nilo-Saharan languages</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gumuz_language" title="Gumuz language">Gumuz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Me%27en_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Me&#39;en language">Me'en</a>, and <a href="/wiki/T%27wampa_language" class="mw-redirect" title="T&#39;wampa language">T'wampa</a>), <a href="/wiki/Mayan_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Mayan language">Mayan language</a> (<a href="/wiki/Yucatec_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Yucatec language">Yucatec</a>), <a href="/wiki/Salishan" class="mw-redirect" title="Salishan">Salishan</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lushootseed_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Lushootseed language">Lushootseed</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Oto-Manguean" class="mw-redirect" title="Oto-Manguean">Oto-Manguean</a> <a href="/wiki/Mazahua_language" title="Mazahua language">Mazahua</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nguni_languages" title="Nguni languages">Nguni languages</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Zulu_language" title="Zulu language">Zulu</a> have an implosive <i>b</i> alongside a series of allophonically ejective stops. <a href="/wiki/Dahalo_language" title="Dahalo language">Dahalo</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, has ejectives, implosives, and <a href="/wiki/Click_consonants" class="mw-redirect" title="Click consonants">click consonants</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ejective_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Almost all ejective consonants in the world's languages are <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">stops</a> or <a href="/wiki/Affricate" class="mw-redirect" title="Affricate">affricates</a>, and all ejective consonants are <a href="/wiki/Obstruent" title="Obstruent">obstruents</a>. <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[kʼ]</span> is the most common ejective, and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[qʼ]</span> is common among languages with <a href="/wiki/Uvular_consonant" title="Uvular consonant">uvulars</a>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[tʼ]</span> less so, and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[pʼ]</span> is uncommon. Among affricates, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[tsʼ], [tʃʼ], [tɬʼ]</span> are all quite common, and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[kxʼ]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ʈʂʼ]</span> are not unusual (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[kxʼ]</span> is particularly common among the <a href="/wiki/Khoisan_languages" title="Khoisan languages">Khoisan languages</a>, where it is the ejective equivalent of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/k/</span>).
</p>

<p>A few languages have ejective fricatives. In some dialects of <a href="/wiki/Hausa_language" title="Hausa language">Hausa</a>, the standard affricate <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[tsʼ]</span> is a fricative <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[sʼ]</span>; <a href="/wiki/Ubykh_language" title="Ubykh language">Ubykh</a> (Northwest Caucasian, now extinct) had an ejective lateral fricative <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɬʼ]</span>; and the related <a href="/wiki/Kabardian_language" title="Kabardian language">Kabardian</a> also has ejective labiodental and alveolopalatal fricatives, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[fʼ], [ʃʼ], and [ɬʼ]</span>. <a href="/wiki/Tlingit_language" title="Tlingit language">Tlingit</a> is an extreme case, with ejective alveolar, lateral, velar, and uvular fricatives, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[sʼ], [ɬʼ], [xʼ], [xʷʼ], [χʼ], [χʷʼ]</span>; it may be the only language with the last type. <a href="/wiki/Totonacan_languages" title="Totonacan languages">Upper Necaxa Totonac</a> is unusual and perhaps unique in that it has ejective fricatives (alveolar, lateral, and postalveolar <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[sʼ], [ʃʼ], [ɬʼ]</span>) but lacks any ejective stop or affricate (Beck 2006). Other languages with ejective fricatives are <a href="/wiki/Yuchi_language" title="Yuchi language">Yuchi</a>, which some sources analyze as having <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɸʼ], [sʼ], [ʃʼ], and [ɬʼ]</span> (but not the analysis of the Wikipedia article), <a href="/wiki/Keresan_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Keresan languages">Keres dialects</a>, with <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[sʼ], [ʂʼ] and [ɕʼ]</span>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Lakota_language" title="Lakota language">Lakota</a>, with <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[sʼ], [ʃʼ], and [xʼ]</span> .<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Amharic" title="Amharic">Amharic</a> is interpreted by many as having an ejective fricative <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[sʼ]</span>, at least historically, but it has been also analyzed as now being a sociolinguistic variant (Takkele Taddese 1992).
</p><p>An ejective retroflex stop <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ʈʼ]</span> is rare. It has been reported from <a href="/wiki/Yawelmani_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Yawelmani language">Yawelmani</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Yokutsan_languages" title="Yokutsan languages">Yokuts languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tolowa_language" title="Tolowa language">Tolowa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gwich%27in_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Gwich&#39;in language">Gwich'in</a>.
</p><p><span id="Voiced_ejective"></span>Because the complete closing of the glottis required to form an ejective makes voicing impossible, the allophonic voicing of ejective phonemes causes them to lose their glottalization; this occurs in <a href="/wiki/Blin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Blin language">Blin</a> (modal voice) and <a href="/wiki/Kabardian_language" title="Kabardian language">Kabardian</a> (creaky voice). A similar historical sound change also occurred in <a href="/wiki/Veinakh_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Veinakh languages">Veinakh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lezgic_languages" title="Lezgic languages">Lezgic</a> in the Caucasus, it and has been postulated by the <a href="/wiki/Glottalic_theory" title="Glottalic theory">glottalic theory</a> for Indo-European.<sup id="cite_ref-Fallon_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fallon-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Some <a href="/wiki/Khoisan_languages" title="Khoisan languages">Khoisan languages</a> have voiced ejective stops and <a href="/wiki/Ejective-contour_click" title="Ejective-contour click">voiced ejective clicks</a>; however, they actually contain <a href="/wiki/Contour_(linguistics)" title="Contour (linguistics)">mixed voicing</a>, and the ejective release is voiceless.
</p><p><span id="Ejective_trill">Ejective trill</span>s are rare, if they exist as distinct sounds at all. An ejective <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[rʼ]</span> would necessarily be voiceless,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> but the vibration of the trill, combined with a lack of the intense voiceless airflow of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[r̥]</span>, gives an impression like that of voicing. Similarly, ejective nasals such as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[mʼ, nʼ, ŋʼ]</span> (also necessarily voiceless) are possible.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> (An apostrophe is commonly seen with <i>r</i>, <i>l</i> and nasals, but that is <a href="/wiki/Americanist_phonetic_notation" title="Americanist phonetic notation">Americanist phonetic notation</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Glottalized_consonant" class="mw-redirect" title="Glottalized consonant">glottalized consonant</a> and does not indicate an ejective.)
</p><p>Other ejective <a href="/wiki/Sonorant" title="Sonorant">sonorants</a> are not known to occur. When sonorants are transcribed with an apostrophe in the literature as if they were ejective, they actually involve a different airstream mechanism: they are <a href="/wiki/Glottalic_consonant" title="Glottalic consonant">glottalized</a> consonants and vowels whose glottalization partially or fully interrupts an otherwise normal voiced pulmonic airstream, somewhat like English <i>uh-uh</i> (either vocalic or nasal) pronounced as a single sound. Often the constriction of the larynx causes it to rise in the vocal tract, but this is individual variation and not the initiator of the airflow. Such sounds generally remain voiced.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">Orthography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ejective_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Orthography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">International Phonetic Alphabet</a>, ejectives are indicated with a "modifier letter apostrophe" &#x27e8;<b>ʼ</b>&#x27e9;, as in this article. A reversed apostrophe is sometimes used to represent light aspiration, as in <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">Armenian</a> linguistics &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">p‘ t‘ k‘</span>&#x27e9;; this usage is obsolete in the IPA. In other transcription traditions, the apostrophe represents <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">palatalization</a>: &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">pʼ</span>&#x27e9; = IPA &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">pʲ</span>&#x27e9;. In some <a href="/wiki/Americanist_phonetic_notation" title="Americanist phonetic notation">Americanist traditions</a>, an apostrophe indicates weak ejection and an exclamation mark strong ejection: &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">k̓ , k!</span>&#x27e9;. In the IPA, the distinction might be written &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">kʼ, kʼʼ</span>&#x27e9;, but it seems that no language distinguishes degrees of ejection. Transcriptions of the Caucasian languages often utilize combining dots above or below a letter to indicate an ejective.
</p><p>In alphabets using the Latin script, an IPA-like apostrophe for ejective consonants is common. However, there are other conventions. In <a href="/wiki/Hausa_language" title="Hausa language">Hausa</a>, the hooked letter <a href="/wiki/%C6%98" title="Ƙ">ƙ</a> is used for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/kʼ/</span>. In <a href="/wiki/Zulu_language" title="Zulu language">Zulu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xhosa_language" title="Xhosa language">Xhosa</a>, whose ejection is variable between speakers, plain consonant letters are used: <i>p t k ts tsh kr</i> for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/pʼ tʼ kʼ tsʼ tʃʼ kxʼ/</span>. In some conventions for <a href="/wiki/Haida_language" title="Haida language">Haida</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hadza_language" title="Hadza language">Hadza</a>, double letters are used: <i>tt kk qq ttl tts</i> for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/tʼ kʼ qʼ tɬʼ tsʼ/</span> (Haida) and <i>zz jj dl gg</i> for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/tsʼ tʃʼ <span style="font-family:Gentium Plus, Charis SIL, Doulos SIL, serif">cʼ</span> kxʼ/</span> (Hadza).
</p>
