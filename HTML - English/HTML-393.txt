		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language" class="extiw" title="w:English language">English language</a></b> is a language that originated in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. It is one of the more widespread and most widely spoken <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">languages</a> in the world.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quotes">Quotes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=English_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Quotes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Now the use of English is really a reflection of chicness. Meaning is not always important.
<ul><li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Breen_(author)" class="extiw" title="w:Michael Breen (author)">Michael Breen</a>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=0V0ODQAAQBAJ&amp;pg=PA41&amp;lpg=PA41&amp;dq=%E2%80%9CI+came+here+expecting+the+Third+World+and+I+just+can%E2%80%99t+get+over+it.%E2%80%9D&amp;source=bl&amp;ots=WF5a7wO1Ip&amp;sig=ACfU3U2Q-_PdCoi13eWYEouHzPRxLkuLHA&amp;hl=en&amp;sa=X&amp;ved=2ahUKEwj1npnj74HjAhXSo1kKHXvPA9kQ6AEwAHoECAIQAQ#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false"><i>The New Koreans: The Story of a Nation</i></a> (2017), p. 21</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>The gift of a common tongue is a <a href="/wiki/Priceless" title="Priceless">priceless</a> inheritance and it may well some day become the foundation of a common citizenship.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>, speech at Harvard University, Cambridge, Massachusetts (September 6, 1943); in Robert Rhodes James, ed., <i>Winston S. Churchill: His Complete Speeches, 1897–1963</i> (1974), vol. 7, p. 6825.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek" class="mw-disambig" title="Star Trek">To boldly go</a> is rhythmically very neat. The <i>Star Trek</i> scriptwriter hasn't been linguistically bold at all.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/David_Crystal" title="David Crystal">David Crystal</a> in <i>Who Cares About English Usage</i></li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>Five generations ago, Britain was ashamed to write books in her own tongue. Now her language is spoken in all quarters of the globe.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Frederick_Douglass" title="Frederick Douglass">Frederick Douglass</a>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://teachingamericanhistory.org/library/document/self-made-men/">"Self-Made Men"</a> (1872)</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>The English-speaking world may be divided into (1) those who neither know nor care what a split infinitive is; (2) those who do not know, but care very much; (3) those who know and condemn; (4) those who know and approve; and (5) those who know and distinguish....
<ul><li><i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fowler%27s_Modern_English_Usage" class="extiw" title="wikipedia:Fowler&#39;s Modern English Usage">Fowler's Modern English Usage</a></i>, second edition, p. 579.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>[A speaker who uses a multiple negative] spreads as it were a thin layer of negative colouring over the whole sentence instead of confining it to a single place.
<ul><li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_Jespersen" class="extiw" title="wikipedia:Otto Jespersen">Otto Jespersen</a>, quoted in <i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fowler%27s_Modern_English_Usage" class="extiw" title="wikipedia:Fowler&#39;s Modern English Usage">Fowler's Modern English Usage</a></i>, p 384</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>The name ["split infinitive"] is misleading, for the preposition <i>to</i> no more belongs to the infinitive as a necessary part of it, than the definite article belongs to the substantive, and no one would think of calling "the good man" a split substantive.
<ul><li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_Jespersen" class="extiw" title="w:Otto Jespersen">Otto Jespersen</a>, referring to split infinitives, in <i>Essentials of English Grammar</i></li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>I say looking at the next 100 years that there are two trends in the world today.  The first trend is toward what we call a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kardashev_scale" class="extiw" title="w:Kardashev scale">type one civilization</a>, a planetary civilization... What is English?  English is the beginning of a type one language.  Everywhere I go around the Earth, people speak English because that is the lingua franca of science, technology, business.  They all speak English.  It is the number one second language on the planet Earth.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Michio_Kaku" title="Michio Kaku">Michio Kaku</a>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://bigthink.com/videos/will-mankind-destroy-itself">Michio Kaku. "Will Mankind Destroy Itself?"</a>.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>The problem with defending the purity of the English language is that English is about as pure as a cribhouse whore. We don't just borrow words; on occasion, English has pursued other languages down alleyways to beat them unconscious and rifle their pockets for new vocabulary.
<ul><li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_D._Nicoll" class="extiw" title="w:James D. Nicoll">James D. Nicoll</a>&#32;(<a href="/w/index.php?title=1990-05-15&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="1990-05-15 (page does not exist)">1990-05-15</a>).&#32;"<i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="news://1990May15.155309.8892@watdragon.waterloo.edu">The King's English</a></i>".&#32;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="news://rec.arts.sf-lovers">rec.arts.sf-lovers</a>.&#32;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://groups.google.com/groups?selm=1990May15.155309.8892@watdragon.waterloo.edu">(Google Groups)</a>.&#32;</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>Fussing about split infinitives is one of the more tiresome pastimes invented by nineteenth-century grammarians.
<ul><li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbara_Strang" class="extiw" title="w:Barbara Strang">Barbara Strang</a> in <i>Modern English Structure</i>.</li></ul></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_vs._American_English">British vs. American English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=English_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: British vs. American English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>It is a misfortune for Anglo-American friendship that the two countries are supposed to have a common language. A Frenchman in America is not expected to talk like an American, but an Englishman speaking his mother tongue is thought to be affected and giving himself airs. Or else he is taken for a German or a Dutchman, and is complimented on his grammatical mastery of the language of another nation.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Russell" title="Bertrand Russell">Bertrand Russell</a>, "Can Americans and Britons Be Friends?", <i>Saturday Evening Post</i>, 3 June 1944.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>The United States and Great Britain are two countries separated by a common language.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/George_Bernard_Shaw" title="George Bernard Shaw">George Bernard Shaw</a>, widely attributed beginning in the 1940s, e.g. <i>Reader’s Digest</i> (November 1942). Not found in his published works.</li>
<li><i>Variant</i>:  The English and the Americans are two peoples divided by a common language.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>We have really everything in common with America nowadays, except, of course, language.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Oscar_Wilde" title="Oscar Wilde">Oscar Wilde</a>, <i>The Canterville Ghost</i> (1887).</li></ul></li></ul>
