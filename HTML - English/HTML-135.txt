<p><b>Warner Bros. Television</b> (<b>WBTV</b>) is an American television <a href="/wiki/Production_company" title="Production company">production</a> arm of <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." class="mw-redirect" title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros. Entertainment</a>, itself part of <a href="/wiki/WarnerMedia" title="WarnerMedia">WarnerMedia</a>. Alongside <a href="/wiki/CBS_Television_Studios" title="CBS Television Studios">CBS Television Studios</a>, it serves as a television production arm of <a href="/wiki/The_CW" title="The CW">The CW</a> (in which WarnerMedia has a 50% ownership stake), though it also produces shows for other networks, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Shameless_(2011_TV_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Shameless (2011 TV series)">Shameless</a></i> on <a href="/wiki/Showtime_(TV_network)" title="Showtime (TV network)">Showtime</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Westworld_(TV_series)" title="Westworld (TV series)">Westworld</a></i> on <a href="/wiki/Home_Box_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="Home Box Office">HBO</a> (though WarnerMedia also owns HBO). As of 2015, it is one of the world's two largest television production companies measured by revenue and library (along with <a href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures_Television" title="Sony Pictures Television">Sony Pictures Television</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_production">History and production</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginning_and_saturation">Beginning and saturation</span></h3>

href="/wiki/Lawman_(TV_series)" title="Lawman (TV series)">Lawman</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kelly_(actor)" title="Jack Kelly (actor)">Jack Kelly</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Maverick_(TV_series)" title="Maverick (TV series)">Maverick</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Ty_Hardin" title="Ty Hardin">Ty Hardin</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Bronco_(TV_series)" title="Bronco (TV series)">Bronco</a></i>), <a <p>The division was started on March 21, 1955, <sup id="cite_ref-broadcasting19550321_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-broadcasting19550321-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> with its first and most successful head being <a href="/wiki/Jack_L._Warner" title="Jack L. Warner">Jack L. Warner</a>'s son-in-law <a href="/wiki/William_T._Orr" title="William T. Orr">William T. Orr</a>. <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> had major success against its competition with <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney" title="Walt Disney">Walt Disney</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_anthology_television_series" title="Walt Disney anthology television series"><i>Disneyland</i> TV series</a> and approached <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." class="mw-redirect" title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a> initially with the idea of purchasing the studio's film library (WB eventually sold the rights to the negatives of pre-1950 films and pre-1948 cartoons and shorts to <a href="/wiki/Associated_Artists_Productions" title="Associated Artists Productions">Associated Artists Productions</a>, or a.a.p., in 1956<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>). WB formally entered television production with the premiere of its self-titled anthology series <i><a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Presents" title="Warner Bros. Presents">Warner Bros. Presents</a></i> on ABC. The one-hour weekly show featured rotating episodes of television series based on the WB films, <i><a href="/wiki/Casablanca_(film)" title="Casablanca (film)">Casablanca</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Kings_Row" title="Kings Row">Kings Row</a></i>, as well as an original series titled <i><a href="/wiki/Cheyenne_(1955_TV_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cheyenne (1955 TV series)">Cheyenne</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Clint_Walker" title="Clint Walker">Clint Walker</a>. The first one-hour television western, <i>Cheyenne</i> became a big hit for the network and the studio with the added advantage of featuring promotions for upcoming Warner Bros. cinema releases in the show's last ten minutes. One such segment for <i><a href="/wiki/Rebel_Without_a_Cause" title="Rebel Without a Cause">Rebel Without a Cause</a></i> featured <a href="/wiki/Gig_Young" title="Gig Young">Gig Young</a> notably talking about road safety with <a href="/wiki/James_Dean" title="James Dean">James Dean</a>.
</p><p>With only <i>Cheyenne</i> being a success, WB ended the ten-minute promotions of new films and replaced <i>Warner Bros. Presents</i> with an <a href="/wiki/Anthology_series" title="Anthology series">anthology series</a> titled <i><a href="/wiki/Conflict_(TV_series)" title="Conflict (TV series)">Conflict</a></i>.  It was felt that "Conflict" was what the previous series lacked.  <i>Conflict</i> showed the pilots for <i><a href="/wiki/Maverick_(TV_series)" title="Maverick (TV series)">Maverick</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/77_Sunset_Strip" title="77 Sunset Strip">77 Sunset Strip</a></i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>.
</p><p>The success of <i>Cheyenne</i> led WBTV to produce many series for ABC such as Westerns <i>(<a href="/wiki/Maverick_(TV_series)" title="Maverick (TV series)">Maverick</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lawman_(TV_series)" title="Lawman (TV series)">Lawman</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Colt_.45_(TV_series)" title="Colt .45 (TV series)">Colt .45</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bronco_(TV_series)" title="Bronco (TV series)">Bronco</a>,</i> a <a href="/wiki/Spin-off_(media)" title="Spin-off (media)">spin off</a> of <i>Cheyenne</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sugarfoot" title="Sugarfoot">Sugarfoot</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Alaskans" title="The Alaskans">The Alaskans</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Crime_drama" class="mw-redirect" title="Crime drama">crime dramas</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/77_Sunset_Strip" title="77 Sunset Strip">77 Sunset Strip</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Eye" title="Hawaiian Eye">Hawaiian Eye</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bourbon_Street_Beat" title="Bourbon Street Beat">Bourbon Street Beat</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Surfside_6" title="Surfside 6">Surfside 6</a></i>), and other shows such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Gallant_Men" title="The Gallant Men">The Gallant Men</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Roaring_Twenties" title="The Roaring Twenties">The Roaring Twenties</a></i> using stock footage from WB <a href="/wiki/War_film" title="War film">war films</a> and gangster films respectively. The company also produced <a href="/wiki/Jack_Webb" title="Jack Webb">Jack Webb</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Nightmare" title="Red Nightmare">Red Nightmare</a></i> for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">U.S. Department of Defense</a> that was later shown on American television on <a href="/wiki/Jack_Webb" title="Jack Webb">Jack Webb</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/GE_True" title="GE True">General Electric True</a></i>.
</p><p>All shows were made in the manner of WB's <a href="/wiki/B_pictures" class="mw-redirect" title="B pictures">B pictures</a> in the 1930s and 1940s;<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> fast-paced, lots of <a href="/wiki/Stock_footage" title="Stock footage">stock footage</a> from other films, stock music from the Warners music library and contracted stars working long hours for comparatively small salaries with restrictions on their career.
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/1960_Writers_Guild_of_America_strike" title="1960 Writers Guild of America strike">1960 Writers Guild of America strike</a>, WB reused many plots from its films and other television shows under the nom de plume of "W. Hermanos".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This was another example of imitating Warner Bros' B Pictures who would remake an "A" film and switch the setting.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:James_Garner_Jack_Kelly_Maverick_1959.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/James_Garner_Jack_Kelly_Maverick_1959.JPG/220px-James_Garner_Jack_Kelly_Maverick_1959.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="276" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/James_Garner_Jack_Kelly_Maverick_1959.JPG/330px-James_Garner_Jack_Kelly_Maverick_1959.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/James_Garner_Jack_Kelly_Maverick_1959.JPG/440px-James_Garner_Jack_Kelly_Maverick_1959.JPG 2x" data-file-width="767" data-file-height="963" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:James_Garner_Jack_Kelly_Maverick_1959.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/James_Garner" title="James Garner">James Garner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kelly_(actor)" title="Jack Kelly (actor)">Jack Kelly</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Maverick_(TV_series)" title="Maverick (TV series)">Maverick</a></i>, 1959</div></div></div>
<p>Two of the most popular stars, <a href="/wiki/James_Garner" title="James Garner">James Garner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clint_Walker" title="Clint Walker">Clint Walker</a>, quit over their conditions. Garner never returned to the Warner's fold during this period. Successful Warner's television stars found themselves in leading roles of many of the studio's films with no increase in salary. <a href="/wiki/Efrem_Zimbalist,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Efrem Zimbalist, Jr.">Efrem Zimbalist, Jr.</a> was simultaneously the lead of <i>77 Sunset Strip</i>, in a recurring role on <i>Maverick</i>, and also headlined several films until exhaustion forced the studio to give him a rest. Many other actors under contract to Warner's at the time, who despite their work conditions, did see their stars rise over time, albeit for most only briefly, included <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kelly_(actor)" title="Jack Kelly (actor)">Jack Kelly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Will_Hutchins" title="Will Hutchins">Will Hutchins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Brown_(actor)" title="Peter Brown (actor)">Peter Brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ty_Hardin" title="Ty Hardin">Ty Hardin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wayde_Preston" title="Wayde Preston">Wayde Preston</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Russell_(actor)" title="John Russell (actor)">John Russell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donald_May" title="Donald May">Donald May</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rex_Reason" title="Rex Reason">Rex Reason</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Long_(actor)" title="Richard Long (actor)">Richard Long</a>, <a href="/wiki/Van_Williams" title="Van Williams">Van Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Smith_(actor)" title="Roger Smith (actor)">Roger Smith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Road" title="Mike Road">Mike Road</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Eisley" title="Anthony Eisley">Anthony Eisley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Conrad" title="Robert Conrad">Robert Conrad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_McQueeney" title="Robert McQueeney">Robert McQueeney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Provine" title="Dorothy Provine">Dorothy Provine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diane_McBain" title="Diane McBain">Diane McBain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Connie_Stevens" title="Connie Stevens">Connie Stevens</a>, who had recorded songs, "Kookie, Kookie (Lend Me Your Comb)" with <a href="/wiki/Edd_Byrnes" title="Edd Byrnes">Edd Byrnes</a> in 1959; and "16 Reasons" in 1960; both would become Top-5 hits. Burns and <a href="/wiki/Troy_Donahue" title="Troy Donahue">Troy Donahue</a> would become teen heartthrobs. Another contract player, Englishman <a href="/wiki/Roger_Moore" title="Roger Moore">Roger Moore</a> (<i>Maverick</i> and <i>The Alaskans</i>), was growing displeased with Warner as his contract was expiring and would relocate to Europe from Hollywood, becoming an international star on TV, and eventually, in films. Warners also contracted established stars such as <a href="/wiki/Ray_Danton" title="Ray Danton">Ray Danton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Breck" title="Peter Breck">Peter Breck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeanne_Cooper" title="Jeanne Cooper">Jeanne Cooper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grant_Williams_(actor)" title="Grant Williams (actor)">Grant Williams</a>.  These stars often appeared as guest stars, sometimes reprising their series role in another TV series.
</p><p>The stars appeared in WB cinema releases with no additional salary, with some such as Zimbalist, Walker, Garner (replacing <a href="/wiki/Charlton_Heston" title="Charlton Heston">Charlton Heston</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Darby%27s_Rangers_(film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Darby&#39;s Rangers (film)">Darby's Rangers</a></i>), and Danton (replacing <a href="/wiki/Robert_Evans_(producer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Evans (producer)">Robert Evans</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rise_and_Fall_of_Legs_Diamond" title="The Rise and Fall of Legs Diamond">The Rise and Fall of Legs Diamond</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>) playing the lead roles; many of the stars appeared in ensemble casts in such films as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chapman_Report" title="The Chapman Report">The Chapman Report</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Merrill%27s_Marauders_(film)" title="Merrill&#39;s Marauders (film)">Merill's Marauders</a></i>. Some stars such as Connie Stevens, Edd Byrnes, Robert Conrad and Roger Smith made albums for <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Warner Bros. Records">Warner Bros. Records</a>.   One particular recording, a novelty tune titled <i><a href="/wiki/Kookie,_Kookie_(Lend_Me_Your_Comb)" title="Kookie, Kookie (Lend Me Your Comb)">Kookie, Kookie (Lend Me Your Comb)</a></i> became a big hit for Edd Byrnes and Connie Stevens (1959). The following year, Connie Stevens had her own hit, with <i><a href="/wiki/Sixteen_Reasons" title="Sixteen Reasons">Sixteen Reasons</a></i>.
</p><p>It was during this period, that shows, particularly Westerns like <i>Cheyenne</i> and <i>Maverick</i>; and the crime dramas like <i><a href="/wiki/77_Sunset_Strip" title="77 Sunset Strip">77 Sunset Strip</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Eye" title="Hawaiian Eye">Hawaiian Eye</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Surfside_6" title="Surfside 6">Surfside 6</a></i> featured catchy theme songs, that became just as much a part of the American pop culture landscape, as the shows themselves. Depending on the particular show (in this case, the Westerns), <a href="/wiki/William_Lava" title="William Lava">William Lava</a> or <a href="/wiki/David_Buttolph" title="David Buttolph">David Buttolph</a> would compose the music, with lyrics by <a href="/wiki/Stan_Jones_(songwriter)" title="Stan Jones (songwriter)">Stan Jones</a> or <a href="/wiki/Paul_Francis_Webster" title="Paul Francis Webster">Paul Francis Webster</a>, among others. For the crime shows, it was up to the songwriting team of <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Livingston" title="Jerry Livingston">Jerry Livingston</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mack_David" title="Mack David">Mack David</a>, who also scored the themes for the sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Room_for_One_More_(TV_series)" title="Room for One More (TV series)">Room for One More</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bugs_Bunny_Show" title="The Bugs Bunny Show">The Bugs Bunny Show</a></i>.
</p><p>In 1960, WBTV turned its attentions to the younger viewer, for one program, anyway, as they brought <a href="/wiki/Bugs_Bunny" title="Bugs Bunny">Bugs Bunny</a> and the other WB cartoon characters to prime time, with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bugs_Bunny_Show" title="The Bugs Bunny Show">The Bugs Bunny Show</a></i>, which featured cartoons released after July 31, 1948 (which had not been sold to <a href="/wiki/Associated_Artists_Productions" title="Associated Artists Productions">Associated Artists Productions</a>.), combined with newly animated introductory material. Also, that year saw the debut of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Roaring_Twenties" title="The Roaring Twenties">The Roaring Twenties</a></i> (which was thought to be a more benign alternative to <a href="/wiki/Desilu" class="mw-redirect" title="Desilu">Desilu</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Untouchables_(1959_TV_series)" title="The Untouchables (1959 TV series)">The Untouchables</a></i>. Whether or not that was the actual case, it was, in fact, much less successful). 
</p><p>WBTV expanded on its existing genre of Westerns and crime dramas, and in January 1962, produced its first sitcom, <i><a href="/wiki/Room_for_One_More_(TV_series)" title="Room for One More (TV series)">Room For One More</a></i>. Based on the memoirs of <a href="/wiki/Anna_Rose" title="Anna Rose">Anna Rose</a>, which in 1952 WB made into a movie starring <a href="/wiki/Cary_Grant" title="Cary Grant">Cary Grant</a> and his then-wife <a href="/wiki/Betsy_Drake" title="Betsy Drake">Betsy Drake</a> (the only movie that they worked together in) about a married couple with two children of their own, who went on to adopt at least two more, the TV series starred <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Duggan" title="Andrew Duggan">Andrew Duggan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peggy_McCay" title="Peggy McCay">Peggy McCay</a> as George and Anna Rose. Acting legend <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Rooney" title="Mickey Rooney">Mickey Rooney</a>'s son <a href="/wiki/Tim_Rooney" title="Tim Rooney">Tim</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ahna_Capri" title="Ahna Capri">Ahna Capri</a>, who would continue to do episodic TV roles and feature films (arguably, her best-known movie was <i><a href="/wiki/Enter_the_Dragon" title="Enter the Dragon">Enter the Dragon</a></i> starring <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Lee" title="Bruce Lee">Bruce Lee</a>) were cast as the Rose's natural children. The show only lasted for half a season. In the fall of that year, a WWII drama <i><a href="/wiki/The_Gallant_Men" title="The Gallant Men">The Gallant Men</a></i> debuted, but lasted for only one season.
</p><p>WBTV exclusively produced shows for the ABC network until 1962, when <i><a href="/wiki/GE_True" title="GE True">GE True</a></i> premiered on <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a>.
</p><p>In 1964, WBTV once again tried to turn a classic film comedy of its own into a sitcom, with <i><a href="/wiki/No_Time_for_Sergeants" title="No Time for Sergeants">No Time for Sergeants</a></i>. Both the sitcom and the 1958 movie were based on the 1955 Broadway play, which starred <a href="/wiki/Andy_Griffith" title="Andy Griffith">Andy Griffith</a> (TV's <i><a href="/wiki/U.S._Steel_Hour" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Steel Hour">U.S. Steel Hour</a></i> also adapted the stage play for TV in 1956). The sitcom starred <a href="/wiki/Sammy_Jackson" title="Sammy Jackson">Sammy Jackson</a> as Will Stockdale, a naive Georgia farm boy drafted into the military. 1965 saw the debut of <i><a href="/wiki/F-Troop" class="mw-redirect" title="F-Troop">F-Troop</a></i>, a Western spoof taking place at a U.S. Army post after the Civil War. Despite lasting two seasons, it is still considered a classic. <a href="/wiki/Forrest_Tucker" title="Forrest Tucker">Forrest Tucker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Larry_Storch" title="Larry Storch">Larry Storch</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ken_Berry" title="Ken Berry">Ken Berry</a> led an ensemble cast featuring military misfits, and an Indian tribe, who, among other things, forgot how to do a rain dance.
</p><p>The streak of identifiable series subsided in 1963 with a halt of using <a href="/wiki/Stock_company_(acting)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stock company (acting)">stock company (acting)</a> contract players and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Webb" title="Jack Webb">Jack Webb</a> taking over WBTV and not being particularly successful. However, many series were still filmed at Warner Bros. such as <i><a href="/wiki/F-Troop" class="mw-redirect" title="F-Troop">F-Troop</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_F.B.I._(TV_series)" title="The F.B.I. (TV series)">The F.B.I.</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span></h3>
<p>In 1976, the company acquired The Wolper Organization, most notably for <i><a href="/wiki/Chico_and_the_Man" title="Chico and the Man">Chico and the Man</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Welcome_Back,_Kotter" title="Welcome Back, Kotter">Welcome Back, Kotter</a></i>. In 1989, it acquired <a href="/wiki/Lorimar-Telepictures" title="Lorimar-Telepictures">Lorimar-Telepictures</a>. <a href="/wiki/Telepictures" title="Telepictures">Telepictures</a> was later folded into WBTV's distribution unit, and in 1990, came back as a television production company. In 1993, <a href="/wiki/Lorimar_Television" title="Lorimar Television">Lorimar Television</a> was folded into WBTV.
</p><p>In 2006, WBTV made its vast library of programs available for free viewing on the Internet (through sister company <a href="/wiki/America_Online" class="mw-redirect" title="America Online">AOL</a>'s <a href="/wiki/IN2TV" class="mw-redirect" title="IN2TV">IN2TV</a> service), with <i>Welcome Back, Kotter</i> as its marquee offering. Some of these programs have not been seen publicly since their last syndicated release in the 1980s.
</p><p>WBTV has had a number of affiliated production houses that have co-produced many of their shows with WBTV. These include but are not limited to: <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Edwards" title="Ralph Edwards">Ralph Edwards-Stu Billett Productions</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/The_People%27s_Court" title="The People&#39;s Television</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Jamie_Kennedy_Experiment" title="The Jamie Kennedy Experiment">The Jamie Kennedy Experiment</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Telepictures" title="Telepictures">AND Syndicated Productions</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Judge_Mathis" title="Judge Mathis">Judge Mathis</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Helford" title="Bruce Helford">Bruce Helford</a>'s <a O'Bannon Television</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miller-Boyett_Productions" title="Miller-Boyett Productions">Miller-Boyett Productions</a> – which was inherited from Lorimar (<i><a href="/wiki/Full_House" title="Full House">Full House</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Family_Matters" title="Family Matters">Family Matters</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Greg_Berlanti" title="Greg Berlanti">Berlanti Productions</a> (<i>Arrowverse</i>) and in 2010, <a href="/wiki/Conan_O%27Brien" title="Conan O&#39;Brien">Conan O'Brien</a>'s production company <a href="/wiki/Conaco" title="Conaco">Conaco</a> switched its affiliation to WBTV from <a href="/wiki/Universal_Television" title="Universal Television">Universal Media Studios</a>, coinciding with O'Brien's move to his new talk show, <i><a href="/wiki/Conan_(talk_show)" title="Conan (talk show)">Conan</a></i> at Time Warner-owned <a href="/wiki/TBS_(TV_network)" class="mw-redirect" title="TBS (TV network)">TBS</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In August 2009 in Australia, The Nine Network and WBTV launched digital free-to-air channel GO! with WBTV holding a 33% stake in the new joint venture with Sony Pictures (titles were later picked up by rival <a href="/wiki/Seven_Network" title="Seven Network">Seven</a> in 2011). During that, the network signed 4 more years with the output between 2011 and 2015.
</p><p>On June 11, 2012, WBTV acquired <a href="/wiki/Alloy_Entertainment" title="Alloy Entertainment">Alloy Entertainment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> On June 2, 2014, Warner Bros. Television Group purchased all of <a href="/wiki/Eyeworks" title="Eyeworks">Eyeworks</a>' companies outside of the United States, rebranding as <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._International_Television_Production" title="Warner Bros. International Television Production">Warner Bros. International Television Production</a>. Eyeworks USA however, will remain independent.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
