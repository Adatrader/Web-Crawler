<p><b>Ian Arthur Bremmer</b> (born November 12, 1969) is an American <a href="/wiki/Political_scientist" class="mw-redirect" title="Political scientist">political scientist</a> specializing in U.S. foreign policy, states in transition, and global <a href="/wiki/Political_risk" title="Political risk">political risk</a>. He is the president and founder of <a href="/wiki/Eurasia_Group" title="Eurasia Group">Eurasia Group</a>, a political risk research and consulting firm with principal offices in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and offices worldwide.
</p><p>Bremmer is also the founder of GZERO Media, where he hosts the television show <i>GZERO WORLD with Ian Bremmer</i>. He is the author of several books, including  <i>The J Curve,</i> <i>Every Nation for Itself,</i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> bestselling <i>Us vs. Them: The Failure of Globalism</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> As of December 2014, he is foreign affairs columnist and editor-at-large at <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)"><i>Time</i></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> I
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_Bremmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bremmer is of <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Americans" title="Armenian Americans">Armenian</a> and <a href="/wiki/German_Americans" title="German Americans">German</a> descent. His father, Arthur, served in the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> and died at the age of 46 when Bremmer was four.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He grew up in housing projects in <a href="/wiki/Chelsea,_Massachusetts" title="Chelsea, Massachusetts">Chelsea, Massachusetts</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Bremmer went to <a href="/wiki/Savio_Preparatory_High_School" title="Savio Preparatory High School">St. Dominic Savio High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_Boston" title="East Boston">East Boston</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He later earned a BA in international relations, <i><a href="/wiki/Magna_cum_laude" class="mw-redirect" title="Magna cum laude">magna cum laude</a></i>, from <a href="/wiki/Tulane_University" title="Tulane University">Tulane University</a> in 1989 and a PhD in political science from Stanford University in 1994, writing "The politics of ethnicity: Russians in the Ukraine".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_Bremmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eurasia_Group">Eurasia Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_Bremmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Eurasia Group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bremmer founded the political risk research and consulting firm <a href="/wiki/Eurasia_Group" title="Eurasia Group">Eurasia Group</a> in 1998, starting with a staff of one based out of a cubicle in the World Policy Institute in New York City<sup id="cite_ref-Politico2018-02-03_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Politico2018-02-03-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The firm opened a London office in 2000, a Washington, DC office in 2005, a Tokyo office in 2015, San Francisco and São Paulo offices in 2016, and Brasilia and Singapore offices in 2017.
</p><p>In 2001, Bremmer and Eurasia created Wall Street's first global political risk index, now the GPRI (Global Political Risk Index), and in 2004,  expanded the coverage to include frontier markets. Bremmer launched Eurasia Group’s annual Top Risks report in 2006, and also created a Global Energy and Natural Resources Strategy group.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GZERO_Media">GZERO Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_Bremmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: GZERO Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2017, Bremmer founded GZERO Media, a media company. It is a subsidiary of Eurasia Group. Its name refers to the term “<a href="/wiki/G-Zero_world" title="G-Zero world">G-Zero</a>”, which was coined by Bremmer to describe a world where no country or group of countries has the political and economic leverage to drive an international agenda or provide global public goods.
</p><p>Bremmer hosts the weekly digital and US national public television show, GZERO World with Ian Bremmer. Bremmer also hosts a weekly digital video series called The World in 60 Seconds.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_Bremmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bremmer has published ten books, including the national bestsellers <i><a href="/wiki/Every_Nation_for_Itself:_Winners_and_Losers_in_a_G-Zero_World" class="mw-redirect" title="Every Nation for Itself: Winners and Losers in a G-Zero World">Every Nation for Itself: Winners and Losers in a G-Zero World</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Portfolio_Hardcover" class="mw-redirect" title="Portfolio Hardcover">Portfolio</a>, May 2012), which details risks and opportunities in a world without global leadership, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_End_of_the_Free_Market:_Who_Wins_the_War_Between_States_and_Corporations" class="mw-redirect" title="The End of the Free Market: Who Wins the War Between States and Corporations">The End of the Free Market: Who Wins the War Between States and Corporations</a></i> (Portfolio, May 2010), which describes the global phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/State_capitalism" title="State capitalism">state capitalism</a> and its implications for economics and politics. He also wrote <i><a href="/wiki/The_J_Curve:_A_New_Way_to_Understand_Why_Nations_Rise_and_Fall" class="mw-redirect" title="The J Curve: A New Way to Understand Why Nations Rise and Fall">The J Curve: A New Way to Understand Why Nations Rise and Fall</a></i> (Simon &amp; Schuster, 2006), selected by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> as one of the best books of 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> His latest book is the New York Times bestseller <i>Us vs Them: The Failure of Globalism</i> (Portfolio, April 2018), which provides an analysis of the global implications of rising populist nationalism and government responses.
</p><p>In addition to his books, Bremmer is the foreign affairs columnist and editor-at-large for <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i>, a contributor for the <i><a href="/wiki/Financial_Times" title="Financial Times">Financial Times</a></i> A-List,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and has published articles in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_Review" title="Harvard Business Review">Harvard Business Review</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Foreign_Affairs" title="Foreign Affairs">Foreign Affairs</a>,</i> and other publications.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="J-Curve">J-Curve</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_Bremmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: J-Curve">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_J_Curve:_A_New_Way_to_Understand_Why_Nations_Rise_and_Fall" class="mw-redirect" title="The J Curve: A New Way to Understand Why Nations Rise and Fall">The J Curve: A New Way to Understand Why Nations Rise and Fall</a></div>
<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:The-J-Curve_blanksm.jpg" class="image"><img alt="The-J-Curve blanksm.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/1/15/The-J-Curve_blanksm.jpg" decoding="async" width="314" height="232" data-file-width="314" data-file-height="232" /></a></div>
<p>Bremmer's <a href="/wiki/The_J_Curve:_A_New_Way_to_Understand_Why_Nations_Rise_and_Fall" class="mw-redirect" title="The J Curve: A New Way to Understand Why Nations Rise and Fall">J curve</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> outlines the link between a country's openness and its stability. While many countries are stable because they are open (the United States, France, Japan), others are stable because they are closed (<a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> under <a href="/wiki/Saddam_Hussein" title="Saddam Hussein">Saddam Hussein</a>). States can travel both forward (right) and backward (left) along this J curve, so stability and openness are never secure. The J is steeper on the left-hand side, as it is easier for a leader in a <a href="/wiki/Failed_state" title="Failed state">failed state</a> to create stability by closing the country than to build a civil society and establish accountable institutions; the curve is higher on the far right because states that prevail in opening their societies (Eastern Europe, for example) ultimately become more stable than <a href="/wiki/Authoritarianism" title="Authoritarianism">authoritarian regimes</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="State_capitalism">State capitalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_Bremmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: State capitalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ian Bremmer describes <a href="/wiki/State_capitalism" title="State capitalism">state capitalism</a> as a system in which <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">the state</a> dominates markets primarily for political gain. In his 2010 book <i><a href="/wiki/The_End_of_the_Free_Market:_Who_Wins_the_War_Between_States_and_Corporations" class="mw-redirect" title="The End of the Free Market: Who Wins the War Between States and Corporations">The End of the Free Market: Who Wins the War Between States and Corporations</a></i>  ), Bremmer describes China as the primary driver for the rise of state capitalism as a challenge to the <a href="/wiki/Market_economy" title="Market economy">free market economies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">the developed world</a>, particularly in the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis" title="Financial crisis">financial crisis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="G-Zero">G-Zero</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_Bremmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: G-Zero">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/G-Zero_world" title="G-Zero world">G-Zero world</a></div>
<p>The term <a href="/wiki/G-Zero_world" title="G-Zero world">G-Zero world</a> refers to a breakdown in global leadership brought about by a decline of Western influence and the inability of other nations to fill the void.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> It is a reference to a perceived shift away from the pre-eminence of the ["G7"] ("<a href="/wiki/G7" class="mw-redirect" title="G7">Group of Seven</a>") industrialized countries and the expanded <a href="/wiki/G-20_major_economies" class="mw-redirect" title="G-20 major economies">Group of Twenty</a>, which includes major emerging powers like China, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, and others. In his book, <i><a href="/wiki/Every_Nation_for_Itself:_Winners_and_Losers_in_a_G-Zero_World" class="mw-redirect" title="Every Nation for Itself: Winners and Losers in a G-Zero World">Every Nation for Itself: Winners and Losers in a G-Zero World</a></i> (New York: Portfolio, 2012), Bremmer explains that, in the G-Zero, no country or group of countries has the political and economic leverage to drive an international agenda or provide <a href="/wiki/Public_good_(economics)" title="Public good (economics)">global public goods</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weaponization_of_finance">Weaponization of finance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_Bremmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Weaponization of finance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Weaponization_of_finance" title="Weaponization of finance">Weaponization of finance</a></div>
<p>The term <i>weaponization of finance</i> refers to the foreign policy strategy of using incentives (access to capital markets) and penalties (varied types of sanctions) as tools of coercive diplomacy. In his <a href="/wiki/Eurasia_Group" title="Eurasia Group">Eurasia Group</a> Top Risks 2015 report,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Bremmer coins the term weaponization of finance to describe the ways in which the United States is using its influence to affect global outcomes. Rather than rely on traditional elements of America's security advantage – including US-led alliances such as <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> and multi-lateral institutions such as the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a> – Bremmer argues that the US is now 'weaponizing finance' by limiting access to the American marketplace and to US banks as an instrument of its foreign and security policy.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pivot_state">Pivot state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_Bremmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Pivot state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bremmer uses <i>pivot state</i> to describe a nation that is able to build profitable relationships with multiple other major powers without becoming overly reliant on any one of them.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This ability to hedge allows a pivot state to avoid capture—in terms of security or economy—at the hands of a single country. In his book, <i>Every Nation for Itself: Winners and Losers in a G-Zero World</i> (New York: Portfolio, 2012),<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Bremmer explains how, in a volatile <a href="/wiki/G-Zero" class="mw-redirect" title="G-Zero">G-Zero</a> world, the ability to pivot will take on increased importance. At the opposite end of the spectrum are shadow states frozen within the influence of a single power. For example, with significant trade ties with both the United States and Asia and formal security ties with <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>, Canada is an example of a pivot state hedged against a slowdown in or conflict with any single major power. <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, on the other hand, is a shadow state due to its overwhelming reliance on the US economy.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geopolitical_recession">Geopolitical recession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_Bremmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Geopolitical recession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bremmer has coined the term “geopolitical recession” to describe the current geopolitical environment, one defined by an unwind of the former US-led global order. Unlike economic recessions, linked to frequent boom and bust cycles, Bremmer see geopolitical recessions as much longer cycles, and accordingly are less likely to be recognized.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  He see the present geopolitical recession as defined by deteriorating relations between the US and its traditional allies—particularly the Europeans—as China is rising but creating alternative international political and economic architecture. Bremmer argues that the overall result is a more fragmented approach to global governance, an increase in geopolitical tail risks, and a reduced ability to respond effectively to major international crises when they do hit.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_Data_Organization">World Data Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_Bremmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: World Data Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In his keynote address<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> at Eurasia Group’s 2019 GZERO Summit<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> in Tokyo, Bremmer proposed creating a “World Data Organization” to counter the threat of a “Splinternet”—a division in technology ecosystems due to conflict between the United States and China. Describing a World Data Organization as a digital version of the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">World Trade Organization</a>, he argued that the United States, Europe, Japan, and other “governments that believe in online openness and transparency” should collaborate to set standards for artificial intelligence, data, privacy, citizens’ rights, and intellectual property, while establishing a permanent secretariat and judiciary mechanism for enforcement. In an ideal world, the organization’s economic and security benefits would then incentivize China to join.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>After appearing on an episode of <i>GZERO World with Ian Bremmer</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> 2020 US presidential candidate and entrepreneur <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Yang" title="Andrew Yang">Andrew Yang</a> credited Bremmer with devising the concept of a World Data Organization,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> which he endorsed at a Democratic presidential debate in November 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Bremmer’s proposal received coverage in a number of media outlets, including <i>Forbes</i><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and <i>Bloomberg</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philanthropic_work">Philanthropic work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_Bremmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Philanthropic work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2016, Bremmer founded the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://egfound.org/">Eurasia Group Foundation</a> (EGF), a 501(c)3 public charity. EGF is committed to helping people make meaning out of the impact geopolitics has on their lives and empowering them to get involved in the issues that matter to them most. It takes complex issues and ensures they are accessible to everyone through traditional and nontraditional media.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, EGF created its first social media campaign to raise awareness in American voters about the foreign policy issues informing the presidential election. From that foundation, EGF created and produced Technology, Power and You, a campaign on technology and the future, and Inevitable Adversaries: The US-Russia Relationship, which explores the complicated US-Russia relationship.
</p><p>In 2019, it launched "Independent America", a multi-year project exploring what a more restrained US foreign policy would look like to Americans and elsewhere. It also partnered with Marginal Revolution University to develop "Globalization and You", a free one-week curriculum exploring globalization. The goal is to educate youth, particularly those with less opportunity to engage with the complicated issues of geopolitics.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Bremmer currently serves as the Eurasia Group Foundation’s board president. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_Bremmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In March 2016, Bremmer sent a weekly note to clients where he unintentionally came up with the <a href="/wiki/America_First_(policy)" title="America First (policy)">“America First”</a> slogan used by Donald Trump. The note described then-candidate Trump’s foreign policy as not isolationist but “America First,” a transactional, unilateralist perspective that was more a Chinese than American framework for foreign policy. Bremmer used the term to help explain Trump’s foreign policy views and not as a campaign slogan.
</p><p>A few weeks later New York Times reporters David Sanger and <a href="/wiki/Maggie_Haberman" title="Maggie Haberman">Maggie Haberman</a>, both of whom receive Bremmer’s weekly note, conducted Trump’s first foreign policy interview and asked him if he would describe himself as an isolationist. He said no. They then asked Trump if he considered himself America First. Trump said yes<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> and liked the term so much he started using it himself. Haberman later credited Bremmer with coming up with “America First” to describe Trump’s foreign policy.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2017, Bremmer broke news of a second, previously undisclosed meeting between Presidents Trump and Putin during the G20 heads of state dinner in Hamburg. He wrote about the meeting in his weekly client note and later appeared on <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Rose_(talk_show)" title="Charlie Rose (talk show)">Charlie Rose</a> to discuss the meeting’s implications. The news was quickly picked up by other major media outlets. <a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a>  profiled Bremmer in an article titled “Who is Eurasia Group’s Ian Bremmer, the risk consultant who exposed the second Trump-Putin meeting?”<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>  Trump initially denied that his second meeting with Putin happened and called Bremmer “fake news.”<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> However, then-White House Press Secretary <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Sanders" title="Sarah Sanders">Sarah Sanders</a> later held a press conference and admitted the second meeting indeed happened.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2019, Bremmer was criticized on Twitter<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> for posting a tweet that appeared to quote Trump. The tweet read: “President Trump in Tokyo: ‘Kim Jong Un is smarter and would make a better President than Sleepy Joe Biden.’” After the tweet went viral, Bremmer acknowledged on Twitter that President Trump did not in fact say that quote, and apologized saying: “My tweet yesterday about Trump preferring Kim Jong Un to Biden as President was meant in jest. The President correctly quoted me as saying it was a ‘completely ludicrous’ statement. I should have been clearer. My apologies.”<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> President Trump used the incident to call for stronger libel laws.<sup id="cite_ref-apology_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-apology-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
