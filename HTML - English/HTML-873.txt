<p><b>Kurt Andersen</b> (born August 22, 1954) is an American writer and host of the <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Award" title="Peabody Award">Peabody</a>-winning public radio program <i><a href="/wiki/Studio_360" title="Studio 360">Studio 360</a></i>, a production of <a href="/wiki/Public_Radio_International" title="Public Radio International">Public Radio International</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Journalism">Journalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kurt_Andersen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Journalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Andersen was born in <a href="/wiki/Omaha,_Nebraska" title="Omaha, Nebraska">Omaha, Nebraska</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and graduated from <a href="/wiki/Westside_High_School_(Omaha)" title="Westside High School (Omaha)">Westside High School</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He graduated <i>magna cum laude</i> from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">Harvard College</a>, where he edited the <i><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Lampoon" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard Lampoon">Harvard Lampoon</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In 1986 with <a href="/wiki/Graydon_Carter" title="Graydon Carter">E. Graydon Carter</a> he co-founded <i><a href="/wiki/Spy_magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Spy magazine">Spy</a></i> magazine, which they sold in 1991; it continued publishing until 1998. While writing for <i>Spy</i>, Andersen (with Carter) coined the notable insult "short-fingered vulgarian" for future United States President <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He has been a writer and columnist for <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_(magazine)" title="New York (magazine)">New York</a></i> ("The Imperial City"), <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i> ("The Culture Industry"), and <i><a href="/wiki/Time_magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Time magazine">Time</a></i> ("Spectator"). He was also the architecture and design critic for <i><a href="/wiki/Time_magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Time magazine">Time</a></i> for nine years.
</p><p>In 1996, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Reilly" title="Bill Reilly">Bill Reilly</a> fired Andersen after two and a half years from his position as editor-in-chief at <i>New York</i>, citing the publication's financial results.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Andersen attributed the firing to his refusal to kill a story about a rivalry between investment bankers <a href="/wiki/Felix_Rohatyn" title="Felix Rohatyn">Felix Rohatyn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steven_Rattner" title="Steven Rattner">Steven Rattner</a> that had upset <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kravis" title="Henry Kravis">Henry Kravis</a>, the principal of the publishing firm's ownership group.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1999 he co-founded an online media news web site and biweekly magazine called <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inside_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Inside (magazine) (page does not exist)">Inside</a></i>, which he and his co-founders sold to <a href="/wiki/Primedia" title="Primedia">Primedia</a>; Primedia closed the site in October 2001. From 2001 to 2004 he served as a senior creative consultant to <a href="/wiki/Barry_Diller" title="Barry Diller">Barry Diller</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Universal_Television" title="Universal Television">Universal Television</a>, and from 2003 to 2005 as editorial director of <i><a href="/wiki/Colors_(magazine)" title="Colors (magazine)">Colors</a></i> magazine. More recently, he co-founded the email cultural curation service Very Short List, was a guest op-ed columnist for <i>The New York Times</i> and editor-at-large for <a href="/wiki/Random_House" title="Random House">Random House</a>.
</p><p>He co-created <i>Studio 360</i>, a weekly program covering the arts and culture, which he has hosted since its launch in 2000. It is broadcast on 240 U.S. stations, and distributed as a podcast as well.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_works">Literary works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kurt_Andersen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Literary works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Andersen is the author of three novels, including <i><a href="/wiki/Turn_of_the_Century" title="Turn of the Century">Turn of the Century</a></i> (Random House, 1999), which was a national bestseller and <i>New York Times</i> Notable Book of the year, and the <i>New York Times</i> bestseller <i><a href="/wiki/Heyday_(novel)" title="Heyday (novel)">Heyday</a></i> (Random House, 2007), which won the <a href="/wiki/Langum_Prize" class="mw-redirect" title="Langum Prize">Langum Prize</a> for the best American historical fiction of 2007. Random House published his third novel, <i>True Believers</i>, in the summer of 2012, and it was named one of the best novels of that year by the <i>San Francisco Chronicle</i> and the <i>Washington Post</i>. His short fiction has been published in anthologies such as <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stories:_All-New_Tales&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stories: All-New Tales (page does not exist)">Stories: All-New Tales</a></i> (HarperCollins, 2010).
</p><p>Andersen has also published a book of humorous essays, <i>The Real Thing</i> (Doubleday, 1980; Holt, 1982; Bison Press, 2008), about "quintessentialism", and co-authored two humor books, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tools_of_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tools of Power (page does not exist)">Tools of Power</a></i> (Viking, 1980), a parody of <a href="/wiki/Self-help" title="Self-help">self-help</a> books on becoming successful, and <i>Loose Lips</i> (<a href="/wiki/Simon_%26_Schuster" title="Simon &amp; Schuster">Simon &amp; Schuster</a>, 1995), an anthology of edited transcripts of real-life conversations involving celebrated people. Along with Carter and George Kalogerakis he assembled a history and greatest-hits anthology of <i><a href="/wiki/Spy_magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Spy magazine">Spy</a></i> called <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spy:_The_Funny_Years&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Spy: The Funny Years (page does not exist)">Spy: The Funny Years</a></i>, published in 2006 by <a href="/wiki/Miramax_Books" title="Miramax Books">Miramax Books</a>.
</p><p>He also wrote <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reset_(book)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Reset (book) (page does not exist)">Reset</a></i> (Random House, 2009), an essay about the causes and aftermath of the Great Recession, and he has contributed to many other books, such as <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spark:_How_Creativity_Works&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Spark: How Creativity Works (page does not exist)">Spark: How Creativity Works</a></i> (HarperCollins, 2011), and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fields_of_Vision:_The_Photographs_of_John_Vachon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fields of Vision: The Photographs of John Vachon (page does not exist)">Fields of Vision: The Photographs of John Vachon</a></i> (Library of Congress, 2010).

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kurt_Andersen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</p>
