<p><b><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.visitflorida.com/">Florida</a></b> is the southernmost state in the continental <a href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America">United States of America</a>. Known as "The Sunshine State", over a century ago it became a popular winter destination for the well-to-do from colder climates; it has grown to become the second-most visited state in the nation. Tourists have long been drawn to Florida by its white-sand beaches and unique and diverse cities. Since the mid-twentieth century, world-class theme parks and attractions have become a top draw as well. 
</p><p>The state's roots in agriculture are still relevant, with oranges being a chief export. Untouched natural landscapes teeming with wildlife exist in vast areas, sometimes very close to cities, and there are miles of rivers and trails for the intrepid visitor. Some of Florida's best secrets are charming small towns and other places in secluded locations, away from the crowded tourist areas but certainly worth seeing. Regardless of preference, Florida has something to offer any kind of traveler.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Understand">Understand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Understand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While Florida is considered to be part of the <a href="/wiki/South_(United_States_of_America)" title="South (United States of America)">South</a>, it originated not as a British colony, but as a Spanish one. As such, the state has a unique history of its own. Despite this, Florida was a founding member of the Confederate States of America, and like other Southern states shared the experience of slavery, racial segregation and intolerance. Today, Florida's population is diverse and many regions are considered quite progressive. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Florida was inhabited by <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_cultures_of_North_America" title="Indigenous cultures of North America">Native Americans</a> for over 13,000 years before the arrival of European explorers, slavers and colonists. It is estimated that there were some 350,000 inhabitants, of many tribes, when the Spanish explorer <b>Juan Ponce de León</b> arrived in 1513. Members of the Calusa tribe fought effectively, so it took a few decades before Europeans were able to establish colonies. The first of those date back to the 1560s with <a href="/wiki/St._Augustine" title="St. Augustine">St. Augustine</a>, established in 1565, holding the distinction of being the oldest permanent European settlement in what's now the United States.
</p><p>Like almost everywhere else in the Americas, the history of European oppression and murder of <b>Native Americans</b> was tragic in Florida. Following the substantial depopulation of the state's native tribes, the Seminole tribe moved in and established themselves there in the 18th century. They, too, fought very hard, and continued to guard their independence as well as they could after Spanish Florida was ceded to the United States in 1821. The new territory functioned as a slave plantation economy, much like the rest of the Southern United States at the time, and the Seminoles gladly welcomed runaway slaves and accepted them into their tribe. Following their defeat in 1842 after the seven-year Second Seminole War, almost the entire tribe was deported west of the Mississippi River as part of the <a href="/wiki/Trail_of_Tears" class="mw-redirect" title="Trail of Tears">Trail of Tears</a>.
</p><p>Florida was admitted to the Union as a slave state in 1845, but seceded and joined the <b>Confederacy</b> with the rest of the South in 1861. While largely untouched by the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, the state enthusiastically enforced Jim Crow segregation laws against its then very large (approximately 44%) black population for a century after the Confederates' defeat.
</p>

<td style="background-color:#effafa; font-size:90%; border:1px solid #ccc; padding-left: 1em; padding-right: 1em; text-align:left; width:100%;">
<p><b>Shooting for the Stars</b>
</p><p>A source of pride and joy in Florida is its outsize role in the history of space exploration. Cape Canaveral began to be used for missile launches in 1949, and following President John F. Kennedy's vow to put American astronauts on the Moon before the end of the 1960s, early space flights took place there on converted missiles. The most important launches by NASA, including the Apollo 11 moon launch watched around the world in 1969, have taken place at the John F. Kennedy Space Center in Cape Canaveral.
</p>
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>However, there were three <b>20th-century migrations</b> that fundamentally changed the character of Florida, to the extent that many people no longer consider much of the state to be culturally Southern: The move of one-fifth of the state's African-American population to the Northern United States as part of the Great Migration during the first decades of the 20th century; the arrival of an increasing number of white retirees from the North after the spread of air conditioning in the 1950s; and the arrival of several waves of Cuban immigrants after the victory of Fidel Castro in 1959, who established themselves above all in South Florida, particularly <a href="/wiki/Miami-Dade_County" title="Miami-Dade County">Dade County</a>. Florida has also seen major influxes of Latino immigrants from Puerto Rico, the Dominican Republic, Central and South America, and Mexico, and there have also been many Haitian immigrants.
</p><p>Today, Florida is a <b>very diverse</b> state, almost evenly divided between Democrats and Republicans, with the North being more Republican and the South being more Democratic. As such, no one accent dominates in the speech of its residents. It's often said that Florida is the only state that gets more "Southern" the farther <i>north</i> you go, and that is indeed true in a cultural sense.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Call-Collins_House,_The_Grove-_Tallahassee,_Florida_(7157983334).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/The_Call-Collins_House%2C_The_Grove-_Tallahassee%2C_Florida_%287157983334%29.jpg/220px-The_Call-Collins_House%2C_The_Grove-_Tallahassee%2C_Florida_%287157983334%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/The_Call-Collins_House%2C_The_Grove-_Tallahassee%2C_Florida_%287157983334%29.jpg/330px-The_Call-Collins_House%2C_The_Grove-_Tallahassee%2C_Florida_%287157983334%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/The_Call-Collins_House%2C_The_Grove-_Tallahassee%2C_Florida_%287157983334%29.jpg/440px-The_Call-Collins_House%2C_The_Grove-_Tallahassee%2C_Florida_%287157983334%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="402" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Call-Collins_House,_The_Grove-_Tallahassee,_Florida_(7157983334).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Call-Collins Plantation House, The Grove- Tallahassee, Florida</div></div></div>
<p>Florida is geographically the southernmost of the contiguous United States, and it is a <b>unique blend</b> of societies. The <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a>, much of <a href="/wiki/North_Florida" title="North Florida">North Florida</a>, rural areas of <a href="/wiki/Central_Florida" title="Central Florida">Central Florida</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Heartland" title="Florida Heartland">Florida Heartland</a> remain part of the cultural region of <a href="/wiki/South_(United_States_of_America)" title="South (United States of America)">the South</a>, where you will find traditional southern cooking, entertainment, dialect, and lifestyles, much as you would expect in states like <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(state)" title="Georgia (state)">Georgia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>. Generally, the further south you go in the state, the more unlike the South it seems, and although Southern culture can be found in every region of the state, it is not always prevalent. 
</p><p>
Cities such as <a href="/wiki/Tampa" title="Tampa">Tampa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orlando" title="Orlando">Orlando</a> offer the feel of the South, alongside many other cultures. There are a lot of Southerners in these areas, but also many people who are from other areas like the <a href="/wiki/Midwest" title="Midwest">Midwest</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_United_States" title="Northeastern United States">Northeast</a>. <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>, on the other hand, is unique in that it feels like a cross between an American metropolis and a major Latin American city (like <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio</a> or <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a>). There are some Seminole Native American reservations and villages throughout southern Florida, namely in the <a href="/wiki/Everglades_National_Park" title="Everglades National Park">Everglades</a>, and their indigenous culture can be experienced by visiting a shop and browsing arts and crafts. The southernmost <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a> offer yet another flavor, full of the casual, slow-paced atmosphere of beach life. In short, Florida is a full-fledged region of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="United States">United States</a> in its own right.</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:269px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Miami_Beach_FL_Arch_Dist_Collins_Ave_down_south01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Miami_Beach_FL_Arch_Dist_Collins_Ave_down_south01.jpg/267px-Miami_Beach_FL_Arch_Dist_Collins_Ave_down_south01.jpg" decoding="async" width="267" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Miami_Beach_FL_Arch_Dist_Collins_Ave_down_south01.jpg/401px-Miami_Beach_FL_Arch_Dist_Collins_Ave_down_south01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Miami_Beach_FL_Arch_Dist_Collins_Ave_down_south01.jpg/534px-Miami_Beach_FL_Arch_Dist_Collins_Ave_down_south01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Miami_Beach_FL_Arch_Dist_Collins_Ave_down_south01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A view south on Collins Avenue in the <a href="/wiki/Miami_Beach/South_Beach" title="Miami Beach/South Beach">Miami Beach Architectural District</a></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Landscape">Landscape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Landscape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Florida's <b>coastline is world class</b>, with several gorgeous beaches, bays, and estuaries. The Floridian landscape is <b>extremely</b> <b>flat</b>, with lakes and wetlands scattered throughout most of the state. The only exceptions are parts of Highlands, Polk, Lake, and a few other counties in the center of the state where rolling hills are common. The highest point in the state is the 345&#160;ft (105&#160;m) Britton Hill on the Panhandle, and the 298&#160;ft (91&#160;m) Iron Mountain in Polk County is the highest point on the Peninsula. Florida's cities tend to be big, sprawling, and well-developed, but far from each other. Despite being a densely-populated state, there are fortunately still several expanses of wilderness left (although they are often found near a large city). Many rural parts of the state grow citrus and sugar cane, but farmland tends to be far from the usual tourist areas. The <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a> and most of <a href="/wiki/North_Florida" title="North Florida">North Florida</a> consist of farmland and pine trees, but as you travel south, you'll see more wetlands and urbanization. The <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a> are a small chain of tropical islands with their own unique geography.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:226px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Frances_05_sept_2004_1815Z.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Hurricane_Frances_05_sept_2004_1815Z.jpg/224px-Hurricane_Frances_05_sept_2004_1815Z.jpg" decoding="async" width="224" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Hurricane_Frances_05_sept_2004_1815Z.jpg/336px-Hurricane_Frances_05_sept_2004_1815Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Hurricane_Frances_05_sept_2004_1815Z.jpg/448px-Hurricane_Frances_05_sept_2004_1815Z.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5200" data-file-height="6800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Frances_05_sept_2004_1815Z.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hurricane Frances makes landfall on the morning of September 5, 2004 near Stuart. Its impact, however, was felt throughout Central Florida and most of Florida's east coast.</div></div></div>
<p>Florida is known around the world for its <b>balmy weather</b>. The state's mild winters have made it a haven for retirees year round as well as temporary residents known as "snowbirds". Summers can be long and hot, with the interior being a few degrees warmer than the immediate coast. Coastal areas experience gentle breezes during the summer, and the beach is usually the coolest place to be.
</p><p>While those coastal breezes are a welcome relief from the scorching temperatures, they are also the cause of the most notorious Florida weather feature: <b>thunderstorms</b>.  While the storms are often brief, they are common, and anyone visiting Florida during the rainy season (mid-June to September) should plan a few indoor activities in the afternoons as a backup. Florida's thunderstorms occur every day during the rainy season and typically form 20-30mi (32-48km) inland and either move toward the center of the state or toward the coast. Due to this, rain can be occurring just a few miles inland from the coast, while those at the beach experience a beautiful day.
</p><p>While the storms cool the air, bringing a welcome relief to stifling temperatures, many produce considerable amounts of <b>dangerous lightning</b> and some bring hail, high winds of 50 mph (80 km/h) or more, and tornadoes. See the "stay safe" section for thunderstorm safety. Many tourist areas, such as <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_World" title="Walt Disney World">Walt Disney World</a>, have multiple attractions available even during downpours.
</p><p>The spring is the driest time of the year, leads to <b>wildfires</b> nearly every May and early June.
</p><p>The six-month <b>hurricane season</b> runs from June 1 through November 30 and Floridians have learned to be ready when a storm threatens the area. If you plan on visiting during the summer or fall, stay aware of the news and weather advisories. Information is available from the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nhc.noaa.gov">National Hurricane Center</a>.
</p><p><b>Average annual temperatures:</b>
</p><p>Summer: 81°F degrees (27°C) in North Florida; 83°F degrees (28°C) in South Florida
</p><p>Winter: 53°F degrees (12°C) in North Florida; 67°F degrees (20°C) in South Florida
</p><p>The above temperatures are average temperatures throughout the day. 
</p><p>During the summer, high temperatures on the peninsula are usually around 90°F (32°C) on the coast, in the high 80°Fs (around 30°C) in the Keys, and in the mid-90°Fs (around 35°C) inland, with low temperatures ranging from around 80°F (26°C) on the coast to the mid-70°Fs (around 24°C) inland. During the winter, temperatures are much more variable. Freezing temperatures (below 32°F/0°C) occur at least once a year as far south as Central Florida, but even on the coldest days it will warm back up into the 50°Fs (around 15°C). It is best to consult individual city pages for temperatures during the winter.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Talk">Talk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Talk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>English</b> is the official language of the state. However, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_phrasebook" title="Spanish phrasebook">Spanish</a> is the native language of approximately 20% of Florida residents, and the further south you go, the more Spanish speakers there will be. In some parts of <a href="/wiki/South_Florida" title="South Florida">South Florida</a>, Spanish is the preferred language in everyday activities. <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a> is most notable, where nearly 80% of residents do not speak English as their native language and 30% do not speak any English at all. <a href="/wiki/Tampa" title="Tampa">Tampa</a> also has a sizable Spanish-speaking population, and neighborhoods where it is almost exclusively spoken. Like anywhere where there is a large presence of another language, expect Spanish words or expressions used or calqued into everyday English.
</p><p>Native-born non-Hispanic Floridians will usually speak with a Southern accent. However, after the migration of millions of Americans from other states to Florida, the Southern dialect is becoming diluted with other accents.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_train">By train</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: By train">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rail_travel_in_the_United_States" title="Rail travel in the United States">Amtrak</a> has two services to Florida:
</p>
<ul><li><b><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.amtrak.com/routes/auto-train.html">Amtrak Auto Train</a></b> <i>(Trains #52 &amp; 53)</i> carries passengers and automobiles between <a href="/wiki/Lorton" title="Lorton">Lorton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanford_(Florida)" title="Sanford (Florida)">Sanford, Florida</a> (north of <a href="/wiki/Orlando" title="Orlando">Orlando</a>), effectively serving as a car-rail link to Florida from the <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> metro area.  This train makes no stops between Lorton &amp; Sanford and is a way to cut back on the extra mileage and wear and tear on the car.</li>
<li><b><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.amtrak.com/routes/silver-service-palmetto-train.html">Amtrak Silver Service</a></b> <i>(Trains #91 &amp; 92 and #97 &amp; 98 respectively)</i> are two routes that both begin in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and end in <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>. The trains follow the same route heading to Florida, except in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>. Within the state, the trains run the same line from <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville" title="Jacksonville">Jacksonville</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kissimmee" title="Kissimmee">Kissimmee</a>. There, they split again, and the Silver Star (Trains #91 &amp; 92) diverges to head west towards <a href="/wiki/Lakeland" title="Lakeland">Lakeland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tampa" title="Tampa">Tampa</a> while the Silver Meteor (Trains #97 &amp; 98) continues south towards <a href="/wiki/Winter_Haven" title="Winter Haven">Winter Haven</a>. Both routes meet again further south and serve the rest of the stations along Florida's east coast to Miami.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_car">By car</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: By car">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Brickell_skyline_2012.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Brickell_skyline_2012.jpg/240px-Brickell_skyline_2012.jpg" decoding="async" width="240" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Brickell_skyline_2012.jpg/360px-Brickell_skyline_2012.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Brickell_skyline_2012.jpg/480px-Brickell_skyline_2012.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4928" data-file-height="3264" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Brickell_skyline_2012.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The skyline of <a href="/wiki/Miami/Brickell" class="mw-redirect" title="Miami/Brickell">Brickell, Miami</a></div></div></div>
<p>Three <a href="/wiki/Driving_in_the_United_States" title="Driving in the United States">Interstate highways</a> connect Florida with adjacent states
</p>
<ul><li><b>Interstate 95</b> enters Florida from <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(state)" title="Georgia (state)">Georgia</a> just north of <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville" title="Jacksonville">Jacksonville</a> and parallels the Atlantic coast, never more than 25 miles inland, until its terminus south of downtown <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>. Interstate 95 provides the most convenient route for people from the East Coast, <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>, eastern parts of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Jacksonville, <a href="/wiki/Daytona_Beach" title="Daytona Beach">Daytona Beach</a>, and the Miami-<a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale" title="Fort Lauderdale">Fort Lauderdale</a>-<a href="/wiki/West_Palm_Beach" title="West Palm Beach">West Palm Beach</a> area are all serviced by I-95, with access to <a href="/wiki/Orlando" title="Orlando">Orlando</a> provided via Interstate 4.</li>
<li><b>Interstate 75</b> also enters Florida from Georgia and passes through the center of the state until the <a href="/wiki/West_Central_Florida" title="West Central Florida">Tampa Bay</a> area, after which it follows the Gulf of Mexico coast (10-20 mi/16-32 km inland) to <a href="/wiki/Naples_(Florida)" title="Naples (Florida)">Naples</a>, where it heads due east across the <a href="/wiki/Everglades_National_Park" title="Everglades National Park">Everglades</a> to Fort Lauderdale. Interstate 75 is most convenient for travelers arriving from <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Midwest" title="Midwest">Midwest</a>.</li>
<li><b>Interstate 10</b> enters Florida from <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> near <a href="/wiki/Pensacola" title="Pensacola">Pensacola</a> and passes through the center of the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Panhandle</a> and across <a href="/wiki/North_Florida" title="North Florida">North Florida</a> until its terminus in Jacksonville. Interstate 10 is most convenient for travelers from <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, and areas further west.</li></ul>
<p>Additional major highways entering Florida include:
</p>
<ul><li><b>US 1</b> enters Florida north of Jacksonville and snakes along the east coast between Interstate 95 and the Intracoastal Waterway/Atlantic Ocean. Unlike I-95, US 1 continues past Miami and is routed over a series of bridges (including the famous Seven Mile Bridge) through the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a> to its terminus at <a href="/wiki/Key_West" title="Key West">Key West</a>.</li>
<li><b>US 231</b> enters Florida from Alabama and crosses the Panhandle north-south to its southern terminus at <a href="/wiki/Panama_City_Beach" title="Panama City Beach">Panama City Beach</a>. US 231 provides convenient access to the Panhandle from the Midwest via it connection with Interstate 65 in <a href="/wiki/Montgomery" title="Montgomery">Montgomery</a>.</li>
<li><b>US 98</b> enters Florida near Pensacola and remains close to the Gulf of Mexico coast until the Panhandle meets the Florida peninsula at the "Big Bend" area. Unlike I-10 to the north, which runs through the interior of the Panhandle away from the coast, US 98 provides a scenic drive and convenient access to the beaches. After leaving the Panhandle, US 98 runs diagonally down the peninsula to West Palm Beach through primarily rural areas.</li>
<li><b>US 27</b> enters Florida from western Georgia and provides access to the state capital, <a href="/wiki/Tallahassee" title="Tallahassee">Tallahassee</a>, before routing through mostly rural areas of the peninsula. Down the center of the state between Florida's Turnpike and Miami, US 27 is a primary trucking route, and as such this route can be a hassle while dealing with heavy traffic.</li>
<li><b>US 301</b> enters Florida just north of Jacksonville and was once the main route from the <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_United_States" title="Northeastern United States">Northeast</a>. It is a very scenic alternative to I-95 with a lot less traffic. Exiting I-95 at Santee, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a> motorists can follow US 301 through Georgia and into Florida and connect to I-95 again in Jacksonville for Atlantic coast destinations, or continue on to join I-75 at <a href="/wiki/Ocala" title="Ocala">Ocala</a> for Tampa and the Gulf coast.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_boat">By boat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: By boat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Florida is possibly the largest state for <a href="/wiki/Cruise_ships" title="Cruise ships">cruise ship</a> vacations in the United States. <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral" title="Cape Canaveral">Cape Canaveral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tampa" title="Tampa">Tampa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale" title="Fort Lauderdale">Fort Lauderdale</a> are all popular starting ports, with cruises heading throughout the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>. There are also many short-term casino cruises that depart from <a href="/wiki/Pinellas_County" title="Pinellas County">Pinellas County</a> and around <a href="/wiki/South_Florida" title="South Florida">South Florida</a>.
</p><p><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.ferryexpress.com/">Ferry service</a> is also available to and from the <a href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, with service between <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale" title="Fort Lauderdale">Fort Lauderdale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freeport_(Bahamas)" title="Freeport (Bahamas)">Freeport</a>, and between Miami and <a href="/wiki/Bimini" title="Bimini">Bimini</a>.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_car_2">By car</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: By car">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Car rental agencies abound in Florida and many are available at every major airport. <a href="/wiki/Orlando" title="Orlando">Orlando</a>, in particular, is known as the "Car Rental Capital of the World". With Florida being the most visited state in the US, car rental rates here are among (if not the) lowest rates in the country due to sheer volume.
</p><p><i> <b>Florida's major highways include:</b> </i>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Interstate 4</b> crosses diagonally from <a href="/wiki/Tampa" title="Tampa">Tampa</a>, heads east through <a href="/wiki/Plant_City" title="Plant City">Plant City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lakeland" title="Lakeland">Lakeland</a>, then continues northeast past <a href="/wiki/Kissimmee" title="Kissimmee">Kissimmee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_World" title="Walt Disney World">Walt Disney World</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Orlando" title="Orlando">Orlando</a>, before ending at Interstate 95 near <a href="/wiki/Daytona_Beach" title="Daytona Beach">Daytona Beach</a>. Interstate 4 is the most travelled highway in Florida and due to the large volume of traffic, high speeds (70 mph,112 km/h outside of urban areas), construction, and large number of tourists it is also the most dangerous highway in the state, in terms of the number of accidents.</li>
<li><b>Interstate 95</b> enters Florida from <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(state)" title="Georgia (state)">Georgia</a> north of <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville" title="Jacksonville">Jacksonville</a> and travels down the Atlantic coast past <a href="/wiki/St._Augustine" title="St. Augustine">St. Augustine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daytona_Beach" title="Daytona Beach">Daytona Beach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral" title="Cape Canaveral">Cape Canaveral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vero_Beach" title="Vero Beach">Vero Beach</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Palm_Beach" title="West Palm Beach">West Palm Beach</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale" title="Fort Lauderdale">Fort Lauderdale</a> before ending at US 1 just south of downtown <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>.</li>
<li><b>Interstate 10</b> enters Florida from <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> near <a href="/wiki/Pensacola" title="Pensacola">Pensacola</a> and travels across the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Panhandle</a>, past <a href="/wiki/Tallahassee" title="Tallahassee">Tallahassee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_City" title="Lake City">Lake City</a>, and through rural <a href="/wiki/North_Florida" title="North Florida">North Florida</a> to its terminus at I-95 in Jacksonville.</li>
<li><b>Interstate 75</b> enters Florida from Georgia and runs south through the center of the peninsula past <a href="/wiki/Gainesville_(Florida)" title="Gainesville (Florida)">Gainesville</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ocala" title="Ocala">Ocala</a> before crossing just east of Tampa, where it then parallels the Gulf coast past <a href="/wiki/Bradenton" title="Bradenton">Bradenton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sarasota" title="Sarasota">Sarasota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fort_Myers" title="Fort Myers">Fort Myers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Naples_(Florida)" title="Naples (Florida)">Naples</a>, after which it travels due east across the <a href="/wiki/Everglades" class="mw-redirect" title="Everglades">Everglades</a> (a section known as 'Alligator Alley') to the Miami suburbs.</li>
<li><b>Florida's Turnpike</b> is a toll road that runs from I-75 south of Ocala, through Orlando, West Palm Beach, and Fort Lauderdale before ending south of Miami. It provides the easiest access to Orlando and southeast Florida for persons entering the state via I-75 or I-10.</li>
<li><b>Interstate 275</b> is a secondary Interstate that runs from I-75 north of the Tampa area, past downtown Tampa and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg_(Florida)" title="Saint Petersburg (Florida)">Saint Petersburg</a>, where it crosses the 5.5-mile-long (8.8km), 193 foot-tall (58.8m) Sunshine Skyway Bridge before it rejoins I-75 south of Bradenton. Interstate 75 <i>does not</i> provide access to these areas, it instead passes through rural and suburban areas 10 miles east of Tampa.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/U.S._Highway_1" title="U.S. Highway 1">U.S. Highway 1</a></b> is a historic and scenic highway that travels down the Atlantic coast between I-95 and the ocean before being routed over a series of bridges (including the famous Seven Mile Bridge) through the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a> to its terminus at <a href="/wiki/Key_West" title="Key West">Key West</a>.</li>
<li><b>State Road A1A</b> runs parallel to US 1 and Interstate 95, but lies to the east of the Intracoastal Waterway (mainly on the barrier islands), running mostly along the oceanfront and offering incredibly scenic views.</li>
<li><b>US 98</b> enters Florida from <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> at Pensacola and travels a very scenic route along the Gulf Coast of the Panhandle, after which it continues diagonally across the peninsula to its terminus in West Palm Beach.</li>
<li><b>US 27</b> is a well-traveled alternative to the Florida's Turnpike and runs from Miami, along Lake Okeechobee and through the mostly-rural <a href="/wiki/Florida_Heartland" title="Florida Heartland">Heartland</a> of Florida before continuing through Ocala, Gainesville, and Tallahassee.</li>
<li><b>US 41</b> runs west from Miami on a scenic 2-lane journey through the Everglades, and then travels along the Gulf Coast, around the east side of Tampa Bay, and north into Georgia.</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_ferry">By ferry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: By ferry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A high speed ferry service, the <b><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.seakeywestexpress.com">Key West Express</a>,</b> operates from the cities of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Myers" title="Fort Myers">Fort Myers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marco_Island" title="Marco Island">Marco Island</a> with daily service to the Historic Seaport district of <a href="/wiki/Key_West" title="Key West">Key West</a>. The ferry ride takes approximately 3½ hours and the Fort Myers vessels have a capacity exceeding 300 passengers. Amenities include outdoor sundecks, flat-screen TVs, galley service and a full bar.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Spaceship_Earth_at_night.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Spaceship_Earth_at_night.jpg/220px-Spaceship_Earth_at_night.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Spaceship_Earth_at_night.jpg/330px-Spaceship_Earth_at_night.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Spaceship_Earth_at_night.jpg/440px-Spaceship_Earth_at_night.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2399" data-file-height="1920" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Spaceship_Earth_at_night.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The world-famous Spaceship Earth at Walt Disney World's <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_World/Epcot" title="Walt Disney World/Epcot">Epcot</a> theme park.</div></div></div>
<ul><li><b>Go to the beach!</b> You have numerous options here: <a href="/wiki/Panama_City_Beach" title="Panama City Beach">Panama City Beach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daytona_Beach" title="Daytona Beach">Daytona Beach</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Pete_Beach" title="St. Pete Beach">St. Pete Beach</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Palm_Beach" title="West Palm Beach">West Palm Beach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siesta_Key" title="Siesta Key">Siesta Key</a> are some of the best.</li>
<li>Visit Florida's world class <b>theme parks and water parks</b>.
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_World" title="Walt Disney World">Walt Disney World</a></b> - The most visited resort in the world, home to four theme parks, two water parks, and shopping, dining, and hotels galore.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Universal_Orlando" title="Universal Orlando"><b>Universal Orlando</b></a> - Disney World's biggest competitor, these two theme parks are home to The Wizarding World of Harry Potter.</li>
<li><b>SeaWorld</b> in <a href="/wiki/Orlando" title="Orlando">Orlando</a> and <b>Legoland</b> in nearby <a href="/wiki/Winter_Haven" title="Winter Haven">Winter Haven</a>.</li>
<li><b>Busch Gardens</b> in <a href="/wiki/Tampa" title="Tampa">Tampa</a>.</li></ul></li>
<li>Visit <b><a href="/wiki/Everglades_National_Park" title="Everglades National Park">Everglades National Park</a></b>, a place like no other on Earth, and take an airboat ride through the swamps. A drive across the Everglades on US 41 is a great way to get a sense of the park's size and scenery. Check out <a href="/wiki/Everglades_City" title="Everglades City">Everglades City</a> for great attractions.</li>
<li>Explore some of the more than 150 <b><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.floridastateparks.org/">Florida State Parks</a></b>. The only three-time winner of the National Recreation and Park Association's Gold Medal for state park systems. Get a Florida State Parks Annual Pass for free admission to most of the state parks, or discounted admission at <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg_(Florida)" title="Saint Petersburg (Florida)">Skyway Fishing Pier State Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homosassa" title="Homosassa">Ellie Schiller Homosassa Springs Wildlife State Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hernando_County" title="Hernando County">Weeki Wachee Springs State Park</a>.</li>
<li>Visit the <b><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.seminoletribe.com/">Seminole</a> or <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://miccosukeetribe.com/">Miccosukee</a> Native American reservations</b> throughout the state. Here you can find out about their history and culture, try their food, and gamble in the casinos on their land, which include several Hard Rock Casinos.</li>
<li><b>Go <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">scuba diving</a> or snorkeling</b> through the many coral reefs, sunken vessels, and diverse array of sea life off of Florida's coasts. The most colorful fish can be seen in the tropical waters of <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a> and further south.</li>
<li><b>Go hiking or <a href="/wiki/Wilderness_backpacking" title="Wilderness backpacking">backpacking</a></b> in the many state and national parks and forests that have nature trails suitable for hiking and camping. A particularly good option is the Florida National Scenic Trail, a network of 1400 miles (2253 km) of hiking trails throughout the state. The most popular section of the trail is the 110-mile (177-km) loop encircling Lake Okeechobee.</li>
<li><b>Go fishing</b>, with some of the best fishing action in the world (both salt and freshwater). Several large and tough fighting species such as Sailfish, Tarpon, and Largemouth Bass can be found lurking in water throughout the state.</li>
<li><b>Take a <a href="/wiki/Cruise_ships" title="Cruise ships">Cruise</a></b> from the Port of <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tampa" title="Tampa">Tampa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale" title="Fort Lauderdale">Fort Lauderdale</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral" title="Cape Canaveral">Cape Canaveral</a> on longer cruises run by the likes of Royal Caribbean, Carnival Cruise Line or <a href="/wiki/Disney_Cruise_Line" title="Disney Cruise Line">Disney Cruise Line</a>. There are also short nightly casino cruises in various places.</li>
<li><b>View a rocket launch</b>, which are visible from virtually anywhere in the Peninsula when skies are clear. But the best viewing is up-close from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral" title="Cape Canaveral">Cape Canaveral's</a> Kennedy Space Center, <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_Beach" title="Cocoa Beach">Cocoa Beach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canaveral_National_Seashore" title="Canaveral National Seashore">Canaveral National Seashore</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Titusville_(Florida)" title="Titusville (Florida)">Titusville</a>.</li>
<li>Check out the <b>Florida State Fair</b>, held every February near Tampa. It hosts an exposition of counties, where each Florida county has a display and a representative to answer questions. In addition, the fair has animal displays and shows, an exhibition dedicated to citrus, various dance &amp; cheerleading competitions, and a large selection of rides and games.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eat">Eat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Eat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Florida_orange_grove.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Florida_orange_grove.JPG/220px-Florida_orange_grove.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Florida_orange_grove.JPG/330px-Florida_orange_grove.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Florida_orange_grove.JPG/440px-Florida_orange_grove.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4320" data-file-height="3240" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Florida_orange_grove.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A typical Florida orange grove near <a href="/wiki/Sebring" title="Sebring">Sebring</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Florida's cuisine is a mix of many influences and its styles vary across the state from North to South. <a href="/wiki/North_Florida" title="North Florida">North Florida</a> has a more Southern style; <a href="/wiki/South_Florida" title="South Florida">South Florida</a> a more Caribbean one. There specifically, early Spanish and African cuisines have been given a new spin with the impact of Cuban and other Caribbean cultures, as well as from "snowbirds" escaping the Northern U.S. winters. Being on a peninsula, Florida's chefs have always had access to fresh seafood, and the long growing season provides fresh native vegetables almost year round.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Citrus</b> is a main export, and a tourist is apt to see many roadside stands offering free samples of orange juice and fruits to be shipped or carried home. Florida also grows grapefruit, avocado, mango, papaya, passion fruit, kumquat, coconut and other tropical fruits. These often provide the base for sauces and marinades and are also used in marmalades, soups, and desserts.  Official state welcome centers located on I-10, I-75, and I-95 as you enter Florida offer free samples of orange juice to all visitors, a tradition that goes back decades.</li>
<li><b>Strawberries</b> are another popular fruit in Florida. <a href="/wiki/Plant_City" title="Plant City">Plant City</a>, off I-4 east of <a href="/wiki/Tampa" title="Tampa">Tampa</a>, is the center of the Florida strawberry industry, where during the peak season (Feb-Mar) many roadside vendors offer flats (16 pints/12 lb/5.4 kg) and half-flats of strawberries for a small fraction of grocery store prices. Since most stands are owned by the individual farmers, the fruit sold was  often harvested that morning or the day before. Fresh Florida strawberries are a treat no tourist should miss, at least if you visit in-season.</li>
<li><b>Grouper</b> is a very popular seafood caught in Florida's coastal waters. Fresh grouper is offered in many coastal cities, where local restaurants buy it straight from fishermen, and it is often served fried or grilled on a sandwich. State inspectors have cracked down to ensure that all restaurants offering "grouper" are in fact serving grouper, and not another less expensive white fish. Snapper, snook, tarpon, marlin, and shark are other Florida fish that you can find at coastal restaurants, although they are not nearly as ubiquitous as grouper.</li>
<li><b>Southern food</b> is available throughout most of <a href="/wiki/North_Florida" title="North Florida">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Florida" title="Central Florida">Central Florida</a>. Barbeque is popular throughout the state, with many small "barbeque shacks" to choose from. Any platter costing over $10 ($15 for ribs) should be avoided as the less expensive restaurants are almost always best. Sweet tea is also common throughout the state, although unlike most areas in the <a href="/wiki/South_(United_States_of_America)" title="South (United States of America)">South</a>, you have a choice between sweet and unsweet tea. Boiled peanuts, which taste nothing like a regular peanut, can be found at roadside vendors and are certainly worth trying. Dishes such as fried chicken, grits, okra, biscuits &amp; gravy, and collard greens can also be found in restaurants and buffets throughout the state.</li>
<li><b>Cuban food</b> is common in the <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tampa" title="Tampa">Tampa</a> areas, with the most common dishes being the Cuban sandwich, flan, and black beans &amp; rice.</li>
<li><b>Local specialties</b>, not readily available in many other locales, include <b>alligator</b>. It is healthy and most say it tastes like chicken, and it is often prepared like chicken nuggets. <b>Key lime pie</b>, found elsewhere now, is a <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a> invention, made from the local key limes.</li></ul>
<p>Florida bans indoor smoking in restaurants, but it is allowed outdoors unless the establishment prohibits it.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drink">Drink</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Drink">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MIAMI-ArtDeco-rj002.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/MIAMI-ArtDeco-rj002.jpg/220px-MIAMI-ArtDeco-rj002.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/MIAMI-ArtDeco-rj002.jpg/330px-MIAMI-ArtDeco-rj002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/MIAMI-ArtDeco-rj002.jpg/440px-MIAMI-ArtDeco-rj002.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2126" data-file-height="1400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MIAMI-ArtDeco-rj002.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The art deco hotels and clubs of Miami's <a href="/wiki/Miami_Beach/South_Beach" title="Miami Beach/South Beach">South Beach</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Alcoholic beverages</b> abound throughout the state. However, five rural counties in the northern third of the state are "dry counties", and no alcohol is sold in them. Stand-alone liquor stores are often built into strip malls, supermarkets, and pharmacies, and most grocery stores, gas stations, and convenience stores sell beer and wine. <b>Bars and clubs</b> are popular throughout the state, with <a href="/wiki/Miami_Beach" title="Miami Beach">Miami Beach</a> being well-known for a variety of themed and upscale bars with innovative mixed drinks. Unique bars and clubs can also be found in downtown <a href="/wiki/Orlando" title="Orlando">Orlando</a> and the nearby tourist areas closer to the theme parks and resorts of <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_World" title="Walt Disney World">Disney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Universal_Orlando" title="Universal Orlando">Universal</a>.
</p><p>No visit to Florida is complete without a cup of their famous <b>orange juice</b>. In most of the welcome centers, you can have a free sample.
</p><p>Like every other U.S. state, the purchase and possession age for alcohol is 21 and it's fairly well enforced. Underage drinking "stings" are frequent in most tourist areas. Florida allows smoking in bars, including those that serve food, but the bar must get less than 10% of their revenue from food.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Buy">Buy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Buy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Florida is increasingly becoming a <b>major destination for shopping</b>. The <a href="/wiki/Orlando" title="Orlando">Orlando</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a> areas are home to a plethora of shopping malls, including many outlet malls home to shops selling brand-name products for discounted prices. There are also a large number of stores selling souvenirs, although most are not locally produced. The <a href="/wiki/Kissimmee" title="Kissimmee">Kissimmee</a> area near Orlando, especially, has a much larger amount of retail stores than typical of U.S. cities. While traditionally these shops catered largely to American families on vacation, most now serve foreign shoppers who flock to these malls to buy products significantly cheaper than at home. It's not uncommon at some shopping malls in Orlando to encounter tourists from around the globe, especially on weekdays when most Americans are working. In the last few years, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> has become the largest source of international visitors to the state, with many coming on shopping group tours and sometimes wearing matching shirts. Due to this, major shopping centers in the Orlando and Miami areas now offer services in <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_phrasebook" title="Portuguese phrasebook">Portuguese</a>.
</p><p>Most goods for sale in the state are subject to sales tax. In most of the state the rate is 7%, but it varies from 6-7.5% (6% state sales tax and up to 1.5% local sales tax). This rate is almost never listed on the advertised or displayed prices.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Connect">Connect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Connect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Siesta_Key_Beach_TV-001-0002.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Siesta_Key_Beach_TV-001-0002.jpg/220px-Siesta_Key_Beach_TV-001-0002.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Siesta_Key_Beach_TV-001-0002.jpg/330px-Siesta_Key_Beach_TV-001-0002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Siesta_Key_Beach_TV-001-0002.jpg/440px-Siesta_Key_Beach_TV-001-0002.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1072" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Siesta_Key_Beach_TV-001-0002.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The gentle white sands of Siesta Key</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internet">Internet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Internet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nearly all <b>hotels</b> offer Wi-Fi internet access for guests; some even have Ethernet ports for higher-speed wired connections. Many <b>businesses</b> also have Wi-Fi internet access, sometimes for free. This even includes some clothing/department, grocery, and convenience stores in addition to the more typical restaurants and shopping malls.
</p><p>Wi-Fi internet access is also available at <b>public libraries</b>. It's almost always free for everyone with their own device (laptop, smartphone, tablet). Computers set up for internet access by patrons require a username and password to access; nearly all libraries will issue a "guest pass" for non-cardholders (i.e. non-residents), either for free or for a small charge ($1-2). Use of library computers is subject to time limits which vary widely.
</p><p>The large majority of "internet cafes" in the state are actually nominally illegal casinos, set up for online gambling in an attempt to circumvent gambling laws. Such internet cafes do not have typical computers for general use. Instead, they are usually enclosed in a slot machine-style cover with only a few buttons to press, and set up to only view gambling websites. Patrons pay for short periods of time, sometimes using odd methods like buying prepaid phone cards. After a high-profile crackdown on an operator of dozens of such internet cafes, the state banned the opening of all new internet cafes in 2013.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stay_safe">Stay safe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Stay safe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dialing <b>911</b> at any telephone will reach the emergency services (police, fire, ambulance, etc.). Any U.S. phone, regardless if it is "active" or not, must be able to dial 911 if it is connected to the network, and such calls are always free.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crime">Crime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Crime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Florida has varying crime intensity from city to city. In certain areas of the large cities it may not be safe to walk alone or even in small groups at night, although these are the exceptions and most of Florida is safe for visitors. Tourist areas rarely have violent crimes, but theft is an occasional occurrence. If the area doesn't feel safe, then it probably isn't.
</p><p><b>Clip joint</b> operators who trick visitors into paying large amounts of money for low-quality services may use local police to shake down voyagers under a Florida law that requires bar and restaurant patrons pay a disputed bill first and take it up later with their credit card company.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weather">Weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="pp_infobox" style="float:right; clear:right; margin-left: 1em; width:40%;" cellpadding="8" cellspacing="1">
<tbody><tr>
<td style="background-color:#effafa; font-size:90%; border:1px solid #ccc; padding-left: 1em; padding-right: 1em; text-align:left; width:100%;">
<p><b>Extreme Temperatures</b>
</p><p><i>Never leave children or pets in a parked car for any length of time!</i> Due to the high temperatures most of the year, the interior of a parked car can easily heat to a <b>lethal</b> temperature very quickly. During the summer, the interior of a parked car can reach 130-170°F (55-75°C) in just 15 minutes, regardless of the color of the exterior or interior, nor whether the windows are open a small amount. You not only risk their lives, but <b>it is illegal</b> and the consequences could be thousands in fines and even imprisonment. Vehicles left containing animals or children <i>will</i> be broken into by police or bystanders.
</p>
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Florida has a high occurrence of <b>hurricanes</b>, though they don't hit the state every year. You might want to check the <a href="/wiki/Cyclones" title="Cyclones">hurricane safety</a> page if you are visiting Florida during the Hurricane Season, which runs June 1 to November 30.
</p><p>Only two places in the world experience more frequent <b>lightning strikes</b> than Florida. The summer thunderstorms in Florida produce frequent lightning, which kills people each year and injures many more. Stay indoors during a thunderstorm and <i>never</i> seek shelter under a tree. Most casualties occur on golf courses, but lightning strikes everywhere. If you must go outdoors during a storm, try to stay away from any tall object, especially trees or anything made of metal. Occasionally, these thunderstorms will bring <b>hail, high winds, and <a href="/wiki/Tornado_safety" title="Tornado safety">tornadoes</a></b>. While the historical number of tornadoes in Florida is somewhat high, the overwhelming majority have occurred during hurricanes (Hurricane Jeanne alone spawned over 200 tornadoes in Florida). While some do occur during winter cold fronts and summer thunderstorms, 99% of them are weak (F-0/F-1). Thus, while statistics may suggest otherwise, tornadoes are not a big hazard in Florida.  
</p><p>Watch where and when you swim. While the beaches are great they sometimes harbor <b>rip currents, bacteria, and jellyfish</b>. Always check with the lifeguard stand or ranger station before heading in if no one is in the water or if the waves are rough. 
</p><p><b>Red tide</b> is the name for harmful algal blooms that can occur on Florida's Gulf coast. They cause fish kills and water discoloration, and release toxins into the air that cause respiratory irritation.  People with severe or chronic respiratory conditions, such as emphysema or asthma, should avoid areas where red tide is occurring. Swimming in water afflicted by red tide can cause skin and eye irritation and is not recommended.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wildlife"><a href="/wiki/Dangerous_animals" title="Dangerous animals">Wildlife</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Wildlife">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Alligators</b> are a threat throughout Florida (even in inland areas like <a href="/wiki/Orlando" title="Orlando">Orlando</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_World" title="Walt Disney World">Walt Disney World</a>), and it should be assumed that they are present in <i>all</i> stagnant or slow moving freshwater. <i>Never</i> swim in any lakes or rivers unless signs tell you swimming is safe, and beware when approaching the water anywhere. Do not allow children to approach the water's edge. 
</p><p><b>Bears</b> and <b>Florida panthers</b>, common before the arrival of Europeans in Florida, are now both endangered in the state. If you do see either, back off slowly or keep your distance, make constant noise to avoid startling the animal, and make yourself appear larger by waving your arms above your head. When hiking, make lots of noise to avoid starling an animal, and always keep small children close to you.  
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Volusia_County" title="Volusia County">Volusia County</a> is known for a high number of <b>shark attacks</b>, but they can occur anywhere in the state, so be careful when surfing. Even so, the number of attacks are less than 50, with a fatal attack every 2-3 years, amongst <i>millions</i> of visitors and residents who swim in the ocean. Swimming near dusk and dawn is the most hazardous.
</p><p><b>Lionfish</b> are a poisonous and invasive fish that are now present on reefs throughout Florida. They can be identified by their red-and white striped bodies and poisonous spines. Stings are extremely painful and often require hospitalization. Due to their status as an invasive species, any sighting of lionfish should be reported to wildlife management authorities immediately.
</p><p><b>Jellyfish</b> are also sometimes common at the beaches, and <b>venomous snakes</b> can be found year round across the state, so it is good to be wary of both.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cope">Cope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Cope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consulates">Consulates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Consulates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A large number of countries have consulates in and around <a href="/wiki/Miami#Consulates" title="Miami">Miami</a>, with a much smaller number in <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville#Consulates" title="Jacksonville">Jacksonville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orlando#Consulates" title="Orlando">Orlando</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tampa#Consulates" title="Tampa">Tampa</a>. Full listings for these consulates and honorary consulates are in the articles for the cities where they are located. Always call ahead to determine if the consulate offers the services you require, such as passports, visas, and other official documents, as these services are increasingly being centralized at other locations. Some websites are available only in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_phrasebook" title="Spanish phrasebook">Spanish</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Go_next">Go next</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Go next">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Georgia_(state)" title="Georgia (state)">Georgia</a></b> - Heading out of Florida to the north is Georgia, with the historic city of <a href="/wiki/Savannah" title="Savannah">Savannah</a> and the resort beaches of <a href="/wiki/Jekyll_Island" title="Jekyll Island">Jekyll Island</a> close by.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a></b> - North of the Panhandle is Alabama, with the historic port of <a href="/wiki/Mobile" title="Mobile">Mobile</a> and popular resort town <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Shores" title="Gulf Shores">Gulf Shores</a> a short drive away.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a></b> - The islands of the Caribbean are accessible by boat and plane from across Florida and offer a variety of both relaxing and adventure travel amidst a tropical paradise.  For those interested in visiting the <a href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, many owners of small boats will make the day-long trip to the island chain, and several small airlines offer flights for under $70 each way.</li></ul>
