<p><b>Akeldama</b> (Aramaic: ◊ó◊ß◊ú ◊ì◊û◊ê or ◊óê°íê°ã ê°Éê°åê°Ä <i>·∏§aqel D'ma</i>, field of blood) is the <a href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">Aramaic</a> name for a place in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> associated with <a href="/wiki/Judas_Iscariot" title="Judas Iscariot">Judas Iscariot</a>, one of the original <a href="/wiki/Apostles" title="Apostles">twelve apostles</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variant_transliterations">Variant transliterations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akeldama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Variant transliterations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most English-language versions of the Bible transliterate the term as <i>Akeldama</i> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/American_Standard_Version" title="American Standard Version">American Standard Version</a> (ASV), <a href="/wiki/English_Standard_Version" title="English Standard Version">English Standard Version</a> (ESV), <a href="/wiki/Good_News_Translation" class="mw-redirect" title="Good News Translation">Good News Translation</a> (GNT), <a href="/wiki/Modern_English_Version" title="Modern English Version">Modern English Version</a> (MEV), and <a href="/wiki/New_International_Version" title="New International Version">New International Version</a> (NIV)) or as <i>Akel Dama</i> (<a href="/wiki/New_King_James_Version" title="New King James Version">New King James Version</a> (NKJV) and 1599 <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Bible" title="Geneva Bible">Geneva Bible</a>). <i>Aceldama</i> is used by the <a href="/wiki/King_James_Version" title="King James Version">King James Version</a> (KJV), <a href="/wiki/Darby_Bible" title="Darby Bible">Darby Bible</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wycliffe_Bible" class="mw-redirect" title="Wycliffe Bible">Wycliffe Bible</a>. <i>Hakeldama</i> is used by the <a href="/wiki/Common_English_Bible" title="Common English Bible">Common English Bible</a> (CEB), <a href="/wiki/New_Revised_Standard_Version" title="New Revised Standard Version">New Revised Standard Version</a> (NRSV) and <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Jewish_Bible" class="mw-redirect" title="Orthodox Jewish Bible">Orthodox Jewish Bible</a> (OJB), whilst the <a href="/wiki/Complete_Jewish_Bible" class="mw-redirect" title="Complete Jewish Bible">Complete Jewish Bible</a> (CJB) uses <i>Hakel-D'ma</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem_Bible" title="Jerusalem Bible">Jerusalem Bible</a> has <i>Hakeldama</i> but uses the English translation <i>Bloody Acre</i> in place of <i>Field of Blood</i>, which is otherwise consistently used as the English translation. In <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">Greek</a>, it is called ·ºâŒ∫ŒµŒªŒ¥Œ±ŒºŒ¨œá (<i>Hakeldamach</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Testament_connection">New Testament connection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akeldama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: New Testament connection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Christian tradition connects the place with Judas Iscariot, who betrayed <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> for 30 pieces of silver. There are two accounts of his death. 
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Gospel of Matthew</a> describes how Judas returned the money to the Temple authorities before hanging himself. Deeming it as blood money, and therefore illegal to put into their treasury, they used it instead to buy a field as a burial ground for foreigners: thus the place gained the name "the Field of Blood" (<span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew+27%3A7&amp;version=NRSV">Matthew 27:7</a></span>, and possibly with allusions to <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Zechariah+11%3A12%E2%80%9313&amp;version=NRSV">Zechariah 11:12‚Äì13</a></span> and <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Jeremiah+18%3A2%E2%80%933&amp;version=NRSV">Jeremiah 18:2‚Äì3</a></span> and <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Jeremiah+32%3A6%E2%80%9315&amp;version=NRSV">Jeremiah 32:6‚Äì15</a></span>). 
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts of the Apostles</a> (<span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Acts+1%3A18%E2%80%9319&amp;version=NRSV">Acts 1:18‚Äì19</a></span>) Judas "acquired a field with the reward of his unjust deed, and falling headfirst he burst open in the middle and all his intestines gushed out. This became known to all who lived in Jerusalem, so that in their own language they called that field Hakeldama, that is, 'Field of Blood.<span style="padding-right:0.15em;">&#39;</span>" 
</p><p>The implication in Matthew is that the location name refers to the blood of Jesus, whereas in Acts the name refers to the blood of Judas.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Barnabas_Lindars" title="Barnabas Lindars">Barnabas Lindars</a> holds the Acts narrative to be prior, and that although the incident is not created out of the Old Testament passages the text of <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Zechariah" title="Book of Zechariah">Zechariah</a> 11:12ff is "freely used to fill up the gaps in the story ... to the early Christian exegetes a perfectly legitimate <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hermeneutical" class="extiw" title="wikt:hermeneutical">hermeneutical</a> procedure".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/I._Howard_Marshall" title="I. Howard Marshall">Ian Howard Marshall</a>, Acts may be recording the (inaccurate) story as told within Jerusalem.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another view holds that the passages (Matthew 27:3-9, Acts 1:18) are complementary. Both in the Matthew and the Acts accounts share the essential details of Judas‚Äô betrayal, remorse, and suicide. Judas bought  the field inadvertently through the priests. Luke states that Judas ‚Äúfell head long and he burst open‚Äù. It has been suggested that the field could have been on a cliff and Judas‚Äô body could have fallen off or it could have been that Judas‚Äô body only rotted away. One interpretation connects the two, suggesting that after Judas hung himself, his decomposing corpse fell to the ground and burst open.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p><br />
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Identification">Identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akeldama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In his <i>Onomasticon</i> (ed. Klostermann, p.&#160;102, 16), <a href="/wiki/Eusebius" title="Eusebius">Eusebius</a> says the "field of Haceldama" lies nearer to "<a href="/wiki/Tophet" title="Tophet">Thafeth</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Gehenna" title="Gehenna">Valley of Ennom</a>". But under the word "Haceldama" (p.&#160;38, 20) he says that this field was pointed out as being "north of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Zion" title="Mount Zion">Mount Sion</a>". <a href="/wiki/St._Jerome" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Jerome">St. Jerome</a> changed this to "south of Mount Sion" (p.&#160;39, 27).
</p>

<p>The earth in this area is composed of rich clay and was formerly used by potters.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The clay had a strong red colour, which may be the origin of the modern name.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It continued to be used as a burial place for non-Jews up to the first quarter of the 19th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crusader_period">Crusader period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akeldama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Crusader period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the era of the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusades</a>, it was used to bury the fifty or more patients who died each day in the hospital run by the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Hospitaller" title="Knights Hospitaller">Knights Hospitaller</a> in Jerusalem.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 12th century, the <a href="/wiki/Crusade" class="mw-redirect" title="Crusade">crusaders</a> erected beyond the field, on the south side of the valley of Hinnom, a large building now in a ruined condition, measuring seventy-eight feet in length from east to west, fifty-eight feet in width and thirty in height on the north. It is roofed and covers towards the southern end several natural grottoes, which were once used as sepulchres of the Jewish type, and a ditch is hollowed out at the northern end which is sixty-eight feet long, twenty-one feet wide and thirty feet deep. It is estimated that the bones and rubbish accumulated there form a bed from ten to fifteen feet thick.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ottoman_period">Ottoman period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akeldama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ottoman period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Akeldama has been the property of the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Patriarchate_of_Jerusalem" title="Armenian Patriarchate of Jerusalem">Armenian Patriarchate of Jerusalem</a> since the 16th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aceldama_PA180088.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Aceldama_PA180088.JPG/200px-Aceldama_PA180088.JPG" decoding="async" width="200" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Aceldama_PA180088.JPG/300px-Aceldama_PA180088.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Aceldama_PA180088.JPG/400px-Aceldama_PA180088.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1712" data-file-height="2288" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aceldama_PA180088.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>St Onuphrius Monastery: close-up of the entrance. Above the doorway is a stone carving of St Onuphrius bowing to an angel. Noticeable are his long beard, the fact that he is naked except for leaves around his loins and his legs.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1892 the Greek Orthodox Church built a monastery at the site, named after Saint <a href="/wiki/Onuphrius" title="Onuphrius">Onuphrius</a>. Many burial caves have been identified in and around the monastery.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akeldama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Akeldama (<b>Hakl-ed-damm</b>)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (September 2018)">dubious</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Akeldama#What_kind_of_unsourced,_but_bold-lettered_etymology_is_that?" title="Talk:Akeldama">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup> today is a large, square sepulchre,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (September 2018)">dubious</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Akeldama#What_structure_does_this_whole_unsourced_paragraph_refer_to?_There_are_several_at_the_site." title="Talk:Akeldama">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup> of which the southern half is excavated in the rock, the remainder being built of massive masonry. In the center stands a huge pillar, constructed partly of rough blocks and partly of polished stones. Much of its clay was taken away by <a href="/wiki/Helena_of_Constantinople" class="mw-redirect" title="Helena of Constantinople">Empress Helena</a> and other prominent Christians, to make sarcophagi.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (September 2018)">dubious</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Akeldama#..._to_say_the_least:_clay_&quot;to_make_sarcophagi&quot;,_and_imperial_ones?_They_were_stone-made." title="Talk:Akeldama">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup> It lies on a narrow, level terrace on the south face of the valley of <a href="/wiki/Gehenna" title="Gehenna">Hinnom</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
