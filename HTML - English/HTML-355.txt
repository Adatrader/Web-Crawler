<p><b>Jens Otto Harry Jespersen</b> (<small>Danish:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Danish" title="Help:IPA/Danish">[ɒtsʰo ˈjɛspɐsn̩]</a></span>; 16 July 1860 – 30 April 1943) was a <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguist</a> who specialized in the grammar of the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Otto_Jespersen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Otto Jespersen was born in <a href="/wiki/Randers" title="Randers">Randers</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jutland" title="Jutland">Jutland</a>. He was inspired by the work of Danish philologist <a href="/wiki/Rasmus_Christian_Rask" class="mw-redirect" title="Rasmus Christian Rask">Rasmus Rask</a> as a boy, and with the help of Rask's grammars taught himself some Icelandic, Italian, and Spanish.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He entered the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Copenhagen" title="University of Copenhagen">University of Copenhagen</a> in 1877 when he was 17, initially studying law but not forgetting his language studies. In 1881 he shifted his focus completely to languages,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and in 1887 earned his master's degree in <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, with English and <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> as his secondary languages. He supported himself during his studies through part-time work as a schoolteacher and as a shorthand reporter in the Danish parliament. In 1887–1888, he traveled to England, Germany and France, meeting linguists like <a href="/wiki/Henry_Sweet" title="Henry Sweet">Henry Sweet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Passy" title="Paul Passy">Paul Passy</a> and attending lectures at institutions like <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">Oxford University</a>. Following the advice of his mentor <a href="/wiki/Vilhelm_Thomsen" title="Vilhelm Thomsen">Vilhelm Thomsen</a>, he returned to Copenhagen in August 1888 and began work on his doctoral dissertation on the English <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">case</a> system. He successfully defended his dissertation in 1891.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_life_and_work">Academic life and work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Otto_Jespersen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Academic life and work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jespersen was a professor of English at the University of Copenhagen from 1893 to 1925, and served as Rector of the university in 1920–21. His early work focused primarily on language teaching reform and on phonetics, but he is best known for his later work on syntax and on language development.
</p><p>He advanced the theories of <i>Rank</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Nexus_grammar" title="Nexus grammar">Nexus</a></i> in Danish in two papers: <i>Sprogets logik</i> (1913) and <i>De to hovedarter af grammatiske forbindelser</i> (1921). Jespersen in this theory of ranks removes the parts of speech from the syntax, and differentiates between  primaries, secondaries, and tertiaries; e.g. in "<i>well honed phrase</i>," "phrase" is a primary, this being defined by a secondary, "honed", which again is defined by a tertiary "well". The term <i>Nexus</i> is applied to sentences, structures similar to sentences and sentences in formation, in which two concepts are expressed in one unit; e.g., <i>it rained, he ran indoors</i>. This term is qualified by a further concept called a <i>junction</i> which represents one idea, expressed by means of two or more elements, whereas a nexus combines two ideas.  Junction and nexus proved valuable in bringing the concept of context to the forefront of the attention of the world of linguistics.
</p><p>He was most widely recognized for some of his books. <i>Language: Its Nature, Development and Origin</i> (1922) is considered by many to be his masterpiece.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <i>Modern English Grammar on Historical Principles</i> (1909–1949), concentrated on morphology and syntax, and <i>Growth and Structure of the English Language</i> (1905) is a comprehensive view of English by someone with another native language, and still in print, over 70 years after his death and more than 100 years after publication. Late in his life he published <i>Analytic Syntax</i> (1937), in which he presents his views on syntactic structure using an idiosyncratic shorthand notation. In <i>The Philosophy of Grammar</i> (1924) he challenged the accepted views of common concepts in <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">Grammar</a> and proposed corrections to the basic definitions of <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">grammatical case</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">pronoun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">object</a>, <a href="/wiki/Voice_(grammar)" title="Voice (grammar)">voice</a> etc., and developed further his notions of <i>Rank</i> and <i>Nexus</i>. In the 21st century this book is still used as one of the basic texts in modern <a href="/wiki/Structural_linguistics" title="Structural linguistics">Structural linguistics</a>. <i>Mankind, Nation and Individual: from a linguistic point of view</i> (1925) is one of the pioneering works on <a href="/wiki/Sociolinguistics" title="Sociolinguistics">Sociolinguistics</a>.
</p><p>Jespersen visited the United States twice: he lectured at the Congress of Arts and Sciences in St. Louis in 1904, and in 1909–1910 he visited both the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California" title="University of California">University of California</a> and <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> While in the U.S., he took occasion to study the country's educational system. His autobiography (see below) was published in English translation as recently as 1995.
</p><p>After his retirement in 1925, Jespersen remained active in the international linguistic community. In addition to continuing to write, he convened and chaired the first International Meeting on Linguistic Research in Geneva in 1930, and acted as president of the Fourth International Congress of Linguists in Copenhagen in 1936.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Jespersen was an important figure in the <a href="/wiki/Universal_language" title="Universal language">international language</a> movement. He was an early supporter of the <a href="/wiki/Esperanto" title="Esperanto">Esperanto</a> offshoot <a href="/wiki/Ido_(language)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ido (language)">Ido</a> and in 1927 published his own project <a href="/wiki/Novial" title="Novial">Novial</a>. He also worked with the <a href="/wiki/International_Auxiliary_Language_Association" title="International Auxiliary Language Association">International Auxiliary Language Association</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Jespersen received honorary degrees from Columbia University in New York (1910), St. Andrews University in Scotland (1925), and the Sorbonne in Paris (1927).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> He was one of the first six international scholars to be elected as honorary members of the <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_Society_of_America" title="Linguistic Society of America">Linguistic Society of America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
