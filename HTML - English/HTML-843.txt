	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Copyright law of Australia</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">&#160;&#160;(Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_copyright_law&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian copyright law">Australian copyright law</a>)</span></div>
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>

The <b>copyright law of Australia </b> defines the legally enforceable rights of creators of creative and artistic works under <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> law. The scope of <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> in Australia is defined in the <a href="#Copyright_Act_1968">Australian Copyright Act 1968</a> (as amended), which applies the national law throughout Australia. Designs may be covered by the Copyright Act (as sculptures or drawings) as well as by the <a href="/wiki/Registered_Designs" class="mw-redirect" title="Registered Designs">Design Act</a>. Since 2007, <a href="/wiki/Performing_arts" title="Performing arts">performers</a> have <a href="/wiki/Moral_rights" title="Moral rights">moral rights</a> in recordings of their work.
</p><p>Until 2004, copyright in Australia was based on the "plus 50" law which restricts works until 50 years after the author's death. In 2004 this was changed to a "plus 70" law in line with the USA and <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, but this change was not made retroactive (unlike the 1995 change in the European Union which brought some e.g. British authors back into copyright).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The consequence is that the work of an Australian author who died before 1955 is normally in the public domain in Australia. However the copyright of authors was extended to 70 years after death for those who died in 1955 or later, so that no more Australian authors will come out of copyright until 1 January 2026 (i.e. those who died in 1955). 
</p><p>The Australian legislation is based on the authority of section 51(xviii) of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian Constitution">Australian Constitution</a>. <a href="/wiki/Copyright_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Copyright law">Copyright law</a> in Australia is <a href="/wiki/Federal_law" title="Federal law">federal law</a> and established by the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian Parliament">Australian Parliament</a>. Historically, Australian copyright law followed <a href="/wiki/British_copyright_law" class="mw-redirect" title="British copyright law">British copyright law</a>, but now also reflects international standards found in the <a href="/wiki/Berne_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_Literary_and_Artistic_Works" class="mw-redirect" title="Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works">Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works</a>, other <a href="/wiki/International_copyright_agreements" class="mw-redirect" title="International copyright agreements">international copyright agreements</a> and multilateral treaties, and more recently, the <a href="/wiki/U.S.-Australia_Free_Trade_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S.-Australia Free Trade Agreement">U.S.-Australia Free Trade Agreement</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Past">Past</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copyright_law_of_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Past">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Australian copyright law has historically been influenced by <a href="/wiki/British_copyright_law" class="mw-redirect" title="British copyright law">British copyright law</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_copyright_agreements" class="mw-redirect" title="International copyright agreements">International copyright agreements</a>. In turn Australian copyright law has influenced copyright law in Britain and the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a>. Australian copyright law originates in British copyright law which was established by the <a href="/wiki/British_parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="British parliament">British parliament</a> through the Australian Courts Act 1828.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The British <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Anne" title="Statute of Anne">Statute of Anne</a> 1709, which awarded copyright protection to books, acted as a blueprint for the extension of copyright to new types of subject matter in the 18th and 19th Century. When copyright law was introduced into Australia in 1928 <a href="/wiki/British_copyright_law" class="mw-redirect" title="British copyright law">British copyright law</a> had been extended beyond <a href="/wiki/Literary_property" title="Literary property">literary property</a> to include <a href="/wiki/Engravings" class="mw-redirect" title="Engravings">engravings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sculptures" class="mw-redirect" title="Sculptures">sculptures</a>. Over the course of the 19th century it was extended to other works, including <a href="/wiki/Paintings" class="mw-redirect" title="Paintings">paintings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drawings" class="mw-redirect" title="Drawings">drawings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Photographs" class="mw-redirect" title="Photographs">photographs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Prior to Australia's federation in 1901, a number of <a href="/wiki/Australian_Colonies" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian Colonies">Australian Colonies</a>, later states, had enacted copyright laws. In part this was done to mitigate the inadequacy of the protection afforded to Australian authors by <a href="/wiki/British_copyright_law" class="mw-redirect" title="British copyright law">British copyright law</a>. The state laws continued to apply after the federal <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Commonwealth of Australia">Commonwealth of Australia</a> was established in 1901. The laws operated in concurrency with the British copyright law that was in force in the colonies. The <a href="/wiki/Australian_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian Constitution">Australian Constitution</a> gives the <a href="/wiki/Australian_parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian parliament">federal parliament</a> power to make laws relating to <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property" title="Intellectual property">intellectual property</a>, concurrently with the states. Section 51(xviii) of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian constitution">Commonwealth Constitution</a> provides that "the Parliament shall, subject to this Constitution, have power to make laws for the peace, order, and good government of the Commonwealth with respect to, inter alia, copyright, patents of inventions and designs, and trademarks". As an immediate consequence copyright law was no longer established at state level, but by the federal parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first Australian copyright statute enacted at the federal level was the Copyright Act 1905, which was a departure from <a href="/wiki/British_copyright_law" class="mw-redirect" title="British copyright law">British copyright law</a>. Australia became part of the British imperial copyright system on 1 July 1912 when the Australian Copyright Act 1912 adopted the British <a href="/wiki/Copyright_Act_1911" title="Copyright Act 1911">Copyright Act 1911</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The British 1911 Act applied throughout the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, including independent countries such as Australia, Canada, New Zealand and South Africa.
</p><p>The 1911 Act made important changes in copyright law and practice. The 1911 Act abolished <a href="/wiki/Common_law_copyright" title="Common law copyright">common law copyright</a> in unpublished works, hence completing the process that began with the 1774 <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> decision in <a href="/wiki/Donaldson_v_Beckett" class="mw-redirect" title="Donaldson v Beckett">Donaldson v Beckett</a>, which held that copyright was a creature of statute. The scope of the imperial copyright system (by changes in the UK Act) was expanded to include <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sound_recordings" class="mw-redirect" title="Sound recordings">sound recordings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Motion_pictures" class="mw-redirect" title="Motion pictures">motion pictures</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Copyright_Act_1968">Copyright Act 1968</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copyright_law_of_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Copyright Act 1968">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The British Copyright Act 1911 continued to apply in Australia until the Australian <a href="/wiki/Australian_Copyright_Act_1968" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian Copyright Act 1968">Copyright Act 1968</a> came into force on 1 May 1969. The 1968 Act was enacted following the collapse of the imperial system after the passage of the British <a href="/wiki/Copyright_Act_1956" title="Copyright Act 1956">Copyright Act 1956</a>, and following recommendations of the Spicer Committee, which had been appointed by the Australian Attorney-General in 1958 to review the 1912 Act to see what changes were necessary for Australia to ratify the Brussels Act of the <a href="/wiki/Berne_Convention" title="Berne Convention">Berne Convention</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1968 Act remains in force today, but has been amended on a number of occasions. The first major review occurred in 1974 when the <a href="/wiki/Whitlam_Government" title="Whitlam Government">Whitlam Government</a> appointed the Copyright Law Committee, chaired by Justice Franki, to examine the impact of <a href="/wiki/Reprography" title="Reprography">reprographic reproduction</a> on copyright law in Australia. The committee was also asked to examine the impact of <a href="/wiki/Photocopying" class="mw-redirect" title="Photocopying">photocopying</a> and "to recommend any alterations to the Australian copyright law to effect a proper balance of interest between owners of copyright and the users of copyright material in respect of reprographic reproduction."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During its deliberation the Franki Committee observed that because Australia was a net importer of copyrighted works it should be careful to not adopt too radical solutions. The Franki Committee recommended, amongst others, the adoption of a statutory licensing scheme. When commencing its review the Committee stated that the primary purpose of copyright law was:<sup id="cite_ref-auto2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"...to give to the author of a creative work his just reward for the benefit he has bestowed on the community and also to encourage the making of further creative works. On the other hand, as copyright in the nature of a monopoly, the law should ensure, as far as possible, that the rights conferred are not abused and that study, research and education are not unduly hampered."<sup id="cite_ref-auto2_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span id="Copyright_Law_Review_Committee_.28CLRC.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Copyright_Law_Review_Committee_(CLRC)">Copyright Law Review Committee (CLRC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copyright_law_of_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Copyright Law Review Committee (CLRC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1980s and 1990s saw a range of inquiries into many aspects of copyright law. A key driver for those reviews was the establishment of the Copyright Law Review Committee (CLRC) in 1983 as an advisory body for copyright reform. The CLRC was disbanded in 2005 by the Australian government after it had produced a number of reports. Notable reports include: <i>The meaning of Publication in the Copyright Act</i> (1984), <i>Use of Copyright materials by Churches</i> (1985), <i>Performers' Protection</i> (1987), <i>Moral Rights</i> (1988), <i>Report of Journalists' Copyright</i> (1994), <i>Computer Software Protection</i> (1994), <i>Simplification of the Copyright Act: Part 1</i> (1998), <i>Simplification of the Copyright Act: Part 2</i> (1999), <i>Jurisdiction and Procedures of the Copyright Tribunal</i> (2002), <i>Copyright and Contract</i> (2002) and <i>Crown Copyright</i> (2005). The CLRC also published reports on specific areas of copyright, including <i>Highways to Change: Copyright in the New Communications Environment: report by the Copyright Convergence Group on technological advancement and the ability of legislation to cope with change</i> (1994), <i>Stopping the Rip-Offs: intellectual Property Protection for Aboriginal &amp; Torres Strait Islander Peoples</i> (1994), the <i>Simpson Report</i> 1995, long title <i>Review of Australian Copyright Collecting Societies</i>, the <i>Bently and Sherman Report</i> 1995, long title <i>Performers' Rights: Options for Reform</i>, the <i>Janke Report 1999</i>, long title <i>Our Culture, Our Future</i>, and the <i>Ergas Report</i> 2000, long title <i>Report on Intellectual Property legislation under Competition Principles Agreement</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-auto2_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Copyright_Amendment_Act_2006">Copyright Amendment Act 2006</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copyright_law_of_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Copyright Amendment Act 2006">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Copyright Amendment Act 2006 made changes required by the <a href="/wiki/US-Australia_Free_Trade_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="US-Australia Free Trade Agreement">US-Australia Free Trade Agreement</a>. In particular, it strengthened anti-circumvention laws, for the first time making it illegal in Australia to circumvent technical measures used by copyright owners to restrict access to their works, and expanding the measures which count as technological restriction measures which may not be circumvented.  Like the FTA language, the new anti-circumvention law is closely modeled on the US <a href="/wiki/Digital_Millennium_Copyright_Act" title="Digital Millennium Copyright Act">Digital Millennium Copyright Act</a>, although it is not identical.
</p><p>The Act also introduced a series of new exceptions into Australian copyright law.  The best known are the private copying exceptions, which follow on from proposals by former <a href="/wiki/Philip_Ruddock" title="Philip Ruddock">Attorney-General Philip Ruddock</a> to allow people to record most television or radio program at home to watch at a later time with family or friends, and to <a href="/wiki/Format-shift" class="mw-redirect" title="Format-shift">format-shift</a> their music (make copies from CDs onto personal computers and portable music players).  Unlike some countries in Europe, or Canada, there is no fee or licence payment on players to compensate copyright owners for these private copies, although the exceptions are narrowly defined and do not allow, for example, making copies for friends or family.  The Act also introduced a copyright exception allowing parody and satire, and an exception to allow certain non-commercial use by public sector institutions like universities, schools, art galleries and archives, provided that an Australian court decides an exception would be consistent with the <a href="/wiki/Berne_three-step_test" title="Berne three-step test">Berne three-step test</a>.
</p><p>The other notable change made by the Act was to expand the provisions concerning criminal copyright infringement.  The Act introduced strict liability offences for some copyright infringements, and a system of 'Infringement Notices' (on the spot fines).  The stated aim of these provisions is to make copyright easier to enforce, particularly against commercial infringers.  After concerns from user groups and the Senate Standing Committee on Legal and Constitutional Affairs,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> many strict liability offences that would have applied to non-commercial acts were removed from the final bill.
</p>
<h2><span id="Protected_subject_matter.2C_exclusive_rights_and_infringement"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Protected_subject_matter,_exclusive_rights_and_infringement">Protected subject matter, exclusive rights and infringement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copyright_law_of_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Protected subject matter, exclusive rights and infringement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Australian copyright law has been influenced significantly by the structure of English law, in addition to the <a href="/wiki/Berne_Convention" title="Berne Convention">Berne Convention</a> and other international copyright treaties.  Thus there is an exhaustive set of types of material protected, and an exhaustive set of exclusive rights.
</p><p>In terms of the <i>types of material</i>, Australian law confers rights in works, also known as "Part III Works" (after the Part of the Act dealing with this): namely, literary works, musical works, artistic works, and dramatic works.  It also confers rights in "other subject matter" (Part IV Subject Matter), which cover the kinds of material protected in some countries by 'neighbouring rights': sound recordings, films, broadcasts, and published editions.  To be protected, material must fall into one of these exclusive categories.  The rights in Part IV subject matters are more limited, because infringement requires exact copying of the actual subject matter (sound-alikes or remakes are not covered).
</p><p>In terms of the <i>exclusive rights</i>, different kinds of subject matter have different rights.  Owners of copyright in works have rights to reproduce, publish (meaning publish for the first time), perform, and adapt the work, and communicate it to the public (including broadcast, or communicate by making available online).  The rights of owners of copyright in artistic works are more limited (there is no right to control public display of artistic works).  Owners of copyright in other subject matter have the exclusive right to make copies, to communicate them to the public, and to cause them to be heard/seen in public.
</p><p>Infringement occurs where a person does an act falling within the copyright owner's exclusive rights, without the authorisation of the copyright owner (assuming that one of the exceptions does not apply).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Copyright_term">Copyright term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copyright_law_of_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Copyright term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Copyright_expiration_in_Australia" title="Copyright expiration in Australia">Copyright expiration in Australia</a></div>
<p>Before the 2004 Amendments, Australia used a "plus 50" rule to determine when a work entered the <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a>. Put simply, a "work" (i.e. a literary, dramatic, musical or artistic work) entered the public domain 50 years following the year of the creator's death, with exceptions. The 2006 Amendments changed the benchmark to "plus 70". This brought Australia into line with the United States of America, the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, and certain other jurisdictions; but is longer than the "plus 50" minimum required by the Berne Convention and still applied in many other jurisdictions, including New Zealand, Papua New Guinea, Canada, and many other Commonwealth countries, as well as China, Japan, and South Korea.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The extension to "plus 70" does not apply to crown copyright, to which the "plus 50" rule continues to apply.
</p><p>Similar to the foreign reciprocity clause in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union_copyright_law" class="mw-redirect" title="European Union copyright law">European Union copyright law</a>, the change to the "plus 70" rule is not retroactive, so that if copyright has expired before the coming into force of the amendment it is not revived. The result is that:
</p>
<ul><li>Any work that was published in the lifetime of the author who died before 1 January 1955, is out of copyright.</li>
<li>Any work that was published in the lifetime of the author who died after 31 December 1954, will be out of copyright 70 years after the author's death.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Now, in Australia, in accordance with s210 of the <i>Copyright Act 1968</i>, copyright does not apply to works that were published before 1 May 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Australia, according to s33 of the <i>Copyright Act 1968</i>, any literary, dramatic, musical or artistic work that was published after the death of the author will continue to subsist under copyright 70 years after the year of first publication.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> e.g. If a work is published 10 years after the authors death, copyright would subsist for 70 years after first publication, i.e. 80 years after author's death.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> It should also be noted indefinite copyright does not apply to artistic works.
</p><p>Photographs, sound recordings, films, and anonymous/pseudonymous works are copyright for 70 years from their first publication. Television and sound broadcasts are copyright for only fifty years after the year of their first broadcast (though the material contained in the broadcast may be separately copyrighted). Most other works are also dated from the first publication/broadcast/performance where this occurred after the author's death.
</p><p>The <i>Copyright (Disabilities and Other Measures) Act</i>, which was passed on 15 June 2017, abolished the indefinite copyright term for unpublished works. As of 1 January 2019, unpublished works out of copyright 70 years after the author's death if the author is known, or 70 years after creation otherwise.<sup id="cite_ref-2017_Act_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017_Act-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The period of 70 years is counted from the end of the relevant calendar year.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Sonny_Bono_Copyright_Term_Extension_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Sonny Bono Copyright Term Extension Act">Sonny Bono Copyright Term Extension Act</a> (1998) defines an entirely different rule based on the year of first publication in the USA. Generally, anything published before 1923 is in the <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a>. An interesting consequence of this for the Internet is that a work may be in the public domain in the US but not in Australia, or vice versa. It is important to note that except for the works falling under the "<a href="/wiki/Rule_of_the_shorter_term" title="Rule of the shorter term">Rule of the shorter term</a>", copyright does not depend on the country of origin, the country of publication, or the nationality of the author. A work published in the US by a British author may still be in the public domain in Australia if the author died more than 70 years ago or died before 1955, whichever is the shorter.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fair_dealing">Fair dealing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copyright_law_of_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fair dealing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main exceptions to copyright infringement in Australia come under the general heading <a href="/wiki/Fair_dealing" title="Fair dealing">fair dealing</a>.  Fair dealing is comparable to the United States' <a href="/wiki/Fair_use" title="Fair use">fair use</a>; it is a use of a work specifically recognised as not being a copyright violation.  In order to be a fair dealing under Australian law a use must fall within a range of specific purposes. These purposes vary by type of work, but the possibilities are:
</p>
<ul><li>review or criticism</li>
<li>research or study</li>
<li>news-reporting</li>
<li>judicial proceedings or professional legal advice</li>
<li>parody or satire (added by the Copyright Amendment Act 2006)</li></ul>
<p>In order for a certain use to be a fair dealing, it must fall within one of these purposes and must also be 'fair'. What is fair will depend on all the circumstances, including the nature of the work, the nature of the use and the effect of the use on any commercial market for the work.
</p><p>Fair dealing is not the same as fair use. This has, for example, been interpreted by US courts to allow for reasonable personal use of works, e.g. media-shifting, which would not necessarily be permitted under Australia's fair dealing laws.  Australian copyright law does, however, have a number of additional specific exceptions which permit uses which may fall outside of both fair dealing and fair use. For example, a number of exceptions exist which permit specific uses of computer software.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fair_Use_proposals">Fair Use proposals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copyright_law_of_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fair Use proposals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Fair_Use_proposals_in_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="History of Fair Use proposals in Australia">History of Fair Use proposals in Australia</a></div>
<p>While Australian copyright exceptions are based on the Fair Dealing system, Since 1998 a series of Australian government inquiries have examined, and in most cases recommended, the introduction of a "flexible and open" Fair Use system into Australian copyright law. From 1998 to 2017 there have been eight Australian government inquiries which have considered the question of whether fair use should be adopted in Australia. Six reviews have recommended Australia adopt a "Fair Use" model of copyright exceptions:<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> two enquiries specifically into the Copyright Act (1998, 2014); and four broader reviews (both 2004, 2013, 2016).  One review (2000) recommended against the introduction of fair use and another (2005) issued no final report.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Two of the recommendations were specifically in response to the stricter copyright rules introduced as part of the <a href="/wiki/Australia%E2%80%93United_States_Free_Trade_Agreement" title="Australia–United States Free Trade Agreement">Australia–United States Free Trade Agreement</a> (AUSFTA), while the most recent two, by the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Law_Reform_Commission" title="Australian Law Reform Commission">Australian Law Reform Commission</a> (ALRC) and the <a href="/wiki/Productivity_Commission" title="Productivity Commission">Productivity Commission</a> (PC) were with reference to strengthening Australia's "digital economy".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_exceptions">Other exceptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copyright_law_of_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other exceptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In late 2006, Australia added several 'private copying' exceptions.  It is no longer an infringement of copyright to record a broadcast to watch or listen at a more convenient time (s 111), or to make a copy of a sound recording for private and domestic use (e.g., copy onto a portable media player) (s 109A), or make a copy of a literary work, magazine, or newspaper article for private use (43C).
</p><p>Australia also has:
</p>
<ul><li>a special division of exceptions applying to computer programs (for interoperability, security testing, normal use),</li>
<li>a special division of exceptions applying to artworks in public places (to allow photography, incidental filming etc.) (see <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_Panorama" class="mw-redirect" title="Freedom of Panorama">Freedom of Panorama</a>),</li>
<li>statutory (i.e. compulsory) licences that allow use by schools, universities, and others on payment of a licence fee set either by agreement or by the Copyright Tribunal (see below).</li></ul>
<p>Because Australian copyright law recognises temporary copies stored in computer memory as 'reproductions' falling within the copyright owner's exclusive rights, there are also various exceptions for temporary copies made in the ordinary course of use or communication of digital copies of works.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Moral_rights">Moral rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copyright_law_of_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Moral rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2000, <a href="/wiki/Moral_rights_(copyright_law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Moral rights (copyright law)">moral rights</a> were recognised in Australian copyright legislation. Only individuals may exercise moral rights.  The moral rights provided under Australian law now are:
</p>
<ul><li>A right of attribution
<ul><li>the right to be clearly and reasonably prominently identified as the author, in any reasonable form</li>
<li>the right to avoid false attribution, where the work is falsely presented as being another's work</li></ul></li>
<li>Integrity of authorship
<ul><li>the right to not have the work treated in a derogatory manner (this is a right to protect the honour and reputation of the author)</li></ul></li></ul>
<p>There are also proposals in Australia for the recognition of "Indigenous communal moral rights", aimed at assisting indigenous people to protect the integrity and sanctity of indigenous culture.  A draft bill was circulated to a limited set of stakeholders in 2003; since then the Bill has been listed as one the government planned to pass, but has yet to be introduced.
</p><p>From mid-2007, performers have also been granted moral rights in recordings of their performances, similar, but not identical, to the moral rights granted to authors.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  These were introduced as a result of Australia's ratification of the <a href="/wiki/WIPO_Performances_and_Phonograms_Treaty" title="WIPO Performances and Phonograms Treaty">WIPO Performances and Phonograms Treaty</a>, which was required by the Australia's free trade agreement with Singapore, and the <a href="/wiki/Australia-United_States_Free_Trade_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="Australia-United States Free Trade Agreement">Australia-United States Free Trade Agreement</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ownership_of_copyright">Ownership of copyright</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copyright_law_of_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ownership of copyright">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Copyright is free and automatic upon creation of the work. In general, the first owner of copyright will be the author (for literary, musical, dramatic and artistic works) or producer (for sound recordings and films) or broadcaster (for broadcasts). Under Australian law, where an employee is the author, the first owner of copyright is the employer (this is slightly different from the US works-made-for-hire doctrine: in Australia, duration of copyright is still measured by the lifetime of the employee author). In 2004–2005, Australia also introduced some complicated provisions that give performers part ownership rights in sound recordings, and directors some limited ownership rights in relation to films.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the case of a photograph commissioned for a "private or domestic purpose", the copyright will be owned by the commissioner of the work. The "private and domestic purposes" condition took effect on 30 July 1998, prior to this the copyright of all commissioned photographs was assigned to the commissioner.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Copyright_notice" title="Copyright notice">copyright notice</a> (©) is not required on a work to gain copyright, but only the copyright owner is entitled to place a notice. It is useful in publishing the date of first publication and the owner. Where a copyright notice is used, the onus in infringement proceedings is on the defendant to show that copyright does not subsist or is not owned by the person stated in the notice.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government-owned_copyright">Government-owned copyright</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copyright_law_of_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Government-owned copyright">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Crown_Copyright" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown Copyright">Crown Copyright</a></div>
<p>The Australian Commonwealth and State governments routinely own copyright in Australia.  While this could be seen as being due to the concept of the Crown being traditionally paramount rather than the people,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> it is more influenced by the then <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">British Commonwealth</a> acting as a copyright policy-making body in the 1950s, which was the basis of the 1968 Copyright Act.
</p><p>The Australian government does not infringe copyright if its actions (or those of an authorised person) are for the government.  A "relevant collecting society" may sample government copies and charge the government.
</p><p>The State governments follow different practices in regard to <a href="/wiki/Licensing" class="mw-redirect" title="Licensing">licensing</a>, fees and <a href="/wiki/Waiver" title="Waiver">waivers</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Attorney_General_of_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Attorney General of Australia">Australian Attorney-General</a>'s Copyright Law Review Committee completed a large review of Crown Copyright in April 2005.  In summary, the Committee recommended that the Crown be treated like any other employer (i.e., owner of material produced by its employees), and that for certain materials (legislation, government reports, commissions of inquiry reports) either copyright be removed, or a generous and generalised licence be granted for re-use.  As of early 2007, several governments appear to be considering the use of open licences modelled along the lines of the <a href="/wiki/Creative_Commons" title="Creative Commons">Creative Commons</a> model.
</p><p>Copyrights owned by the Crown in Australia have different durations to publicly held copyrights, as below:
</p>
<table>
<tbody><tr>
<td>Published literary, dramatic or musical works (includes published official records)
</td>
<td>50 years after the end of the year in which the work is first published
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>Unpublished literary, dramatic, musical works
</td>
<td>Copyright subsists indefinitely (see below)
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>Artistic works
</td>
<td>50 years from the end of the year when made
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>Photographs
</td>
<td>50 years from the end of the year when made
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<ul><li>The indefinite copyright term for unpublished works was abolished by the Copyright (Disabilities and Other Measures) Act 2017. This part of the Act comes into effect on 1 January 2019. As of that date, Crown Copyright for unpublished works expires 50 years after it was made.<sup id="cite_ref-2017_Act_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017_Act-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composite_copyright">Composite copyright</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copyright_law_of_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Composite copyright">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Material can contain multiple copyrights, that are not diminished by their combination or mingling. For example, each portion of a television broadcast is treated separately, such as the visual images, soundtrack, and any scripts.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Copyright_Tribunal">Copyright Tribunal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copyright_law_of_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Copyright Tribunal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Copyright Tribunal was established under the Copyright Act 1968, and has certain powers relating to royalties and licensing. It receives operational support from the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Court_of_Australia" title="Federal Court of Australia">Federal Court of Australia</a>.
</p><p>It has been suggested that it is somewhat more sympathetic to rights-owner interests rather than user interests; for instance, by ruling to increase royalty rates under the Part VB Educational Statutory licence without a submission to this effect from either party in c.2003. This would normally be considered unusual in a common-law <a href="/wiki/Adversarial_system" title="Adversarial system">adversarial system</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collecting_societies">Collecting societies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copyright_law_of_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Collecting societies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of copyright collecting societies operate in Australia.  Collecting societies are established to collect royalties for uses of copyright material on behalf of authors and copyright owners: they assist to overcome the significant transaction costs that would face individual creators in monitoring, enforcing, and licensing their rights.  A notable feature of some of the Australian collecting societies is that some are 'declared' to be the society with the function of being the sole collector of royalties under the statutory licences.  The collecting societies in Australia are:
</p>
<ul><li>Australasian Mechanical Copyright Owners Society Ltd (AMCOS): represents music publishers and writers from for rights in the reproduction of musical works;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Australasian_Performing_Right_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="Australasian Performing Right Association">Australasian Performing Right Association</a> (APRA): collects royalties for the owners of copyright in musical works for the performance of their musical works;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_Copyright_Licensing_International" title="Christian Copyright Licensing International">Christian Copyright Licensing International</a> (CCLI)</li>
<li>Copyright Agency Limited (CAL): collects royalties for the reproduction of printed material, and in particular, administers the statutory licences that allow educational institutions to copy and communicate printed material;
<ul><li>Educational institutions undertook to pay "equitable remuneration" to CAL for their right to make copies of literacy, dramatic, musical and artistic works for educational purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
<ul><li>"Equitable remuneration" is should be fair and reasonable or just in all circumstances to both the copyright owner and the licensee.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>New equitable remuneration rates were decided with a starting point from the 1985 rate of 2 cents per page copied and adjusted for the Consumer Price Index, then further increased, on the basis of changing methods of teaching, technology and volume of copying. The new rate came into operation on 1 January 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li></ul></li>
<li>Phonographic Performance Company of Australia Ltd (PPCA): collects royalties for performers for the broadcast, communication or public playing of recorded music;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Screenrights:_The_Audio_Visual_Copyright_Society" title="Screenrights: The Audio Visual Copyright Society">Screenrights</a>: set up in 1990 to administer statutory licences that let educational institutions copy from TV and radio for teaching;</li>
<li>Visual Arts Copyright Collecting Agency (VISCOPY): licenses the copyright in artistic works.</li></ul>

<ul><li>The High Court struck down the 1989 levy as, essentially, badly located and unfair tax law and not a royalty.</li></ul></li>
<li>1994 (3x)&#160;&#8211;&#32;various amendments</li>
<li>14 August 1997&#160;&#8211;&#32;<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telstra_Corporation_Ltd_v_Australasian_Performing_Right_Association_Ltd&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Telstra Corporation Ltd v Australasian Performing Right Association Ltd (page does not exist)">Telstra Corporation Ltd v Australasian Performing Right Association Ltd</a></i> ("music on hold case").<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
<ul><li>The High Court found that music-on-hold transmitted by <a href="/wiki/Telstra" title="Telstra">Telstra</a> was a copyright infringement</li></ul></li>
<li>1998 (3x), and 1999 (2x)&#160;&#8211;&#32;various amendments</li>
<li>2000&#160;&#8211;&#32;<i>Copyright Amendment (Digital Agenda) Act</i></li>
<li>2000&#160;&#8211;&#32;<i>Copyright Amendment (Moral Rights) Act</i></li>
<li>2001&#160;&#8211;&#32;<i>Law and Justice Legislation Amendment (Application of Criminal Code) Act</i>
<ul><li>26 July 2002&#160;&#8211;&#32;<i>Kabushiki Kaisha Sony Computer Entertainment v Stevens</i><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Federal Court decides that <a href="/wiki/Modchip" title="Modchip">mod chips</a> for Sony <a href="/wiki/Game_console" class="mw-redirect" title="Game console">game consoles</a> do not contravene copyright, due to representations from the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Competition_and_Consumer_Commission" title="Australian Competition and Consumer Commission">Australian Competition and Consumer Commission</a> (ACCC) as <i><a href="/wiki/Amicus_curiae" title="Amicus curiae">amicus curiae</a></i>.</li></ul></li>
<li>Federal Court decision ultimately upheld by the High Court in <i><a href="/wiki/Stevens_v_Kabushiki_Kaisha_Sony_Computer_Entertainment" title="Stevens v Kabushiki Kaisha Sony Computer Entertainment">Stevens v Kabushiki Kaisha Sony Computer Entertainment</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>2003&#160;&#8211;&#32;<i>Copyright Amendment (Parallel Importation) Act</i>
<ul><li>Made some provisions for parallel importing, affecting the '<a href="/wiki/Grey_market" title="Grey market">grey market</a>'.</li></ul></li>
<li>2003&#160;&#8211;&#32;<i>Designs (Consequential Amendments) Act</i></li>
<li>November 2003&#160;&#8211;&#32;Three Australian students received criminal convictions for copyright infringement, receiving a mix of suspended sentences, a fine, and community service.</li>
<li>7 February 2004&#160;&#8211;&#32;<a href="/wiki/KaZaA" class="mw-redirect" title="KaZaA">KaZaA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Sharman_Networks" title="Sharman Networks">Sharman Networks</a> and Brilliant Digital Entertainment in Australia were raided for copyright violations using <a href="/wiki/Anton_Piller_order" title="Anton Piller order">Anton Piller orders</a>, along with the <a href="/wiki/University_of_New_South_Wales" title="University of New South Wales">University of NSW</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Queensland" title="University of Queensland">University of Queensland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monash_University" title="Monash University">Monash University</a>, Telstra BigPond and three Sydney Internet service providers.  The investigation was backed by <a href="/wiki/Universal_Music_Group" title="Universal Music Group">Universal</a>, <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bertelsmann_Music_Group" title="Bertelsmann Music Group">BMG</a>, <a href="/wiki/Festival_Mushroom_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Festival Mushroom Records">Festival Mushroom Records</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warner_Music" class="mw-redirect" title="Warner Music">Warner Music</a>.</li>
<li>8 February 2004 - Australia and the United States agree to text of bilateral free trade agreement (AUSFTA). The copyright-related parts of the Intellectual Property Chapter were:
<ul><li>Longer duration of copyright</li>
<li>Agreed standards for: copyright protection, copyright infringement, remedies and penalties</li>
<li>WIPO Internet Treaties to be implemented by "entry into force" of the FTA</li>
<li>Fast-tracking copyright owners' engagement with <a href="/wiki/Internet_Service_Provider" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet Service Provider">Internet Service Providers</a> and subscribers to deal with allegedly infringing copyright material on the Internet</li>
<li>Tighter controls on circumventing technological restriction of copyrighted material, with a possibility of public submissions</li>
<li>Tougher on unauthorised satellite Pay-TV signal decoding</li></ul></li>
<li>9 February 2004&#160;&#8211;&#32;Australia and the United States sign the FTA.</li>
<li>August 2004&#160;&#8211;&#32; US FTA Implementation Act passes Senate, with amendments. References to documents and commentary.</li>
<li>November 2004&#160;&#8211;&#32;<a href="/wiki/KaZaA" class="mw-redirect" title="KaZaA">KaZaA</a> case starts in Federal Court.</li>
<li>December 2004&#160;&#8211;&#32;Copyright Legislation Amendment Act passes, affecting parallel importing, temporary copies and <a href="/wiki/Internet_Service_Provider" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet Service Provider">Internet Service Providers</a>' liability for taking down alleged infringing material.</li>
<li>1 January 2005&#160;&#8211;&#32;The <a href="/wiki/U.S.%E2%80%93Australia_Free_Trade_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S.–Australia Free Trade Agreement">U.S.–Australia Free Trade Agreement</a> (FTA) officially comes into force.</li>
<li>September 2005&#160;&#8211;&#32;Federal Court of Australia finds <a href="/wiki/Kazaa" title="Kazaa">Kazaa</a> liable for copyright infringement and hands down judgment in favour of Universal Music Australia Pty Ltd.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>December 2006&#160;&#8211;&#32;<i>Copyright Amendment Act</i> is passed.  Effective January 2007, the Act (a) strengthens criminal infringement provisions, (b) adds new exceptions including for parody or satire, and private copying, (c) strengthens anti-circumvention law to make it more like the US Digital Millennium Copyright Act (as required by the Australia–US FTA), and (d) makes some changes to provisions affecting libraries and educational institutions.</li>
<li>June 2015 &#160;&#8211;&#32;The Copyright Amendment (Online Infringement) Bill 2015 introduced into parliament to curb online piracy passed with Coalition and Labor's support 37-13<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>22 December 2017&#160;&#8211;&#32;The Copyright Amendment (Disability and Other Measures) Act 2017 becomes effective.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
