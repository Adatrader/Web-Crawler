<p><b>Implosive consonants</b> are a group of <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">stop consonants</a> (and possibly also some <a href="/wiki/Affricate" class="mw-redirect" title="Affricate">affricates</a>) with a mixed <a href="/wiki/Glottalic_ingressive" class="mw-redirect" title="Glottalic ingressive">glottalic ingressive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pulmonic_egressive" class="mw-redirect" title="Pulmonic egressive">pulmonic egressive</a> <a href="/wiki/Airstream_mechanism" title="Airstream mechanism">airstream mechanism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ball_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ball-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> That is, the airstream is controlled by moving the <a href="/wiki/Glottis" title="Glottis">glottis</a> downward in addition to expelling air from the lungs. Therefore, unlike the purely glottalic <a href="/wiki/Ejective_consonant" title="Ejective consonant">ejective consonants</a>, implosives can be modified by <a href="/wiki/Phonation" title="Phonation">phonation</a>. Contrastive implosives are found in approximately 13%<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> of the world's languages.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">International Phonetic Alphabet</a>, implosives are indicated by modifying the top of a letter (voiced stop) with a rightward-facing hook: &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ɓ ɗ ʄ ɠ ʛ</span>&#x27e9;.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Articulation">Articulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Implosive_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Articulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the occlusion of the stop, pulling the glottis downward rarefies the air in the vocal tract. The stop is then released. In languages whose implosives are particularly salient, that may result in air rushing into the mouth before it flows out again with the next vowel. To take in air sharply in that way is to implode a sound.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, probably more typically, there is no movement of air at all, which contrasts with the burst of the pulmonary plosives. This  is the case with many of the <a href="/wiki/Kru_languages" title="Kru languages">Kru languages</a>, for example. That means that implosives are phonetically sonorants (not obstruents) as the concept of sonorant is usually defined. However, implosives can phonologically pattern as both; that is, they may be phonological <a href="/wiki/Sonorant" title="Sonorant">sonorants</a> or <a href="/wiki/Obstruent" title="Obstruent">obstruents</a> depending on the language.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/George_N._Clements" class="mw-redirect" title="George N. Clements">George N. Clements</a> (2002) actually proposes that implosives are phonologically neither obstruents nor sonorants.
</p><p>The vast majority of implosive consonants are <a href="/wiki/Voiced_consonant" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiced consonant">voiced</a>, so the glottis is only partially closed. Because the airflow required for voicing reduces the vacuum being created in the mouth, implosives are easiest to make with a large oral cavity.
</p><p>Bilabial <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɓ]</span> is thus the easiest implosive to pronounce and so it is the most common one around the world. Velar <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɠ]</span>, on the other hand, is quite rare (uvular <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ʛ]</span> is even rarer). That is the opposite pattern to the <a href="/wiki/Ejective_consonant" title="Ejective consonant">ejective consonant</a> in which it is the velar articulation that is most common and the bilabial articulation that is rare.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Implosive_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Implosives are most often voiced stops, occasionally voiceless stops. Individual tokens of glottalized sonorants (nasals, trills, laterals, etc.) may also be pronounced with a lowering of the glottis by some individuals, occasionally to the extent that they are noticeably implosive, but no language is known where implosion is a general characteristic of such sounds.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voiced_implosives">Voiced implosives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Implosive_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Voiced implosives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</p>

<p>There are no IPA symbols for implosive fricatives. Implosive fricatives are unknown, and implosive affricates unlikely. A few affricates have been reported (like <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɗʒ]</span> in <a href="/wiki/Roglai_language" title="Roglai language">Roglai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Komo_language" title="Komo language">Komo</a>; allophonic <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɗz]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɗɮ]</span> in <a href="/wiki/Gitxsan_language" title="Gitxsan language">Gitxsan</a>), but more investigation may reanalyze them as something else.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voiceless_implosives">Voiceless implosives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Implosive_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Voiceless implosives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Consonants variously called "voiceless implosives," "implosives with glottal closure,"<sup id="cite_ref-SOWL_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SOWL-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> or "reverse ejectives" involve a slightly different airstream mechanism, purely glottalic ingressive.<sup id="cite_ref-Ball_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ball-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The glottis is closed so no pulmonic airstream is possible. The IPA once dedicated symbols &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ƥ ƭ ƈ ƙ ʠ</span>&#x27e9; to such sounds, but they were withdrawn in 1993. They are now transcribed &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ɓ̥ ɗ̥ ʄ̊ ɠ̊ ʛ̥</span>&#x27e9; or occasionally &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">pʼ↓ tʼ↓ cʼ↓ kʼ↓</span>&#x27e9;. Some authors use a superscript left pointer, &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">p˂ t˂ c˂ k˂</span>&#x27e9;, but that is not an IPA symbol and has other uses.

<li><a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_implosive" title="Voiceless alveolar implosive">voiceless alveolar implosive</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɗ̥]</span></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Implosive_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Implosives are widespread among the languages of <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and are found in a few languages of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Basin" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon Basin">Amazon Basin</a>. They are rarely reported elsewhere but occur in scattered languages such as the <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">Mayan languages</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saraiki_language" title="Saraiki language">Saraiki</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sindhi_language" title="Sindhi language">Sindhi</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>. They appear to be entirely absent from <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and from <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, even from the Australian ceremonial language <a href="/wiki/Damin" title="Damin">Damin</a>, which uses every other possible airstream mechanism.
</p><p>However, fully voiced stops are often slightly implosive, but it is not always described explicitly if there is no contrast with modal-voiced plosives, as occurs around the world, from <a href="/wiki/Maidu_language" title="Maidu language">Maidu</a> to <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a> to many <a href="/wiki/Bantu_languages" title="Bantu languages">Bantu languages</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili language">Swahili</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sindhi_language" title="Sindhi language">Sindhi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saraiki_language" title="Saraiki language">Saraiki</a> have an unusually large number of contrastive implosives, with <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɓ ᶑ ʄ ɠ/</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-SOWL_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SOWL-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Although Sindhi has a dental–retroflex distinction in its plosives, with <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/b d ɖ ɟ ɡ/</span>, the contrast is neutralized in the implosives. A contrastive retroflex implosive <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ᶑ/</span> may, however, occur in <a href="/wiki/Ngadha_language" title="Ngadha language">Ngad'a</a>, a language spoken in <a href="/wiki/Flores" title="Flores">Flores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>More examples can be found in the articles on individual implosives.
</p><p>Voiceless implosives are quite rare but are found in languages as varied as the Owere dialect of <a href="/wiki/Igbo_language" title="Igbo language">Igbo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> (<span class="IPA nowrap" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_bilabial_implosive" title="Voiceless bilabial implosive">ƥ</a>/</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_implosive" title="Voiceless alveolar implosive">ƭ</a>/</span>), <a href="/wiki/Krongo_language" title="Krongo language">Krongo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, the Uzere dialect of <a href="/wiki/Isoko_language" title="Isoko language">Isoko</a>, the closely related <a href="/wiki/Lendu_language" title="Lendu language">Lendu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ngiti_language" title="Ngiti language">Ngiti</a> languages in the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Congo" class="mw-redirect" title="Democratic Republic of Congo">Democratic Republic of Congo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serer_language" title="Serer language">Serer</a> in <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ƥ ƭ <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_palatal_implosive" title="Voiceless palatal implosive">ƈ</a>/</span>), and some dialects of the <a href="/wiki/Poqomchi%27_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Poqomchi&#39; language">Poqomchi’</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quiche_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Quiche language">Quiche languages</a> in <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a> (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ƥ ƭ/</span>). Owere Igbo has a seven-way contrast among bilabial stops, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/pʰ p ƥ bʱ b ɓ m/</span>, and its alveolar stops are similar. The <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_consonant" title="Dorsal consonant">dorsal stops</a> <span class="IPA nowrap" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_implosive" title="Voiceless velar implosive">ƙ</a>&#93;</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_uvular_implosive" title="Voiceless uvular implosive">ʠ</a>&#93;</span> do not seem to be attested in the literature as speech sounds,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> but <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʠ/</span> has been claimed for <a href="/wiki/Kaqchikel_language" title="Kaqchikel language">Kaqchikel</a>. <a href="/wiki/Lendu_language" title="Lendu language">Lendu</a> has been claimed to have voiceless <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɓ̥ ɗ̥ ɠ̊/</span>, but they may actually be <a href="/wiki/Creaky-voiced" class="mw-redirect" title="Creaky-voiced">creaky-voiced</a> implosives.<sup id="cite_ref-SOWL_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SOWL-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some English speakers use a voiceless velar implosive <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ƙ]</span> to imitate the "glug-glug" sound of liquid being poured from a bottle, but others use a voiced implosive <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɠ]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
