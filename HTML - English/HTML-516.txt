<p><b>"Columbia, the Gem of the Ocean"</b> is an American patriotic song which was popular in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> during the 19th and early 20th centuries. Composed <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1843, it was long used as an unofficial <a href="/wiki/National_anthem" title="National anthem">national anthem</a> of the United States, in competition with other songs. It was featured in the 1957 musical <i><a href="/wiki/The_Music_Man" title="The Music Man">The Music Man</a></i>. In 1969, "Columbia, the Gem of the Ocean" was the music performed by a U.S. Navy Band embarked aboard <a href="/wiki/USS_Hornet_(CV-12)" title="USS Hornet (CV-12)">USS&#160;<i>Hornet</i></a> as one of the ship's helicopters recovered the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> astronauts from their capsule named <i>Columbia</i> after a splashdown in the Pacific Ocean.
</p><p>The melody of "Columbia, the Gem of the Ocean" is identical to that of the British patriotic song "Britannia, the Pride of the Ocean" and there is disagreement as to which song was adapted from the other.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Columbia,_the_Gem_of_the_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origins and authorship of "Columbia, the Gem of the Ocean" have been the source of some uncertainty.<sup id="cite_ref-songs_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-songs-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is believed that <a href="/wiki/Thomas_A%27Becket_(composer)" title="Thomas A&#39;Becket (composer)">Thomas A'Becket, Sr.</a>, a British musician and longtime resident of Philadelphia who performed at that city's Chestnut Street Theater, wrote the lyrics ‚Äì and possibly the melody ‚Äì at the request of David Shaw for the latter's performance at a benefit concert, probably in the autumn of 1843.<sup id="cite_ref-fuld_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fuld-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-songs_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-songs-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-holsinger_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holsinger-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> That year, the copyright for the song was registered by the Philadelphia publishing house of George Willig under the name "Columbia, the Land of the Brave".<sup id="cite_ref-fuld_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fuld-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Shaw subsequently published the song under his own name, though A'Becket later claimed sole authorship and presented an original handwritten composition as proof.<sup id="cite_ref-holsinger_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holsinger-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The song invokes the historic informal name "<a href="/wiki/Columbia_(name)" title="Columbia (name)">Columbia</a>" for the United States and borrows and modifies the phrase "land of the free and the home of the brave" from <a href="/wiki/Francis_Scott_Key" title="Francis Scott Key">Francis Scott Key</a>'s earlier "<a href="/wiki/Star-Spangled_Banner" class="mw-redirect" title="Star-Spangled Banner">Star-Spangled Banner</a>" as "the home of the brave and the free".<sup id="cite_ref-songs_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-songs-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-holsinger_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holsinger-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fuld_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fuld-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span id=".22Britannia.2C_the_Pride_of_the_Ocean.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Britannia,_the_Pride_of_the_Ocean&quot;">"Britannia, the Pride of the Ocean"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Columbia,_the_Gem_of_the_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: &quot;Britannia, the Pride of the Ocean&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There has been some controversy as to whether "Columbia, the Gem of the Ocean" is an appropriation of a similar, British patriotic song, "Britannia, the Pride of the Ocean" or whether the latter song is, in fact, an appropriation of "Columbia, the Gem of the Ocean".<sup id="cite_ref-fresno_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fresno-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Both songs have occasionally been referred to by the alternate name "The Red, White and Blue".<sup id="cite_ref-jhu_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jhu-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a 1919 analysis of the song's lyrics, Arthur Johnston stated that "Britannia, the Pride of the Ocean" had come first, "Columbia, the Gem of the Ocean" being adapted from it.<sup id="cite_ref-thsj_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thsj-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Johnston claimed that "the phrase, 'the service united' referred to the <a href="/wiki/United_Service_Club" title="United Service Club">United Service Club</a>, for which the song was written, the members of which belong both to the army and the navy".<sup id="cite_ref-thsj_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thsj-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Johnston also opines that, to refer to the United States as "the gem of the ocean" would be "an absurdity" and the phrase more likely was an original reference to <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-thsj_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thsj-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/W._H._Grattan_Flood" title="W. H. Grattan Flood">W. H. Grattan Flood</a> also arrived at Johnston's conclusion of the British provenance of the song. He stated that Irish journalist Stephen Joseph Meany penned the lyrics to <i>Britannia, the Pride of the Ocean</i> in 1842 which he then showed to a friend in London, Thomas E. Williams, who composed the accompanying melody.<sup id="cite_ref-flood_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flood-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In 1915 Flood recalled speaking to an elderly relative of his who claimed to have heard the song performed in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> in 1842 as a 12-year-old boy.<sup id="cite_ref-flood_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flood-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, the earliest printed version of either song was the 1843 copyright version registered by American George Willig.<sup id="cite_ref-fresno_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fresno-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The first printed version of the song in the United Kingdom ("Brittania, the Pride of the Ocean") did not appear until 1852, in a filing with the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> by T.E. Purday.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fresno_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fresno-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Further, that version credits the song to David Shaw.<sup id="cite_ref-fresno_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fresno-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, Meany ‚Äì the Irishman whom Flood credits with the lyrics to "Britannia, the Pride of the Ocean" ‚Äì was a <a href="/wiki/Fenian" title="Fenian">Fenian</a> who would eventually be imprisoned by the British government.<sup id="cite_ref-flood_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flood-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Citing the dates of printing, <a href="/wiki/William_Studwell" title="William Studwell">William Studwell</a> concludes that "the song was probably created in the United States".<sup id="cite_ref-studwell_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-studwell-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_use">Cultural use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Columbia,_the_Gem_of_the_Ocean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cultural use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to William Studwell, "Columbia, the Gem of the Ocean" is the "oldest well-known song of entirely American origin which could, by style or content, qualify as a <a href="/wiki/National_anthem" title="National anthem">national anthem</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-studwell_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-studwell-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In the mid-1800s, "Columbia the Gem of the Ocean" vied with other songs in the American "Patriotic Big Five" (also including "<a href="/wiki/Hail,_Columbia" title="Hail, Columbia">Hail, Columbia</a>", the "Star-Spangled Banner", "<a href="/wiki/Yankee_Doodle" title="Yankee Doodle">Yankee Doodle</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/My_Country_Tis_of_Thee" class="mw-redirect" title="My Country Tis of Thee">My Country Tis of Thee</a>") for use as a national anthem, the United States at the time having no song officially designated as such.<sup id="cite_ref-jhu_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jhu-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:220px"><audio id="mwe_player_2" controls="" preload="none" style="width:220px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="15.743129251701" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="VOA_transmitter_Tangier.ogg" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/44/VOA_transmitter_Tangier.ogg" type="audio/ogg; codecs=&quot;vorbis&quot;" data-title="Original Ogg file (127 kbps)" data-shorttitle="Ogg source" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="127012" /><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/4/44/VOA_transmitter_Tangier.ogg/VOA_transmitter_Tangier.ogg.mp3" type="audio/mpeg" data-title="MP3" data-shorttitle="MP3" data-transcodekey="mp3" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="178120" /></audio></div>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:VOA_transmitter_Tangier.ogg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"Columbia, the Gem of the Ocean" used as an interval signal during a 1962 <a href="/wiki/Voice_of_America" title="Voice of America">Voice of America</a> broadcast from <a href="/wiki/Tangier,_Morocco" class="mw-redirect" title="Tangier, Morocco">Tangier, Morocco</a></div></div></div>
<p>"Columbia, the Gem of the Ocean" reached a height of popularity during the <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Abraham_Lincoln" title="Presidency of Abraham Lincoln">presidency of Abraham Lincoln</a> and subsequently became a staple in the repertoire of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Band" title="United States Marine Band">United States Marine Band</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The tune was later used repeatedly by the composer <a href="/wiki/Charles_Ives" title="Charles Ives">Charles Ives</a>, featuring
