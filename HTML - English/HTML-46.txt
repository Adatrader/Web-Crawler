<p><b>William Cushing</b> (March 1, 1732 – September 13, 1810) was one of the original five <a href="/wiki/Associate_Justice_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Associate Justice of the Supreme Court of the United States">associate justices</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a>; <a href="/wiki/Advice_and_consent" title="Advice and consent">confirmed</a> by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> on September 26, 1789, he served until his death.<sup id="cite_ref-FJC-Cushing_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FJC-Cushing-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> His Supreme Court tenure of 20 years and 11 months was the longest among the Court's inaugural members.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In January 1796 he was nominated by <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> to become the Court's <a href="/wiki/Chief_Justice_of_the_United_States" title="Chief Justice of the United States">Chief Justice</a>; though confirmed, he declined the appointment.<sup id="cite_ref-FJC-Cushing_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FJC-Cushing-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He was the last judge in the United States to wear a full wig (<a href="/wiki/Court_dress" title="Court dress">Court dress</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Cushing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cushing was born in <a href="/wiki/Scituate,_Massachusetts" title="Scituate, Massachusetts">Scituate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Massachusetts_Bay" title="Province of Massachusetts Bay">Massachusetts Bay</a>, on March 1, 1732. The Cushing family had a long history in the area, settling <a href="/wiki/Hingham,_Massachusetts" title="Hingham, Massachusetts">Hingham</a> in 1638.  Cushing's father <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Cushing_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John Cushing Jr. (page does not exist)">John Cushing</a> (1695–1778) was a provincial magistrate who in 1747 became an associate justice of the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Supreme_Judicial_Court" title="Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court">Superior Court of Judicature</a>, the province's high court. William Cushing's grandfather John Cushing (1662–1737/38) was also a superior court judge and member of the governor's council.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cushing's mother, Mary Cotton Cushing, was a daughter of <a href="/wiki/Josiah_Cotton" title="Josiah Cotton">Josiah Cotton</a> (1679/80–1756). They were descended from Rev. <a href="/wiki/John_Cotton_(minister)" title="John Cotton (minister)">John Cotton</a>, the great 17th century Puritan theologian. Josiah Cotton and Richard Fitzgerald, a teacher at a local Latin school, were responsible for young Cushing's early education.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cushing graduated from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">Harvard College</a> in 1751 and became a <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_bar" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of the bar">member of the bar</a> of <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> in 1755. After briefly practicing law in Scituate, he moved to Pownalborough (present-day <a href="/wiki/Dresden,_Maine" title="Dresden, Maine">Dresden, Maine</a>, then part of Massachusetts), and became the first practicing attorney in the province's eastern district (as Maine was then known).  In 1762 he was called to become a <a href="/wiki/Barrister" title="Barrister">barrister</a>, again the first in Maine.  He practiced law until 1772, when he was appointed by Governor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hutchinson_(governor)" title="Thomas Hutchinson (governor)">Thomas Hutchinson</a> to replace his father (who had resigned) on the Superior Court bench.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Cushing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Not long after his tenure on the Massachusetts bench began, a controversy arose over revelations that court judges were to be paid by crown funds from <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> rather than by an appropriation of the provincial assembly.  Cushing did not express any opinion on the matter, but declined the crown payment in preference to a provincial appropriation.
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a> broke out in April 1775, the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Provincial_Congress" title="Massachusetts Provincial Congress">Massachusetts Provincial Congress</a> (which exercised <i>de facto</i> control over the province outside <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Boston" title="Siege of Boston">besieged Boston</a>), sought to reorganize the courts to remove the trappings of British sovereignty.  Consequently, it essentially dissolved the Superior Court and reformed it in November 1775.  Of all its justices, Cushing was the only one retained.
</p><p>The congress offered the seat of Chief Justice first to <a href="/wiki/John_Adams" title="John Adams">John Adams</a>, but he never sat, and resigned the post in 1776. The provincial congress appointed Cushing to be the court's first sitting Chief Justice in 1777. He was a charter member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> (1780).<sup id="cite_ref-AAAS_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He would sit as Massachusetts Chief Justice until 1789, during which period the court ruled in 1783 that slavery was irreconcilable with the new state constitution, and it was ended in the state.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Massachusetts_chief_justice">Massachusetts chief justice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Cushing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Massachusetts chief justice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1783, Cushing presided over a series of cases involving <a href="/wiki/Quock_Walker" title="Quock Walker">Quock Walker</a>, a slave who filed a <a href="/wiki/Freedom_suit" title="Freedom suit">freedom suit</a> based on the language of the new state constitution. In <i><a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_v._Jennison" title="Commonwealth v. Jennison">Commonwealth v. Jennison</a></i>, Cushing stated the following principles, in his charge to the jury:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>As to the doctrine of slavery and the right of Christians to hold Africans in perpetual servitude, and sell and treat them as we do our horses and cattle, that (it is true) has been heretofore countenanced by the Province Laws formerly, but nowhere is it expressly enacted or established. It has been a usage – a usage which took its origin from the practice of some of the European nations, and the regulations of British government respecting the then Colonies, for the benefit of trade and wealth. But whatever sentiments have formerly prevailed in this particular or slid in upon us by the example of others, a different idea has taken place with the people of America, more favorable to the natural rights of mankind, and to that natural, innate desire of Liberty, with which Heaven (without regard to color, complexion, or shape of noses-features) has inspired all the human race. And upon this ground our Constitution of Government, by which the people of this Commonwealth have solemnly bound themselves, sets out with declaring that all men are born free and equal – and that every subject is entitled to liberty, and to have it guarded by the laws, as well as life and property – and in short is totally repugnant to the idea of being born slaves. This being the case, I think the idea of slavery is inconsistent with our own conduct and Constitution; and there can be no such thing as perpetual servitude of a rational creature, unless his liberty is forfeited by some criminal conduct or given up by personal consent or contract ...<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>This was taken to mean that slavery was incompatible with <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Massachusetts Constitution">the state constitution</a> ratified in 1779, and that slavery was therefore ended in the state.<sup id="cite_ref-SJC_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SJC-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The case relied on a 1781 freedom suit brought by slave <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Freeman_(Mum_Bett)" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth Freeman (Mum Bett)">Elizabeth Freeman (Mum Bett)</a>, also known as Mum Bett, on the same grounds; a Massachusetts county court ruled in her favor in 1781.
</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/Shays%27_Rebellion" title="Shays&#39; Rebellion">Shays' Rebellion</a> (1786–87), Cushing ensured that court sessions continued, despite the aggressive protests of the armed rebels, and later presided over their trials. A year later, in 1788, he served as vice president of the Massachusetts convention, which narrowly ratified the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_United_States" title="Constitution of the United States">United States Constitution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Supreme_Court">U.S. Supreme Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Cushing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: U.S. Supreme Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On September 24, 1789, President George Washington nominated Cushing for one of the five associate justice positions on the newly established Supreme Court. His appointment (along with those of: <a href="/wiki/John_Blair_Jr." title="John Blair Jr.">John Blair Jr.</a>; <a href="/wiki/Robert_H._Harrison" title="Robert H. Harrison">Robert H. Harrison</a>; <a href="/wiki/John_Rutledge" title="John Rutledge">John Rutledge</a>; and <a href="/wiki/James_Wilson_(justice)" title="James Wilson (justice)">James Wilson</a>; plus that of <a href="/wiki/John_Jay" title="John Jay">John Jay</a> for Chief Justice) was confirmed by the Senate two days later.<sup id="cite_ref-SCNomsSenate_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCNomsSenate-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Cushing's service on the Court officially began February 2, 1790, when he took the Judicial oath.<sup id="cite_ref-SCjustices_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCjustices-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He generally held a <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalist</a> view typically in line with the views of the <a href="/wiki/Federalist_Party_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Federalist Party (United States)">Federalist Party</a>, and often disagreed with <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Democratic-Republican_Party_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Democratic-Republican Party (United States)">Democratic-Republicans</a>.  His two most important decisions were probably <i><a href="/wiki/Chisholm_v._Georgia" title="Chisholm v. Georgia">Chisholm v. Georgia</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ware_v._Hylton" title="Ware v. Hylton">Ware v. Hylton</a></i>, which held that <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaties</a> made under the Constitution supersede <a href="/wiki/State_law_(United_States)" title="State law (United States)">state law</a>. Though he served on the Court for two decades, only 19 of his decisions appear in the <i><a href="/wiki/United_States_Reports" title="United States Reports">United States Reports</a></i>.
</p><p>Cushing administered the <a href="/wiki/Oath_of_office_of_the_President_of_the_United_States" title="Oath of office of the President of the United States">oath of office</a> at <a href="/wiki/Second_inauguration_of_George_Washington" class="mw-redirect" title="Second inauguration of George Washington">Washington's second inauguration</a> on March 4, 1793. This was the first inauguration to take place in Philadelphia (<a href="/wiki/List_of_capitals_in_the_United_States#Former_national_capitals" title="List of capitals in the United States">then the nation's capital</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When Chief Justice <a href="/wiki/John_Jay" title="John Jay">John Jay</a> resigned from the Court in June 1795, during a long Senate recess, Washington appointed <a href="/wiki/John_Rutledge" title="John Rutledge">John Rutledge</a> as the new chief justice by a <a href="/wiki/Recess_appointment" title="Recess appointment">recess appointment</a>.  On December 15, 1795, during the Senate's next session, it rejected Rutledge's nomination. Washington subsequently nominated Cushing on January 26, 1796; the Senate confirmed the nomination the following day.<sup id="cite_ref-SCNomsSenate_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCNomsSenate-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cushing received his commission on January 27, but returned it to Washington on February 2, declining appointment.<sup id="cite_ref-Decline_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Decline-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  An error in the rough minutes of the Court on February 3 and 4, 1796, lists Cushing as Chief Justice, although this entry was later crossed out.  This error can be explained by the text of the <a href="/wiki/Judiciary_Act_of_1789" title="Judiciary Act of 1789">Judiciary Act of 1789</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> which allowed for the Court to hear cases with a <a href="/wiki/Quorum" title="Quorum">quorum</a> of only four justices; that is, the Chief Justice need not always be present for the Court to conduct business. As Cushing was the most senior associate justice present on those dates, he would have been expected to serve as the presiding justice, directing the Court's business.
</p><p>Washington then nominated <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Ellsworth" title="Oliver Ellsworth">Oliver Ellsworth</a> to be chief justice, transmitting the nomination to the Senate in a March 3 message stating that Ellsworth would replace "William Cushing, resigned."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  Subsequent histories of the Court have not counted Cushing as chief justice, but instead report that he declined the appointment.  Had Cushing accepted promotion to chief justice and then resigned, he would have had to leave the Court entirely; accepting the appointment would have implicitly required Cushing to resign his place as associate justice.  That he continued on the Court as an Associate Justice for years afterward lends weight to the assertion that Cushing declined promotion.  Additionally, Cushing's February 2 letter explicitly stated his return of the commission for chief justice, and his desire to retain his seat as Associate Justice.<sup id="cite_ref-Decline_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Decline-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life_and_death">Later life and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Cushing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later life and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1810, Cushing died in his hometown of Scituate, Massachusetts. He is buried in a small cemetery there which is also a state park.
</p>
