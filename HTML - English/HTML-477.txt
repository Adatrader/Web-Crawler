<p><b>Jacksonville International Airport</b> (<a href="/wiki/IATA_airport_code" title="IATA airport code">IATA</a>: <b>JAX</b>, <a href="/wiki/ICAO_airport_code" title="ICAO airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>KJAX</b>, <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">FAA</a> <a href="/wiki/Location_identifier" title="Location identifier">LID</a>: <b>JAX</b>) is a civil-military public airport 13 miles (21&#160;km) north of <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Jacksonville" title="Downtown Jacksonville">Downtown</a> <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville,_Florida" title="Jacksonville, Florida">Jacksonville</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Duval_County,_Florida" title="Duval County, Florida">Duval County, Florida</a>. It is owned and operated by the <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville_Aviation_Authority" title="Jacksonville Aviation Authority">Jacksonville Aviation Authority</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jacksonville_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Construction started in 1965 on a new airport to handle travel to nearby <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Station_Jacksonville" title="Naval Air Station Jacksonville">naval bases</a>. The new airport was dedicated on September 1, 1968, replacing <a href="/wiki/Imeson_Field" title="Imeson Field">Imeson Field</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Terrain precluded lengthening the runways at Imeson, a necessity with the inception of commercial jet airliners. A new idea at JIA was separating departing and arriving passengers on different sides of the terminal (as can be seen in the photo on this page). This is no longer the case, and the airport (which has greatly expanded since the picture was taken) now uses the more typical layout with departing passengers on an upper level with an elevated roadway, and arriving passengers on the lower level.
</p>

		<li class="gallerybox" style="width: 312px"><div style="width: 312px">

<p>The new airport was slow to expand, only serving two million passengers a year by 1982, but it served over five million annually by 1999 and an expansion plan was approved in 2000. The first phase, which included rebuilding the landside terminal, the central square and main concessions area, as well as consolidating the security checkpoints at one location, and more parking capacity was completed in 2004–2005. In 2007, 6,319,016 passengers were processed.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JacksonvilleInternationalAirport-2010-a.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/JacksonvilleInternationalAirport-2010-a.JPG/240px-JacksonvilleInternationalAirport-2010-a.JPG" decoding="async" width="240" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/JacksonvilleInternationalAirport-2010-a.JPG/360px-JacksonvilleInternationalAirport-2010-a.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/JacksonvilleInternationalAirport-2010-a.JPG/480px-JacksonvilleInternationalAirport-2010-a.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2779" data-file-height="1852" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JacksonvilleInternationalAirport-2010-a.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jacksonville International Airport Concourse C</div></div></div>
<p>The second phase of the expansion program<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> was carried out over three years, commencing in mid-2006 and projected to cost about $170 million. Concourses A and C were completely rebuilt; the former concourses have been demolished. Work on Concourse B was given a low priority because the capacities of the rebuilt Concourses A and C were more than adequate for existing demand. The expansion was designed by <a href="/wiki/Reynolds,_Smith_%26_Hills" title="Reynolds, Smith &amp; Hills">Reynolds, Smith &amp; Hills</a> (RS&amp;H).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The economic downturn of 2009 caused a decrease in passengers and flights. This led the JAA to commence the demolition of Concourse B in June 2009 because it was safer and easier for the contractor. After the debris was removed, asphalt was laid to provide space for ground equipment parking. The concourse will be rebuilt when passenger traffic increases, which the JAA had originally projected would occur in 2013 but did not materialize.<sup id="cite_ref-timeunion1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeunion1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> A section of the old concourse eventually became part of an airline club lounge which opened in 2019.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion">Expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jacksonville_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2018, the airport handled 6,460,253 passengers, breaking the previous record set in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This increase in traffic prompted the JAA to revive the plan to rebuild concourse B.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The new concourse could open as early as 2022, providing six additional gates and could be expanded later with six more.<sup id="cite_ref-timeunion1_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeunion1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The design of concourses A and C also allow them to be extended to accommodate additional gates. In 2019, RS&amp;H and <a href="/wiki/Jacobs_Engineering_Group" title="Jacobs Engineering Group">Jacobs Engineering</a> were chosen to perform the design, while <a href="/wiki/Balfour_Beatty" title="Balfour Beatty">Balfour Beatty</a> was selected as the construction manager for the concourse B project.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jacksonville_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jacksonville_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The airport covers 7,911 acres (3,201&#160;ha) and has two concrete <a href="/wiki/Runway" title="Runway">runways</a>: 8/26, 10,000 x 150&#160;ft (3,048 x 46 m) and 14/32, 7,701 x 150&#160;ft (2,347 x 46 m).<sup id="cite_ref-FAA_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAA-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Airport_terminal" title="Airport terminal">terminal</a> at JIA is composed of a baggage claim area, on the first floor and a ticketing area on the second floor, at the front of the structure. Past baggage claim and ticketing is the mezzanine, where shops, restaurants and the security checkpoint are located. Beyond the mezzanine are the airport's Concourses A and C, which include 10 <a href="/wiki/Gate_(airport)" title="Gate (airport)">gates</a> each (for a total of 20), along with other shops and restaurants.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The airport also has a <a href="/wiki/Delta_Sky_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="Delta Sky Club">Delta Sky Club</a> on Concourse A and a multi-airline passenger club located behind the airside food court.
</p><p>There are three galleries located off of the main courtyard before the security checkpoint. One features an art exhibit, the second houses a revolving exhibit about a Jacksonville-area landmark or institution, and the third houses a permanent exhibit highlighting the history of aviation in the region.
</p><p>The airport's two runways form a "V" pattern (with the tip of the "V" pointing west). A plan exists to build two more runways, each paralleling one existing runway. The one alongside the existing southern runway will be built first. No date has been set.
</p><p>In the fiscal year ending September 2016 the airport had 101,575 aircraft operations, an average of 278 per day: 58% scheduled commercial, 19% <a href="/wiki/Air_taxi" title="Air taxi">air taxi</a>, 15% <a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">general aviation</a> and 8% military. In August 2017, there were 54 aircraft based at this airport: 3 single-engine, 8 multi-engine, 25 jet and 18 military.<sup id="cite_ref-FAA_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAA-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_facilities">Military facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jacksonville_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Military facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:US-AirNationalGuard-2007Emblem.svg" class="image"><img alt="US-AirNationalGuard-2007Emblem.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/US-AirNationalGuard-2007Emblem.svg/100px-US-AirNationalGuard-2007Emblem.svg.png" decoding="async" width="100" height="101" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/US-AirNationalGuard-2007Emblem.svg/150px-US-AirNationalGuard-2007Emblem.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/US-AirNationalGuard-2007Emblem.svg/200px-US-AirNationalGuard-2007Emblem.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="712" data-file-height="720" /></a></div>
<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:125th_Fighter_Wing.png" class="image"><img alt="125th Fighter Wing.png" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/125th_Fighter_Wing.png/100px-125th_Fighter_Wing.png" decoding="async" width="100" height="97" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/125th_Fighter_Wing.png/150px-125th_Fighter_Wing.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/125th_Fighter_Wing.png/200px-125th_Fighter_Wing.png 2x" data-file-width="851" data-file-height="825" /></a></div>
<p>Concurrent with the closure of Imeson Airport, the <a href="/wiki/125th_Fighter_Wing" title="125th Fighter Wing">125th Fighter-Interceptor Group</a> (125 FIG) of the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Air_National_Guard" title="Florida Air National Guard">Florida Air National Guard</a> (FANG) relocated to Jacksonville International Airport. Military Construction (MILCON) funds provided for the establishment of <b>Jacksonville Air National Guard Base</b> in the southwest quadrant of the airport and placement of <a href="/wiki/USAF" class="mw-redirect" title="USAF">USAF</a>-style emergency <a href="/wiki/Arresting_gear" title="Arresting gear">arresting gear</a> on the JAX runways.  Upgraded from group to wing status and redesignated as the <a href="/wiki/125th_Fighter_Wing" title="125th Fighter Wing">125th Fighter Wing</a> (125 FW) in the early 1990s, the wing is the host unit for Jacksonville ANGB and operates <a href="/wiki/F-15_Eagle" class="mw-redirect" title="F-15 Eagle">F-15C</a> and <a href="/wiki/F-15_Eagle" class="mw-redirect" title="F-15 Eagle">F-15D Eagle</a> aircraft.  The 125 FW is operationally-gained by the <a href="/wiki/Air_Combat_Command" title="Air Combat Command">Air Combat Command</a> (ACC).
</p><p>Jacksonville ANGB is basically a small <a href="/wiki/Air_force_base" class="mw-redirect" title="Air force base">air force base</a>, albeit without the military housing, military hospital or other infrastructure of major <a href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> installations. The <a href="/wiki/Air_National_Guard" title="Air National Guard">Air National Guard</a> provides a fully equipped USAF Crash Fire Rescue station to augment the airport's own fire department for both on-airport structural fires and <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_rescue_and_firefighting" title="Aircraft rescue and firefighting">aircraft rescue and firefighting</a> (ARFF) purposes. The base employs approximately 300 full-time military personnel (<a href="/wiki/Air_Reserve_Technician_Program" title="Air Reserve Technician Program">ART</a> and <a href="/wiki/Active_Guard_Reserve" title="Active Guard Reserve">AGR</a>) and 1,000 part-time military personnel who are traditional air national guardsmen.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Passenger_traffic">Passenger traffic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jacksonville_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Passenger traffic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 2018 fiscal year set a record for passenger numbers at Jacksonville International Airport. Between September 2018 and August 2019, the airport handled 7,067,882 passengers, which was a 16.41% increase from the prior year.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>

<p>Jacksonville International Airport has direct public transit service to <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville_Transportation_Authority" title="Jacksonville Transportation Authority">Jacksonville Transportation Authority</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville_Transportation_Authority#Bus_routes" title="Jacksonville Transportation Authority">bus</a> network. The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20110122031353/http://www.jtafla.com/Schedules/showPage.aspx?Sel=155">CT3</a> "AirJTA" bus connects the airport to downtown Jacksonville, with connections to <a href="/wiki/Greyhound_Bus_Lines" class="mw-redirect" title="Greyhound Bus Lines">Greyhound Bus Lines</a> and to the <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville_Skyway" title="Jacksonville Skyway">Jacksonville Skyway</a> monorail system.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jacksonville_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On October 4, 1971, George M. Giffe Jr. hijacked a plane in <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville, Tennessee</a>, then forced the pilot to fly to Jacksonville, where he killed his wife, the pilot and himself when cornered by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">FBI</a>. <sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On December 6, 1984, <a href="/wiki/PBA_Flight_1039" title="PBA Flight 1039">PBA Flight 1039</a> crashed on takeoff, killing all 11 passengers and 2 crew on board.
</p><p>On October 1, 2013, at around 6:30&#160;p.m. EDT, the airport was evacuated due to a suspicious package. Police responded and at approximately 11:00 p.m. EDT, after the bomb squad removed the 'destructive' device, the airport was given the all clear and reopened.<sup id="cite_ref-wjxt_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wjxt-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
