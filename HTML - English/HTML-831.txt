<p><b>Katharine Lee Bates</b> (August 12, 1859 – March 28, 1929) was a prolific American writer, college professor, scholar, and social activist.  Although she published volumes of poetry, <a href="/wiki/Travel_literature" title="Travel literature">travel books</a>, essays, children's <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_literature" title="Children&#39;s literature">books</a>, books for young adults,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and editions of many earlier writers' works, today Bates is primarily remembered as the author of "<a href="/wiki/America_the_Beautiful" title="America the Beautiful">America the Beautiful</a>".  While on the <a href="/wiki/Wellesley_College" title="Wellesley College">Wellesley College</a> faculty, Bates mentored many young poets (including some, like <a href="/wiki/Robert_Frost" title="Robert Frost">Robert Frost</a>, not enrolled at Wellesley)<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and helped establish <a href="/wiki/American_literature" title="American literature">American literature</a> as a field for college study by creating an early course on the genre and writing a textbook for the field (the first woman to do the latter).<sup id="cite_ref-:4_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Some late 20th-century scholars have asserted that Bates was a lesbian who lived for 25 years with long-time friend and companion <a href="/wiki/Katharine_Coman" title="Katharine Coman">Katharine Coman</a>. Biographer Melinda M. Ponder has presented conflicting evidence and reached a different conclusion.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katharine_Lee_Bates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Goody_Santa_Claus_1889.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Goody_Santa_Claus_1889.JPG/220px-Goody_Santa_Claus_1889.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="268" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Goody_Santa_Claus_1889.JPG/330px-Goody_Santa_Claus_1889.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Goody_Santa_Claus_1889.JPG/440px-Goody_Santa_Claus_1889.JPG 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="853" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Goody_Santa_Claus_1889.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cover of an early edition of <i>Goody Santa Claus</i></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Statue_of_Katharine_Lee_Bates.jpg" class="image"><img alt="photograph of a statue of Katharine Lee Bates at the Falmouth Public Library" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Statue_of_Katharine_Lee_Bates.jpg/220px-Statue_of_Katharine_Lee_Bates.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="372" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Statue_of_Katharine_Lee_Bates.jpg/330px-Statue_of_Katharine_Lee_Bates.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Statue_of_Katharine_Lee_Bates.jpg/440px-Statue_of_Katharine_Lee_Bates.jpg 2x" data-file-width="849" data-file-height="1434" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Statue_of_Katharine_Lee_Bates.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Statue of Katharine Lee Bates at the Falmouth Public Library in Falmouth, Massachusetts.</div></div></div>
<p>Bates was born in <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Massachusetts" title="Falmouth, Massachusetts">Falmouth, Massachusetts</a> to the town's <a href="/wiki/Congregational_church" title="Congregational church">Congregational</a> minister William Bates and Cornelia Frances Lee. Her father died a few weeks after she was born, and she was primarily raised by her mother and a literary aunt, both of whom had graduated from the all-women's <a href="/wiki/Mount_Holyoke_College" title="Mount Holyoke College">Mount Holyoke Seminary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> She attended <a href="/wiki/Wellesley_High_School" title="Wellesley High School">Wellesley High School</a> (then called Needham High School) in 1872 and then <a href="/wiki/Newton_North_High_School" title="Newton North High School">Newton High School</a> until 1876.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Bates entered <a href="/wiki/Wellesley_College" title="Wellesley College">Wellesley College</a>, a women's college, as part of its second class in 1876. She graduated with a B.A. in 1880.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> She taught at <a href="/wiki/Natick_High_School" title="Natick High School">Natick High School</a> in 1880–81 and at <a href="/wiki/Dana_Hall_School" title="Dana Hall School">Dana Hall School</a> from 1881 until 1885.
</p><p>In 1889 Bates's young adult novel <i>Rose and Thorn</i> won a prize awarded by the Congregational Sunday School and Publishing Society.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It incorporated poor and working class women as characters to teach readers about social reform.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> She popularized the concept of <a href="/wiki/Mrs._Claus" title="Mrs. Claus">Mrs. Claus</a> in her poem "Goody Santa Claus on a Sleigh Ride" from the collection <i>Sunshine and other Verses for Children</i> (1889). The Mrs. Claus character is the chief organizer of Christmas Eve.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Taking advantage of new educational opportunities available to women after the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Bates used prize money from <i>Rose and Thorn</i><sup id="cite_ref-:4_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> to travel to England and study at <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a> in 1890–91.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> She then returned to Wellesley as an associate professor in 1891, earned her M.A. there, and was promoted to a full professor of English literature.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Near the end of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a>, she worked as a war correspondent for <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, and strove to reduce widely-circulating negative stereotypes about Spaniards.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> She contributed regularly to periodicals (sometimes under the pseudonym James Lincoln), including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Atlantic_Monthly" class="mw-redirect" title="The Atlantic Monthly">The Atlantic Monthly</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Congregationalist&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Congregationalist (page does not exist)">The Congregationalist</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Boston_Evening_Transcript" title="Boston Evening Transcript">Boston Evening Transcript</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Christian_Century" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Century">Christian Century</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contemporary_Verse&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Contemporary Verse (page does not exist)">Contemporary Verse</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lippincott%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="Lippincott&#39;s">Lippincott's</a>,</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Delineator" title="The Delineator">The Delineator</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ContemparyBiography_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ContemparyBiography-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1906 Bates and her brother Arthur signed a mortgage for a Wellesley houselot and house (now 70 Curve St.) to be built on it for the Bates family (Cornelia, Jeanne, and Katharine) and their tenants. Among the latter was <a href="/wiki/Katharine_Coman" title="Katharine Coman">Katharine Coman</a>, who would eventually rent an attic bedroom and photographic darkroom.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> While the house was being built, Bates traveled to Egypt and the Holy Land with Wellesley College president <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Hazard" title="Caroline Hazard">Caroline Hazard</a>. Upon returning to Wellesley, Bates named the house "The <a href="/wiki/Scarabaeus_sacer" title="Scarabaeus sacer">Scarab</a>," after the sacred Egyptian insect she admired as "always climbing."
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:I_Oak_Grove_Cemetery,_Falmouth,_MA,_USA_2_(2).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/I_Oak_Grove_Cemetery%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA%2C_USA_2_%282%29.jpg/170px-I_Oak_Grove_Cemetery%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA%2C_USA_2_%282%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="254" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/I_Oak_Grove_Cemetery%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA%2C_USA_2_%282%29.jpg/255px-I_Oak_Grove_Cemetery%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA%2C_USA_2_%282%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/I_Oak_Grove_Cemetery%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA%2C_USA_2_%282%29.jpg/340px-I_Oak_Grove_Cemetery%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA%2C_USA_2_%282%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2583" data-file-height="3859" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:I_Oak_Grove_Cemetery,_Falmouth,_MA,_USA_2_(2).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Oak Grove Cemetery, Falmouth, MA, Original tombstone</div></div></div><p> While working at Wellesley, Bates was elected a member of the newly-formed <a href="/wiki/Pi_Gamma_Mu" title="Pi Gamma Mu">Pi Gamma Mu</a> honor society for the social sciences because of her interest in history and politics. She retired from Wellesley in 1925 at the age of 66.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>274</span></sup> In retirement, Bates continued to write and to publish poetry, and was in great demand as a writer and speaker.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>274</span></sup>
</p><p>Bates was also a social activist interested in the struggles of women, workers, <a href="/wiki/Person_of_color" title="Person of color">people of color</a>, tenement residents, immigrants, and poor people.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> She helped organize the <a href="/wiki/Denison_House_(Boston)" title="Denison House (Boston)">Denison House</a>, a college women's <a href="/wiki/Settlement_movement" title="Settlement movement">settlement house</a>, with other women friends and colleagues in 1892.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>110</span></sup> She wrote and spoke extensively about the need for social reform<sup id="cite_ref-:4_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and was an avid advocate for the global <a href="/wiki/Peace_movement" title="Peace movement">peace movement</a> that emerged after World War I. She was especially active in attempts to establish the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Long an active <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)#20th_century" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a>, Bates broke with the party to endorse <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/John_W._Davis" title="John W. Davis">John W. Davis</a> in 1924 because of Republican opposition to American participation in the League of Nations. She said: "Though born and bred in the Republican camp, I cannot bear their betrayal of <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Mr. Wilson</a> and their rejection of the League of Nations, our one hope of peace on earth."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Thinking of herself as a "global citizen," Bates decried the American policy of <a href="/wiki/Isolationism" title="Isolationism">isolationism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>262</span></sup>
</p><p>Bates died in <a href="/wiki/Wellesley,_Massachusetts" title="Wellesley, Massachusetts">Wellesley, Massachusetts</a>, on March 28, 1929, while listening to a friend read poetry to her. She is buried in <a href="/wiki/Oak_Grove_Cemetery_(Falmouth,_Massachusetts)" title="Oak Grove Cemetery (Falmouth, Massachusetts)">Oak Grove Cemetery</a> at Falmouth.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Most of her papers are housed at the Wellesley College Archives and include "diaries, correspondences, musical scores, publications, scrapbooks, manuscripts, reports, memorials and tributes, memorabilia; concerning "America the Beautiful" and other writings of Katharine Lee Bates, her travels, and her life at Wellesley and in Falmouth, Mass."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id=".22America_the_Beautiful.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;America_the_Beautiful&quot;">"America the Beautiful"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katharine_Lee_Bates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: &quot;America the Beautiful&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/America_the_Beautiful" title="America the Beautiful">"America the Beautiful"</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:I_Oak_Grove_Cemetery,_Falmouth,_MA,_USA_(2).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/I_Oak_Grove_Cemetery%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA%2C_USA_%282%29.jpg/170px-I_Oak_Grove_Cemetery%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA%2C_USA_%282%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="254" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/I_Oak_Grove_Cemetery%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA%2C_USA_%282%29.jpg/255px-I_Oak_Grove_Cemetery%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA%2C_USA_%282%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/I_Oak_Grove_Cemetery%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA%2C_USA_%282%29.jpg/340px-I_Oak_Grove_Cemetery%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA%2C_USA_%282%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="3872" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:I_Oak_Grove_Cemetery,_Falmouth,_MA,_USA_(2).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Oak Grove Cemetery, Falmouth, MA, new monument from the Falmouth town</div></div></div>
<p>The first draft of "<a href="/wiki/America_the_Beautiful" title="America the Beautiful">America the Beautiful</a>" was hastily jotted down in a notebook during the summer of 1893, which Bates spent teaching English at The Colorado Summer School (affiliated with <a href="/wiki/Colorado_College" title="Colorado College">Colorado College</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Springs,_Colorado" title="Colorado Springs, Colorado">Colorado Springs, Colorado</a>. Later she remembered:
</p>
<blockquote><p>One day some of the other teachers and I decided to go on a trip to 14,000-foot <a href="/wiki/Pikes_Peak" title="Pikes Peak">Pikes Peak</a>. We hired a prairie wagon. Near the top we had to leave the wagon and go the rest of the way on <a href="/wiki/Mule" title="Mule">mules</a>. I was very tired. But when I saw the view, I felt great joy. All the wonder of America seemed displayed there, with the sea-like expanse.</p></blockquote>
<p>Ponder explains how Bates had personally experienced <a href="/wiki/Sexism" title="Sexism">sexist prejudice</a> and discrimination, had witnessed the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution#Social_effects" title="Industrial Revolution">ravages of the industrial revolution</a> in both America and Britain, had seen first hand <a href="/wiki/Urban_poverty" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban poverty">urban poverty</a> and misery, and keenly wished for equality. Ponder shows that it was this desire for an all-inclusive <a href="/wiki/Egalitarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Egalitarian">egalitarian</a> American community that inspired the poem, which was written during the severe <a href="/wiki/Panic_of_1893" title="Panic of 1893">economic depression of 1893</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The words to her famous poem first appeared in print in <i>The Congregationalist</i>, a weekly journal, for <a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(United_States)" title="Independence Day (United States)">Independence Day</a>, 1895. The poem reached a wider audience when her revised version was printed in the <i><a href="/wiki/Boston_Evening_Transcript" title="Boston Evening Transcript">Boston Evening Transcript</a></i> on November 19, 1904. Her final expanded version was written in 1913. When a version appeared in her collection <i>America the Beautiful, and Other Poems</i> (1912), a reviewer in the <i>New York Times</i> wrote: "we intend no derogation to Miss Katharine Lee Bates when we say that she is a good minor poet."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> On November 11, 1918, a battalion of the <a href="/wiki/26th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="26th Infantry Division (United States)">26th Infantry Division</a> of the US Army (colloquially known as the Yankee Division) sang "America the Beautiful" upon hearing the announcement of the <a href="/wiki/Armistice_of_11_November_1918" title="Armistice of 11 November 1918">Armistice</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>xvii</span></sup> The hymn has been sung to several tunes, but the familiar one is by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_A._Ward" title="Samuel A. Ward">Samuel A. Ward</a> (1847–1903), written for his hymn "Materna" (1882).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_Katharine_Coman">Relationship with Katharine Coman</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katharine_Lee_Bates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relationship with Katharine Coman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Katharine_Coman.JPG" class="image"><img alt="photograph of Katharine Coman" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Katharine_Coman.JPG/220px-Katharine_Coman.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="279" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Katharine_Coman.JPG/330px-Katharine_Coman.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c2/Katharine_Coman.JPG 2x" data-file-width="332" data-file-height="421" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Katharine_Coman.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Photo of Katharine Coman, also a professor at Wellesley.</div></div></div>
<p>The nature of Bates's relationship with her Wellesley College faculty colleague, friend, occasional traveling companion, and "Scarab House" tenant Katharine Coman has been the subject of scholarly discussion for four decades.  In her 2017 biography of Bates, Melinda M. Ponder describes Bates as an independent-minded social activist who set an example for women's intellectualism and independence in the late 1800s.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Relying on Bates' adult diaries and surviving letters, Ponder documents Bates's warm friendships with several female peers, as well as her intense emotional involvement with, and delight in the companionship of, two men: Oscar Triggs (whom she met while at Oxford) and subsequently Theophilus Huntington Root (the brother of one of her Wellesley classmates).<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> But Bates never married.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Had she done so, she would have lost her hard-won tenure-track Wellesley faculty position and, of course, at least some of the independence that her childhood in a woman-led household and subsequent life-course had, to one degree or another, accustomed her.  That said, the end of her courtship with Theophilus Root precipitated in Bates a period of suicidal depression.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  Still, as Ponder notes, Bates destroyed most of the letters she and Coman had written to each other.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>104</span></sup> One of the few to survive was written by Bates to Coman in 1893, just before she left Oxford to return to Wellesley: "You are always in my heart and in my longings... It was the living away from you that made, at first, the prospect of leaving Wellesley so heartachy ... and it seemed least of all possible when I had just found the long-desire way to your dearest heart."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>104</span></sup> In her final analysis, Ponder stresses Coman's importance to Bates in showing her how college professors like themselves could "challenge accepted attitudes towards women's social, economic, cultural, and gender roles".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>263</span></sup> In her virtuosic corona of <a href="/wiki/Sonnets" class="mw-redirect" title="Sonnets">sonnets</a> "In Bohemia," Bates celebrates the "vitality, adventurous spirit, and abiding spiritual presence of their love".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>267</span></sup>
</p><p>In earlier commentary Judith Schwarz interpreted Bates's letters and poems to Coman as evidence of a lesbian relationship,<sup id="cite_ref-:2_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> citing as an example Bates's 1891 letter to Coman: "It was never very possible to leave Wellesley [for good], because so many love-anchors held me there, and it seemed least of all possible when I had just found the long-desired way to your dearest heart... Of course I want to come to you, very much as I want to come to Heaven."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> And in 1999 historian <a href="/wiki/Lillian_Faderman" title="Lillian Faderman">Lillian Faderman</a> also concluded that the relationship between Bates and Coman was a "lesbian arrangement," including them among the other women faculty at Wellesley who paired off with each other.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>196</span></sup>  Other scholars contest the use of the term lesbian to describe what was characterized at the time as a "<a href="/wiki/Boston_marriage" title="Boston marriage">Boston marriage</a>". Writes one: "We cannot say with certainty what sexual connotations these relationships conveyed. We do know that these relationships were deeply intellectual; they fostered verbal and physical expressions of love."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Certainly, Bates long shared rental housing with various Wellesley faculty members, all of whom thereby economized while earning small salaries.  Occasionally she traveled with her long-time friend, Katharine Coman.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> And in 1910, when a colleague described "free-flying spinsters" as "fringe on the garment of life", Bates answered: "I always thought the fringe had the best of it. I don't think I mind not being woven in."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Bates's and Coman's connection is perhaps destined to be interpreted differently by different readers forever.  The facts remain that Coman and Bates met at Wellesley in 1885<sup id="cite_ref-:1_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> when the president of Wellesley College, <a href="/wiki/Alice_Freeman_Palmer" title="Alice Freeman Palmer">Alice Freeman Palmer</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:4_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> determined to add women to the college's faculty.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Coman served as a history and political economy professor, and founded the Wellesley College Economics Department. During her lifetime, Coman was nearly as well known as Bates.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>61</span></sup> Both colleagues became influential independent women within their fields during their lifetimes; and Bates's work has continued to influence American life and literature to this day.  In the days after Coman's death from breast cancer in 1915, Bates wrote a memorial to Coman, which is thought to be the first American narrative about breast cancer.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Bates intended that the manuscript be privately circulated among the women's close circle of friends and family, writing on the title page: "For Katharine Coman's family and innermost circle of friends: Not for print nor in any way for general circulation."<sup id="cite_ref-:1_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>63</span></sup> In 1922, Bates published <i>Yellow Clover: A Book of Remembrance</i>,  a collection of poems she had addressed to Coman while alive or since her death. She dedicated the volume to Coman, referred to her as "my Friend [sic]", and included as a "Prefatory Note" a three-page biography of Coman largely focused on her career as an economist and historian, but written in a tone personal enough to allow a reference to her "vigorous and adventurous personality" and her "undaunted courage" in continuing to work during her final illness.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors">Honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katharine_Lee_Bates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Katherine_Lee_Bates_House,_Falmouth,_MA.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Katherine_Lee_Bates_House%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA.jpg/220px-Katherine_Lee_Bates_House%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Katherine_Lee_Bates_House%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA.jpg/330px-Katherine_Lee_Bates_House%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Katherine_Lee_Bates_House%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA.jpg/440px-Katherine_Lee_Bates_House%2C_Falmouth%2C_MA.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3872" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Katherine_Lee_Bates_House,_Falmouth,_MA.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Katharine Lee Bates House, Falmouth, Massachusetts</div></div></div> 
<p>The Bates family home on Falmouth's Main Street is preserved by the Falmouth Historical Society. There is also a street named in her honor, "Katharine Lee Bates Road" in Falmouth. The <a href="/wiki/Shining_Sea_Bikeway" title="Shining Sea Bikeway">Shining Sea Bikeway</a>, named in honor of Bates, extends 11 miles from North Falmouth to Woods Hole, passing just a block from the Bates home.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> A plaque marks the site of the home where she lived as an adult on Centre Street in <a href="/wiki/Newton,_Massachusetts" title="Newton, Massachusetts">Newton, Massachusetts</a>. The historic home and birthplace of Bates in Falmouth, was sold to Ruth P. Clark in November 2013 for $1,200,000.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Katharine Lee Bates Elementary School on Elmwood Road in <a href="/wiki/Wellesley,_Massachusetts" title="Wellesley, Massachusetts">Wellesley, Massachusetts</a>, and the Katharine Lee Bates Elementary School, founded in 1957 in <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Springs,_Colorado" title="Colorado Springs, Colorado">Colorado Springs, Colorado</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and Bates Hall dormitory at Wellesley College are named for her. The Katharine Lee Bates Chair in English Composition and Literature was established at Wellesley shortly after her death.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Bates was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Songwriters_Hall_of_Fame" title="Songwriters Hall of Fame">Songwriters Hall of Fame</a> in 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Collections of Bates's manuscripts are housed at the <a href="/wiki/Schlesinger_Library" title="Schlesinger Library">Arthur and Elizabeth Schlesinger Library on the History of Women in America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_College" title="Radcliffe College">Radcliffe College</a>; Falmouth Historical Society; <a href="/wiki/Houghton_Library" title="Houghton Library">Houghton Library</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>; Wellesley College Archives.<sup id="cite_ref-ContemparyBiography_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ContemparyBiography-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, she was named by <a href="/wiki/Equality_Forum" class="mw-redirect" title="Equality Forum">Equality Forum</a> as one of their 31 Icons of the 2015 <a href="/wiki/LGBT_History_Month" title="LGBT History Month">LGBT History Month</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
