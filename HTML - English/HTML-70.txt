<p><b>John Day</b> (1574–1638?) was an English dramatist of the <a href="/wiki/Elizabethan_era" title="Elizabethan era">Elizabethan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Literature_in_English#Jacobean_literature" class="mw-redirect" title="Literature in English">Jacobean</a> periods.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Day_(dramatist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was born at <a href="/wiki/Cawston,_Norfolk" title="Cawston, Norfolk">Cawston, Norfolk</a>, and educated at <a href="/wiki/Ely,_Cambridgeshire" title="Ely, Cambridgeshire">Ely</a>. He became a <a href="/wiki/Sizar" title="Sizar">sizar</a> of <a href="/wiki/Caius_College,_Cambridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Caius College, Cambridge">Caius College, Cambridge</a>, in 1592, but was expelled in the next year for stealing a book.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He became one of <a href="/wiki/Philip_Henslowe" title="Philip Henslowe">Philip Henslowe</a>'s playwrights, collaborating with <a href="/wiki/Henry_Chettle" title="Henry Chettle">Henry Chettle</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Haughton_(playwright)" title="William Haughton (playwright)">William Haughton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Dekker_(poet)" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Dekker (poet)">Thomas Dekker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hathwaye" title="Richard Hathwaye">Richard Hathwaye</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wentworth_Smith" title="Wentworth Smith">Wentworth Smith</a>. There are 22 plays to which he is linked.
</p><p>However his almost incessant activity does not seem to have paid, to judge by the small loans, of five <a href="/wiki/Shilling" title="Shilling">shillings</a> and even two shillings, that he obtained from Henslowe. Little is known of his life beyond these small details, and disparaging references by Ben Jonson in 1618/19, describing him, (with Dekker and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Sharpham" title="Edward Sharpham">Edward Sharpham</a>) as a "rogue" and (with <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Middleton" title="Thomas Middleton">Thomas Middleton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gervase_Markham" title="Gervase Markham">Gervase Markham</a>) as a "base fellow". It may be indicative of his abilities that of all the writers who did a substantial amount of work for Henslowe's companies Day is one of only two not mentioned and praised by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Meres" title="Francis Meres">Francis Meres</a> in his lists of "the best" writers in 1598. In <i>Peregrinatio Scholastica, or Learning's Pilgrimage</i>, a collection of 22 <i>morall Tractes</i> written towards the end of his life, but not published until 1881, he laments that "notwithstanding . . . Industry . . . he was forct to take a napp at Beggars Bushe", and elsewhere he refers to "being becalmde in a fogg of necessity" having been passed over by "Credit" and "Opinion". It seems likely that he was the "John Daye, yeoman" who killed fellow dramatist <a href="/wiki/Henry_Porter_(playwright)" title="Henry Porter (playwright)">Henry Porter</a> in Southwark 1599. If so it does not seem have to interrupted his career; he continued to collaborate with writers such as Henry Chettle, who had written with Porter.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Day_(dramatist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first play in which Day appears as part-author is <i>The Conquest of Brute, with the finding of the Bath</i> (1598), which, with most of his early work, is lost. Day's earliest extant work, written in collaboration with Chettle, is <i>The Blind Beggar of Bethnal Green</i> (acted 1600, printed 1659), a drama dealing with the early years of the reign of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VI_of_England" title="Henry VI of England">Henry VI</a>. It bore the sub-title of <i>The Merry Humor of Tom Strowd, the Norfolk Yeoman</i>, and was so popular that second and third parts, by Day and Haughton, were produced in the next year. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Isle_of_Gulls" title="The Isle of Gulls">The Isle of Gulls</a></i> (printed 1606), a prose comedy founded upon <a href="/wiki/Sir_Philip_Sidney" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Philip Sidney">Sir Philip Sidney</a>'s <i>Arcadia</i>, contains in its light dialogue much satire to which the key is now lost, but <a href="/wiki/Algernon_Charles_Swinburne" title="Algernon Charles Swinburne">Algernon Charles Swinburne</a> notes in Manasses's burlesque of a <a href="/wiki/Puritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> sermon is a curious anticipation of the eloquence of Mr. Chadband in <i><a href="/wiki/Bleak_House" title="Bleak House">Bleak House</a></i>. In 1607 Day produced, in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/William_Rowley" title="William Rowley">William Rowley</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Wilkins" title="George Wilkins">George Wilkins</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Travels_of_the_Three_English_Brothers" title="The Travels of the Three English Brothers">The Travels of the Three English Brothers</a></i>, which detailed the adventures of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Shirley" title="Thomas Shirley">Sir Thomas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Shirley" title="Anthony Shirley">Sir Anthony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Shirley" title="Robert Shirley">Robert Shirley</a>. This play is a dramatic <a href="/wiki/Romance_(heroic_literature)" class="mw-redirect" title="Romance (heroic literature)">romance</a> of a type that hearkened back to the early decades of the public stage in London. In 1608 Day published two comedies, <i>Law Tricks, or Who Would have Thought it?</i> and <i>Humour out of Breath</i>.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Parliament_of_Bees" title="The Parliament of Bees">The Parliament of Bees</a></i> is the work on which Day's reputation chiefly rests. The piece contains much for which parallel passages are found in Thomas Dekker's <i>Wonder of a Kingdom</i> (1636) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Noble_Spanish_Soldier" title="The Noble Spanish Soldier">The Noble Spanish Soldier</a></i> (printed 1634). The passages which echo <i>The Noble Spanish Soldier</i> include references to speaking Spanish which are only meaningful in the context of Dekker's play; this suggests that the Dekker play is the original, a possibility reinforced by the consideration that there is no known edition of <i>The Parliament of Bees</i> earlier than 1641.
</p><p>The six dramas by Day which we possess show a delicate fancy and dainty inventiveness all his own. He preserved, in a great measure, the dramatic tradition of <a href="/wiki/John_Lyly" title="John Lyly">John Lyly</a>, and affected a kind of subdued <a href="/wiki/Euphuism" title="Euphuism">euphuism</a>. Without ever wholly abandoning these characteristics, Day's comedy also reveals some influence of early Jacobean satirists such as <a href="/wiki/John_Marston_(poet)" title="John Marston (poet)">John Marston</a>, who like Day wrote for the children's companies. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Maid%27s_Metamorphosis" title="The Maid&#39;s Metamorphosis">The Maid's Metamorphosis</a></i> (1600), once supposed to be a posthumous work of Lyly's, may be an early work of Day's. It possesses, at all events, many of his marked characteristics. His prose <i>Peregrinatio Scholastica or Learninges Pilgrimage</i>, dating from his later years, was printed by <a href="/wiki/A._H._Bullen" class="mw-redirect" title="A. H. Bullen">A. H. Bullen</a> from a manuscript of Day's. Considerations partly based on this work have suggested that he had a share in the anonymous <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pilgrimage_to_Parnassus" class="mw-redirect" title="The Pilgrimage to Parnassus">The Pilgrimage to Parnassus</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Return_from_Parnassus" class="mw-redirect" title="Return from Parnassus">Return from Parnassus</a></i>. The beauty and ingenuity of <i>The Parliament of Bees</i> were noted and warmly extolled by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lamb" title="Charles Lamb">Charles Lamb</a>; and Day's work has since found many admirers.
The date of his death is unknown, but an elegy on him by <a href="/wiki/John_Tatham" title="John Tatham">John Tatham</a>, the city poet, was published in 1640.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication">Publication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Day_(dramatist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Publication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His works, edited by Bullen, were printed at the Chiswick Press in 1881. The same editor included <i><a href="/wiki/The_Maid%27s_Metamorphosis" title="The Maid&#39;s Metamorphosis">The Maid's Metamorphosis</a></i> in Vol. 1 of his <i>Collection of Old Plays</i>. <i>The Parliament of Bees</i> and <i>Humour out of Breath</i> were printed in <i>Nero and other Plays</i> (<a href="/wiki/Mermaid_Series" title="Mermaid Series">Mermaid Series</a>, 1888), with an introduction by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Symons" title="Arthur Symons">Arthur Symons</a>. An appreciation by <a href="/wiki/Algernon_Charles_Swinburne" title="Algernon Charles Swinburne">Algernon Charles Swinburne</a> appeared in <i>The Nineteenth Century</i> (October 1897).
</p>
