	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Geist</i></h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about the German word. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Geist_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Geist (disambiguation)">Geist (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><i><b>Geist</b></i> (<small>German pronunciation:&#32;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈɡaɪst]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> noun with a degree of importance in <a href="/wiki/German_philosophy" title="German philosophy">German philosophy</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/Semantic_field" title="Semantic field">semantic field</a> corresponds to English <a href="/wiki/Ghost" title="Ghost">ghost</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spirit" title="Spirit">spirit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mind" title="Mind">mind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intellect" title="Intellect">intellect</a>. Some English translators resort to using "spirit/mind" or "spirit (mind)" to help convey the meaning of the term.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p><i>Geist</i> is also a central concept in <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel" title="Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel">Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel</a>'s 1807 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Phenomenology_of_Spirit" title="The Phenomenology of Spirit">The Phenomenology of Spirit</a></i> (<i>Phänomenologie des Geistes</i>). Notable compounds, all associated with Hegel's view of <a href="/wiki/World_history" title="World history">world history</a> of the late 18th century, include  <i><b>Weltgeist</b></i> "world-spirit", <i><b>Volksgeist</b></i> "national spirit"  and  <i><b>Zeitgeist</b></i>   "spirit of the age".
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_translation">Etymology and translation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and translation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Spirit" title="Spirit">Spirit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pneuma" title="Pneuma">Pneuma</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psyche_(psychology)" title="Psyche (psychology)">Psyche (psychology)</a></div>
<p>German <i>Geist</i> (masculine gender) continues <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <i>geist</i>, attested as the translation of Latin <i>spiritus</i>. 
It is the direct cognate of English <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ghost" class="extiw" title="wikt:ghost">ghost</a></i>, from a <a href="/wiki/West_Germanic" class="mw-redirect" title="West Germanic">West Germanic</a> <i>gaistaz</i>. Its derivation from a <a href="/wiki/PIE_root" class="mw-redirect" title="PIE root">PIE root</a> <i>g̑heis-</i> "to be agitated, frightened" suggests that the Germanic word originally referred to frightening  (c.f. English <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ghastly" class="extiw" title="wikt:ghastly">ghastly</a>) apparitions or <a href="/wiki/Ghost" title="Ghost">ghosts</a>, and may also have carried the connotation of "ecstatic agitation, <i><a href="/wiki/Odhr" class="mw-redirect" title="Odhr">furor</a></i>" related to the cult of <a href="/wiki/Germanic_Mercury" class="mw-redirect" title="Germanic Mercury">Germanic Mercury</a>. 
As the translation of biblical Latin <i>spiritus</i> (Greek πνεῦμα) "<a href="/wiki/Pneuma" title="Pneuma">spirit, breath</a>" the Germanic word acquires a Christian meaning from an early time, notably in reference to the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit" title="Holy Spirit">Holy Spirit</a> (Old English <i>sē hālga gāst</i> "the Holy Ghost", OHG <i> ther heilago geist</i>, Modern German <i>der Heilige Geist</i>).
The English word is in competition with Latinate <i>spirit</i> from the Middle English period, but its broader meaning is preserved well into the early modern period.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The German noun much like English <i>spirit</i> could refer to spooks or ghostly apparitions of the dead,  to the religious concept, as in the Holy Spirit, as well as to the "spirit of wine", i.e. <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>. 
However, its special meaning of "<a href="/wiki/Mind" title="Mind">mind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intellect" title="Intellect">intellect</a>" never shared by English <i>ghost</i> is acquired only in the 18th century, under the influence of French <i>esprit</i>.
In this sense it became extremely productive in the German language of the 18th century in general as well as in 18th-century German philosophy.
<i>Geist</i> could now refer to the quality of intellectual brilliance, to wit, innovation, erudition, etc.
It is also in this time that the adjectival distinction of <i>geistlich</i> "spiritual, pertaining to religion" vs. <i>geistig</i> "intellectual, pertaining to the mind" begins to be made. Reference to spooks or ghosts  is made by the adjective <i>geisterhaft</i> "ghostly, spectral".<sup id="cite_ref-Pfeifer_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pfeifer-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Numerous <a href="/wiki/German_compounds" class="mw-redirect" title="German compounds">compounds</a> are formed in the 18th to 19th centuries, some of them loan translations of French expressions, such as <i>Geistesgegenwart</i> = <i>présence d'esprit</i> ("mental presence, acuity"), <i>Geistesabwesenheit</i> = <i>absence d’esprit</i> ("mental absence,  distraction"), <i>geisteskrank</i> "mentally ill", <i>geistreich</i> "witty, intellectually brilliant", <i>geistlos</i> "unintelligent, unimaginative, vacuous" etc. 
It is from these developments that certain German compounds containing <i>-geist</i> have been loaned into English, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Zeitgeist" title="Zeitgeist">Zeitgeist</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>German <i>Geist</i> in this particular sense of "mind, wit, erudition; intangible essence, spirit" has no precise English-language equivalent, for which reason translators sometimes retain <i>Geist</i> as a German loanword.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hegelianism">Hegelianism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hegelianism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Geist</i> is a central concept in <a href="/wiki/Hegel" class="mw-redirect" title="Hegel">Hegel</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Phenomenology_of_Spirit" title="The Phenomenology of Spirit">The Phenomenology of Spirit</a></i> (<i>Phänomenologie des Geistes</i>).  According to Hegel, the <i>Weltgeist</i> ("world spirit") is not an actual object or a transcendental, Godlike thing, but a means of philosophizing about history.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  <i>Weltgeist</i> is effected in history through the <a href="/wiki/Mediation" title="Mediation">mediation</a> of various <i><a href="/wiki/Volksgeist" class="mw-redirect" title="Volksgeist">Volksgeister</a></i> ("national spirits"), the <a href="/wiki/Hero" title="Hero">great men</a> of history, such as <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>, are the "<a href="/wiki/Concrete_(philosophy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Concrete (philosophy)">concrete</a> <a href="/wiki/Universality_(philosophy)" title="Universality (philosophy)">universal</a>".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>This has led some to claim that Hegel favored the <a href="/wiki/Great_man_theory" title="Great man theory">great man theory</a>, although his <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_history" title="Philosophy of history">philosophy of history</a>, in particular concerning the role of the "<a href="/wiki/Universality_(philosophy)" title="Universality (philosophy)">universal</a> state" (<i>Universalstaat</i>, which means a universal "order" or "statute" rather than "<a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a>"), and of an "End of History" is much more complex.
</p><p>For Hegel, the great hero is unwittingly utilized by <i>Geist</i> or <i>absolute spirit</i>, by a "ruse of reason" as he puts it, and is irrelevant to history once his historic mission is accomplished; he is thus subjected to the <a href="/wiki/Teleology" title="Teleology">teleological</a> principle of history, a principle which allows Hegel to reread the history of philosophy as culminating in his philosophy of history.
</p><p><i>Weltgeist</i>, the world spirit concept, designates an <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">idealistic</a> principle of world explanation, which can be found from the beginnings of philosophy up to more recent time. The concept of world spirit was already accepted by the idealistic schools of ancient Indian philosophy, whereby one explained <a href="/wiki/Objective_reality" class="mw-redirect" title="Objective reality">objective reality</a> as its product. (See <a href="/wiki/Metaphysical_objectivism" class="mw-redirect" title="Metaphysical objectivism">metaphysical objectivism</a>) In the early philosophy of Greek antiquity, <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> all paid homage, amongst other things, to the <a href="/wiki/Concept" title="Concept">concept</a> of world spirit. Hegel later based his philosophy of history on it.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weltgeist"><i>Weltgeist</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Weltgeist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Anima_Mundi" class="mw-redirect" title="Anima Mundi">Anima Mundi</a></div>
<p><i>Weltgeist</i> "world-spirit" is older than the 18th century, at first (16th century) in the sense of "secularism, impiety, irreligiosity" (<i>spiritus mundi</i>), in the 17th century also personalised in the sense of  "man of the world", "mundane or secular person".
Also from the 17th century, <i>Weltgeist</i> acquired a philosophical or spiritual sense of "world-spirit" or "world-soul" (<i>anima mundi, spiritus universi</i>) in the sense of <a href="/wiki/Panentheism" title="Panentheism">Panentheism</a>, a spiritual essence permeating all of nature, or the active principle animating the universe, including the physical sense, such as the attraction  between <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_force" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnetic force">magnet and iron</a> or between <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">Moon and tide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This idea of <i>Weltgeist</i> in the sense of <i>anima mundi</i> became very influential in 18th-century German philosophy. In philosophical contexts, <i>der Geist</i> on its own could refer to this concept, as in <a href="/wiki/Christian_Thomasius" title="Christian Thomasius">Christian Thomasius</a>, <i>Versuch vom Wesen des Geistes</i> (1709).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
Belief in a <i>Weltgeist</i> as animating principle immanent to the universe became dominant in German thought due to the influence of <a href="/wiki/Goethe" class="mw-redirect" title="Goethe">Goethe</a>, in the later part of the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Already in the  poetical language of Johann Ulrich von König (d. 1745), the <i>Weltgeist</i> 
appears as the active, masculine principle opposite the feminine principle of <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">Nature</a>.
<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
<i>Weltgeist</i> in the sense of Goethe comes close to being a synonym of <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> and can be attributed agency and will. 
<a href="/wiki/Johann_Gottfried_Herder" title="Johann Gottfried Herder">Herder</a>, who tended to prefer the form <i>Weltengeist</i> (as it were "spirit of worlds"), pushes this to the point of composing prayers addressed to this world-spirit:
</p>
<dl><dd><i> O Weltengeist, Bist du so gütig, wie du mächtig bist, Enthülle mir, den du mitfühlend zwar, Und doch so grausam schufst, erkläre mir Das Loos der Fühlenden, die durch mich leiden.</i></dd>
<dd>"O World-spirit, be as benevolent as you are powerful and reveal to me, whom you have created with compassion and yet cruelly, explain to me the lot of the sentient, who suffer through me"<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hegel-and-Napoleon-in-Jena-1806.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Hegel-and-Napoleon-in-Jena-1806.jpg/170px-Hegel-and-Napoleon-in-Jena-1806.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="199" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Hegel-and-Napoleon-in-Jena-1806.jpg/255px-Hegel-and-Napoleon-in-Jena-1806.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/22/Hegel-and-Napoleon-in-Jena-1806.jpg 2x" data-file-width="303" data-file-height="354" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hegel-and-Napoleon-in-Jena-1806.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"Hegel and Napoleon in Jena" (illustration from <i>Harper's Magazine</i>, 1895)</div></div></div>
<p>The term was notably embraced by <a href="/wiki/Hegel" class="mw-redirect" title="Hegel">Hegel</a> and his followers in the early 19th century.
For the 19th century, the term as used by <a href="/wiki/The_Phenomenology_of_Spirit" title="The Phenomenology of Spirit">Hegel (1807)</a> became prevalent, less in the sense of an animating principle of nature or the universe but as the invisible force advancing <a href="/wiki/World_history" title="World history">world history</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>Im Gange der Geschichte ist das eine wesentliche Moment die Erhaltung eines Volkes [...] das andere Moment aber ist, daß der Bestand eines Volksgeistes, wie er ist, durchbrochen wird, weil er sich ausgeschöpft und ausgearbeitet hat, daß die Weltgeschichte, der Weltgeist fortgeht.</i></dd>
<dd>"In the course of history one relevant factor is the preservation of a <a href="/wiki/Volk" title="Volk">nation</a> [...] while the other factor is that the continued existence of a national spirit [<i>Volksgeist</i>] is interrupted because it has exhausted and spent itself, so that world history, the world spirit [<i>Weltgeist</i>], proceeds."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Hegel's description of <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a> as "the world-soul on horseback" (<i>die Weltseele zu Pferde</i>) became proverbial.
The phrase is a shortened paraphrase of Hegel's words in  a letter written  on 13 October 1806, the day before the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Jena" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Jena">Battle of Jena</a>, to his friend   <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Immanuel_Niethammer" title="Friedrich Immanuel Niethammer">Friedrich Immanuel Niethammer</a>:
</p>
<blockquote><p>I saw the Emperor – this world-soul – riding out of the city on reconnaissance. It is indeed a wonderful sensation to see such an individual, who, concentrated here at a single point, astride a horse, reaches out over the world and masters it.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>The letter was not published in Hegel's time, but the expression was attributed to Hegel anecdotally, appearing in print from 1859.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
It is used without attribution by <a href="/wiki/Meyer_Kayserling" title="Meyer Kayserling">Meyer Kayserling</a> in his <i>Sephardim</i> (1859:103), and is apparently not recognized as a reference to Hegel by the reviewer in <i><a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6ttingische_gelehrte_Anzeigen" class="mw-redirect" title="Göttingische gelehrte Anzeigen">Göttingische gelehrte Anzeigen</a></i>, who notes it disapprovingly, as one of Kayserling's "bad jokes"  (<i>schlechte Witze</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
The phrase become widely associated with Hegel later in the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Volksgeist"><i>Volksgeist</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Volksgeist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"National spirit" redirects here. For the racehorse, see <a href="/wiki/National_Spirit_(horse)" title="National Spirit (horse)">National Spirit (horse)</a>. For the horse race, see <a href="/wiki/National_Spirit_Hurdle" title="National Spirit Hurdle">National Spirit Hurdle</a>.</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/National_character" class="mw-redirect" title="National character">National character</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_essentialism" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnic essentialism">Ethnic essentialism</a></div>
<p><i>Volksgeist</i> or <i>Nationalgeist</i> refers to a "spirit" of an individual <a href="/wiki/Volk" title="Volk">people (<i>Volk</i>)</a>, its "national spirit" or "national character".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The term <i>Nationalgeist</i> is used in the 1760s by <a href="/wiki/Justus_M%C3%B6ser" title="Justus Möser">Justus Möser</a> and by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Gottfried_Herder" title="Johann Gottfried Herder">Johann Gottfried Herder</a>. The term <i>Nation</i> at this time is used in the sense of <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/natio#Latin" class="extiw" title="wikt:natio">natio</a></i> "nation, ethnic group, race", mostly replaced by the term <i>Volk</i> after 1800.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
In the early 19th century, the term <i>Volksgeist</i> was used by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Carl_von_Savigny" title="Friedrich Carl von Savigny">Friedrich Carl von Savigny</a> in order to express the "popular" sense of <a href="/wiki/Justice" title="Justice">justice</a>.
Savigniy explicitly referred to the concept of an <i>esprit des nations </i> used by <a href="/wiki/Voltaire" title="Voltaire">Voltaire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and of the <i>esprit général</i> invoked by  <a href="/wiki/Montesquieu" title="Montesquieu">Montesquieu</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hegel uses the term in his <i><a href="/wiki/Lectures_on_the_Philosophy_of_History" title="Lectures on the Philosophy of History">Lectures on the Philosophy of History</a></i>.
Based on the Hegelian use of the term, <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Wundt" title="Wilhelm Wundt">Wilhelm Wundt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moritz_Lazarus" title="Moritz Lazarus">Moritz Lazarus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heymann_Steinthal" title="Heymann Steinthal">Heymann Steinthal</a> in the mid-19th-century established the field of <i><a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lkerpsychologie" title="Völkerpsychologie">Völkerpsychologie</a></i> ("psychology of nations").
</p><p>In Germany the concept of Volksgeist has developed and changed its meaning through eras and fields. The most important examples are: In the literary field, <a href="/wiki/August_Wilhelm_Schlegel" title="August Wilhelm Schlegel">Schlegel</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Brothers_Grimm" title="Brothers Grimm">Brothers Grimm</a>. In the history of cultures, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Gottfried_Herder" title="Johann Gottfried Herder">Herder</a>. In the history of the State or political history, <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel" title="Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel">Hegel</a>. In the field of law, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Carl_von_Savigny" title="Friedrich Carl von Savigny">Savigny</a> and in the field of psychology <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Wundt" title="Wilhelm Wundt">Wundt</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> This means that the concept is ambiguous. Furthermore it is not limited to <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romanticism</a> as it is commonly known.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The concept of was also influential in American cultural anthropology. According to the historian of anthropology <a href="/wiki/George_W._Stocking,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="George W. Stocking, Jr.">George W. Stocking, Jr.</a>, "… one may trace the later American anthropological idea of culture back through Bastian's Volkergedanken and the folk psychologist's Volksgeister to Wilhelm von Humboldt's Nationalcharakter -- and behind that, although not without a paradoxical and portentous residue of conceptual and ideological ambiguity, to the Herderian ideal of Volksgeist."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="Need the year this event took place (March 2018)">year&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (March 2018)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Zeitgeist"><i>Zeitgeist</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Zeitgeist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Zeitgeist" title="Zeitgeist">Zeitgeist</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Multiple_discovery" title="Multiple discovery">Multiple discovery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Epochalism" title="Epochalism">Epochalism</a></div>
<p>The compound <i>Zeitgeist</i>  (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;z&#39; in &#39;zoom&#39;">z</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/ɡ/: &#39;g&#39; in &#39;guy&#39;">ɡ</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>, "spirit of the age" or "spirit of the times") similarly to <i>Weltgeist</i> describes 
an invisible agent or force dominating the characteristics of a given epoch in <a href="/wiki/World_history" title="World history">world history</a>.
The term is now mostly associated with <a href="/wiki/Hegel" class="mw-redirect" title="Hegel">Hegel</a>, contrasting with Hegel's use of <i>Volksgeist</i> "national spirit" and <i>Weltgeist</i> "world-spirit", 
but its coinage and popularization precedes Hegel, and is mostly due to <a href="/wiki/Johann_Gottfried_Herder" title="Johann Gottfried Herder">Herder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goethe" class="mw-redirect" title="Goethe">Goethe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term as used contemporarily may more pragmatically refer to  a <a href="/wiki/Fad" title="Fad">fashion or fad</a> which prescribes what is acceptable or tasteful, e.g. in the field of <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-StF_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StF-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hegel in <i><a href="/wiki/Phenomenology_of_the_Spirit" class="mw-redirect" title="Phenomenology of the Spirit">Phenomenology of the Spirit</a></i> (1807) uses both <i>Weltgeist</i> and <i>Volksgeist</i> but prefers the phrase <i>Geist der Zeiten</i> "spirit of the times" over the <a href="/wiki/German_compound" class="mw-redirect" title="German compound">compound</a> <i>Zeitgeist</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hegel believed that culture and art reflected its time. Thus, he argued<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="Need the year this event took place (March 2018)">year&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (March 2018)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> that it would be impossible to produce classical art in the modern world, as modernity is essentially a  "free and ethical culture".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term has also been used more widely in the  sense of an intellectual or aesthetic <a href="/wiki/Fashion" title="Fashion">fashion</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fad" title="Fad">fad</a>.
For example, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>'s 1859 proposition that <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> occurs by <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a> has been cited as a case of the <i>zeitgeist</i> of the epoch, an idea "whose time had come", seeing that his contemporary, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Russel_Wallace" title="Alfred Russel Wallace">Alfred Russel Wallace</a>, was outlining similar models during the same period.<sup id="cite_ref-Hothersall_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hothersall-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> 
Similarly, intellectual fashions such as the emergence of <a href="/wiki/Logical_positivism" title="Logical positivism">logical positivism</a> in the 1920s, leading to a focus on <a href="/wiki/Behaviorism" title="Behaviorism">behaviorism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blank-slatism" class="mw-redirect" title="Blank-slatism">blank-slatism</a> over the following decades, and later, during the 1950s to 1960s,  the  shift from behaviorism to <a href="/wiki/Post-modernism" class="mw-redirect" title="Post-modernism">post-modernism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Critical_theory" title="Critical theory">critical theory</a>  can be argued to be an expression of the intellectual or academic "zeitgeist".<sup id="cite_ref-Hothersall_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hothersall-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
<i>Zeitgeist</i> in more recent usage has been used by Forsyth (2009) in reference to his "theory of <a href="/wiki/Leadership" title="Leadership">leadership</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-Forsyth,_D._R._2009_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forsyth,_D._R._2009-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> and in other publications describing models of business or industry.
<a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Gladwell" title="Malcolm Gladwell">Malcolm Gladwell</a> argued in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Outliers_(book)" title="Outliers (book)">Outliers</a></i> that entrepreneurs who succeeded in the early stages of a nascent industry often share similar characteristics.
</p>
