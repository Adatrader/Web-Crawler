<p><b>Charles du Fresne, sieur du Cange</b> or <b>Du Cange</b> (<small>French:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[dy kɑ̃ʒ]</a></span>; December 18, 1610 in <a href="/wiki/Amiens" title="Amiens">Amiens</a> – October 23, 1688 in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, aged 77) was a distinguished <a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">philologist</a> and historian of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantium</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_du_Fresne,_sieur_du_Cange&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Educated by <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Jesuits</a>, du Cange studied law and practiced for several years before assuming the office of Treasurer of France. Du Cange was a busy, energetic man who pursued historical scholarship alongside his demanding official duties and his role as head of a large family.
</p><p>Du Cange's most important work is his <i>Glossarium ad scriptores mediae et infimae Latinitatis</i> (Glossary of writers in medieval and late <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, Paris, 1678, 3 vol.), revised and expanded under various titles, for example, <i>Glossarium manuale ad scriptores mediae et infimae Latinitatis</i> (Halae, 1772-84) or from 1840 onward, <i>Glossarium mediae et infimae Latinitatis</i> (Glossary of medieval and late Latin). This work, together with a glossary of medieval and late <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> that he published ten years later, has gone through numerous editions and revisions and is still consulted frequently by scholars today. Du Cange's pioneering work distinguished medieval Latin and Greek from their earlier classical forms, marking the beginning of the study of the historical development of languages.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Du Cange mastered languages in order to pursue his main scholarly interests, medieval and Byzantine history. He corresponded voluminously with his fellow scholars. His great historical and linguistic knowledge was complemented by equally deep learning in archaeology, geography and law. In addition to his glossaries, he produced important new editions of Byzantine historians and a number of other works. His extensive history of <a href="/wiki/Illyria" title="Illyria">Illyria</a> was not published until 1746 by Joseph <a href="/wiki/House_of_Keglevi%C4%87" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Keglević">Keglevich</a>, who partially corrected it.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Du Cange is one of the historians <a href="/wiki/Edward_Gibbon" title="Edward Gibbon">Edward Gibbon</a> cites most frequently in his <a href="/wiki/The_History_of_the_Decline_and_Fall_of_the_Roman_Empire" title="The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire"><i>Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire</i></a>. In one footnote he calls du Cange "our sure and indefatigable guide in the Middle Ages and Byzantine history."
</p>
