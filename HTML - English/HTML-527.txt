	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">American Anthropological Association</h1>

<p>The <b>American Anthropological Association</b> (<b>AAA</b>) is an organization of scholars and practitioners in the field of <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>. With 10,000 members, the association, based in <a href="/wiki/Arlington,_Virginia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arlington, Virginia">Arlington, Virginia</a>, includes <a href="/wiki/Archaeologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Archaeologist">archaeologists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cultural_anthropologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Cultural anthropologist">cultural anthropologists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biological_anthropology" title="Biological anthropology">biological (or physical) anthropologists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_anthropology" title="Linguistic anthropology">linguistic anthropologists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linguist" class="mw-redirect" title="Linguist">linguists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medical_anthropology" title="Medical anthropology">medical anthropologists</a> and applied anthropologists in universities and colleges, research institutions, government agencies, museums, corporations and non-profits throughout the world. The AAA publishes more than 20 peer-reviewed <a href="/wiki/Scholarly_journal" class="mw-redirect" title="Scholarly journal">scholarly journals</a>, available in print and online through AnthroSource. The AAA was founded in 1902.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Anthropological_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first anthropological society in the US was the <a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Ethnological_Society_of_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="American Ethnological Society of New York (page does not exist)">American Ethnological Society of New York</a>, which was founded by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Gallatin" title="Albert Gallatin">Albert Gallatin</a> and revived in 1899 by Franz Boas after a hiatus. 1879 saw the establishment of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropological_Society_of_Washington&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Anthropological Society of Washington (page does not exist)">Anthropological Society of Washington</a> (which first published the journal <i><a href="/wiki/American_Anthropologist" title="American Anthropologist">American Anthropologist</a></i>, before it became a national journal), and 1882 saw the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="American Association for the Advancement of Science">American Association for the Advancement of Science</a> established an anthropological section. Boas and other anthropologist discussed the possibility of creating a single national society already in 1898, but fears that it might damage the AAAS caused a long discussion. In 1901 the AES and ASW sent members to attend the meeting of the AAAS anthropologists in Chicago in which discussions continued and there was general agreement that a national society should be formed. Boas advocated a restricted membership of 40 "professional anthropologists", but the AAA's first president, <a href="/wiki/W._J._McGee" class="mw-redirect" title="W. J. McGee">W. J. McGee</a>, ensured that membership would be open to everyone with an interest in the discipline.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Centennial_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Centennial-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> At its incorporation, it assumed responsibility for the journal<i> <a href="/wiki/American_Anthropologist" title="American Anthropologist">American Anthropologist</a></i>, created in 1888 by the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropological_Society_of_Washington&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Anthropological Society of Washington (page does not exist)">Anthropological Society of Washington</a> (ASW).
</p><p>Business affairs are conducted by a 41-member Section Assembly representing each of the association's constituent sections, and a 15-member Executive Board.
</p><p>According to its articles of incorporation, the AAA was formed to:
</p>
<blockquote><p>promote the science of anthropology, to stimulate and coordinate the efforts of American anthropologists, to foster local and other societies devoted to anthropology, to serve as a bond among American anthropologists and anthropologic[al] organizations present and prospective, and to publish and encourage the publication of matter pertaining to anthropology.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>From an initial membership of 175, the AAA grew slowly during the first half of the 20th century. Annual meetings were held primarily in the Northeast and accommodated all attendees in a single room.
</p><p>The Association describes itself as "a democratic organization since its beginning."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010, AAA Executive Board stripped the word "science" from a draft statement of its long-range plan, instead pledging to advance "the public understanding of humankind." The change set off a wide-ranging controversy over the definition of the discipline, with many archaeologists and physical anthropologists describing themselves as marginalized within the AAA.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The final version of the long-range plan begins, "The strength of Anthropology lies in its distinctive position at the nexus of the sciences and humanities" and declares, "The purpose of the Association shall be to advance scholarly understanding of humankind in all its aspects ... drawing from and building upon knowledge from biological and physical sciences as well as the humanities and social sciences."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The offices of the AAA are located in Arlington, Virginia.<sup id="cite_ref-Centennial_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Centennial-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sections">Sections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Anthropological_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The AAA is composed of 40 sections, which are groups organized around identity affiliations or intellectual interests within the discipline of anthropology. Sections each have an elected president or chair;  many publish journals and host meetings.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publications">Publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Anthropological_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The AAA publishes more than 20 section publications including <i><a href="/wiki/American_Anthropologist" title="American Anthropologist">American Anthropologist</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/American_Ethnologist" class="mw-redirect" title="American Ethnologist">American Ethnologist</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cultural_Anthropology_(journal)" title="Cultural Anthropology (journal)">Cultural Anthropology</a></i>, <i>Anthropology &amp; Education Quarterly</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Medical_Anthropology_Quarterly" title="Medical Anthropology Quarterly">Medical Anthropology Quarterly</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The AAA's official magazine, <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.anthropology-news.org/">Anthropology News</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> is published bimonthly. AAA publications are available online through <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.anthrosource.net/">AnthroSource</a>. Since 2007, journals have been published in partnership with <a href="/wiki/Wiley-Blackwell" title="Wiley-Blackwell">Wiley-Blackwell</a> since 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Since 1962 the association has published the AAA AnthroGuide, giving staff and program information about anthropology departments.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meetings">Meetings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Anthropological_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Meetings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1902, the society has held annual meetings. The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.americananthro.org/AttendEvents/Content.aspx?ItemNumber=1578&amp;navItemNumber=566">AAA Annual Meeting</a>, with more than 6,000 attendees, is the world's largest gathering of anthropologists.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The meeting is held in a different location each year.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_issues_involvement">Public issues involvement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Anthropological_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Public issues involvement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The AAA supported the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Antiquities_Act_of_1906" class="mw-redirect" title="Antiquities Act of 1906">Antiquities Act of 1906</a>, protested the discontinuance of anthropological research in the Philippines (1915), urged the teaching of anthropology in high schools (1927), spoke out for the preservation of archaeological materials when dams were built by the <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Valley_Authority" title="Tennessee Valley Authority">Tennessee Valley Authority</a> (1935), passed a pre-WWII resolution against racism (1938), and expressed the need to "guard against the dangers, and utilize the promise, inherent in the use of atomic energy" (1945).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In the 1960s and early 1970s, the association examined the issues of government-sponsored classified research, use of anthropologists by the military in Vietnam, secret research in Thailand, and the general problem of a code of ethics for anthropological research, particularly for the protection of the rights of those studied. Other issues addressed from the 1970s through the 1980s included illegal <a href="/wiki/Antiquities_trade" title="Antiquities trade">antiquities trade</a>, the insertion of religious beliefs into social science texts, the preservation of endangered nonhuman primates, and the religious significance of <a href="/wiki/Peyote" title="Peyote">peyote</a> to <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In 2004, in response to President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>'s call for a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_amendment" title="Constitutional amendment">constitutional amendment</a> banning <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex marriage</a>, the Association issued a statement on marriage and the family. It states:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The results of more than a century of anthropological research on households, kinship relationships, and families, across cultures and through time, provide no support whatsoever for the view that either civilization or viable social orders depend upon marriage as an exclusively heterosexual institution. Rather, anthropological research supports the conclusion that a vast array of family types, including families built upon same-sex partnerships, can contribute to stable and humane societies.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>The Association also has adopted resolutions against the <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">2003 invasion of Iraq</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> against the use of anthropological knowledge as an element for physical or psychological torture,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and against any covert or overt U.S. military action against <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A number of ideologically polarized debates within the discipline of anthropology have prompted the association to conduct investigations. These include the dispute between <a href="/wiki/Derek_Freeman" title="Derek Freeman">Derek Freeman</a> and defenders of <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Mead" title="Margaret Mead">Margaret Mead</a>, as well as the controversy over the book <i><a href="/wiki/Darkness_in_El_Dorado" title="Darkness in El Dorado">Darkness in El Dorado</a></i>. In the latter case, <a href="/wiki/Alice_Dreger" title="Alice Dreger">Alice Dreger</a>, an historian of medicine and science, and an outsider to the debate, concluded after a year of research that the American Anthropological Association was complicit and irresponsible in helping spread the falsehoods contained in the book, and not protecting "scholars from baseless and sensationalistic charges".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Race">Race</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Anthropological_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Race">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The AAA has issued a number of statements on the topic of <a href="/wiki/Race_(classification_of_humans)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (classification of humans)">race</a>, and since the 1950s has argued publicly that race is best understood as a cultural or bio-cultural rather than mostly biological construction.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1990s, in response to  what it felt was public confusion about the meaning of "<a href="/wiki/Race_(anthropology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (anthropology)">race</a>," particularly perceived public misconceptions about <a href="/wiki/Race_and_intelligence" title="Race and intelligence">race and intelligence</a>, the AAA Executive Board commissioned the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Anthropological_Association_Statement_on_Race&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="American Anthropological Association Statement on Race (page does not exist)">American Anthropological Association Statement on Race</a></i> as a constructed <a href="/wiki/Social_mechanism" class="mw-redirect" title="Social mechanism">social mechanism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In 2006,   the association developed and continues to  manage  a public education program titled "RACE: Are We So Different?" The program includes a traveling museum exhibit, an interactive website, and educational materials.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_rights">Human rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Anthropological_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Human rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Initially, AAA was highly skeptical of the concept of universal <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a>, with some anthropologists arguing that because of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_relativism" title="Cultural relativism">cultural relativism</a> there are no principles that can be universally valid for humans of all cultures. IN 1947 the AAA issued a statement on Human rights, noting that value judgments are culturally contextual and arguing that a declaration about universal human rights ought to take into consideration and encompass all the different human value systems.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This stance has gradually been abandoned by most anthropologists, many of whom today see universal human rights as an important way through which discrimination, oppression of cultural minorities can be reduced.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Immigration_policy">Immigration policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Anthropological_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Immigration policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Arizona">Arizona</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Anthropological_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Arizona">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On May 22, 2010, the AAA Executive Board issued a resolution that declared <a href="/wiki/Arizona%27s_SB1070" class="mw-redirect" title="Arizona&#39;s SB1070">Arizona's SB1070</a>, a law which empowers state law enforcement to assist with the enforcement of federal law, to be "<a href="/wiki/Unconstitutional" class="mw-redirect" title="Unconstitutional">unconstitutional</a>." The Board claimed it would boycott Arizona, but would not boycott "<a href="/wiki/Indian_Reservations" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian Reservations">Indian Reservations</a>" <a href="/wiki/List_of_Indian_reservations_in_Arizona" title="List of Indian reservations in Arizona">within the state</a>, until the law "is either repealed or struck down as constitutionally invalid." The Board did not state what it will do if the courts uphold SB1070 as constitutionally valid.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The Board stated that "The AAA has a long and rich history of supporting policies that prohibit discrimination based on ... national origin<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>..."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 19, 2016, the U.S. District Court in Arizona entered a permanent injunction barring enforcement of the remaining provisions. With the law's repeal, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.americananthro.org/StayInformed/NewsDetail.aspx?ItemNumber=20970">AAA’s ban on considering AAA conferences in Arizona was lifted</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Engaging_with_the_military">Engaging with the military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Anthropological_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Engaging with the military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vietnam_War">Vietnam War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Anthropological_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Vietnam War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In March 1967, during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, the Council of the AAA adopted a "Statement on Problems of Anthropological Research and Ethics" that stated:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>... Except in the event of a declaration of war by Congress, academic institutions should not undertake activities or accept contracts in anthropology that are not related to their normal functions of teaching, research, and public service. They should not lend themselves to clandestine activities. ... The international reputation of anthropology has been damaged by the activities of unqualified individuals who have falsely claimed to be anthropologists, or who have pretended to be engaged in anthropological research while in fact pursuing other ends. There is also good reason to believe that some anthropologists have used their professional standing and the names of their academic institutions as cloaks for the collection of <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_(information_gathering)" class="mw-redirect" title="Intelligence (information gathering)">intelligence</a> information and for intelligence operations. Academic institutions and individual members of the academic community, including students, should scrupulously avoid both involvement in clandestine intelligence activities and the use of the name of anthropology, or the title of anthropologist, as a cover for intelligence activities.<sup id="cite_ref-AAAStatement_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAAStatement-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_Terrain_System">Human Terrain System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Anthropological_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Human Terrain System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Through 2007 and 2008, debates surrounding anthropologists and the military resurfaced in response to the <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">Pentagon</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Human_Terrain_System" title="Human Terrain System">Human Terrain System</a> (HTS) project. Following a number of national news articles on the project, anthropologists began to debate the project and related ethical issues. Proponents of the program argued that anthropologists were providing much-needed cultural knowledge about local populations and helping to decrease violence in their areas of operation. Critics, however, argued that HTS anthropologists could not receive <a href="/wiki/Informed_consent" title="Informed consent">informed consent</a> from their research subjects in a war zone and that information provided by anthropologists might put populations in danger.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>To address these issues, the Association's Executive Board released a statement on 31 October 2007.  It cited "sufficiently troubling and urgent ethical issues" raised by the   project, including the difficulties for HTS anthropologists to receive informed consent without coercion from their research subjects and to uphold their ethical mandate to "do no harm" to those they study.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The AAA urged members to adhere to its code of ethics, which outlines principles and guidelines for ethical behavior. However, the association does not adjudicate cases involving charges of unethical behavior or bar members from participating in the HTS program.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition, the Association's Commission on the Engagement of Anthropology with US Security and Intelligence Communities  (CEAUSSIC) issued a final report released during the AAA's 2007 annual meeting, based on over a year of work. It neither endorsed nor condemned anthropological work with military, intelligence and security organizations, but instead outlined the opportunities and challenges of working in these sectors.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>  Opposition to military cooperation was evident during that meeting. Some critics of the HTS program have suggested that scholars who perform classified work with the military be expelled from the organization.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> During an event organized by the Network of Concerned Anthropologists, a graduate student who had recently been expelled from the HTS program spoke out about her experiences. She argued that the program was poorly run but was doing positive work in helping military officers with "<a href="/wiki/Nation-building" title="Nation-building">nation-building</a>" activities.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="AnthroSource">AnthroSource</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Anthropological_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: AnthroSource">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:Asrc.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Asrc.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f7/Asrc.jpg" decoding="async" width="126" height="107" data-file-width="126" data-file-height="107" /></a></div>
<p><i>AnthroSource</i> is the online repository of the journals of the American Anthropological Association. Launched in 2004, it contains current issues for fifteen of the Association's peer-reviewed publications, as well as an archive of the journals, newsletters, and bulletins published by the Association and its member sections. Members of the association receive access to AnthroSource as a benefit of membership, and institutions may receive access via paid subscription.
</p><p>Until August 2007, AnthroSource was a collaboration between the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California_Press" title="University of California Press">University of California Press</a> and the Association. It, along with all their journals, has since been removed from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California_Press" title="University of California Press">University of California Press</a> by the AAA Board and transferred to Wiley-Blackwell. Commencing 2008, AnthroSource was to be hosted and managed by Wiley-Blackwell as part of the five-year publishing contract awarded.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, the Association announced that it would experiment with making <i>Cultural Anthropology</i> an <a href="/wiki/Open-access_journal" class="mw-redirect" title="Open-access journal">open-access journal</a>; Brad Weiss, the society's president, said in a statement posted on the group's Web site,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>  that "Starting with the first issue of 2014, CA will provide worldwide, instant, free (to the user), and permanent access to all of our content (as well as 10 years of our back catalog)," and  that "Cultural Anthropology will be the first major, established, high-impact journal in anthropology to offer open access to all of its research" <sup id="cite_ref-CHE_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHE-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
