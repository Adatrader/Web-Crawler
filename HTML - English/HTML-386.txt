</tr><tr><td style="text-align:right;font-size:115%"><div class="plainlinks hlist navbar mini"><ul><li class="nv-view"><a href="/wiki/Template:English_language" title="Template:English language"><abbr title="View this template">v</abbr></a></li><li class="nv-talk"><a href="/wiki/Template_talk:English_language" title="Template talk:English language"><abbr title="Discuss this template">t</abbr></a></li><li class="nv-edit"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Template:English_language&amp;action=edit"><abbr title="Edit this template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> is a <a href="/wiki/West_Germanic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="West Germanic language">West Germanic language</a> that originated from <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Frisian" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Frisian">Anglo-Frisian</a> dialects brought to <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> in the mid 5th to 7th centuries AD by <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_settlement_of_Britain" title="Anglo-Saxon settlement of Britain">Anglo-Saxon settlers</a>. With the end of Roman rule in 410 AD, Latin ceased to be a major influence on the Celtic languages spoken by the majority of the population.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> People from what is now northwest <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, west <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> settled in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> from the mid-5th century and came to culturally dominate the bulk of southern Great Britain until the 7th century. The Anglo-Saxon language, now called <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a>, originated as a group of <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Frisian_languages" title="Anglo-Frisian languages">Anglo-Frisian</a> <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialects</a> which were spoken, at least by the settlers, in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and southern and eastern <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> in the early <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. It <a href="/wiki/Celtic_language-death_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic language-death in England">displaced</a> to some extent the Celtic languages that predominated previously. Old English also reflected the varied origins of the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_kingdoms" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon kingdoms">Anglo-Saxon kingdoms</a> established in different parts of Britain. The <a href="/wiki/Late_West_Saxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Late West Saxon">Late West Saxon</a> dialect eventually became dominant. A significant subsequent influence on the shaping of Old English came from contact with the <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">North Germanic languages</a> spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Viking_invasions_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking invasions of England">Scandinavian Vikings</a> who conquered and colonized parts of Britain during the 8th and 9th centuries, which led to much lexical borrowing and grammatical simplification. The <a href="/wiki/Anglian_dialects" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglian dialects">Anglian dialects</a> had a greater influence on Middle English.
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman conquest">Norman conquest</a> in 1066, Old English was replaced, for a time, by <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">Anglo-Norman</a> as the language of the upper classes. This is regarded as marking the end of the Old English or Anglo-Saxon era, as during this period the English language was heavily influenced by Anglo-Norman, developing into a phase known now as <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a>. The conquering Normans spoke a <a href="/wiki/Romantic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Romantic languages">Romance</a> <a href="/wiki/Langue_d%27o%C3%AFl" class="mw-redirect" title="Langue d&#39;oïl">langue d'oïl</a> called <a href="/wiki/Old_Norman" title="Old Norman">Old Norman</a>, which in Britain developed into Anglo-Norman. Many Norman and French loanwords entered the local language in this period, especially in vocabulary related to the church, the court system and the government. As Normans are descendants of Vikings who invaded France, Norman French was influenced by Old Norse, and many Norse loanwords in English came directly from French. Middle English was spoken to the late 15th century. The system of <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a> that was established during the Middle English period is largely still in use today. Later changes in pronunciation, however, combined with the adoption of various foreign spellings, mean that the <a href="/wiki/English_orthography" title="English orthography">spelling of modern English words</a> appears highly irregular.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_English" title="Early Modern English">Early Modern English</a> – the language used by <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> – is dated from around 1500. It incorporated many <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>-era loans from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>, as well as borrowings from other European languages, including <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>. Significant pronunciation changes in this period included the ongoing <a href="/wiki/Great_Vowel_Shift" title="Great Vowel Shift">Great Vowel Shift</a>, which affected the qualities of most <a href="/wiki/Long_vowel" class="mw-redirect" title="Long vowel">long vowels</a>. <a href="/wiki/Modern_English" title="Modern English">Modern English</a> proper, similar in most respects to that spoken today, was in place by the late 17th century. The English language came to be exported to other parts of the world through <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British colonisation</a>, and is now the dominant language in Britain and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and many smaller former colonies, as well as being widely spoken in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, parts of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, and elsewhere. Partially due to United States influence, English gradually took on the status of a global <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a> in the second half of the 20th century. This is especially true in Europe, where English has largely taken over the former roles of French and (much earlier) Latin as a common language used to conduct business and diplomacy, share scientific and technological information, and otherwise communicate across national boundaries. The efforts of English-speaking Christian missionaries has resulted in English becoming a second language for many other groups.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Global variation among different <a href="/wiki/English_dialects" class="mw-redirect" title="English dialects">English dialects</a> and <a href="/wiki/English_accents" class="mw-redirect" title="English accents">accents</a> remains significant today. <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a>, a form of English traditionally spoken in parts of Scotland and the north of Ireland, is sometimes treated as a separate language.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proto-English">Proto-English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Proto-English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Celtic_language_decline_in_England" title="Celtic language decline in England">Celtic language decline in England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saxon_Shore" title="Saxon Shore">Saxon Shore</a></div>
<p>English has its roots in the languages of the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic peoples</a> of northern Europe. During the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, most of the Germanic-inhabited area (<a href="/wiki/Germania" title="Germania">Germania</a>) remained independent from Rome, although some southwestern parts were within the empire. Some Germanics served in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_military" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman military">Roman military</a>, and troops from Germanic tribes such as the <a href="/wiki/Tungri" title="Tungri">Tungri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Batavi_(Germanic_tribe)" title="Batavi (Germanic tribe)">Batavi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Menapii" title="Menapii">Menapii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frisii" title="Frisii">Frisii</a> served in Britain (<a href="/wiki/Britannia" title="Britannia">Britannia</a>) under Roman command. Germanic settlement and power expanded during the <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">Migration Period</a>, which saw the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_the_Western_Roman_Empire" title="Fall of the Western Roman Empire">fall of the Western Roman Empire</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_settlement_of_Britain" title="Anglo-Saxon settlement of Britain">Germanic settlement of Britain</a> took place from the 5th to the 7th century, following the <a href="/wiki/End_of_Roman_rule_in_Britain" title="End of Roman rule in Britain">end of Roman rule</a> on the island. The <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i> relates that around the year 449 <a href="/wiki/Vortigern" title="Vortigern">Vortigern</a>, king of the <a href="/wiki/Brython" class="mw-redirect" title="Brython">Britons</a>, invited the "Angle kin" (Angles allegedly led by the Germanic brothers <a href="/wiki/Hengist_and_Horsa" title="Hengist and Horsa">Hengist and Horsa</a>) to help repel invading <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a>, in return for lands in the southeast of Britain. This led to waves of settlers who eventually established seven kingdoms, known as the <a href="/wiki/Heptarchy" title="Heptarchy">heptarchy</a>. (The <i>Chronicle</i> was not a contemporaneous work, however, and cannot be regarded as an accurate record of such early events.)<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">Bede</a>, who wrote his <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_ecclesiastica_gentis_Anglorum" class="mw-redirect" title="Historia ecclesiastica gentis Anglorum">Ecclesiastical History</a></i> in AD 731, writes of invasion by <a href="/wiki/Angles" title="Angles">Angles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jutes" title="Jutes">Jutes</a>, although the precise nature of the invasion and settlement and the contributions made by these particular groups are the subject of much dispute among historians.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The languages spoken by the Germanic peoples who initially settled in Britain were part of the <a href="/wiki/West_Germanic" class="mw-redirect" title="West Germanic">West Germanic</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Germanic language">Germanic language</a> family. They consisted of dialects from the <a href="/wiki/Ingvaeonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ingvaeonic">Ingvaeonic</a> grouping, spoken mainly around the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> coast, in regions that lie within modern <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, north-west <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>. Due to specific similarities between early English and <a href="/wiki/Old_Frisian" title="Old Frisian">Old Frisian</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Frisian" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Frisian">Anglo-Frisian</a> grouping is also identified.
</p><p>These dialects had most of the typical West Germanic features, including a significant amount of grammatical <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">inflection</a>. Vocabulary came largely from the core Germanic stock, although due to the Germanic peoples' extensive contacts with the Roman world, the settlers' languages already included a number of <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanwords</a> from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> For instance, the predecessor of Modern English <i>wine</i> had been borrowed into early Germanic from the Latin <i>vinum</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_English">Old English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Old English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Beowulf.firstpage.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Beowulf.firstpage.jpeg/220px-Beowulf.firstpage.jpeg" decoding="async" width="220" height="349" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Beowulf.firstpage.jpeg/330px-Beowulf.firstpage.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/08/Beowulf.firstpage.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="363" data-file-height="576" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Beowulf.firstpage.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The first page of the <i><a href="/wiki/Beowulf" title="Beowulf">Beowulf</a></i> manuscript</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a></div>
<p>The Germanic settlers in the British Isles initially spoke a number of different dialects, which would develop into a language that came to be called Anglo-Saxon, or now more commonly <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-OriginOfAngloSaxon_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OriginOfAngloSaxon-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It displaced the so-called indigenous <a href="/wiki/Old_Brittonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Brittonic">Brittonic</a> Celtic (and the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_occupation_of_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman occupation of Britain">former Roman rulers</a>) in parts of the areas of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> that later formed the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a>, while Celtic languages remained in most of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, and many compound Celtic-Germanic place names survive, hinting at early language mixing.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Old English continued to exhibit local variation, the remnants of which continue to be found in dialects of Modern English.<sup id="cite_ref-OriginOfAngloSaxon_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OriginOfAngloSaxon-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The four main dialects were <a href="/wiki/Mercian_dialect" title="Mercian dialect">Mercian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northumbrian_dialect" title="Northumbrian dialect">Northumbrian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kentish_dialect_(Old_English)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kentish dialect (Old English)">Kentish</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Saxon_dialect" title="West Saxon dialect">West Saxon</a>; the last of these formed the basis for the literary standard of the later Old English period, although the dominant forms of Middle and Modern English would develop mainly from Mercian.
</p><p>Old English was first written using a <a href="/wiki/Rune" class="mw-redirect" title="Rune">runic</a> script called the <a href="/wiki/Futhorc" class="mw-redirect" title="Futhorc">futhorc</a>, but this was replaced by a <a href="/wiki/Old_English_Latin_alphabet" title="Old English Latin alphabet">version of the Latin alphabet</a> introduced by Irish missionaries in the 8th century. Most literary output was in either the Early West Saxon of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">Alfred the Great</a>'s time, or the Late West Saxon (regarded as the "classical" form of Old English) of the Winchester school inspired by Bishop <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelwold_of_Winchester" title="Æthelwold of Winchester">Æthelwold of Winchester</a> and followed by such writers as the prolific <a href="/wiki/%C3%86lfric_of_Eynsham" title="Ælfric of Eynsham">Ælfric of Eynsham</a> ("the Grammarian"). The most famous surviving work from the Old English period is the <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic</a> poem <i><a href="/wiki/Beowulf" title="Beowulf">Beowulf</a></i>, composed by an unknown poet.
</p><p>The introduction of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> from around the year 600 encouraged the addition of over 400 <a href="/wiki/Latin_influence_in_English" title="Latin influence in English">Latin loan words</a> into Old English, such as the predecessors of the modern <i>priest</i>, <i>paper</i>, and <i>school</i>, and a smaller number of <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> loan words.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The speech of eastern and northern parts of England was also subject to strong <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> influence due to <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">Scandinavian rule</a> and settlement beginning in the 9th century (see below).
</p><p>Most native English speakers today find Old English unintelligible, even though about half of the most commonly used words in Modern English have Old English roots.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Old_English_grammar" title="Old English grammar">grammar of Old English</a> was much more <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">inflected</a> than modern English, combined with freer <a href="/wiki/Word_order" title="Word order">word order</a>, and was grammatically quite similar in some respects to modern <a href="/wiki/German_grammar" title="German grammar">German</a>. The language had demonstrative pronouns (equivalent to <i>this</i> and <i>that</i>) but did not have definite article <i>the</i>. The Old English period is considered to have evolved into the <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> period some time after the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman conquest">Norman conquest</a> of 1066, when the language came to be influenced significantly by the new ruling class's language, <a href="/wiki/Old_Norman" title="Old Norman">Old Norman</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scandinavian_influence">Scandinavian influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scandinavian influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_norse,_ca_900.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Old_norse%2C_ca_900.PNG/250px-Old_norse%2C_ca_900.PNG" decoding="async" width="250" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Old_norse%2C_ca_900.PNG/375px-Old_norse%2C_ca_900.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Old_norse%2C_ca_900.PNG/500px-Old_norse%2C_ca_900.PNG 2x" data-file-width="1235" data-file-height="909" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_norse,_ca_900.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The approximate extent of Old Norse and related languages in the early 10th century: <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #ff0000; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;<b>Old West Norse dialect</b></div> <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #ff9933; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;<b>Old East Norse dialect</b></div> <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #ff00ff; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;<b><a href="/wiki/Old_Gutnish" title="Old Gutnish">Old Gutnish</a></b></div> <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #ffff00; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;<b><a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a></b></div> <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #0000ff; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;<b><a href="/wiki/Crimean_Gothic" title="Crimean Gothic">Crimean Gothic</a></b></div> <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #00ff00; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;Other <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic languages</a> with which Old Norse still retained some mutual intelligibility</div></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a> from modern-day <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> began to <a href="/wiki/Viking_invasions_of_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking invasions of Britain">raid parts of Britain</a> from the late 8th century onward. In 865, however, a major invasion was launched by what the Anglo-Saxons called the <a href="/wiki/Great_Heathen_Army" title="Great Heathen Army">Great Heathen Army</a>, which eventually brought large parts of northern and eastern England (the <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">Danelaw</a>) under Scandinavian control. Most of these areas were retaken by the English under <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Elder" title="Edward the Elder">Edward the Elder</a> in the early 10th century, although <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_York" title="Scandinavian York">York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Northumbria">Northumbria</a> were not permanently regained until the death of <a href="/wiki/Eric_Bloodaxe" title="Eric Bloodaxe">Eric Bloodaxe</a> in 954. Scandinavian raids resumed in the late 10th century during the reign of <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelred_the_Unready" title="Æthelred the Unready">Æthelred the Unready</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sweyn_Forkbeard" title="Sweyn Forkbeard">Sweyn Forkbeard</a> eventually succeeded in briefly being declared king of England in 1013, followed by the longer reign of his son <a href="/wiki/Cnut_the_Great" title="Cnut the Great">Cnut</a> from 1016 to 1035, and Cnut's sons <a href="/wiki/Harold_Harefoot" title="Harold Harefoot">Harold Harefoot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harthacnut" title="Harthacnut">Harthacnut</a> until 1042.
</p><p>The Scandinavians, or <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norsemen</a>, spoke dialects of a <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">North Germanic</a> language known as <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a>. The Anglo-Saxons and the Scandinavians thus spoke related languages from different branches (West and North) of the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a> family; many of their lexical roots were the same or similar, although their grammatical systems were more divergent. Probably significant numbers of Norse speakers settled in the Danelaw during the period of Scandinavian control. Many <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">place-names</a> in those areas are of Scandinavian provenance (those ending in <i>-by</i>, for example); it is believed that the settlers often established new communities in places that had not previously been developed by the Anglo-Saxons. The extensive <a href="/wiki/Language_contact" title="Language contact">contact</a> between Old English and Old Norse speakers, including the possibility of intermarriage that resulted from the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Wedmore" title="Treaty of Wedmore">acceptance of Christianity by the Danes</a> in 878,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> undoubtedly influenced the varieties of those languages spoken in the areas of contact. <a href="/wiki/Middle_English_creole_hypothesis" title="Middle English creole hypothesis">Some scholars even believe</a> that Old English and Old Norse underwent a kind of fusion and that the resulting English language might be described as a <a href="/wiki/Mixed_language" title="Mixed language">mixed language</a> or <a href="/wiki/Creole_(language)" class="mw-redirect" title="Creole (language)">creole</a>. During the rule of Cnut and other Danish kings in the first half of the 11th century, a kind of <a href="/wiki/Diglossia" title="Diglossia">diglossia</a> may have come about, with the West Saxon literary language existing alongside the Norse-influenced Midland dialect of English, which could have served as a <a href="/wiki/Koin%C3%A9_language" title="Koiné language">koine</a> or spoken lingua franca. When Danish rule ended, and particularly after the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a>, the status of the minority Norse language presumably declined relative to that of English, and its remaining speakers assimilated to English in a process involving <a href="/wiki/Language_shift" title="Language shift">language shift</a> and <a href="/wiki/Language_death" title="Language death">language death</a>. The widespread <a href="/wiki/Bilingualism" class="mw-redirect" title="Bilingualism">bilingualism</a> that must have existed during the process possibly contributed to the rate of borrowings from Norse into English.<sup id="cite_ref-CHEL320_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHEL320-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Only about 100 or 150 Norse words, mainly connected with government and administration, are found in Old English writing. The borrowing of words of this type was stimulated by Scandinavian rule in the Danelaw and during the later reign of Cnut. However, most surviving Old English texts are based on the <a href="/wiki/West_Saxon_dialect" title="West Saxon dialect">West Saxon</a> standard that developed outside the Danelaw; it is not clear to what extent Norse influenced the forms of the language spoken in eastern and northern England at that time. Later texts from the Middle English era, now based on an eastern Midland rather than a Wessex standard, reflect the significant impact that Norse had on the language. In all, English borrowed about <a href="/wiki/List_of_English_words_of_Old_Norse_origin" title="List of English words of Old Norse origin">2000 words from Old Norse</a>, several hundred surviving in <a href="/wiki/Modern_English" title="Modern English">Modern English</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CHEL320_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHEL320-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Norse borrowings include many very common words, such as <i>anger</i>, <i>bag</i>, <i>both</i>, <i>hit</i>, <i>law</i>, <i>leg</i>, <i>same</i>, <i>skill</i>, <i>sky</i>, <i>take</i>, <i>window</i>, and even the pronoun <i><a href="/wiki/They" title="They">they</a></i>. Norse influence is also believed to have reinforced the adoption of the plural <a href="/wiki/Copula_(linguistics)" title="Copula (linguistics)">copular</a> verb form <i><a href="/wiki/To_be" class="mw-redirect" title="To be">are</a></i> rather than alternative Old English forms like <i>sind</i>. It is also considered to have stimulated and accelerated the <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">morphological</a> simplification found in Middle English, such as the loss of <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">grammatical gender</a> and explicitly marked <a href="/wiki/Case_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Case (linguistics)">case</a> (except in pronouns).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> That is possibly confirmed by observations that simplification of the case endings occurred earliest in the north and latest in the southwest. The spread of <a href="/wiki/Phrasal_verb" title="Phrasal verb">phrasal verbs</a> in English is another grammatical development to which Norse may have contributed (although here a possible <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a> influence is also noted).<sup id="cite_ref-CHEL320_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHEL320-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_English">Middle English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Middle English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Influence_of_French_on_English" title="Influence of French on English">Influence of French on English</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> is the form of English spoken roughly from the time of the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a> in 1066 until the end of the 15th century.
</p><p>For centuries after the Conquest, the Norman kings and high-ranking nobles in England and to some extent elsewhere in the British Isles spoke <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">Anglo-Norman</a>, a variety of <a href="/wiki/Old_Norman" title="Old Norman">Old Norman</a>, originating from a northern <a href="/wiki/Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langues d&#39;oïl">langue d'oïl</a> dialect. Merchants and lower-ranked nobles were often bilingual in Anglo-Norman and English, whilst English continued to be the language of the common people. Middle English was influenced by both Anglo-Norman, and later Anglo-French (see <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language#Characteristics" title="Anglo-Norman language">characteristics of the Anglo-Norman language</a>).
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wife-of-Bath-ms.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Wife-of-Bath-ms.jpg/220px-Wife-of-Bath-ms.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="311" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Wife-of-Bath-ms.jpg/330px-Wife-of-Bath-ms.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Wife-of-Bath-ms.jpg/440px-Wife-of-Bath-ms.jpg 2x" data-file-width="707" data-file-height="1000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wife-of-Bath-ms.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Opening prologue of "<a href="/wiki/The_Wife_of_Bath%27s_Tale" title="The Wife of Bath&#39;s Tale">The Wife of Bath's Tale</a>" from the <i>Canterbury Tales</i></div></div></div>
<p>Until the 14th century, Anglo-Norman and then French were the language of the courts and government. Even after the decline of Norman, standard French retained the status of a formal or <a href="/wiki/Prestige_(sociolinguistics)" title="Prestige (sociolinguistics)">prestige language</a>, and about 10,000 French (and Norman) loan words entered Middle English, particularly terms associated with government, church, law, the military, fashion, and food<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> (see <a href="/wiki/English_language#Word_origins" title="English language">English language word origins</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_English_words_of_French_origin" title="List of English words of French origin">List of English words of French origin</a>). The strong influence of <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> on English (described in the previous section) also becomes apparent during this period. The impact of the native <a href="/wiki/British_Celtic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="British Celtic languages">British Celtic languages</a> that English continued to displace is generally held to be much smaller, although some attribute such analytic verb forms as the <a href="/wiki/Continuous_aspect" class="mw-redirect" title="Continuous aspect">continuous aspect</a> ("to be doing" or "to have been doing") to Celtic influence.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Some scholars have also put forward <a href="/wiki/Middle_English_creole_hypothesis" title="Middle English creole hypothesis">hypotheses that Middle English was a kind of creole</a> language resulting from contact between Old English and either Old Norse or Anglo-Norman.
</p><p>English literature began to reappear after 1200, when a changing political climate and the decline in <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">Anglo-Norman</a> made it more respectable. The <a href="/wiki/Provisions_of_Oxford" title="Provisions of Oxford">Provisions of Oxford</a>, released in 1258, was the first English government document to be published in the English language after the Norman Conquest. In 1362, <a href="/wiki/Edward_III_of_England" title="Edward III of England">Edward III</a> became the first king to address Parliament in English. The <a href="/wiki/Pleading_in_English_Act_1362" title="Pleading in English Act 1362">Pleading in English Act 1362</a> made English the only language in which court proceedings could be held, though the official record remained in Latin.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> By the end of the century, even the royal court had switched to English. Anglo-Norman remained in use in limited circles somewhat longer, but it had ceased to be a living language. Official documents began to be produced regularly in English during the 15th century. <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a>, who lived in the late 14th century, is the most famous writer from the Middle English period, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Canterbury_Tales" title="The Canterbury Tales">The Canterbury Tales</a></i> is his best-known work.
</p><p>The English language changed enormously during the Middle English period, both in vocabulary and pronunciation, and in grammar. While Old English is a heavily inflected language (<a href="/wiki/Synthetic_language" title="Synthetic language">synthetic</a>), the use of <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">grammatical endings</a> diminished in Middle English (<a href="/wiki/Analytic_language" title="Analytic language">analytic</a>). Grammar distinctions were lost as many noun and adjective endings were <a href="/wiki/Morphological_leveling" title="Morphological leveling">levelled</a> to <i>-e</i>. The older <a href="/wiki/English_plural" class="mw-redirect" title="English plural">plural noun</a> marker <i>-en</i> (retained in a few cases such as <i>children</i> and <i>oxen</i>) largely gave way to <i>-s</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">grammatical gender</a> was discarded. Definite article <i>þe</i> appears around 1200, later spelled as <i>the</i>, first appearing in East and North England as a substitute for Old English <i>se</i> and <i>seo</i>, nominative forms of "that."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/English_orthography" title="English orthography">English spelling</a> was also influenced by Norman in this period, with the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/θ/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ð/</span> sounds being spelled <i>th</i> rather than with the Old English letters <a href="/wiki/Thorn_(letter)" title="Thorn (letter)">þ (thorn)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eth" title="Eth">ð (eth)</a>, which did not exist in Norman. These letters remain in the modern <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_orthography" title="Icelandic orthography">Icelandic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Faroese_orthography" title="Faroese orthography">Faroese alphabets</a>, having been borrowed from Old English via <a href="/wiki/Old_West_Norse" class="mw-redirect" title="Old West Norse">Old West Norse</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Modern_English">Early Modern English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early Modern English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_English" title="Early Modern English">Early Modern English</a></div>
<p>English underwent extensive sound changes during the 15th century, while its spelling conventions remained largely constant. <a href="/wiki/Modern_English" title="Modern English">Modern English</a> is often dated from the <a href="/wiki/Great_Vowel_Shift" title="Great Vowel Shift">Great Vowel Shift</a>, which took place mainly during the 15th century. The language was further transformed by the spread of a standardized London-based dialect in government and administration and by the standardizing effect of printing, which also tended to regularize <a href="/wiki/Capitalization_in_English" title="Capitalization in English">capitalization</a>. As a result, the language acquired self-conscious terms such as "accent" and "dialect".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> As most early presses come from continental Europe, a few native English letters such as þ and ð die out; for some time <i>þe</i> is written as <i>ye.</i> By the time of <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> (mid 16th - early 17th century),<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> the language had become clearly recognizable as Modern English. In 1604, the first English dictionary was published, the <i><a href="/wiki/Table_Alphabeticall" title="Table Alphabeticall">Table Alphabeticall</a></i>.
</p><p>Increased literacy and travel facilitated the adoption of many foreign words, especially borrowings from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> from the time of the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>. In the 17th century, Latin words were often used with their original inflections, but these eventually disappeared. As there are many words from different languages and English spelling is variable, the risk of <a href="/wiki/Mispronunciation" title="Mispronunciation">mispronunciation</a> is high, but remnants of the older forms remain in a few regional dialects, most notably in the <a href="/wiki/West_Country_dialects" class="mw-redirect" title="West Country dialects">West Country</a>. During the period, loan words were borrowed from Italian, German, and Yiddish. British acceptance of and resistance to <a href="/wiki/Americanization" title="Americanization">Americanisms</a> began during this period.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_English">Modern English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Modern English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JohnsonDictionary.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/JohnsonDictionary.png/220px-JohnsonDictionary.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="352" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/JohnsonDictionary.png/330px-JohnsonDictionary.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/JohnsonDictionary.png/440px-JohnsonDictionary.png 2x" data-file-width="714" data-file-height="1141" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JohnsonDictionary.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Title page from the second edition of the <i>Dictionary</i></div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Modern_English" title="Modern English">Modern English</a></div>
<p>The first authoritative and full-featured English dictionary, the <i><a href="/wiki/Dictionary_of_the_English_Language" class="mw-redirect" title="Dictionary of the English Language">Dictionary of the English Language</a></i>, was published by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Johnson" title="Samuel Johnson">Samuel Johnson</a> in 1755. To a high degree, the dictionary standardized both English spelling and word usage. Meanwhile, grammar texts by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Lowth" title="Robert Lowth">Lowth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lindley_Murray" title="Lindley Murray">Murray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestly" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph Priestly">Priestly</a>, and others attempted to prescribe standard usage even further.
</p><p>Early Modern English and Late Modern English, also called Present-Day English (PDE), differ essentially in vocabulary. Late Modern English has many more words, arising from the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> and technologies that created a need for new words, as well as international development of the language. The <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> at its height covered one quarter of the Earth's land surface, and the English language adopted foreign words from many countries. British English and North American English, the two major varieties of the language, are together spoken by 400 million people. The total number of English speakers worldwide may exceed one billion.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> will almost certainly continue to evolve over time. With the development of computer and online environments (such as chat rooms, social media expressions, and apps), and the adoption of English as a worldwide <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a> across cultures, customs, and traditions, it should not be surprising to see further shortening of words, phrases, and/or sentences.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Over the last 1,200 years or so, English has undergone extensive changes in its vowel system but many fewer changes to its consonants.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> period, a number of <a href="/wiki/Umlaut_(linguistics)" title="Umlaut (linguistics)">umlaut</a> processes affected vowels in complex ways, and unstressed vowels were gradually eroded, eventually leading to a loss of <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">grammatical case</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">grammatical gender</a> in the Early Middle English period. The most important umlaut process was *<a href="/wiki/Germanic_umlaut#I-mutation_in_Old_English" title="Germanic umlaut">i-mutation</a> (c. 500 CE), which led to pervasive alternations of all sorts, many of which survive in the modern language: e.g. in noun paradigms (<i>foot</i> vs. <i>feet</i>, <i>mouse</i> vs. <i>mice</i>, <i>brother</i> vs. <i>brethren</i>); in verb paradigms (<i>sold</i> vs. <i>sell</i>); nominal derivatives from adjectives ("strong" vs. "strength", <i>broad</i> vs. <i>breadth</i>, <i>foul</i> vs. <i>filth</i>) and from other nouns (<i>fox</i> vs. "vixen"); verbal derivatives ("food" vs. "to feed"); and comparative adjectives ("old" vs. "elder"). Consonants were more stable, although <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">velar consonants</a> were significantly modified by <a href="/wiki/Old_English_phonology#Distribution_of_velars_and_palatals" title="Old English phonology">palatalization</a>, which produced alternations such as <i>speak</i> vs. <i>speech</i>, <i>drink</i> vs. <i>drench</i>, <i>wake</i> vs. <i>watch</i>, <i>bake</i> vs. <i>batch</i>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> period saw further vowel changes. Most significant was the <a href="/wiki/Great_Vowel_Shift" title="Great Vowel Shift">Great Vowel Shift</a> (c. 1500 CE), which transformed the pronunciation of all long vowels. This occurred after the spelling system was fixed, and accounts for the drastic differences in pronunciation between "short" <i>mat, met, bit, cot</i> vs. "long" <i>mate, mete/meet, bite, coat</i>. Other changes that left echoes in the modern language were <a href="/wiki/Middle_English_phonology#Homorganic_lengthening" title="Middle English phonology">homorganic lengthening</a> before <i>ld</i>, <i>mb</i>, <i>nd</i>, which accounts for the long vowels in <i>child</i>, <i>mind</i>, <i>climb</i>, etc.; <a href="/wiki/Middle_English_phonology#Pre-cluster_shortening" title="Middle English phonology">pre-cluster shortening</a>, which resulted in the vowel alternations in <i>child</i> vs. <i>children</i>, <i>keep</i> vs. <i>kept</i>, <i>meet</i> vs. <i>met</i>; and <a href="/wiki/Trisyllabic_laxing" title="Trisyllabic laxing">trisyllabic laxing</a>, which is responsible for alternations such as <i>grateful</i> vs. <i>gratitude</i>, <i>divine</i> vs. <i>divinity</i>, <i>sole</i> vs. <i>solitary</i>.
</p><p>Among the more significant recent changes to the language have been the development of <a href="/wiki/Rhotic_and_non-rhotic_accents" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhotic and non-rhotic accents">rhotic and non-rhotic accents</a> (i.e. "r-dropping"); the <a href="/wiki/Trap-bath_split" class="mw-redirect" title="Trap-bath split">trap-bath split</a> in many dialects of <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Flapping" title="Flapping">flapping</a> of <i>t</i> and <i>d</i> between vowels in <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australian_English" title="Australian English">Australian English</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammatical_changes">Grammatical changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Grammatical changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> once had an extensive <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">declension</a> system similar to <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, modern <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> and <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a>. <a href="/wiki/Old_English_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old English language">Old English</a> distinguished among the <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">nominative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">accusative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">dative</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">genitive</a> cases, and for strongly declined adjectives and some pronouns also a separate <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_case" title="Instrumental case">instrumental case</a> (which otherwise and later completely coincided with the dative). In addition, the <a href="/wiki/Dual_(grammatical_number)" title="Dual (grammatical number)">dual number</a> was distinguished from the singular and plural.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
Declension was greatly simplified during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> period, when the <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">accusative</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">dative</a> cases of the pronouns merged into a single <a href="/wiki/Oblique_case" title="Oblique case">oblique case</a> that also replaced the genitive case after prepositions. Nouns in Modern English no longer decline for case, except for the <a href="/wiki/Genitive" class="mw-redirect" title="Genitive">genitive</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_of_English_pronouns">Evolution of English pronouns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Evolution of English pronouns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pronouns such as <i>whom</i> and <i>him</i> (contrasted with <i>who</i> and <i>he</i>), are a conflation of the old accusative and dative cases, as well as of the genitive case after prepositions (while <i>her</i> also includes the genitive case). This conflated form is called the <i><a href="/wiki/Oblique_case" title="Oblique case">oblique case</a></i> or the <i>object (objective) case</i>, because it is used for objects of verbs (direct, indirect, or oblique) as well as for objects of prepositions. (See <a href="/wiki/Object_pronoun" title="Object pronoun">object pronoun</a>.) The information formerly conveyed by distinct case forms is now mostly provided by <a href="/wiki/Preposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Preposition">prepositions</a> and word order. In Old English as well as modern <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> and <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a> as further examples, these cases had distinct forms.
</p><p>Although some grammarians continue to use the traditional terms "accusative" and "dative", these are functions rather than morphological cases in Modern English. That is, the form <i>whom</i> may play accusative or dative roles (as well as instrumental or prepositional roles), but it is a single <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">morphological</a> form, contrasting with nominative <i>who</i> and genitive <i>whose</i>. Many grammarians use the labels "subjective", "objective", and "possessive" for nominative, oblique, and genitive pronouns.
</p><p>Modern English nouns exhibit only one inflection of the reference form: the <a href="/wiki/Possessive_case" class="mw-redirect" title="Possessive case">possessive case</a>, which some linguists argue is not a case at all, but a <a href="/wiki/Clitic" title="Clitic">clitic</a> (see the entry for <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">genitive case</a> for more information).
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voyages_of_Ohthere_and_Wulfstan">Voyages of Ohthere and Wulfstan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Voyages of Ohthere and Wulfstan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This is the beginning of <i>The Voyages of Ohthere and Wulfstan</i>, a prose text in Old English dated to the late 9th century. The full text can be found at <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bright%27s_Anglo-Saxon_Reader/The_Voyages_of_Ohthere_and_Wulfstan" class="extiw" title="s:Bright&#39;s Anglo-Saxon Reader/The Voyages of Ohthere and Wulfstan">Wikisource</a>.
</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>Ōhthere sǣde his hlāforde, Ælfrēde cyninge, ðæt hē ealra Norðmonna norþmest būde. Hē cwæð þæt hē būde on þǣm lande norþweardum wiþ þā Westsǣ. Hē sǣde þēah þæt þæt land sīe swīþe lang norþ þonan; ac hit is eal wēste, būton on fēawum stōwum styccemǣlum wīciað Finnas, on huntoðe on wintra, ond on sumera on fiscaþe be þǣre sǣ. Hē sǣde þæt hē æt sumum cirre wolde fandian hū longe þæt land norþryhte lǣge, oþþe hwæðer ǣnig mon be norðan þǣm wēstenne būde. Þā fōr hē norþryhte be þǣm lande: lēt him ealne weg þæt wēste land on ðæt stēorbord, ond þā wīdsǣ on ðæt bæcbord þrīe dagas. Þā wæs hē swā feor norþ swā þā hwælhuntan firrest faraþ. Þā fōr hē þā giet norþryhte swā feor swā hē meahte on þǣm ōþrum þrīm dagum gesiglau. Þā bēag þæt land, þǣr ēastryhte, oþþe sēo sǣ in on ðæt lond, hē nysse hwæðer, būton hē wisse ðæt hē ðǣr bād westanwindes ond hwōn norþan, ond siglde ðā ēast be lande swā swā hē meahte on fēower dagum gesiglan. Þā sceolde hē ðǣr bīdan ryhtnorþanwindes, for ðǣm þæt land bēag þǣr sūþryhte, oþþe sēo sǣ in on ðæt land, hē nysse hwæþer. Þā siglde hē þonan sūðryhte be lande swā swā hē meahte on fīf dagum gesiglan. Ðā læg þǣr ān micel ēa ūp on þæt land. Ðā cirdon hīe ūp in on ðā ēa for þǣm hīe ne dorston forþ bī þǣre ēa siglan for unfriþe; for þǣm ðæt land wæs eall gebūn on ōþre healfe þǣre ēas. Ne mētte hē ǣr nān gebūn land, siþþan hē from his āgnum hām fōr; ac him wæs ealne weg wēste land on þæt stēorbord, būtan fiscerum ond fugelerum ond huntum, ond þæt wǣron eall Finnas; ond him wæs āwīdsǣ on þæt bæcbord. Þā Boermas heafdon sīþe wel gebūd hira land: ac hīe ne dorston þǣr on cuman. Ac þāra Terfinna land wæs eal wēste, būton ðǣr huntan gewīcodon, oþþe fisceras, oþþe fugeleras.</i>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>A translation:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ohthere_from_H%C3%A5logaland" class="mw-redirect" title="Ohthere from Hålogaland">Ohthere</a> said to his lord, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">King Alfred</a>, that he of all Norsemen lived north-most. He quoth that he lived in the land northward along the North Sea. He said though that the land was very long from there, but it is all wasteland, except that in a few places here and there Finns [i.e. <a href="/wiki/Sami_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Sami people">Sami</a>] encamp, hunting in winter and in summer fishing by the sea. He said that at some time he wanted to find out how long the land lay northward or whether any man lived north of the wasteland. Then he traveled north by the land. All the way he kept the waste land on his starboard and the wide sea on his port three days. Then he was as far north as whale hunters furthest travel. Then he traveled still north as far as he might sail in another three days. Then the land bowed east (or the sea into the land — he did not know which). But he knew that he waited there for west winds (and somewhat north), and sailed east by the land so as he might sail in four days. Then he had to wait for due-north winds, because the land bowed south (or the sea into the land — he did not know which). Then he sailed from there south by the land so as he might sail in five days. Then a large river lay there up into the land. Then they turned up into the river, because they dared not sail forth past the river for hostility, because the land was all settled on the other side of the river. He had not encountered earlier any settled land since he travelled from his own home, but all the way waste land was on his starboard (except fishers, fowlers and hunters, who were all Finns). And the wide sea was always on his port. The <a href="/wiki/Bjarmaland" title="Bjarmaland">Bjarmians</a> have cultivated their land very well, but they did not dare go in there. But the Terfinn’s land was all waste except where hunters encamped, or fishers or fowlers.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ayenbite_of_Inwyt">Ayenbite of Inwyt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Ayenbite of Inwyt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

</p>

<div class="poem">
<p>Nou <span title="I" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ich</span> wille þet ye <span title="know" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ywite</span> hou <span title="it" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">hit</span> is <span title="gone" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ywent</span><br />

Þis boc is <span title="made" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ymad</span> vor <span title="lay" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">lewede</span> men<br />
Vor vader and vor moder and vor oþer <span title="kin" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ken</span><br />
<span title="them" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ham</span> vor to <span title="save, preserve" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">berȝe</span> vram alle manyere <span title="sin" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">zen</span><br />
þet ine <span title="their" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">hare</span> <span title="inwit, conscience" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">inwytte</span> ne <span title="blive, remain" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">bleve</span> no voul <span title="wen, cyst, blemish" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">wen</span>.<br />
'<span title="Who" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">Huo</span> ase god' in his name <span title="said" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">yzed</span>,<br />

Amen.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canterbury_Tales">Canterbury Tales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Canterbury Tales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The beginning of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Canterbury_Tales" title="The Canterbury Tales">The Canterbury Tales</a></i>, a collection of stories in poetry and prose written in the London dialect of Middle English by <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a> at the end of the 14th century:<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote>
<div class="poem">
<p>Whan that Aprill with his shoures <span title="sweet" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">soote</span><br />
The droghte of March hath perced to the roote,<br />
And bathed every veyne in <span title="such liquid" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">swich licour</span><br />
Of which vertu engendred is the <span title="flower" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">flour</span>;<br />
Whan <a href="/wiki/Zephyrus" class="mw-redirect" title="Zephyrus">Zephirus</a> <span title="also (Dutch ook; German auch)" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">eek</span> with his sweete breeth<br />
Inspired hath in every <span title="wood (German &#39;&#39;Holz&#39;&#39;)" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">holt</span> and heeth<br />
The tendre croppes, and the yonge sonne<br />
Hath in the <span title="Aries, the first sign of the Zodiac" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">Ram</span> his half cours <span title="run (German gerannt)" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">yronne</span>,<br />
And smale <span title="fowls (birds)" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">foweles</span> maken melodye,<br />
That slepen al the nyght with open <span title="eye" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">yë</span><br />
(So <span title="nature pricks them" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">priketh hem nature</span> in <span title="their hearts" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">hir corages</span>),<br />
Thanne longen folk to goon on pilgrimages,<br />
And <a href="/wiki/Palmer_(pilgrim)" title="Palmer (pilgrim)">palmeres</a> for to seken straunge <span title="strands (shores)" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">strondes</span>,<br />
To <span title="far" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ferne</span> <span title="hallows (shrines)" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">halwes</span>, <span title="couth (known)" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">kowthe</span> in sondry londes;<br />
And specially from every shires ende<br />
Of Engelond to Caunterbury they <span title="wend (proceed)" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">wende</span>,<br />
The <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Becket" title="Thomas Becket">hooly blisful martir</a> for to seke,<br />
That <span title="has helped them" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">hem hath holpen</span> whan that they were <span title="sick" class="rt-commentedText" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">seeke</span>.<br />
</p>
</div></blockquote>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paradise_Lost">Paradise Lost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Paradise Lost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The beginning of <i><a href="/wiki/Paradise_Lost" title="Paradise Lost">Paradise Lost</a></i>, an <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic poem</a> in unrhymed <a href="/wiki/Iambic_pentameter" title="Iambic pentameter">iambic pentameter</a> written in Early Modern English by <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">John Milton</a> and first published in 1667:
</p>
<blockquote>
<div class="poem">
<p>Of Mans First Disobedience, and the Fruit<br />
Of that Forbidden Tree, whose mortal tast<br />
Brought Death into the World, and all our woe,<br />
With loss of Eden, till one greater Man<br />
Restore us, and regain the blissful Seat,<br />
Sing Heav'nly Muse, that on the secret top<br />
Of Oreb, or of Sinai, didst inspire<br />
That Shepherd, who first taught the chosen Seed,<br />
In the Beginning how the Heav'ns and Earth<br />
Rose out of Chaos: Or if Sion Hill<br />
Delight thee more, and Siloa's Brook that flow'd<br />
Fast by the Oracle of God; I thence<br />
Invoke thy aid to my adventrous Song,<br />
That with no middle flight intends to soar<br />
Above th' Aonian Mount, while it pursues<br />
Things unattempted yet in Prose or Rhime.
</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oliver_Twist">Oliver Twist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Oliver Twist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A selection from the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Oliver_Twist" title="Oliver Twist">Oliver Twist</a></i>, written by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a> in Modern English and published in 1838:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The evening arrived: the boys took their places; the master in his cook's uniform stationed himself at the copper; his pauper assistants ranged themselves behind him; the gruel was served out, and a long grace was said over the short commons. The gruel disappeared, the boys whispered each other and winked at Oliver, while his next neighbours nudged him. Child as he was, he was desperate with hunger and reckless with misery. He rose from the table, and advancing, basin and spoon in hand, to the master, said, somewhat alarmed at his own temerity—
</p><p>"Please, sir, I want some more."
</p><p>The master was a fat, healthy man, but he turned very pale. He gazed in stupefied astonishment on the small rebel for some seconds, and then clung for support to the copper. The assistants were paralysed with wonder, and the boys with fear.
</p><p>"What!" said the master at length, in a faint voice.
</p><p>"Please, sir," replied Oliver, "I want some more."
</p><p>The master aimed a blow at Oliver's head with the ladle, pinioned him in his arms, and shrieked aloud for the <a href="/wiki/Beadle" title="Beadle">beadle</a>.
</p>
</blockquote>
