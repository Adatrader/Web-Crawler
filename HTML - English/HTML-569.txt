<p><i><b>Harper &amp; Row v. Nation Enterprises</b></i>, 471 U.S. 539 (1985), was a <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">United States Supreme Court</a> decision in which public interest in learning about a historical figure’s impressions of a historic event was held not to be sufficient to show fair use of material otherwise protected by copyright.<sup id="cite_ref-SCOTUS_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCOTUS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Defendant, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nation" title="The Nation">The Nation</a></i>, had summarized and quoted substantially from <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Time_to_Heal_(memoir)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="A Time to Heal (memoir) (page does not exist)">A Time to Heal</a></i>, President <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Gerald Ford</a>'s forthcoming memoir of his decision to pardon former president <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>. When <a href="/wiki/Harper_%26_Row" class="mw-redirect" title="Harper &amp; Row">Harper &amp; Row</a>, who held the rights to <i>A Time to Heal</i>, brought suit, <i>The Nation</i> asserted that its use of the book was protected under the doctrine of fair use, because of the great public interest in a historical figure’s account of a historic incident. The Court rejected this argument holding that the right of first publication was important enough to find in favor of Harper.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facts">Facts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harper_%26_Row_v._Nation_Enterprises&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Facts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Former President Gerald Ford had written a memoir, <i>A Time to Heal</i>, including an account of his decision to pardon Richard Nixon. Ford had licensed his publication rights to Harper &amp; Row, which had contracted for excerpts of the memoir to be printed in <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine. Instead, <i>The Nation</i> magazine published 300 to 400 words of verbatim quotes from the 500-page book without the permission of Ford, Harper &amp; Row, or <i>Time</i>. Based on this prior publication, <i>Time</i> withdrew from the contract (as it was permitted to by a clause therein), and Harper &amp; Row filed a <a href="/wiki/Lawsuit" title="Lawsuit">lawsuit</a> against <i>The Nation</i> for <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">copyright infringement</a>. <i>The Nation</i> asserted as a defense that Ford was a public figure, and his reasons for pardoning Nixon were of vital interest, and that appropriation in such circumstances should qualify as a fair use.
</p><p>The federal trial judge, Richard Owen, ruled in favor of Harper &amp; Row and awarded damages.<sup id="cite_ref-trial_501_F._Supp._848_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trial_501_F._Supp._848-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-557_F._Supp_1067_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-557_F._Supp_1067-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Second_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit">Second Circuit Court of Appeals</a> reversed the ruling, finding that <i>The Nation</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s actions in quoting the memoirs were protected by fair use privilege.<sup id="cite_ref-723_F.2d_195_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-723_F.2d_195-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Harper &amp; Row appealed this ruling to the Supreme Court.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Issue">Issue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harper_%26_Row_v._Nation_Enterprises&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Issue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The issue before the Court was whether a fair use existed where the purported infringer published a public figure’s unpublished work on an important public event.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Majority_and_minority_opinions">Majority and minority opinions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harper_%26_Row_v._Nation_Enterprises&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Majority and minority opinions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Court, in an opinion by <a href="/wiki/Sandra_Day_O%27Connor" title="Sandra Day O&#39;Connor">Justice O'Connor</a> noted that the right of first publication is a particularly strong right, and held that there was no 'public figure' exception to copyright protection, asserting that "the promise of copyright would be an empty one if it could be avoided merely by dubbing the infringement a fair use 'news report' of the book."<sup id="cite_ref-SCOTUS_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCOTUS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The Court applied the statutory <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/United_States_Code/Title_17/Chapter_1/Section_107" class="extiw" title="s:United States Code/Title 17/Chapter 1/Section 107">four factor test</a> to determine if the use was fair, and made the following findings:
</p>
<ol><li>The purpose or character of the use weighed against a finding of fair use because, "<i>The Nation</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s use had not merely the incidental effect but the intended purpose of supplanting the copyright holder's commercially valuable right of first publication." <i>The Nation</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s intent to benefit by depriving the copyright holders of their right to first publication suggest that this use was not "fair."</li>
<li>Although the nature of <i>A Time to Heal</i> was primarily informative or factual, and thus deserving of less copyright protection, the work had substantial expressive value. If <i>The Nation</i> had limited themselves to only reporting the factual descriptions in the work, the second factor would have weighed in favor of finding fair use. (Justice Brennan, dissenting, suggests that the Court does not take enough account of how much of <i>The Nation</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s report was factual.) However, <i>The Nation</i> "did not stop at isolated phrases and instead excerpted subjective descriptions and portraits of public figures whose power lies in the author's individualized expression."</li>
<li>The amount and substantiality of the portion used in relation to the copyrighted work as a whole weighed against a finding of fair use. Although the "amount" was small, it constituted a "substantial" portion of the work because the excerpt was the "heart of the work". The Court noted that an infringer could not defend plagiarism by pointing to how much else they could have plagiarized, but did not.</li>
<li>The effect of the use on the potential market for the value of the copyrighted work also weighed against a finding of fair use. <i>The Nation</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s infringement led to actual, particular harm, Time's cancellation of their publishing contract.</li></ol>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_J._Brennan" class="mw-redirect" title="William J. Brennan">Justice Brennan</a> dissented, joined by Justices <a href="/wiki/Byron_White" title="Byron White">White</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thurgood_Marshall" title="Thurgood Marshall">Marshall</a>. Citing the text of the law under which Harper's brought their lawsuit ("The enactment of copyright legislation by Congress under the terms of the Constitution is not based upon any natural right that the author has in his writings ... but upon the ground that the welfare of the public will be served and progress of science and useful arts will be promoted by securing to authors for limited periods the exclusive rights to their writings." <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>), Brennan found that the importance of "the robust debate of public issues" here outweighed the limited power of copyright ownership.  He stated that the descriptions of the work were non-copyrightable, and the direct quotations were so few, compared to the size of the work as a whole, that <i>The Nation'</i>s article did not constitute an appropriation of Harper's copyright.  Brennan criticized the court for finding copyright infringement technically based on the 300 words that were quoted in order to protect Harper's interest in being the first to publish the non-copyrightable historical information in the work.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_treatment">Later treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harper_%26_Row_v._Nation_Enterprises&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The opinion was later cited by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Second_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit">United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit</a> hearing the case of <i><a href="/wiki/Salinger_v._Random_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Salinger v. Random House">Salinger v. Random House</a></i> (1987), in which J.D. Salinger had objected to the publication of his unpublished letters. The court noted that the Supreme Court ruling on <i>Harper &amp; Row v. Nation Enterprises</i> (1985) had observed "the scope of fair use is narrower with respect to unpublished works," but denied that the unpublished nature of Salinger's letters was decisive. ("[The Supreme Court] stressed the tailoring of fair use analysis to the particular case... It neither stated nor implied a categorical rule barring fair use of unpublished works."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWelch1993114_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWelch1993114-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The court went on to note that the meaning of <i>Harper</i> is somewhat ambiguous. Either, they reasoned, there are fewer cases in which fair use may be found when the original work is unpublished or less material may be copied from unpublished works. The court ultimately decided that the first was true, and that unpublished works normally have complete protection against any copying of protected expression.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
