	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">In God We Trust</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">&#160;&#160;(Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=In_God_we_trust&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="In God we trust">In God we trust</a>)</span></div>
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Official motto of the United States</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/In_God_We_Trust_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="In God We Trust (disambiguation)">In God We Trust (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1in_god_we_trust.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/1in_god_we_trust.jpg/220px-1in_god_we_trust.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/1in_god_we_trust.jpg/330px-1in_god_we_trust.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/1in_god_we_trust.jpg/440px-1in_god_we_trust.jpg 2x" data-file-width="550" data-file-height="356" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1in_god_we_trust.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Capitalized "<span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">IN GOD WE TRUST</span></span>" on the reverse of a <a href="/wiki/United_States_twenty-dollar_bill" title="United States twenty-dollar bill">United States twenty-dollar bill</a></div></div></div>
<p>"<b>In God We Trust</b>", also written as "<b>In God we trust</b>", is the <a href="/wiki/United_States_national_motto" title="United States national motto">official motto</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States of America</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. It was adopted by the U.S. Congress in 1956, supplanting <i><a href="/wiki/E_pluribus_unum" title="E pluribus unum">E pluribus unum</a></i>, in use since the initial 1776 design of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Seal_of_the_United_States" title="Great Seal of the United States">Great Seal of the United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RSAL_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSAL-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The capitalized form "IN GOD WE TRUST" first appeared on the <a href="/wiki/Two-cent_piece_(United_States)" title="Two-cent piece (United States)">two-cent piece</a> in 1864<sup id="cite_ref-USTreasury_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USTreasury-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and has appeared on <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_Note" title="Federal Reserve Note">paper currency</a> since 1957. A law passed in a Joint Resolution by the 84th Congress (P.L. 84–140) and approved by President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_Eisenhower" class="mw-redirect" title="Dwight Eisenhower">Dwight Eisenhower</a> on July 30, 1956, requires that "In God We Trust" appear on American currency. The following year, the phrase was used on paper money for the first time—on the updated one-dollar <a href="/wiki/Silver_certificate_(United_States)" title="Silver certificate (United States)">silver certificate</a> that entered circulation on October 1, 1957.<sup id="cite_ref-USTreasury_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USTreasury-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The 84th Congress later passed legislation (P.L. 84–851), also signed by President Eisenhower on July 30, 1956, declaring the phrase to be the national motto.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-USTFS_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USTFS-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-USPL84-851_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USPL84-851-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some groups and people have objected to its use, contending that its religious reference violates the <a href="/wiki/Establishment_Clause" title="Establishment Clause">Establishment Clause</a> of the <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment" class="mw-redirect" title="First Amendment">First Amendment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> These groups believe the phrase should be removed from currency and public property. In lawsuits, this argument has not overcome the interpretational doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Accommodationism" title="Accommodationism">accommodationism</a>, which allows government to endorse religious establishments as long as they are all treated equally.<sup id="cite_ref-Drakeman1991_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Drakeman1991-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> According to a 2003 joint poll by <a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a>, <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Gallup_Organization" class="mw-redirect" title="The Gallup Organization">Gallup</a>, 90% of Americans support the inscription "In God We Trust" on U.S. coins.<sup id="cite_ref-Poll_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Poll-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006, "In God We Trust" was designated as the motto of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-myflorida.com_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-myflorida.com-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> equivalent of "In God We Trust," <i>En Dios Confiamos,</i> is the motto of the <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Republic of Nicaragua</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Heraldic_motto" class="mw-redirect" title="Heraldic motto">heraldic motto</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brighton,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Brighton, England">Brighton, England</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> equivalent, <i>In Deo Fidemus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_God_We_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chase_to_Pollock_1863-12-09_motto_only.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Chase_to_Pollock_1863-12-09_motto_only.png/220px-Chase_to_Pollock_1863-12-09_motto_only.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="87" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c9/Chase_to_Pollock_1863-12-09_motto_only.png 1.5x" data-file-width="253" data-file-height="100" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chase_to_Pollock_1863-12-09_motto_only.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Salmon_P._Chase" title="Salmon P. Chase">Salmon P. Chase, Treasury Secretary</a>, scribes "In God is Our Trust," scratches out "is Our" and overwrites "We" to arrive at "In God We Trust" in a December 9, 1863, letter to <a href="/wiki/James_Pollock" title="James Pollock">James Pollock</a>, Director of the Philadelphia Mint.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1864_2C_Small_Motto_Red_(obv).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/1864_2C_Small_Motto_Red_%28obv%29.jpg/220px-1864_2C_Small_Motto_Red_%28obv%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/1864_2C_Small_Motto_Red_%28obv%29.jpg/330px-1864_2C_Small_Motto_Red_%28obv%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/1864_2C_Small_Motto_Red_%28obv%29.jpg/440px-1864_2C_Small_Motto_Red_%28obv%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1888" data-file-height="1888" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1864_2C_Small_Motto_Red_(obv).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"<span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">IN GOD WE TRUST</span></span>" first appeared on the obverse side of the <a href="/wiki/Two-cent_piece_(United_States)" title="Two-cent piece (United States)">Two-cent piece</a> in 1864<sup id="cite_ref-USTreasury_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USTreasury-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The patriotic anthem <i><a href="/wiki/Chester_(song)" title="Chester (song)">Chester</a></i>, composed by <a href="/wiki/William_Billings" title="William Billings">William Billings</a> for his 1770 songbook <i>The New England Psalm Singer</i> and sung during the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>, has a similar phrase in its lyric:<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><div class="poem">
<p>Let tyrants shake their iron rod,<br />
And Slav'ry clank her galling chains,<br />
We fear them not, we trust in God,<br />
New England's God forever reigns.
</p>
</div></blockquote>
<p>In 1860, the phrase was used in the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_New_Westminster" title="Coat of arms of New Westminster">coat of arms of New Westminster</a>, Canada. During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/125th_Pennsylvania_Infantry" class="mw-redirect" title="125th Pennsylvania Infantry">125th Pennsylvania Infantry</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Union_(American_Civil_War)" title="Union (American Civil War)">Union</a> <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Army</a> assumed the motto "In God we trust" in early August 1862.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> William W. Wallace, coiner, circa August 1862, of the motto "In God We Trust"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERegimental_Committee1906151_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERegimental_Committee1906151-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> was Captain of Company C of the 125th Pennsylvania Infantry.
</p><p>The Reverend <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_R._Watkinson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mark R. Watkinson (page does not exist)">Mark R. Watkinson</a> of '<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ridleyville&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ridleyville (page does not exist)">Ridleyville</a>', <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, (pastor of Prospect Hill Baptist Church in present-day <a href="/wiki/Prospect_Park,_Pennsylvania" title="Prospect Park, Pennsylvania">Prospect Park, Pennsylvania</a>) in a letter dated November 13, 1861, petitioned the Treasury Department to add a statement recognizing "<a href="/wiki/Almighty_God" class="mw-redirect" title="Almighty God">Almighty God</a> in some form on our coins" in order to "relieve us from the ignominy of heathenism".<sup id="cite_ref-Treasury_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Treasury-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-US-CSS_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US-CSS-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> At least part of the motivation was to declare that <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> was on the Union side of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War.</a><sup id="cite_ref-history_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Treasury Secretary <a href="/wiki/Salmon_P._Chase" title="Salmon P. Chase">Salmon P. Chase</a> acted on this proposal and directed the then-Philadelphia <a href="/wiki/Director_of_the_United_States_Mint" title="Director of the United States Mint">Director of the Mint</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Pollock" title="James Pollock">James Pollock</a>, to begin drawing up possible designs that would include the religious phrase. Chase chose his favorite designs and presented a proposal to Congress for the new designs in late 1863.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 1863, <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>'s Secretary of the Treasury decided on a new motto, "In God We Trust," to engrave on U.S. coins. Lincoln's involvement in this decision is unclear.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A version of the motto made an early appearance on obverse side of the <a href="/wiki/Interest_bearing_note#Denominational_set_of_interest_bearing_notes" title="Interest bearing note">twenty dollar interest bearing note</a> issued in 1864 along with the motto "God and our Right".
</p><p>As Chase was preparing his recommendation to Congress, it was found that the Act of Congress dated January 18, 1837, prescribed the mottoes and devices that should be placed upon the coins of the United States. This meant that the mint could make no changes without the enactment of additional legislation by the Congress. Such legislation was introduced and passed as the <a href="/wiki/Coinage_Act_of_1864" title="Coinage Act of 1864">Coinage Act of 1864</a> on April 22, 1864, allowing the Secretary of the Treasury to authorize the inclusion of the phrase on one-cent and two-cent coins.<sup id="cite_ref-history_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An Act of Congress passed on March 3, 1865, allowed the Mint Director, with the Secretary's approval, to place the motto on all gold and silver coins that "shall admit the inscription thereon".<sup id="cite_ref-history_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CR1956p13917_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CR1956p13917-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In 1873, Congress passed the Coinage Act, granting that the Secretary of the Treasury "may cause the motto IN GOD WE TRUST to be inscribed on such coins as shall admit of such motto".
</p><p>A similar phrase, 'In God is our Trust', appears in "<a href="/wiki/The_Star-Spangled_Banner" title="The Star-Spangled Banner">The Star-Spangled Banner</a>", the song adopted as the national anthem of the United States in 1931. Written by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Scott_Key" title="Francis Scott Key">Francis Scott Key</a> during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a>, the fourth stanza includes the words, "And this be our motto: 'In God is our Trust'".
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1879S_Morgan_Dollar_NGC_MS67plus_Reverse.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/1879S_Morgan_Dollar_NGC_MS67plus_Reverse.png/220px-1879S_Morgan_Dollar_NGC_MS67plus_Reverse.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/1879S_Morgan_Dollar_NGC_MS67plus_Reverse.png/330px-1879S_Morgan_Dollar_NGC_MS67plus_Reverse.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/1879S_Morgan_Dollar_NGC_MS67plus_Reverse.png/440px-1879S_Morgan_Dollar_NGC_MS67plus_Reverse.png 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="1500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1879S_Morgan_Dollar_NGC_MS67plus_Reverse.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The reverse of the <a href="/wiki/Morgan_dollar" title="Morgan dollar">Morgan dollar</a> presented the lower-cased "In God we trust"</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2014_ATB_Quarter_Obv.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/44/2014_ATB_Quarter_Obv.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="200" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="200" data-file-height="200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2014_ATB_Quarter_Obv.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Quarter_dollar" title="Quarter dollar">quarter dollar</a> with the United States' official motto "<span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">IN GOD WE TRUST</span></span>" on the obverse side</div></div></div>
<p>After use of the motto on coins began during the 1860's, there have been some coins on which the motto did not appear. The motto disappeared from the five-cent coin in 1883, and did not reappear until production of the Jefferson nickel began in 1938.<sup id="cite_ref-history_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> However, at least two other coins minted in every year in the interim still bore the motto,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> including the <a href="/wiki/Morgan_dollar" title="Morgan dollar">Morgan dollar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Seated_Liberty_coinage" title="United States Seated Liberty coinage">Seated Liberty half dollar</a>. The omission of the motto "In God We Trust" on the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Head_eagle" title="Indian Head eagle">Indian Head eagle</a> coin caused public outrage, and prompted <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> to pass a bill mandating its inclusion.  Mint Chief Engraver <a href="/wiki/Charles_E._Barber" title="Charles E. Barber">Charles E. Barber</a> added the words and made minor modifications to the design. In 1908, Congress made it mandatory that the phrase be printed on all coins upon which it had previously appeared. This decision was motivated after a public outcry following the release of a $20 coin which did not bear the motto.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The motto has been in continuous use on the one-cent coin since 1909, and on the ten-cent coin since 1916. It also has appeared on all gold coins and silver dollar coins, half-dollar coins, and quarter-dollar coins struck since July 1, 1908.<sup id="cite_ref-history_24-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Since 1938, all US coins have borne the motto.<sup id="cite_ref-USTreasury_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USTreasury-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> era, the government of the United States sought to distinguish itself from the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, which promoted <a href="/wiki/State_atheism" title="State atheism">state atheism</a> and thus implemented <a href="/wiki/Soviet_anti-religious_legislation" title="Soviet anti-religious legislation">antireligious legislation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/84th_United_States_Congress" title="84th United States Congress">84th Congress</a> passed a joint resolution "declaring IN GOD WE TRUST the national motto of the United States". The resolution passed both the House and the Senate unanimously and without debate.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> H.R. 619, a bill that required that the inscription "In God we trust" appear on all paper and coin currency, was signed into law by President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Eisenhower</a> on July 30, 1956.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-History,_Art_&amp;_Archives_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History,_Art_&amp;_Archives-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>. The United States Code at <a href="/wiki/Title_36_of_the_United_States_Code" title="Title 36 of the United States Code">36 U.S.C.</a>&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/36/302">§&#160;302</a>, now states: "'In God we trust' is the national motto."
</p><p>On paper currency, it first appeared on the <a href="/wiki/Silver_certificate_(United_States)" title="Silver certificate (United States)">silver certificate</a> in 1957, followed by other certificates.  <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_Note" title="Federal Reserve Note">Federal Reserve Notes</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Note" title="United States Note">United States Notes</a> were circulated with the motto starting from 1964 to 1966, depending on the denomination.<sup id="cite_ref-history_24-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> (Of these, only Federal Reserve Notes are still circulated.)
</p><p>Representative <a href="/wiki/Charles_Edward_Bennett" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Edward Bennett">Charles Edward Bennett</a> of Florida cited the Cold War when he introduced the bill in the House, saying "In these days when imperialistic and materialistic communism seeks to attack and destroy freedom, we should continually look for ways to strengthen the foundations of our freedom".
<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Aronow_v._United_States" title="Aronow v. United States">Aronow v. United States</a></i> was the first case to challenge the inclusion of "In God We Trust" on <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">U.S. currency</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AronowUS_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AronowUS-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The law it challenged was "31 U.S.C. § 324a "the inscription 'In God we Trust'...shall appear on all United States currency and coins".<sup id="cite_ref-AronowUS_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AronowUS-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> <i>O'Hair v. Blumenthal</i> (1978) challenged the inclusion of the phrase "In God We Trust" on U.S. currency. A similar decision was reached by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Fifth_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit">Fifth Circuit</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Madalyn_Murray_O%27Hair" title="Madalyn Murray O&#39;Hair">Madalyn Murray O'Hair</a> vs <a href="/wiki/W._Michael_Blumenthal" title="W. Michael Blumenthal">W. Michael Blumenthal</a></i> in 1979, which affirmed that the "primary purpose of the slogan was secular".<sup id="cite_ref-Duncan_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duncan-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2001, <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Mississippi" class="mw-redirect" title="Governor of Mississippi">Governor of Mississippi</a> <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Musgrove" title="Ronnie Musgrove">Ronnie Musgrove</a> signed legislation requiring the motto "In God We Trust" to be displayed in every <a href="/wiki/Public_school_(government_funded)" class="mw-redirect" title="Public school (government funded)">public school</a> classroom, as well as the school auditoriums and cafeterias, throughout the state.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a> in 2001, many public schools across the United States posted "In God We Trust" framed posters in their "libraries, cafeterias and classrooms". The <a href="/wiki/American_Family_Association" title="American Family Association">American Family Association</a> supplied several 11-by-14-inch posters to school systems and vowed to defend any legal challenges to the displaying of the posters.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to a 2003 joint poll by <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Gallup_Organization" class="mw-redirect" title="The Gallup Organization">Gallup</a>, 90% of Americans support the inscription "In God We Trust" on U.S. coins.<sup id="cite_ref-Poll_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Poll-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006, on the 50th anniversary of its adoption, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a> reaffirmed "In God We Trust" as the official national motto of the United States of America.<sup id="cite_ref-Senate_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Senate-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> In Florida House Bill no. 1145, Florida adopted 'In God We Trust' as the official state motto, effective July 1, 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-myflorida.com_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-myflorida.com-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011 the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a> passed an additional resolution reaffirming "In God We Trust" as the official motto of the United States, in a 396–9 vote.<sup id="cite_ref-Reaffirm_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reaffirm-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Iqraar_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Iqraar-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, a federal court rejected a challenge, brought by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Newdow" title="Michael Newdow">Michael Newdow</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_From_Religion_Foundation" title="Freedom From Religion Foundation">Freedom From Religion Foundation</a>, to remove "In God We Trust" from American currency.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Seal_of_Mississippi_(2014%E2%80%93present).svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Seal_of_Mississippi_%282014%E2%80%93present%29.svg/220px-Seal_of_Mississippi_%282014%E2%80%93present%29.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Seal_of_Mississippi_%282014%E2%80%93present%29.svg/330px-Seal_of_Mississippi_%282014%E2%80%93present%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Seal_of_Mississippi_%282014%E2%80%93present%29.svg/440px-Seal_of_Mississippi_%282014%E2%80%93present%29.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Seal_of_Mississippi_(2014%E2%80%93present).svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"<span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">IN GOD WE TRUST</span></span>" on the <a href="/wiki/Seal_of_Mississippi" title="Seal of Mississippi">Seal of Mississippi</a></div></div></div>
<p>On January 31, 2014, purporting to defend religious freedom, the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a> senate voted to add the words, "In God We Trust" to the state seal and the change was made effective on July 1, 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-2014Seal_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2014Seal-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015 the county police department of <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_County,_Illinois" title="Jefferson County, Illinois">Jefferson County, Illinois</a> announced that the words "In God We Trust" will be on police squad cars.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> In 2015, the Freedom from Religion Foundation demanded that local authorities remove decals of the motto from Childress, Texas Police Department patrol vehicles. In response, Police Chief Adrian Garcia told the organization, in a written letter, to "go fly a kite."<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2017, Act 911, sponsored by State Rep. <a href="/wiki/Jim_Dotson" title="Jim Dotson">Jim Dotson</a>, made it a requirement of <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a> state law for schools to display posters with the national motto ("In God We Trust").<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>  
</p><p>Also in March, the same requirement was enacted <sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> in Kentucky schools for the 2019-2020 school year requiring the slogan to be displayed "in a prominent location." To protest the requirement, <a href="/wiki/Fayette_County_Public_Schools_(Kentucky)" title="Fayette County Public Schools (Kentucky)">Fayette County Public Schools (Kentucky)</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_KY" class="mw-redirect" title="Lexington, KY">Lexington, KY</a> complied by posting a framed <a href="/wiki/United_States_one-dollar_bill" title="United States one-dollar bill">United States one-dollar bill</a> which bears the slogan.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In early 2018, <a href="/wiki/Kimberly_Daniels" title="Kimberly Daniels">Kimberly Daniels</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Pastor" title="Pastor">pastor</a><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> who currently serves as the representative for <a href="/wiki/Florida_House_of_Representatives" title="Florida House of Representatives">Florida House of Representatives</a> District 14 as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a>, introduced HB 839, a bill that requires public schools to display the motto "In God We Trust" in a conspicuous place. On Tuesday, January 23, 2018, the bill received unanimous approval from the House PreK-12 Innovation Subcommittee.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Later, in a vote on February 21, 2018, the bill passed 97 to 10 in the House.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> As part of Florida's March 2018 K-12 education law, Gov. <a href="/wiki/Rick_Scott" title="Rick Scott">Rick Scott</a> mandated that all public schools post the state motto ("In God We Trust") in a prominent location.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2018, a bill requiring Tennessee schools to prominently display the national motto ("In God We Trust") sponsored by Rep. <a href="/wiki/Susan_Lynn" title="Susan Lynn">Susan Lynn</a> passed the state House with 81 of the 99 members voting in favor of it.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2019, the <a href="/wiki/Bakersfield,_California" title="Bakersfield, California">Bakersfield, California</a> City Council voted 4-2 to put an "In God We Trust" sticker on the city's police and fire vehicles. The issue was debated for two hours by 19 people including a lawyer from the <a href="/wiki/ACLU" class="mw-redirect" title="ACLU">ACLU</a><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_and_culture">Society and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_God_We_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Society and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_God_We_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, the official motto "In God We Trust" is not found verbatim in any verses from the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>, but very closely in the Old Testament in <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Psalms#91:2" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Psalms">Psalm 91:2</a>, "I will say of the LORD, <i>He is</i> my refuge and my fortress: my God; in him will I trust" and in the New Testament in <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/2_Corinthians#1:10" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/2 Corinthians">2 Corinthians 1:10</a>, "Who delivered us from so great a death, and doth deliver: in whom we trust that he will yet deliver <i>us</i>."  The concept is paraphrased in <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Psalms#118:8" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Psalms">Psalm 118:8</a>, <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Psalms#40:3" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Psalms">Psalm 40:3</a>, <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Psalms#73:28" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Psalms">Psalm 73:28</a>, and <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Proverbs#29:25" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Proverbs">Proverbs 29:25</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> the word for the concept of reliance on God is called <i><a href="/wiki/Tawakkul" title="Tawakkul">Tawakkul</a></i>; the phrase "In God We Trust" is literally found in two places of the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Surah" title="Surah">Surah</a> 10 <a href="/wiki/Yunus_(surah)" title="Yunus (surah)">Yunus</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Surah" title="Surah">Surah</a> 7 <a href="/wiki/Al-A%27raf" title="Al-A&#39;raf">Al-A'raf</a>, and several other verses reinforce this concept.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Melkote Ramaswamy, a Hindu American scholar, writes that the presence of the phrase "In God We Trust" on American currency is a reminder that "there is God everywhere, whether we are conscious or not."<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_God_We_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An e-mail conspiracy theory is that "In God We Trust" was intentionally omitted from new U.S. dollar coins in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>  The first coins produced under the <a href="/wiki/Presidential_$1_Coin_Program" class="mw-redirect" title="Presidential $1 Coin Program">Presidential $1 Coin Program</a> did indeed lack the "In God We Trust" inscription along their edges (along with the "E Pluribus Unum" inscription, the year of production, and the <a href="/wiki/Mint_mark" title="Mint mark">mint mark</a>; these coins, unlike normal dollar coins, had completely blank edges), but these coins, known as "<a href="/wiki/Presidential_$1_Coin_Program#Minting_errors" class="mw-redirect" title="Presidential $1 Coin Program">godless dollars</a>", were the result of a minting error, not a deliberate omission.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The film <i><a href="/wiki/They_Live" title="They Live">They Live</a></i> (1988) plays on the idea. Special sunglasses allow the wearers to see simple hidden messages instead of the signs they see without them. Advertising is seen as "OBEY", "CONSUME" and "MARRY AND REPRODUCE". Dollar bills are all marked "THIS IS YOUR GOD".<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2006, <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Llewelyn-Bowen" title="Laurence Llewelyn-Bowen">Laurence Llewelyn-Bowen</a> and his wife Jackie were offered a place on the <a href="/wiki/Valentine%27s_Day" title="Valentine&#39;s Day">Valentine's Day</a> celebrity couples edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Who_Wants_to_Be_a_Millionaire%3F_(UK_game_show)" class="mw-redirect" title="Who Wants to Be a Millionaire? (UK game show)">Who Wants to Be a Millionaire?</a></i> They appeared on the show managing to reach the £1 million question, before answering it incorrectly and dropping from £500,000 all the way down to just £32,000 (a loss of £468,000). Celador allowed Llewelyn-Bowen and his wife retry the show after the company claimed that the last question "didn't meet their standards". The allegedly misleading question was "Translated from the <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>, what is the motto of the United States?" The answer given was "In God We Trust" which is originally English and has in fact been the motto of the United States since 1956. The intended answer had been "One Out of Many" which is a translation of the Latin phrase <i><a href="/wiki/E_pluribus_unum" title="E pluribus unum">E pluribus unum</a></i>, which is not actually the current United States motto. <i>E pluribus unum</i> had been the de facto motto but was never legally declared as such.<sup id="cite_ref-secondchance_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-secondchance-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="License_plates">License plates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_God_We_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: License plates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>As of April 1, 2016 the following U.S. states currently offer an "In God We Trust" license plate as a specialty plate for an additional normal vehicle registration processing which vary from state to state: Alaska, Arizona, Arkansas, Florida, Indiana, Kansas, Kentucky, Louisiana, North Carolina, Ohio,<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Oklahoma, Pennsylvania, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, Virginia, West Virginia, and Wisconsin.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Florida — which also offers a specialty plate — and Georgia which both display the county of issuance on their License Plate offer the option of "In God We Trust" in place of the County Name.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_God_We_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The constitutionality of the phrase "In God We Trust" has been upheld according to the judicial interpretation of <a href="/wiki/Accommodationism" title="Accommodationism">accommodationism</a>, whose adherents state that this entrenched practice has not historically presented any constitutional difficulty, is not coercive, and does not prefer one religious denomination over another.<sup id="cite_ref-Views_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Views-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>  In <i><a href="/wiki/Zorach_v._Clauson" title="Zorach v. Clauson">Zorach v. Clauson</a></i> (1952), the Supreme Court also wrote that the nation's "institutions presuppose a Supreme Being" and that government recognition of <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> does not constitute the establishment of a state church as the Constitution's authors intended to prohibit.<sup id="cite_ref-ABA_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABA-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>On the other hand, advocates of <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_church_and_state_in_the_United_States" title="Separation of church and state in the United States">separation of church and state</a> have questioned the legality of this motto asserting their opinion that it is a violation of the United States Constitution, prohibiting the government from passing any law respecting an establishment of religion.<sup id="cite_ref-Views_70-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Views-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> As such "In God We Trust" as a national motto and on U.S. currency has been the subject of numerous unsuccessful lawsuits by these individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-AtheistLawsuit_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AtheistLawsuit-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> The motto was first challenged in <i><a href="/wiki/Aronow_v._United_States" title="Aronow v. United States">Aronow v. United States</a></i> in 1970, but the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Ninth_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit">United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit</a> ruled: "It is quite obvious that the national motto and the slogan on coinage and currency 'In God We Trust' has nothing whatsoever to do with the establishment of religion. Its use is of patriotic or ceremonial character and bears no true resemblance to a governmental sponsorship of a religious exercise."<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Lynch_v._Donnelly" title="Lynch v. Donnelly">Lynch v. Donnelly</a></i> (1984), the Supreme Court wrote that acts of "<a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_deism" title="Ceremonial deism">ceremonial deism</a>" are "protected from Establishment Clause scrutiny chiefly because they have lost through rote repetition any significant religious content".<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2006, a federal judge rejected <a href="/wiki/Michael_Newdow" title="Michael Newdow">Michael Newdow</a>'s Establishment Clause lawsuit on the grounds that the minted words amount to a secular national slogan, and do not dictate anyone's beliefs. Newdow stated that he would appeal the ruling,<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> although <i>Aronow</i> was decided on the same grounds in the Ninth Circuit and the lower court was required to return the same ruling, likewise the Ninth Circuit does not traditionally overrule previous Ninth Circuit rulings. On December 4, 2007, Newdow argued before a three-judge panel of the Ninth Circuit to remove both "under God" from the Pledge of Allegiance (<i>Roe v. Rio Linda Union School District</i>),<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and "In God We Trust" from United States currency.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> The Ninth Circuit rejected Newdow's challenge. In a decision published March 11, 2010, the court held that its earlier decision in <i>Aronow</i>, which "held the national motto is of a "patriotic or ceremonial character," has no "theological or ritualistic impact," and does not constitute "governmental sponsorship of a religious exercise," foreclosed Newdow's argument.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> In an opinion concurring only in the judgment, even the Judge <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Reinhardt" title="Stephen Reinhardt">Stephen Reinhardt</a><sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> agreed that <i>Aronow</i> was controlling precedent.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> <i>Newdow v. Congress</i>, 598 F.3d 638 (9th Cir. 2010) cert. denied 131 S. Ct. 1612 (U.S. 2011). AKA: The "In God We Trust Case" – A prominent atheist, Michael Newdow, filed a suit to declare the national motto – In God We Trust – unconstitutional and to have it removed from coins and currency.<sup id="cite_ref-egelko_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-egelko-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ninth-circuit_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ninth-circuit-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-oral-arguments_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oral-arguments-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Justice_Institute" title="Pacific Justice Institute">Pacific Justice Institute</a> intervened as a defendant and defended against the suit.<sup id="cite_ref-egelko_80-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-egelko-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ninth-circuit_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ninth-circuit-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-oral-arguments_82-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oral-arguments-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>  The case was dismissed by the trial court and the Ninth Circuit affirmed that decision.<sup id="cite_ref-egelko_80-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-egelko-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ninth-circuit_81-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ninth-circuit-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-oral-arguments_82-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oral-arguments-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, <a href="/wiki/David_F._Bauman" title="David F. Bauman">David F. Bauman</a> dismissed a case against the <a href="/wiki/Matawan-Aberdeen_Regional_School_District" title="Matawan-Aberdeen Regional School District">Matawan-Aberdeen Regional School District</a> brought by a student of the district and the <a href="/wiki/American_Humanist_Association" title="American Humanist Association">American Humanist Association</a> that argued that the phrase "under God" in the <a href="/wiki/Pledge_of_Allegiance_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pledge of Allegiance (United States)">Pledge of Allegiance</a> created a climate of discrimination because it promoted religion, making non-believers "second-class citizens".<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> He noted; "As a matter of historical tradition, the words 'under God' can no more be expunged from the national consciousness than the words 'In God We Trust' from every coin in the land, than the words 'so help me God' from every presidential oath since 1789, or than the prayer that has opened every congressional session of legislative business since 1787."
</p><p>
President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> took issue with using the motto on coinage considering its usage "dangerously close to <a href="/wiki/Sacrilege" title="Sacrilege">sacrilege</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"... My own feeling in the matter is due to my very firm conviction that to put such a motto on coins, or to use it in any kindred manner, not only does no good, but does positive harm, and is in effect irreverence, which comes dangerously close to sacrilege. ...&#160; Any use which tends to cheapen it, and, above all, any use which tends to secure its being treated in a spirit of levity, is from every standpoint profoundly to be regretted. ... it seems to me eminently unwise to cheapen such a motto by use on coins ...&#160; In all my life I have never heard any human being speak reverently of this motto on the coins or show any signs of its having appealed to any high emotion in him, but I have literally, hundreds of times, heard it used as an occasion of and incitement to ... sneering ...&#160; Every one must remember the innumerable cartoons and articles based on phrases like 'In God we trust for the 8 cents,' ...&#160; Surely, I am well within bounds when I say that a use of the phrase which invites constant levity of this type is most undesirable. ..." - Theodore Roosevelt, November 1907
</p></blockquote>
