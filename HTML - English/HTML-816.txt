<p><b>General American English</b> or <b>General American</b> (abbreviated <b>GA</b> or <b>GenAm</b>) is the umbrella <a href="/wiki/Accent_(sociolinguistics)" title="Accent (sociolinguistics)">accent</a> of <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a> spoken by a majority of Americans and widely perceived, among Americans, as lacking any distinctly regional, ethnic, or socioeconomic characteristics.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014123_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014123-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKövecses200081–82_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKövecses200081–82-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells198234_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells198234-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In reality, it encompasses a continuum of <a href="/wiki/Accent_(sociolinguistics)" title="Accent (sociolinguistics)">accents</a> rather than a single unified accent.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982470_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982470-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Americans with high education,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKortmannSchneider2004257_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKortmannSchneider2004257-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> or from the <a href="/wiki/Midland_American_English" title="Midland American English">North Midland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_New_England_English" title="Western New England English">Western New England</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Western_American_English" title="Western American English">Western</a> regions of the country, are the most likely to be perceived as having "General American" accents.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014128–9_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014128–9-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The precise definition and usefulness of the term "General American" continue to be debated,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982118_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982118-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014124,_126_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014124,_126-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKortmannSchneider2004262_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKortmannSchneider2004262-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and the scholars who use it today admittedly do so as a convenient basis for comparison rather than for exactness.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982118_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982118-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELabovAshBoberg2006263_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELabovAshBoberg2006263-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Other scholars prefer the term <b>Standard American English</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells198234_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells198234-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKortmannSchneider2004257_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKortmannSchneider2004257-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Standard_Canadian_English" title="Standard Canadian English">Standard Canadian English</a> is sometimes considered to fall under the <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonological</a> spectrum of General American,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoberg2004159_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoberg2004159-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> especially rather than the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Received_Pronunciation" title="Received Pronunciation">Received Pronunciation</a>; in fact, spoken Canadian English aligns with General American in nearly every situation where <a href="/wiki/Comparison_of_General_American_and_Received_Pronunciation" title="Comparison of General American and Received Pronunciation">British and American accents differ</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982491_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982491-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_American_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_modern_definition">History and modern definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_American_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History and modern definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term "General American" was first disseminated by American English scholar <a href="/wiki/George_Philip_Krapp" title="George Philip Krapp">George Philip Krapp</a>, who, in 1925, described it as an American type of speech that was "<a href="/wiki/American_West" class="mw-redirect" title="American West">Western</a>" but "not local in character".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014124_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014124-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In 1930, American linguist <a href="/wiki/John_Samuel_Kenyon" title="John Samuel Kenyon">John Samuel Kenyon</a>, who largely popularized the term, considered it equivalent to the speech of "the North" or "Northern American",<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014124_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014124-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> but, in 1934, "Western and Midwestern".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014125_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014125-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Now typically regarded as falling under the General American umbrella are the regional accents of the <a href="/wiki/Western_American_English" title="Western American English">West</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELabovAshBoberg2006146_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELabovAshBoberg2006146-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014130_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014130-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Western_New_England_English" title="Western New England English">Western New England</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014128,_130_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014128,_130-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Midland_American_English" title="Midland American English">North Midland</a> (a band spanning central Ohio, central Indiana, central Illinois, northern Missouri, southern Iowa, and southeastern Nebraska),<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014129–130_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014129–130-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELabovAshBoberg2006268_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELabovAshBoberg2006268-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> plus the accents of highly educated Americans nationwide.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKortmannSchneider2004257_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKortmannSchneider2004257-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Arguably, all <a href="/wiki/Canadian_English" title="Canadian English">Canadian English</a> accents west of <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> are also General American,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoberg2004159_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoberg2004159-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> though <a href="/wiki/Canadian_raising" title="Canadian raising">Canadian vowel raising</a> and certain newly-developing features may serve to increasingly distinguish such accents from American ones.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, <a href="/wiki/William_Labov" title="William Labov">William Labov</a> et al.'s 2006 <i><a href="/wiki/Atlas_of_North_American_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlas of North American English">Atlas of North American English</a></i> identified these three accent regions—the Western U.S., Midland U.S., and Canada—as sharing those pronunciation features whose convergence would form a hypothetical "General American" accent.
</p><p>Regarded as having General American accents in the earlier 20th century, but not by the middle of the 20th century, are the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Mid-Atlantic United States">Mid-Atlantic United States</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014128–9_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014128–9-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Inland_North" class="mw-redirect" title="Inland North">Inland Northern United States</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982470_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982470-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Pennsylvania_English" title="Western Pennsylvania English">Western Pennsylvania</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014128–9_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014128–9-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> However, many younger speakers within these regions have reversed away from mid-20th century accent innovations back towards General American features.<sup id="cite_ref-syracuse_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-syracuse-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lansing_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lansing-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Accents that have never been labeled "General American", even since the term's popularization in the 1930s, are the regional accents (especially the <a href="/wiki/Rhoticity_in_English" title="Rhoticity in English"><i>r</i>-dropping</a> ones) of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_New_England_English" title="Eastern New England English">Eastern New England</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_English" title="New York City English">New York City</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Southern_American_English" title="Southern American English">American South</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014123,_129_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014123,_129-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In 1982, British phonetician <a href="/wiki/John_C._Wells" title="John C. Wells">John C. Wells</a> wrote that two-thirds of the American population spoke with a General American accent.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells198234_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells198234-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disputed_usage">Disputed usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_American_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Disputed usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/English_studies" title="English studies">English-language scholar</a> William A. Kretzchmar, Jr. explains in a 2004 article that the term "General American" came to refer to "a presumed most common or 'default' form of American English, especially to be distinguished from marked regional speech of New England or the South" and especially to speech associated with the vaguely-defined "<a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwest</a>", despite any historical or present evidence supporting this notion. Kretzschmar argues that a General American accent is simply the result of American speakers suppressing regional and social features that have become widely noticed and stigmatized.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since calling one variety of American speech the "general" variety can imply <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">privileging and prejudice</a>, Kretzchmar instead promotes the term <i>Standard American English</i>, which he defines as a level of American English pronunciation "employed by educated speakers in formal settings", while still being variable within the U.S. from place to place, and even from speaker to speaker.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKortmannSchneider2004257_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKortmannSchneider2004257-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> However, the term "standard" may also be interpreted as problematically implying a superior or "best" form of speech.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The term <i>Standard North American English</i>, in an effort to incorporate <a href="/wiki/Canadian_English" title="Canadian English">Canadian speakers</a> under the accent continuum, was also first suggested by <a href="#CITEREFBoberg2004">Boberg (2004)</a>.
</p><p>Modern language scholars discredit the original notion of General American as a single unified accent, or a <a href="/wiki/Standard_English" title="Standard English">standardized form of English</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982118_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982118-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELabovAshBoberg2006263_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELabovAshBoberg2006263-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>—except perhaps as used by  <a href="/wiki/Television_network" title="Television network">television networks</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">mass media</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982470_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982470-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Today, the term is understood to refer to a continuum of American speech, with some slight internal variation,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982118_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982118-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> but otherwise characterized by the absence of "<a href="/wiki/Markedness" title="Markedness">marked</a>" pronunciation features: those perceived by Americans as strongly indicative of a fellow American speaker's regional origin, ethnicity, or socioeconomic status. Despite confusion arising from the evolving definition and vagueness of the term "General American" and its consequent rejection by some linguists,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014129_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014129-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> the term persists mainly as a reference point to compare a baseline "typical" American English accent with other <a href="/wiki/World_Englishes" title="World Englishes">Englishes around the world</a> (for instance, see: <a href="/wiki/Comparison_of_General_American_and_Received_Pronunciation" title="Comparison of General American and Received Pronunciation">Comparison of General American and Received Pronunciation</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982118_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982118-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_American_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_origins">Regional origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_American_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Regional origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though General American accents are not commonly perceived as associated with any region, their <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">sound system</a> does have traceable regional origins: specifically, the English of the non-coastal Northeastern United States in the very early twentieth century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELabovAshBoberg2006190_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELabovAshBoberg2006190-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> This includes western <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> and the area to its immediate west, settled by members of the same dialect community:<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBonfiglio200243_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBonfiglio200243-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> interior <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Upstate_New_York" title="Upstate New York">upstate New York</a>, and the adjacent <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">"Midwest"</a> or <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes_region" title="Great Lakes region">Great Lakes region</a>. However, since the early to middle twentieth century,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982470_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982470-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> deviance away from General American sounds started occurring, and may be ongoing, in the eastern Great Lakes region due to its <a href="/wiki/Northern_Cities_Vowel_Shift" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Cities Vowel Shift">Northern Cities Vowel Shift</a> (NCVS) towards a unique <a href="/wiki/Inland_North" class="mw-redirect" title="Inland North">Inland Northern accent</a> (often now associated with the region's urban centers, like Chicago and Detroit) and in the western Great Lakes region towards a unique <a href="/wiki/North_Central_American_English" class="mw-redirect" title="North Central American English">North Central accent</a> (often associated with Minnesota, Wisconsin, and North Dakota).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Popularity">Popularity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_American_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Popularity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Linguists have proposed multiple factors contributing to the popularity of a <a href="/wiki/Rhoticity_in_English" title="Rhoticity in English">rhotic</a> "General American" class of accents throughout the United States. Most factors focus on the first half of the twentieth century, though a basic General American pronunciation system may have existed even before the twentieth century, since most American English dialects have diverged very little from each other anyway, when compared to dialects of single languages in other countries where there has been more time for <a href="/wiki/Language_change" title="Language change">language change</a> (such as the <a href="/wiki/English_language_in_England" title="English language in England">English dialects of England</a> or <a href="/wiki/German_dialects" title="German dialects">German dialects of Germany</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One factor fueling General American's popularity was the major demographic change of twentieth-century American society: increased <a href="/wiki/Suburbanization" title="Suburbanization">suburbanization</a>, leading to less mingling of different social classes and less density and diversity of linguistic interactions. As a result, wealthier and higher-educated Americans' communications became more restricted to their own demographic. This, alongside their new marketplace that transcended regional boundaries (arising from the century's faster transportation methods), reinforced a widespread belief that highly educated Americans should not possess a regional accent.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKortmannSchneider2004260-262_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKortmannSchneider2004260-262-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> A General American sound, then, originated from both suburbanization and suppression of regional accent by highly educated Americans in formal settings. A second factor was a rise in immigration to the Great Lakes area (one native region of supposed "General American" speech) following the region's rapid industrialization period after the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, when this region's speakers went on to form a successful and highly mobile business elite, who traveled around the country in the mid-twentieth century, spreading the high status of their accents.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBonfiglio200269-70_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBonfiglio200269-70-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> A third factor is that various sociological (often race- and class-based) forces repelled socially-conscious Americans away from accents negatively associated with certain minority groups, such as <a href="/wiki/African_Americans" title="African Americans">African Americans</a> and poor white communities in the South and with Southern and Eastern European immigrant groups (for example, Jewish communities) in the coastal Northeast.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBonfiglio20024,_97-98_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBonfiglio20024,_97-98-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Instead, socially-conscious Americans settled upon accents more prestigiously associated with <a href="/wiki/White_Anglo-Saxon_Protestant" title="White Anglo-Saxon Protestant">White Anglo-Saxon Protestant</a> communities in the remainder of the country: namely, the West, the Midwest, and the non-coastal Northeast.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014123,_128-130_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVan_Riper2014123,_128-130-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kenyon, author of <i>American Pronunciation</i> (1924) and pronunciation editor for the second edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Webster%27s_Dictionary" title="Webster&#39;s Dictionary">Webster's New International Dictionary</a></i> (1934), was influential in codifying General American pronunciation standards in writing. He used as a basis his native Midwestern (specifically, northern Ohio) pronunciation.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESeabrook2005_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeabrook2005-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Kenyon's home state of Ohio, however, far from being an area of "non-regional" accents, has emerged now as a crossroads for at least four distinct regional accents, according to late twentieth-century research.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, Kenyon himself was vocally opposed to the notion of any superior variety of American speech.<sup id="cite_ref-Hampton_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hampton-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_media">In the media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_American_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In the media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>General American, like the British <a href="/wiki/Received_Pronunciation" title="Received Pronunciation">Received Pronunciation</a> (RP) and <a href="/wiki/Prestige_(sociolinguistics)" title="Prestige (sociolinguistics)">prestige accents</a> of many other societies, has never been the accent of the entire nation, and, unlike RP, does not constitute a homogeneous national standard. Starting in the 1930s, nationwide radio networks adopted non-coastal <a href="/wiki/Northern_American_English" title="Northern American English">Northern U.S.</a> rhotic pronunciations for their "General American" standard.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The entertainment industry similarly shifted from a <a href="/wiki/Rhoticity_in_English" title="Rhoticity in English">non-rhotic</a> standard to a rhotic one in the late 1940s, after the triumph of the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, with the patriotic incentive for a more wide-ranging and unpretentious "heartland variety" in television and radio.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>General American is thus sometimes associated with the speech of North American radio and television announcers, promoted as prestigious in their industry,<sup id="cite_ref-freshair1_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freshair1-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#Sound_recordings" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific time or range of times at which the material appears in audio-visual material (August 2019)">time&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-60minutes_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60minutes-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> where it is sometimes called "Broadcast English"<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> "Network English",<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982470_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982470-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cruttenden_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cruttenden-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Melchers_&amp;_Shaw_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Melchers_&amp;_Shaw-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> or "Network Standard".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKövecses200081–82_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKövecses200081–82-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Melchers_&amp;_Shaw_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Melchers_&amp;_Shaw-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Instructional classes in the United States that promise "<a href="/wiki/Accent_reduction" title="Accent reduction">accent reduction</a>", "accent modification", or "accent neutralization" usually attempt to teach General American patterns.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> A common experience among many American celebrities is having worked hard to lose their native regional accents in favor of a more mainstream General American sound,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> including television journalist <a href="/wiki/Linda_Ellerbee" title="Linda Ellerbee">Linda Ellerbee</a>, who stated that "in television you are not supposed to sound like you're <i>from</i> anywhere",<sup id="cite_ref-Burner_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burner-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> as well as political comedian <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Colbert" title="Stephen Colbert">Stephen Colbert</a>, who said he consciously avoided developing a <a href="/wiki/Southern_American_accent" class="mw-redirect" title="Southern American accent">Southern American accent</a> in response to media portrayals of Southerners as stupid and uneducated.<sup id="cite_ref-freshair1_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freshair1-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60minutes_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60minutes-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_American_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Phonology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Typical General American accent features (for example, in contrast to British English) include features that concern consonants, such as <a href="/wiki/Rhoticity_in_English" title="Rhoticity in English">rhoticity</a> (full pronunciation of all <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> sounds), <a href="/wiki/T-glottalization" title="T-glottalization">T-glottalization</a> (with <i>satin</i> pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈsæʔn̩]</span>, not <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈsætn̩]</span>), <a href="/wiki/Flapping" title="Flapping">T- and D-flapping</a> (with <i>metal</i> and <i>medal</i> pronounced the same, as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈmɛɾɫ̩]</span>), <a href="/wiki/Dark_L" class="mw-redirect" title="Dark L">L-velarization</a> (with <i>filling</i> pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈfɪɫɪŋ]</span>, not <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈfɪlɪŋ]</span>), as well as features that concern vowel sounds, such as various vowel mergers before <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> (so that, <i>Mary</i>, <i>marry</i>, and <i>merry</i> are all commonly <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">pronounced the same</a>), <a href="/wiki/Yod-dropping" class="mw-redirect" title="Yod-dropping">yod-dropping</a> (with <i>new</i> pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/nu/</span>, not <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/nju/</span>), raising of pre-voiceless <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/aɪ/</span> (with <i>price</i> and <i>bright</i> using a higher vowel sound than <i>prize</i> and <i>bride</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Weak_vowel_merger" class="mw-redirect" title="Weak vowel merger">weak vowel merger</a> (with <i>affected</i> and <i>effected</i> often pronounced the same), and at least one of the <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r920966791">.mw-parser-output span.smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}.mw-parser-output span.smallcaps-smaller{font-size:85%}</style><span class="smallcaps smallcaps-smaller">LOT</span> vowel mergers (the <a href="/wiki/Father%E2%80%93bother_merger" class="mw-redirect" title="Father–bother merger"><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r920966791"/><span class="smallcaps smallcaps-smaller">LOT</span>–<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r920966791"/><span class="smallcaps smallcaps-smaller">PALM</span> merger</a> is completed among virtually all Americans and the <a href="/wiki/Cot-caught_merger" class="mw-redirect" title="Cot-caught merger"><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r920966791"/><span class="smallcaps smallcaps-smaller">LOT</span>–<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r920966791"/><span class="smallcaps smallcaps-smaller">THOUGHT</span> merger</a> among nearly half). All of these phenomena are explained in further detail under <a href="/wiki/American_English#Phonology" title="American English">American English's phonology section</a>. The following provides all the General American consonant and vowel sounds.
</p>

<ul><li>Vowel length is not <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phonemic</a> in General American, and therefore vowels such as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/i/</span> are usually transcribed without the length mark. Phonetically, the vowels of GA are short <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɪ, i, ʊ, u, eɪ, oʊ, ɛ, ʌ, ɔ, æ, ɑ, aɪ, ɔɪ, aʊ]</span> when they precede the <a href="/wiki/Fortis_and_lenis" title="Fortis and lenis">fortis</a> consonants <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/p, t, k, tʃ, f, θ, s, ʃ/</span> within the same syllable and long <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɪˑ, iˑ, ʊˑ, uˑ, eˑɪ, oˑʊ, ɛˑ, ʌˑ, ɔˑ, æˑ, ɑˑ, aˑɪ, ɔˑɪ, aˑʊ]</span> elsewhere. (Listen to the minimal pair of <span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:General_American_kit_kid_minimal_pair.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" decoding="async" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a></span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e7/General_American_kit_kid_minimal_pair.ogg" class="internal" title="General American kit kid minimal pair.ogg"><i>kit</i> and <i>kid</i></a></span></span>) This applies to all vowels but the schwa <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ə/</span> (which is typically very short <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ə̆]</span>), so when e.g. <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/i/</span> is realized as a diphthong <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɪ̝i]</span> it has the same allophones as the other diphthongs, whereas the sequence <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɜr/</span> (which corresponds to the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r920966791"/><span class="smallcaps smallcaps-smaller">NURSE</span> vowel <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɜː/</span> in RP) has the same allophones as phonemic monophthongs: short <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɚ]</span> before fortis consonants and long <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɚˑ]</span> elsewhere. The short <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɚ]</span> is also used for the sequence <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ər/</span> (the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r920966791"/><span class="smallcaps smallcaps-smaller">LETTER</span> vowel). All unstressed vowels are also shorter than the stressed ones, and the more unstressed syllables follow a stressed one, the shorter it is, so that <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/i/</span> in <i>l<u>ea</u>d</i> is noticeably longer than in <i>l<u>ea</u>dership</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982120,_480–481_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982120,_480–481-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells2008_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells2008-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> (See <a href="/wiki/Stress_and_vowel_reduction_in_English" title="Stress and vowel reduction in English">Stress and vowel reduction in English</a>.)</li>
<li><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/i, u, eɪ, oʊ, ɑ/</span> are considered to compose a <a href="/wiki/Natural_class" title="Natural class">natural class</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tenseness" title="Tenseness">tense</a> monophthongs in General American, especially for speakers with the <a href="/wiki/Cot%E2%80%93caught_merger" title="Cot–caught merger"><i>cot–caught</i> merger</a>. The class manifests in how GA speakers treat loanwords, as in the majority of cases stressed syllables of foreign words are assigned one of these five vowels, regardless of whether the original pronunciation has a tense or a lax vowel. An example of that is the surname of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Mann" title="Thomas Mann">Thomas Mann</a>, which is pronounced with the tense <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɑ/</span> rather than lax <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/æ/</span> (as in RP, which mirrors the German pronunciation <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/man/</span>, which also has a lax vowel).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELindsey1990_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELindsey1990-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> All of the tense vowels except <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɑ/</span> can have either monophthongal or diphthongal pronunciations (i.e. <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[<a href="/wiki/Close_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close front unrounded vowel">i</a>, <a href="/wiki/Close_back_rounded_vowel" title="Close back rounded vowel">u</a> ~ <a href="/wiki/Close_central_rounded_vowel" title="Close central rounded vowel">ʉ</a>, <a href="/wiki/Close-mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close-mid front unrounded vowel">e</a>, <a href="/wiki/Close-mid_back_rounded_vowel" title="Close-mid back rounded vowel">o</a>]</span> vs <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɪ̝i, ʊ̝u ~ ʊ̝̈ʉ, eɪ, oʊ]</span>). The diphthongs are the most usual realizations of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/eɪ/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/oʊ/</span>, which is reflected in the way they are transcribed. In the case of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/i/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/u/</span>, the monophthongal pronunciations are in <a href="/wiki/Free_variation" title="Free variation">free variation</a> with diphthongs. Even the diphthongal pronunciations themselves vary between the very narrow (i.e. <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɪ̝i, ʊ̝u ~ ʊ̝̈ʉ]</span>) and somewhat wider (i.e. <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɪi ~ ɪ̈i, ʊu ~ ʊ̈ʉ]</span>), with the former being more common. As indicated in above phonetic transcriptions, the backness of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/u/</span> varies from fairly back to central; the same applies to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɑ/</span>, which is realized as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[<a href="/wiki/Open_back_unrounded_vowel" title="Open back unrounded vowel">ɑ</a> ~ <a href="/wiki/Open_central_unrounded_vowel" title="Open central unrounded vowel">ä</a>]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982476,_487_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982476,_487-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Before the dark <span style="font-family: &#39;Georgia Pro&#39;, Georgia, &#39;DejaVu Serif&#39;, Times, &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, serif;"><i>l</i></span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/i, u/</span> and sometimes also <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/eɪ, oʊ/</span> are realized as centering diphthongs <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[iə, uə, eə, oə]</span> or even as disyllabic sequences <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[i.jə, u.wə, e.jə, o.wə]</span>. Therefore, words such as <i>peel</i> and <i>fool</i> and sometimes also <i>rail</i> and <i>role</i> are pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈpʰiəɫ ~ ˈpʰi.jəɫ]</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈfuəɫ ~ ˈfu.wəɫ]</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈɹeəɫ ~ ˈɹe.jəɫ]</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈɹoəɫ ~ ˈɹo.wəɫ]</span>. This can even happen word-internally before another morpheme, as in <i>peeling</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈpʰiəɫɪŋ ~ ˈpʰi.jəɫɪŋ]</span> and <i>fooling</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈfuəɫɪŋ ~ ˈfu.wəɫɪŋ]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982487_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982487-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>When prosodically salient, the lax vowels <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɪ, ʊ, ɛ, ʌ, æ/</span> tend to be realized as centering diphthongs <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɪə, ʊə, ɛə, ʌə, æə]</span> instead of the more usual long monophthongs <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɪˑ, ʊˑ, ɛˑ, ʌˑ, æˑ]</span> when they precede a word-final voiced consonant, so that the word <i>good</i> in the sentence <i>that's very good!</i> tends to be pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɡʊəd]</span> instead of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɡʊˑd]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982485_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982485-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>General American does not have the opposition between <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɜr/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ər/</span>, which are both rendered <span class="IPA nowrap" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/R-colored_vowel" title="R-colored vowel">ɚ</a>&#93;</span> <span class="nowrap" style="font-size:85%">(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:En-us-er.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" therefore, the vowels in <i>further</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈfɜrðər/</span> are typically realized with the same segmental quality as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈfɚðɚ]</span> <span style="font-size:85%;">(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:En-us-further.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" makes <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">homophonous</a> the words <i>forward</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈfɔrwərd/</span> and <i>foreword</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈfɔrwɜrd/</span> as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈfɔɹwɚd]</span>, which are distinguished in Received Pronunciation as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈfɔːwəd]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈfɔːwɜːd]</span>, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982121_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982121-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Therefore, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɜ/</span> is not a true phoneme in General American but merely a different notation of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ə/</span> preserved for when this phoneme precedes <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> and is stressed—a convention adopted in literature to facilitate comparisons with other accents.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982480–1_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982480–1-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> What is historically <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʌr/</span>, as in <i>hurry</i>, is also pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɚ]</span> <span style="font-size:85%;">(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:En-us-er.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" decoding="async" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a></span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e1/En-us-er.ogg" class="internal" title="En-us-er.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</span>, so <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʌ/</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɜ/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ə/</span> are all <a href="/wiki/Phonemic_merger" class="mw-redirect" title="Phonemic merger">neutralized</a> before <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982480–1_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982480–1-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, some analyze <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʌ/</span> as an allophone of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ə/</span> that surfaces when stressed, so <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʌ/</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɜ/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ə/</span> may be considered to be in <a href="/wiki/Complementary_distribution" title="Complementary distribution">complementary distribution</a> and thus comprising one phoneme.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982132_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982132-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The phonetic quality of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʌ/</span> is typically somewhat advanced <span class="IPA nowrap" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Open-mid_back_unrounded_vowel" title="Open-mid back unrounded vowel">ʌ̟</a>&#93;</span>, which is still somewhat more back than the corresponding RP realization <span class="IPA nowrap" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Near-open_central_vowel" title="Near-open central vowel">ɐ</a>&#93;</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells1982485_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells1982485-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERocaJohnson1999190_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERocaJohnson1999190-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The 2006 <i><a href="/wiki/Atlas_of_North_American_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlas of North American English">Atlas of North American English</a></i> surmises that "if one were to recognize a type of <a href="/wiki/North_American_English" title="North American English">North American English</a> to be called 'General American'" according to data measurements of vowel pronunciations, "it would be the configuration formed by these three" dialect regions: <a href="/wiki/Canadian_English#Standard_Canadian_English" title="Canadian English">Canada</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Western_American_English" title="Western American English">American West</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Midland_American_English" title="Midland American English">American Midland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> The following charts (as well as the one above) present the vowels that these three dialects encompass as a perceived General American sound system.

"General American" has often been considered to be the relatively unmarked speech of "the Midwest", a vague designation for anywhere in the vast midsection of the country from Ohio west to Nebraska, and from the Canadian border as far south as Missouri or Kansas. No historical justification for this term exists, and neither do present circumstances support its use... [I]t implies that there is some exemplary state of American English from which other varieties deviate. On the contrary, [it] can best be characterized as what is left over after speakers suppress the regional and social features that have risen to salience and become noticeable.'</span>
