<p><i><b>A Latin Dictionary</b></i> (or <i><b>Harpers' Latin Dictionary</b></i>, often referred to as <b>Lewis and Short</b> or <b>L&amp;S</b>) is a popular English-language <a href="/wiki/Lexicography" title="Lexicography">lexicographical</a> work of the <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin language</a>, published by <a href="/wiki/Harper_(publisher)" title="Harper (publisher)">Harper and Brothers</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a> in 1879 and printed simultaneously in the United Kingdom by <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University_Press" title="Oxford University Press">Oxford University Press</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Latin_Dictionary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The work is usually referred to as Lewis and Short after the names of its editors, <a href="/wiki/Charlton_T._Lewis" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlton T. Lewis">Charlton T. Lewis</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Short&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Charles Short (page does not exist)">Charles Short</a>. It was derived from the 1850 English translation by <a href="/wiki/Ethan_Allen_Andrews_(lexicographer)" title="Ethan Allen Andrews (lexicographer)">Ethan Allen Andrews</a> of an earlier Latin-German dictionary, <i>Wörterbuch der Lateinischen Sprache</i>, by the German <a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">philologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Freund" title="Wilhelm Freund">Wilhelm Freund</a>, in turn based on <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Johann_Gerhard_Scheller" title="Immanuel Johann Gerhard Scheller">I.J.G. Scheller</a>’s Latin–German dictionary of 1783. The Andrews translation was partially revised by Freund himself, then by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Drisler" title="Henry Drisler">Henry Drisler</a>, and was finally edited by Short and Lewis.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The division of labour between the two editors was remarkably unequal. Short, a very thorough but slow worker, produced material for the letters A through C, but B and C were lost by Harpers, meaning that his work now only appears in the letter A (216 pages), while Lewis, who worked in the time he could spare from his law practice, was solely responsible for the entries beginning with the letters B through Z (1803 pages).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 1890 Lewis published a heavily abridged version of the dictionary, entitled <i>An Elementary Latin Dictionary,</i> for the use of students. Sometimes called the <i>Elementary Lewis,</i> it is still in print today.
</p><p>The adoption of the book by Oxford University Press was the result of the failure of its own project to create a new Latin-English dictionary in 1875. <a href="/wiki/Henry_Nettleship" title="Henry Nettleship">Henry Nettleship</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Eyton_Bickersteth_Mayor" class="mw-redirect" title="John Eyton Bickersteth Mayor">John Mayor</a> had been commissioned to produce a new Latin dictionary based on a fresh reading of the sources, but after Mayor withdrew from the project, Nettleship was unable to complete it on his own; he eventually published his research as notes on Lewis and Short.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> While the Press had earlier published John Riddle’s 1835 translation of Scheller’s Latin–German dictionary, this was a much more expensive book. The Press thus adopted <i>Harpers' Latin Dictionary</i> as a stopgap measure, paying Harper and Brothers 10 per cent royalties.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Harper and Brothers sold its rights to the <a href="/wiki/American_Book_Company_(1890)" title="American Book Company (1890)">American Book Company</a> in 1899, shortly before its bankruptcy.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From the time of its publication, many scholars have criticized the dictionary for its errors and inconsistencies.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  Because of various circumstances, however, no replacement was attempted until 1933, with the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_Latin_Dictionary" title="Oxford Latin Dictionary">Oxford Latin Dictionary</a></i>, which was completed in 1983.
</p><p>The dictionary's <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus:text:1999.04.0059">full text</a> is available on-line from the <a href="/wiki/Perseus_Project" title="Perseus Project">Perseus Project</a>. Lewis and Short is also available for offline consultation, by means of various applications.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_other_dictionaries">Comparison with other dictionaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Latin_Dictionary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Comparison with other dictionaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Among classicists, Lewis and Short has been largely superseded by the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_Latin_Dictionary" title="Oxford Latin Dictionary">Oxford Latin Dictionary</a></i>, called the <i><b>OLD</b></i> for short. Lewis and Short incorporated material from existing Latin dictionaries; the <i>OLD</i>, by contrast, started from scratch, following procedures similar to those of the well-regarded <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i>. Thanks to the increased availability of modern editions, the <i>OLD</i> editors had access to a larger variety of classical works.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Although classicists still consult Lewis and Short, they tend to prefer the <i>OLD</i>.
</p><p>On the other hand, Lewis and Short remains a standard reference work for medievalists, renaissance specialists, and early modernists, as the dictionary covers Late and Medieval Latin, if somewhat inconsistently. The <i>OLD</i>, when used on its own, rarely meets their needs, since it was decided early in the <i>OLD'</i>s planning that the work would not encompass works written later than AD 200. A few exceptions were made for especially important texts from the late classical period, such as <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Augustine</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/City_of_God_(book)" class="mw-redirect" title="City of God (book)">De Civitate Dei</a></i>, but for periods later than that the <i>OLD</i> is considerably less useful. The <i><a href="/wiki/Dictionary_of_Medieval_Latin_from_British_Sources" title="Dictionary of Medieval Latin from British Sources">Dictionary of Medieval Latin from British Sources</a></i> supplements the <i>OLD</i> for medieval usage of Latin words.
</p><p>In cases where Lewis and Short do not answer a medieval usage question, J. F. Niermeyer's <i>Mediae Latinitatis Lexicon Minus</i> often supplies an answer. The <i>Lexicon Minus</i> was completed in 1976 by C. van de Kieft after Niermeyer's death, and has since become a standard reference work.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> More recent editions of the <i>Lexicon Minus</i> have corrections and expansions; also, in later editions all words are defined in English, French and German, making it of greater international importance than Lewis and Short. The <i>Glossarium ad scriptores mediae et infimae latinitatis</i> completed in 1678 by <a href="/wiki/Charles_du_Fresne,_sieur_du_Cange" title="Charles du Fresne, sieur du Cange">Charles du Fresne</a> (commonly referred to as <i><b>Du Cange</b></i> after the author's title, the <i>Sieur du Cange</i>) is now less frequently used, as Niermeyer's <i>Lexicon Minus</i> incorporates much of its information.
</p><p>On occasion people confuse Lewis and Short (or <b>L&amp;S</b>) with <b>Liddell and Scott</b>, its <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> counterpart, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/A_Greek%E2%80%93English_Lexicon" title="A Greek–English Lexicon">A Greek–English Lexicon</a></i>. The 1945 and later editions of Liddell and Scott are commonly referred to by the abbreviation <i><b>LSJ</b></i> after the names of its editors <a href="/wiki/Henry_Liddell" title="Henry Liddell">Liddell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Scott_(philologist)" title="Robert Scott (philologist)">Scott</a> and the editor of the 1945 revision, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Stuart_Jones" title="Henry Stuart Jones">Jones</a>.
</p>
