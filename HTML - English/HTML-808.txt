		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Marston_(poet)" class="extiw" title="w:John Marston (poet)">John Marston</a></b> (<a href="/wiki/1576" class="mw-disambig" title="1576">1576</a> – <a href="/wiki/June_25" title="June 25">June 25</a>, <a href="/wiki/1634" class="mw-disambig" title="1634">1634</a>) was an English poet, playwright and satirist.  In a short and stormy literary career two of his books were burned by order of the Archbishop of Canterbury, and he himself suffered imprisonment on account of a third.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quotes">Quotes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Marston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Quotes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Foul canker of fair virtuous action,<br />Vile blaster of the freshest blooms on earth,<br />Envys abhorrèd child, Detraction,<br />I here expose, to thy all-tainting breath,<br />The issue of my brain: snarl, rail, bark, bite,<br />Know that my spirit scorns Detraction's spite.
<ul><li>"To Detraction I Present My Poesy", line 1, from <i>The Scourge of  Villainy</i> (1598-99).</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>Who winks and shuts his apprehension up.
<ul><li><i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonio%27s_Revenge" class="extiw" title="w:Antonio&#39;s Revenge">Antonio's Revenge</a></i>, Prologue, line 17. (1600)</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>For see, the dapple-grey coursers of the morn<br />Beat up the light with their bright silver hoofs,<br />And chase it through the sky.
<ul><li><i>Antonio's Revenge</i>, Act I, sc. i.</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>Sing of the nature of women, and then the song shall be surely full of variety; old crotchets and most sweet closes. It shall be humorous, grave, fantastic, amorous, melancholy, sprightly, one in all, and all in one.
<ul><li><i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Malcontent" class="extiw" title="w:The Malcontent">The Malcontent</a></i>, Act III, sc. iv. (1603-04)</li></ul></li></ul>
<ul><li>Speak, speak, let terror strike slaves mute,<br />Much danger makes great hearts most resolute.
<ul><li><i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Wonder_of_Women" class="extiw" title="w:The Wonder of Women">The Wonder of Women, or The Tragedy of Sophonisba</a></i>, Act II, sc. ii. (1606)</li></ul></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Marston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Marston is a writer of great merit, who rose to tragedy from the ground of comedy, and whose <i>forte</i> was not sympathy, either with the stronger or softer emotions, but an impatient scorn and bitter indignation against the vices and follies of men, which vented itself either in comic irony or in lofty invective.  He was properly a satirist.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/William_Hazlitt" title="William Hazlitt">William Hazlitt</a> <i>Lectures on the Dramatic Literature of the Age of Elizabeth</i> ([1820] 1845) Lecture 3, p. 57.</li></ul></li></ul>
