<p><b>John Marston</b> (baptised 7 October 1576 – 25 June 1634) was an English poet, playwright and satirist during the late <a href="/wiki/Elizabethan_era" title="Elizabethan era">Elizabethan</a> and early <a href="/wiki/Jacobean_era" title="Jacobean era">Jacobean</a> periods. His career as a writer lasted only a decade. His work is remembered for its energetic and often obscure style, its contributions to the development of a distinctively Jacobean style in poetry, and its idiosyncratic vocabulary.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Marston_(poet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Marston was born to John and Maria Marston <i>née</i> Guarsi, and baptised October 7, 1576 at <a href="/wiki/Wardington" title="Wardington">Wardington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a>. His father was an eminent lawyer of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Temple" title="Middle Temple">Middle Temple</a> who first argued in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and then became the counsel to <a href="/wiki/Coventry" title="Coventry">Coventry</a> and ultimately its steward. John Marston entered <a href="/wiki/Brasenose_College,_Oxford" title="Brasenose College, Oxford">Brasenose College, Oxford</a> in 1592 and received his BA in 1594. By 1595, he was in London, living in the Middle Temple, where he had been admitted a member three years previously.  He had an interest in poetry and play writing, although his father's will of 1599 expresses the hope that he would give up such vanities. He married Mary Wilkes in 1605, daughter of the Reverend William Wilkes, one of <a href="/wiki/King_James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="King James I of England">King James's</a> <a href="/wiki/Chaplain" title="Chaplain">chaplains</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Marston_(poet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Marston's brief career in literature began with a foray into the then-fashionable genres of erotic <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epyllion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satire</a>. In 1598, he published <i>The Metamorphosis of Pigmalion's Image and Certaine Satyres</i>, a book of poetry in imitation of, on the one hand, <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>, and, on the other, the <a href="/wiki/Satires_of_Juvenal" class="mw-redirect" title="Satires of Juvenal">Satires of Juvenal</a>.  He also published another book of satires, <i>The Scourge of Villanie</i>, in 1598. (Marston issued these satires under the pseudonym "W. Kinsayder.")  The satire in these books is even more savage and misanthropic than was typical for the decade's satirists. Marston's style is, moreover, in places contorted to the point of unintelligibility: he believed that satire should be rough and obscure, perhaps because he thought (as did many other writers of the time) that the term 'satire' was derived from the Greek '<a href="/wiki/Satyr_play" title="Satyr play">satyr plays</a>'. Marston seems to have been enraged by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Hall_(English_Bishop_and_satyrist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph Hall (English Bishop and satyrist)">Joseph Hall</a>'s claim to be the first satirist in English; Hall comes in for some indirect <a href="/wiki/Flyting" title="Flyting">flyting</a> in at least one of the satires. Some see <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Thersites" title="Thersites">Thersites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iago" title="Iago">Iago</a>, as well as the mad speeches of <a href="/wiki/King_Lear" title="King Lear">King Lear</a> as influenced by <i>The Scourge of Villanie.</i>  Marston had, however, arrived on the literary scene as the fad for verse satire was to be checked by censors. The <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Whitgift" title="John Whitgift">John Whitgift</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_London" title="Bishop of London">Bishop of London</a> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bancroft" title="Richard Bancroft">Richard Bancroft</a> banned the <i>Scourge</i> and had it publicly burned, along with copies of works by other satirists, on 4 June 1599.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Playwriting_with_Henslowe">Playwriting with Henslowe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Marston_(poet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Playwriting with Henslowe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 1599, John Marston began to work for <a href="/wiki/Philip_Henslowe" title="Philip Henslowe">Philip Henslowe</a> as a playwright. Following the work of O. J. Campbell, it has commonly been thought that Marston turned to the theatre in response to the <a href="/wiki/Bishops%27_Ban_of_1599" title="Bishops&#39; Ban of 1599">Bishops' Ban of 1599</a>; more recent scholars have noted that the ban was not enforced with great rigor and might not have intimidated prospective satirists at all.  At any rate, Marston proved a good match for the stage—not the public stage of Henslowe, but the "private"  playhouses where <a href="/wiki/Boy_player" title="Boy player">boy players</a> performed racy dramas for an audience of city gallants and young members of the <a href="/wiki/Inns_of_Court" title="Inns of Court">Inns of Court</a>. Traditionally, though without strong external attribution, <i>Histriomastix</i> has been regarded as his first play; performed by either the Children of Paul's or the students of the Middle Temple in around <a href="/wiki/1599_in_literature" title="1599 in literature">1599</a>, it appears to have sparked the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Theatres" title="War of the Theatres">War of the Theatres</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Literary_feud" title="Literary feud">literary feud</a> between Marston, Jonson and Dekker that took place between around 1599 and 1602.  In c. 1600, Marston wrote <i>Jack Drum's Entertainment</i> and <i>Antonio and Mellida</i>, and in 1601 he wrote <i>Antonio's Revenge</i>, a sequel to the latter play; all three were performed by the company at Paul's.  In 1601, he contributed poems to <a href="/wiki/Robert_Chester_(poet)" title="Robert Chester (poet)">Robert Chester</a>'s <i>Love's Martyr.</i>  For Henslowe, he may have collaborated with Dekker, Day, and Haughton on <i><a href="/wiki/Lust%27s_Dominion" title="Lust&#39;s Dominion">Lust's Dominion</a></i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feud_with_Jonson">Feud with Jonson</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Marston_(poet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Feud with Jonson">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ben_Jonson_by_George_Vertue_1730_(cropped).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Ben_Jonson_by_George_Vertue_1730_%28cropped%29.jpg/220px-Ben_Jonson_by_George_Vertue_1730_%28cropped%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Ben_Jonson_by_George_Vertue_1730_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-Ben_Jonson_by_George_Vertue_1730_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Ben_Jonson_by_George_Vertue_1730_%28cropped%29.jpg/440px-Ben_Jonson_by_George_Vertue_1730_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="776" data-file-height="1032" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ben_Jonson_by_George_Vertue_1730_(cropped).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ben_Jonson" title="Ben Jonson">Ben Jonson</a>: rival, co-author, <a href="/wiki/Frenemy" title="Frenemy">frenemy</a></div></div></div>
<p>By 1601, he was well known in London literary circles, particularly in his role as enemy to the equally pugnacious <a href="/wiki/Ben_Jonson" title="Ben Jonson">Ben Jonson</a>. Jonson, who reported to <a href="/wiki/William_Drummond_of_Hawthornden" title="William Drummond of Hawthornden">Drummond</a> that Marston had accused him of sexual profligacy, satirized Marston as Clove in <i><a href="/wiki/Every_Man_Out_of_His_Humour" class="mw-redirect" title="Every Man Out of His Humour">Every Man Out of His Humour</a></i>, as Crispinus in <i>Poetaster</i>, and as Hedon in <i>Cynthia's Revels</i>. Jonson criticised Marston for being a false poet, a vain, careless writer who plagiarised the works of others and whose own works were marked by bizarre diction and ugly neologisms. For his part, Marston may have satirized Jonson as the complacent, arrogant critic Brabant Senior in <i>Jack Drum's Entertainment</i> and as the envious, misanthropic playwright and satirist Lampatho Doria in <i><a href="/wiki/What_You_Will" title="What You Will">What You Will</a></i>.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Parnassus_plays" title="Parnassus plays">The Return from Parnassus (II)</a></i>, a satirical play performed at <a href="/wiki/St._John%27s_College,_Cambridge" class="mw-redirect" title="St. John&#39;s College, Cambridge">St. John's College, Cambridge</a> in 1601 and 1602, characterised Marston as a poet whose writings see him "pissing against the world".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>If Jonson can be trusted, the animosity between himself and Marston went beyond the literary. He claimed to have beaten Marston and taken his pistol. However, the two playwrights were reconciled soon after the so-called War; Marston wrote a prefatory poem for Jonson's <i>Sejanus</i> in 1605 and dedicated <i>The Malcontent</i> to Jonson. Yet in <a href="/wiki/1607_in_literature" title="1607 in literature">1607</a>, he criticized Jonson for being too pedantic to make allowances for his audience or the needs of aesthetics.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blackfriars">Blackfriars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Marston_(poet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Blackfriars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Outside of these tensions, Marston's career continued to prosper.  In 1603, he became a shareholder in the Children of Blackfriars company, at that time known for steadily pushing the allowable limits of personal satire, violence, and lewdness on stage.  He wrote and produced two plays with the company.  The first was <i>The Malcontent</i> in 1603; this satiric tragicomedy is Marston's most famous play. This work was originally written for the children at Blackfriars, and was later taken over (perhaps stolen) by the Kings' Men at the Globe, with additions by John Webster and (perhaps) Marston himself.
</p>

<p>Marston's second play for the Blackfriars children was <i>The Dutch Courtesan</i>, a satire on lust and hypocrisy, in 1604-5.  In 1605, he worked with <a href="/wiki/George_Chapman" title="George Chapman">George Chapman</a> and Ben Jonson on <i><a href="/wiki/Eastward_Ho" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastward Ho">Eastward Ho</a>,</i> a satire of popular taste and the vain imaginings of wealth to be found in <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>.  Chapman and Jonson were arrested for, according to Jonson, a few clauses that offended the Scots, but Marston escaped any imprisonment.  The actual cause of arrest and details of the brief detainment are not certainly known; in the event, charges were dropped.
</p><p>In 1606, Marston seems to have offended and then soothed King James.  First, in <i>Parasitaster, or, The Fawn,</i> he satirized the king specifically.  However, in the summer of that year, he put on a production of <i>The Dutch Courtesan</i> for the King of <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>'s visit, with a <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> verse on King James that was presented by hand to the king.  Finally, in 1607, he wrote <i>The Entertainment at Ashby,</i> a <a href="/wiki/Masque" title="Masque">masque</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Huntingdon" title="Earl of Huntingdon">Earl of Huntingdon</a>.  At that point, he stopped his dramatic career altogether, selling his shares in the company of Blackfriars. His departure from the literary scene may have been because of another play, now lost, which offended the king. It seems that the French ambassador complained to King James about the disrespectful treatment of the French court in plays by Chapman performed at Blackfriars. To strengthen his case he added that another play had been performed in which James himself was depicted drunk. Incensed, James suspended performances at Blackfriars and had Marston imprisoned. This suggests that he was the author of the offending play.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrote2002171_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrote2002171-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Marston_(poet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the end of his literary career, he moved into his father-in-law's house and began studying <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>.  In 1609, he became a reader at the <a href="/wiki/Bodleian_library" class="mw-redirect" title="Bodleian library">Bodleian library</a> at <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>, was made a <a href="/wiki/Deacon" title="Deacon">deacon</a> on 24 September and a <a href="/wiki/Priest" title="Priest">priest</a> on 24 December 1609.  Contemporary authors were bemused or surprised by Marston's change of career, with several of them commenting on its seeming abruptness.  In October 1616, Marston was assigned the living of <a href="/wiki/Christchurch,_Hampshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Christchurch, Hampshire">Christchurch, Hampshire</a>. He died on 24 June 1634, aged 57, in London and was buried in the <a href="/wiki/Temple_Church" title="Temple Church">Middle Temple Church</a>.
</p><p>Tombs at that time often started with the formulaic "<i>Memoriae Sacrum</i>" ("Sacred to the memory") followed by the name of the tomb's occupant and an account of their achievements even though such words are <a href="/wiki/Hubris" title="Hubris">hubristic</a> and a contradiction to the Christian virtues of modesty. According to <a href="/wiki/Anthony_%C3%A0_Wood" class="mw-redirect" title="Anthony à Wood">Anthony à Wood</a> John Marston's tomb stone bore the legend "<i>Oblivioni Sacrum</i>" ("Sacred to Oblivion"), which was probably composed by Marston, and, according to Joshua Scodel, the short "epitaph is thus both self-abasing and witty in its novel inversion of tradition".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEScodel199157_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEScodel199157-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception_and_criticism">Reception and criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Marston_(poet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reception and criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Marston's reputation has varied widely, like that of most of the minor Renaissance dramatists. Both <i>The Malcontent</i> and <i>The Dutch Courtesan</i> remained on stage in altered forms through the <a href="/wiki/English_Restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="English Restoration">Restoration</a>. The subplot of the latter was converted to a <a href="/wiki/Droll" title="Droll">droll</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_England" title="Commonwealth of England">Commonwealth</a>; after the Stuart Restoration, either <a href="/wiki/Aphra_Behn" title="Aphra Behn">Aphra Behn</a> or <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Betterton" title="Thomas Betterton">Thomas Betterton</a> updated the main plot for <i>The Revenge, or The Match in Newgate</i>, although this adaptation makes the play both more sentimental and less morally complex. <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Langbaine" title="Gerard Langbaine">Gerard Langbaine</a> makes a laudatory but superficial comment about Marston in his survey of English dramatic poets.
</p><p>After the Restoration, Marston's works were largely reduced to the status of a curiosity of literary history. The general resemblance of <i>Antonio's Revenge</i> to <i><a href="/wiki/Hamlet" title="Hamlet">Hamlet</a></i> and Marston's role in the war of the poets ensured that his plays would receive some scholarly attention, but they were not performed and were not even widely read. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Warton" title="Thomas Warton">Thomas Warton</a> preferred Marston's satires to Bishop Hall's; in the next century, however, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hallam" title="Henry Hallam">Henry Hallam</a> reversed this judgment.  <a href="/wiki/William_Gifford" title="William Gifford">William Gifford</a>, perhaps the eighteenth century's most devoted reader of Jonson, called Marston "the most scurrilous, filthy and obscene writer of his time".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic</a> movement in English literature resuscitated Marston's reputation, albeit unevenly.  In his lectures, <a href="/wiki/William_Hazlitt" title="William Hazlitt">William Hazlitt</a> praised Marston's genius for satire; however, if the romantic critics and their successors were willing to grant Marston's best work a place among the great accomplishments of the period, they remained aware of his inconsistency, what <a href="/wiki/Algernon_Charles_Swinburne" title="Algernon Charles Swinburne">Swinburne</a> in a later generation called his "uneven and irregular demesne".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In the twentieth century, however, a few critics were willing to consider Marston as a writer who was very much in control of the world he creates. <a href="/wiki/T._S._Eliot" title="T. S. Eliot">T. S. Eliot</a> saw that this "irregular demesne" was a part of Marston's world and declared that "It is … by giving us the sense of something behind, more real than any of the personages and their action, that Marston establishes himself among the writers of genius".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  Marston's tragic style is Senecan and although his characters may appear, on Eliot's own admission, "lifeless", they are instead used as types to convey their "theoretical implications".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Eliot in particular admired <i>Sophonisba</i> and saw how Marston's plays, with their apparently stylised characters and bitter portrayal of a world where virtue and honour only arouse "dangerous envy" (<i>Sophonisba</i>; Act 1, scene 1, line 45) in those around them, actually bring to life "a pattern behind the pattern into which the characters deliberately involve themselves: the kind of pattern which we perceive in our own lives only at rare moments of inattention and detachment".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
