<p><b>William Jacob "Will" Cuppy</b> (August 23, 1884 – September 19, 1949) was an American <a href="/wiki/Humorist" title="Humorist">humorist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Literary_criticism" title="Literary criticism">literary critic</a>, known for his <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satirical</a> books about <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Historical_figure" title="Historical figure">historical figures</a>. Writing funny but factual magazine articles was Cuppy's real talent. He enjoyed a brief success in 1933 with a humorous talk show on <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> radio with actress and gourmet cook Jeanne Owen, but failed to capitalize on it. His best-known work, a satire on history called <i>The Decline and Fall of Practically Everybody</i>, was unfinished when he died. It was completed and published in 1950 by his friend Fred Feldkamp, who sifted through nearly 15,000 of Cuppy's carefully filed note cards to get the book into print. Feldkamp also edited a second posthumous volume, a comic almanac titled <i>How to Get from January to December</i>, that appeared in 1951.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Will_Cuppy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cuppy was born in <a href="/wiki/Auburn,_Indiana" title="Auburn, Indiana">Auburn, Indiana</a>. He was named "Will" in memory of an older brother of his father's who died of wounds he received as a <a href="/wiki/Union_(American_Civil_War)" title="Union (American Civil War)">Union</a> officer at the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a> <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fort_Donelson" title="Battle of Fort Donelson">Battle of Fort Donelson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Cuppy's father, Thomas Jefferson Cuppy (1844–1912), was at different times a grain dealer, a seller of <a href="/wiki/List_of_farm_implements" class="mw-redirect" title="List of farm implements">farm implements</a> and a lumber buyer for the <a href="/wiki/Butler_Branch_(Indiana)" title="Butler Branch (Indiana)">Eel River branch</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Wabash_Railroad" title="Wabash Railroad">Wabash Railroad</a>. His mother, Frances Stahl Cuppy (1855–1927), was a <a href="/wiki/Sewing" title="Sewing">seamstress</a> and worked in a small shop located next to the family home in Auburn.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  Young Cuppy spent summers at a farm belonging to his grandmother, Sarah Collins Cuppy (1813–1900), on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Eel_River_(Wabash_River)" class="mw-redirect" title="Eel River (Wabash River)">Eel River</a> near <a href="/wiki/South_Whitley,_Indiana" title="South Whitley, Indiana">South Whitley, Indiana</a>. He later said that this was where he acquired his early knowledge of the natural world which he satirized in his writings.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cuppy graduated from Auburn High School in 1902 and went on to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>, where he received a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor&#39;s degree">bachelor's degree</a> in 1907. As an undergraduate, he belonged to <a href="/wiki/Phi_Gamma_Delta" title="Phi Gamma Delta">Phi Gamma Delta</a>, acted in amateur theater and worked as campus reporter for several Chicago newspapers, notably the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Record_Herald" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago Record Herald">Record Herald</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Daily_News" title="Chicago Daily News">Daily News</a></i>. He lingered at Chicago seven more years as a <a href="/wiki/Graduate_student" class="mw-redirect" title="Graduate student">graduate student</a> in English literature. He did not show much interest in his studies, but in 1910 produced his first book, <i>Maroon Tales</i>, a collection of short stories about university life. In 1914 he pulled together a short master's <a href="/wiki/Thesis" title="Thesis">thesis</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> took his degree and left for New York.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_career">Literary career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Will_Cuppy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Literary career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:204px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Will_Cuppy_home_2004.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bf/Will_Cuppy_home_2004.jpg" decoding="async" width="202" height="138" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="202" data-file-height="138" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Will_Cuppy_home_2004.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Will Cuppy's childhood home in Auburn, Indiana, in 2004.  Cuppy's maternal grandfather George W. Stahl built the house, later extensively modified, in 1851. It received an <a href="/wiki/List_of_Indiana_state_historical_markers" title="List of Indiana state historical markers">Indiana State Historical Marker</a> in 2019</div></div></div>
<p>Cuppy supported himself in New York by writing advertising copy while he tried unsuccessfully to write a play.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  He served briefly stateside in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> as a <a href="/wiki/US_Second_Lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="US Second Lieutenant">second lieutenant</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Transportation_Corps#World_War_I" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Army Transportation Corps">U.S. Army Motor Transport Corps</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  Later he began contributing book reviews to the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Tribune" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Tribune">New York Tribune</a></i>, where his college friend <a href="/wiki/Burton_Rascoe" title="Burton Rascoe">Burton Rascoe</a> (1892–1957) was literary <a href="/wiki/Editing" title="Editing">editor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> According to Rascoe, it was his assistant <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Paterson" title="Isabel Paterson">Isabel Paterson</a> who "coaxed and coddled" Cuppy into writing reviews and making a success of his career as a writer.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  In 1926, Cuppy began writing a weekly "Light Reading" column, later renamed "Mystery and Adventure", for the Tribune's successor, the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Herald_Tribune" title="New York Herald Tribune">New York Herald Tribune</a></i>. He continued writing the column until his death 23 years later, reviewing a career total of more than 4,000 titles of crime and detective fiction.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Seeking refuge from city noise and hay fever (which he referred to as "rose cold"), Cuppy "<a href="/wiki/Hermit" title="Hermit">hermited</a>" from 1921 to 1929 in a shack on <a href="/wiki/Jones_Beach_Island" title="Jones Beach Island">Jones Island</a>, just off <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>'s South Shore. The literary result of Cuppy's seaside exile was <i>How to be a Hermit</i>, a humorous look at <a href="/wiki/Home_economics" title="Home economics">home economics</a> that went through six printings in four months when it appeared in 1929. The book's subtitle, <i>A Bachelor Keeps House</i>, reflects the fact that Cuppy never married. The crew at the nearby <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.uscg.mil/history/stations/ZachsInlet.pdf">Zachs Inlet Coast Guard Station</a> shared their food and recipes with Cuppy and helped him repair his shack.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Encroachment by the new <a href="/wiki/Jones_Beach_State_Park" title="Jones Beach State Park">Jones Beach State Park</a> forced Cuppy to abandon full-time residence on the island and return to New York's noise and soot. A special dispensation from New York's parks <a href="/wiki/Tsar#Metaphorical_uses" title="Tsar">czar</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Moses" title="Robert Moses">Robert Moses</a> (1888–1981) let Cuppy keep his shack. He made regular visits to his place at the beach until the end of his life.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From his <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Village" title="Greenwich Village">Greenwich Village</a> apartment, Cuppy continued to turn out magazine articles and books. He always worked from notes jotted on 3x5-inch index cards. Cuppy would amass hundreds of cards even for a short article. His friend and literary <a href="/wiki/Executor" title="Executor">executor</a> <a href="/wiki/Fred_Feldkamp" title="Fred Feldkamp">Fred Feldkamp</a> (1914–1981) reported that Cuppy sometimes read more than 25 thick books on a subject before he wrote a single word about it.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Writing funny but factual magazine articles was Cuppy's real talent. He enjoyed a brief success in 1933 with a humorous talk show on <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> radio with actress and gourmet cook Jeanne Owen,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> but he flopped on the lecture circuit.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Basically shy, Cuppy was happiest when he was rummaging through scholarly journals prizing out facts to copy out on his note cards. According to Feldkamp, one of Cuppy's favorite places was the <a href="/wiki/Bronx_Zoo" title="Bronx Zoo">Bronx Zoo</a>, "where he felt really relaxed."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many of Cuppy's articles for <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i> and other magazines were later collected as books: <i>How to Tell Your Friends from the Apes</i> (1931); and <i>How to Become Extinct</i> (1941). Cuppy also edited three collections of mystery stories: <i>World's Great Mystery Stories</i> (1943); <i>World's Great Detective Stories</i> (1943); and <i>Murder Without Tears</i> (1946). His last animal book, <i>How to Attract the Wombat</i>, appeared two months after his death in 1949.
</p><p>Cuppy's best-known work, a satire on history called <i>The Decline and Fall of Practically Everybody</i>, was unfinished when he died. Its humor ranges from the remark that, when the Nile floods receded, the land, as far as the eye can see, is "covered by Egyptologists", to the detailed dissection, quotation, and parody, in the chapter on <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>, of the picture of Alexander as an idealist for world peace. The book's appeal can be gauged by the fact that <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> <a href="/wiki/News_presenter" title="News presenter">broadcaster</a> <a href="/wiki/Edward_R._Murrow" title="Edward R. Murrow">Edward R. Murrow</a> and his colleague <a href="/wiki/Don_Hollenbeck" title="Don Hollenbeck">Don Hollenbeck</a> took turns reading from it on the air "until the announcer cracked up."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>The Decline and Fall</i> was completed and published in 1950 by Fred Feldkamp, who sifted through nearly 15,000 of Cuppy's carefully filed note cards to get the book into print within a year of his friend's death. Feldkamp also edited a second posthumous volume, a comic almanac titled <i>How to Get from January to December</i>, that appeared in 1951.
</p><p>Cuppy's last years were marked by poor physical health and increasing depression. Facing eviction from his apartment, he took an overdose of sleeping pills and died ten days later on September 19, 1949, at <a href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent%27s_Catholic_Medical_Center" title="Saint Vincent&#39;s Catholic Medical Center">St. Vincent's Hospital</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Will_Cuppy_grave_marker.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Will_Cuppy_grave_marker.jpg/220px-Will_Cuppy_grave_marker.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Will_Cuppy_grave_marker.jpg/330px-Will_Cuppy_grave_marker.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Will_Cuppy_grave_marker.jpg/440px-Will_Cuppy_grave_marker.jpg 2x" data-file-width="928" data-file-height="589" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Will_Cuppy_grave_marker.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Will Cuppy's grave marker in Evergreen Cemetery in Auburn, Indiana.</div></div></div>
<p>Cuppy's cremated remains were returned to his hometown and buried in a grave next to his mother's in Evergreen Cemetery. His grave was unmarked until 1985, when local donors purchased a granite headstone with the inscription, "American Humorist". In 2003, Cuppy received another memorial when a committee of the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> approved the name "<a href="/wiki/15017_Cuppy" title="15017 Cuppy">15017 Cuppy</a>" for an <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>  In 2019, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Indiana_State_Library_and_Historical_Bureau_Historical_Bureau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Indiana State Library and Historical Bureau Historical Bureau (page does not exist)">Indiana Historical Bureau</a> approved placing a state historic marker at Cuppy's family home in Auburn. <sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although Cuppy was reclusive and cultivated the image of a curmudgeon, he had many friends in New York's literary circles. One of them was the poet <a href="/wiki/William_Rose_Ben%C3%A9t" title="William Rose Benét">William Rose Benét</a> (1886–1950) who, writing in the <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Review_(US_magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Saturday Review (US magazine)">Saturday Review of Literature</a></i>, penned this remembrance of him:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>He had the haunted look of the true humorist. All his friends loved him.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuppy_documents">Cuppy documents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Will_Cuppy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cuppy documents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cuppy's papers, including thousands of his notecards, are archived at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago_Library" title="University of Chicago Library">University of Chicago Library</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> A number of his letters to his friend and <i>Herald Tribune</i> colleague <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Paterson" title="Isabel Paterson">Isabel Paterson</a> are among Paterson's papers archived at the <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hoover_Presidential_Library_and_Museum" title="Herbert Hoover Presidential Library and Museum">Herbert Hoover Presidential Library and Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Branch,_Iowa" title="West Branch, Iowa">West Branch, Iowa</a>.
<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Two of Cuppy's letters to <a href="/wiki/Max_Eastman" title="Max Eastman">Max Eastman</a> are among Eastman's papers at <a href="/wiki/Indiana_University_Bloomington" title="Indiana University Bloomington">Indiana University's</a> <a href="/wiki/Indiana_University_Bloomington#The_Lilly_Library" title="Indiana University Bloomington">Lilly Library</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sullivan_(writer)" title="Frank Sullivan (writer)">Frank Sullivan</a> Collection at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> also contains correspondence from Cuppy.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The papers of <a href="/wiki/John_T._Frederick" title="John T. Frederick">John Towner Frederick</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iowa" title="University of Iowa">University of Iowa</a> include letters written by Cuppy in the 1940s relating to Frederick's <i>Of Men and Books</i> series for <a href="/wiki/CBS#Early_years" title="CBS">CBS Radio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  Four letters from Cuppy to children's author <a href="/wiki/Anne_Carroll_Moore" title="Anne Carroll Moore">Anne Carroll Moore</a> are among her papers at the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Public_Library" title="New York Public Library">New York Public Library</a> <sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Iranian_controversy">Iranian controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Will_Cuppy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Iranian controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A Persian translation by <a href="/wiki/Najaf_Daryabandari" title="Najaf Daryabandari">Najaf Daryabandari</a> of Cuppy's <i>The Decline and Fall of Practically Everybody</i> was published in 1972 under the title of <i>Čenin konand bozorgān</i> (چنین کنند بزرگان, <i>Thus Act the Great</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>  The good quality of the Persian prose and the fact of Cuppy's being unknown in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> led to speculation that the book was not a translation, but an original book by Daryabandari and possibly a collaborator, who was speculated to be <a href="/wiki/Ahmad_Shamlou" title="Ahmad Shamlou">Ahmad Shamlou</a>. It was guessed that this had been done in order to bypass the <a href="/wiki/Pahlavi_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Pahlavi era">Pahlavi era</a> censor. Daryabandari denied it several times, even after the <a href="/wiki/Iranian_Revolution" title="Iranian Revolution">Iranian Revolution</a>. The issue was not publicly settled until the satire magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Golagha" class="mw-redirect" title="Golagha">Golagha</a></i> ran an article about their "discovery" of Cuppy, which proved Daryabandari right.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
