<p><b>James Davis Nicoll</b> (born March 18, 1961<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) of <a href="/wiki/Kitchener,_Ontario" title="Kitchener, Ontario">Kitchener</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, is a freelance game and <a href="/wiki/Speculative_fiction" title="Speculative fiction">speculative fiction</a> reviewer, former <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="Role-playing game">role-playing game</a> store owner,  and also works as a <a href="/wiki/Publisher%27s_reader" title="Publisher&#39;s reader">first reader</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Science_Fiction_Book_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="Science Fiction Book Club">Science Fiction Book Club</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> As a
<a href="/wiki/Usenet" title="Usenet">Usenet</a> personality,  Nicoll is known for writing a widely quoted <a href="/wiki/Epigram" title="Epigram">epigram</a> on the English language, as well as for his accounts of suffering a high number of accidents, which he has narrated over the years in Usenet groups like rec.arts.sf.written and rec.arts.sf.fandom. He is now a <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blogger</a> on <a href="/wiki/Dreamwidth" title="Dreamwidth">Dreamwidth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a>, and an occasional columnist on <a href="/wiki/Tor.com" title="Tor.com">Tor.com</a>. In 2014, he started his website, jamesdavisnicoll.com, dedicated to his <a href="/wiki/Book_reviews" class="mw-redirect" title="Book reviews">book reviews</a> of works old and new; and later added <i>Young People Read Old SFF</i>, where his panel of younger readers read pre-1980 science fiction and fantasy, and Nicoll and his collaborators report on the younger readers' reactions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_SF_genre">Influence on SF genre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Nicoll&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Influence on SF genre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to his influence as a <a href="/wiki/Publisher%27s_reader" title="Publisher&#39;s reader">first reader</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Science_Fiction_Book_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="Science Fiction Book Club">Science Fiction Book Club</a>, a book reviewer for <i><a href="/wiki/Bookspan" title="Bookspan">Bookspan</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Publishers_Weekly" title="Publishers Weekly">Publishers Weekly</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Romantic_Times" title="Romantic Times">Romantic Times</a></i>, and a juror for the <a href="/wiki/James_Tiptree,_Jr._Award" class="mw-redirect" title="James Tiptree, Jr. Award">James Tiptree, Jr. Award</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
Nicoll often offers ideas and concepts to other writers, primarily through the medium of <a href="/wiki/Usenet" title="Usenet">Usenet</a>. After winning the 2006 <a href="/wiki/Locus_Award" title="Locus Award">Locus Award</a> for his novella <i><a href="/wiki/Missile_Gap" title="Missile Gap">Missile Gap</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Stross" title="Charles Stross">Charles Stross</a> thanked him, writing that Nicoll "came up with the original insane setting<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>—then kindly gave me permission to take his idea and run with it."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id=".22The_Purity_of_the_English_Language.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;The_Purity_of_the_English_Language&quot;">"The Purity of the English Language"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Nicoll&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: &quot;The Purity of the English Language&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1990, in the Usenet group rec.arts.sf-lovers, Nicoll wrote the following <a href="/wiki/Epigram" title="Epigram">epigram</a> on the English language:
</p>
<blockquote><p>The problem with defending the purity of the English language is that English is about as pure as a cribhouse whore. We don't just borrow words; on occasion, English has pursued other languages down alleyways to beat them unconscious and riffle their pockets for new vocabulary.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>(A followup to the original post acknowledged that the spelling of "riffle" was a misspelling of "rifle".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>)
</p><p>Over the years it has spread over the internet, often misattributed to other individuals including <a href="/wiki/Booker_T._Washington" title="Booker T. Washington">Booker T. Washington</a> and a nineteenth-century painter also named <a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Nicoll_(painter)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="James Nicoll (painter) (page does not exist)">James Nicoll</a>. In recent years however the epigram has also been quoted, with proper attribution, in books by  professor of <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetoric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Communication_design" title="Communication design">communication design</a> Randy Harris.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Amateur linguists Jeremy Smith,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lederer" title="Richard Lederer">Richard Lederer</a>, <sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>, the Chinese Newspaper <a href="/wiki/Ming_Pao" title="Ming Pao">Ming Pao</a> <sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Anu_Garg" title="Anu Garg">Anu Garg</a><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> have also referenced Nicoll's quote.
</p><p>Professional linguists who have referenced the quotation online include Professor of <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">Linguistics</a> <a href="/wiki/Mark_Liberman" title="Mark Liberman">Mark Liberman</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Language_Log" title="Language Log">Language Log</a></i>;<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Associate Professor of Linguistics Suzanne Kemmer<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Rice_University" title="Rice University">Rice University</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> who also posted her research into the quote at the <i><a href="/wiki/LINGUIST_List" class="mw-redirect" title="LINGUIST List">LINGUIST</a></i> mailing list;<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Language_Acquisition" class="mw-redirect" title="Second Language Acquisition">Second Language Acquisition</a> Ph.D. student Rong Liu.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  There are also amateur philologists who have used the quote, including journalist <a href="/wiki/Suw_Charman" class="mw-redirect" title="Suw Charman">Suw Charman</a><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and journalist Vale White.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id=".22Nicoll_events.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Nicoll_events&quot;">"Nicoll events"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Nicoll&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: &quot;Nicoll events&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nicoll relates a number of life- and/or limb-threatening accidents that have happened to him, which he has told and retold on various <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_fandom" title="Science fiction fandom">science fiction fandom</a>–related newsgroups. Over the years these stories have also been collected into Cally Soukup's List of Nicoll events.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Inspired by Nicoll's collection of accidents, as well as his tendency to take in any stray cat that comes knocking, <a href="/wiki/Fantasy_author" class="mw-redirect" title="Fantasy author">fantasy author</a> <a href="/wiki/Jo_Walton" title="Jo Walton">Jo Walton</a> wrote him a <a href="/wiki/Poem" class="mw-redirect" title="Poem">poem</a> in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id=".22Brain_eater.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Brain_eater&quot;">"Brain eater"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Nicoll&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: &quot;Brain eater&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A post on soc.history.what-if credits Nicoll with coining the phrase "brain eater"<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> which is supposed to "get" certain writers such as <a href="/wiki/Poul_Anderson" title="Poul Anderson">Poul Anderson</a><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/James_P._Hogan_(writer)" title="James P. Hogan (writer)">James P. Hogan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Nicoll claims the 'brain eater' affected Hogan, because of Hogan's expressions of belief in <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Velikovsky" title="Immanuel Velikovsky">Immanuel Velikovsky</a>'s version of <a href="/wiki/Catastrophism" title="Catastrophism">catastrophism</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> and his advocacy of the hypothesis that <a href="/wiki/AIDS" class="mw-redirect" title="AIDS">AIDS</a> is caused by pharmaceutical use rather than <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a> (see <a href="/wiki/AIDS_denialism" class="mw-redirect" title="AIDS denialism">AIDS denialism</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The term has been adopted by other Usenet posters,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> as well as elsewhere on the Internet<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and use of the term within Usenet has been criticised.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nicoll-Dyson_Laser">Nicoll-Dyson Laser</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Nicoll&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nicoll-Dyson Laser">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nicoll proposed the Nicoll-Dyson Laser concept where the satellites of a <a href="/wiki/Dyson_swarm" class="mw-redirect" title="Dyson swarm">Dyson swarm</a> act as a <a href="/wiki/Phased_array" title="Phased array">phased array</a> <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> emitter capable of delivering their energy to a planet-sized target at a range of millions of <a href="/wiki/Light_years" class="mw-redirect" title="Light years">light years</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/E._E._Smith" title="E. E. Smith">E. E. Smith</a> first used the general idea of concentrating the sun's energy in a weapon in the <a href="/wiki/Lensman" class="mw-redirect" title="Lensman">Lensman</a> series when the <a href="/wiki/Galactic_Patrol" title="Galactic Patrol">Galactic Patrol</a> developed the <a href="/wiki/Lensman#Weapons" class="mw-redirect" title="Lensman">sunbeam</a> (in <i>Second Stage Lensmen</i>); however, his concept did not extend to the details of the Nicoll-Dyson Laser. The 2012 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rapture_of_the_Nerds" title="The Rapture of the Nerds">The Rapture of the Nerds</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Cory_Doctorow" title="Cory Doctorow">Cory Doctorow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Stross" title="Charles Stross">Charles Stross</a> uses the Nicoll-Dyson Laser concept by name as the means by which the Galactic Federation threatens to destroy the Earth.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Science-fictional_Lysenkoism">Science-fictional Lysenkoism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Nicoll&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Science-fictional Lysenkoism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a discussion on rec.arts.sf.written about why Golden Age science fiction so often uses aliens said to derive from short-lived but well-known stars such as <a href="/wiki/Rigel" title="Rigel">Rigel</a> whose lifespan is probably too brief to ever allow the rise of life due to the long-established <a href="/wiki/Mass-luminosity_relationship" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass-luminosity relationship">mass-luminosity relationship</a> for <a href="/wiki/Main-sequence_stars" class="mw-redirect" title="Main-sequence stars">main-sequence stars</a>, Nicoll identified what he termed the "SFnal <a href="/wiki/Lysenkoism" title="Lysenkoism">Lysenkoist</a> Tendency when actual, tested science contradicts some detail in an SF story, attack the science." He expanded on this idea in an article for online science fiction and fantasy magazine <a href="/wiki/Tor.com" title="Tor.com">Tor.com</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Nicoll&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nicoll was one of the finalists for the 2010, 2011, and 2019 <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Hugo Awards">Hugo Awards</a> for <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award_for_Best_Fan_Writer" title="Hugo Award for Best Fan Writer">Best Fan Writer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
He has also been Fan Guest of Honor (GoH) at <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">SF</a> conventions including 2013's <a href="/wiki/ConFusion_(convention)" class="mw-redirect" title="ConFusion (convention)">ConFusion</a> in Detroit<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
and 2014's <a href="/wiki/Arisia" title="Arisia">Arisia</a> in Boston.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In 2020, he has been nominated for delegate for the <a href="/wiki/Down_Under_Fan_Fund" title="Down Under Fan Fund">Down Under Fan Fund</a>, to visit <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_fandom" title="Science fiction fandom">science fiction fandom</a> in Australasia as a representative of their North American counterparts.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
