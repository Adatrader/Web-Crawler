<p><b>Consensus decision-making</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Group_decision-making" title="Group decision-making">group decision-making</a> process in which group members develop, and agree to support a decision in the best interest of the whole group or common goal. Consensus may be defined professionally as an acceptable resolution, one that can be supported, even if not the "favourite" of each individual. It has its origin in the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word <i>cōnsēnsus</i> (agreement), which is from <i>cōnsentiō</i> meaning literally <i>feel together</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is used to describe both the decision and the process of reaching a decision. Consensus decision-making is thus concerned with the process of deliberating and finalizing a decision, and the social, economic, legal, environmental and political effects of applying this process.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Objectives">Objectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Objectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Characteristics of consensus decision-making include:
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Collaboration" title="Collaboration">Collaborative</a>:</b> Participants contribute to a shared proposal and shape it into a decision that meets the concerns of all group members as much as possible.<sup id="cite_ref-CODM_book_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CODM_book-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Cooperation" title="Cooperation">Cooperative</a>:</b> Participants in an effective consensus process should strive to reach the best possible decision for the group and all of its members, rather than competing for personal preferences.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Egalitarianism" title="Egalitarianism">Egalitarian</a>:</b> All members of a consensus decision-making body should be afforded, as much as possible, equal input into the process. All members have the opportunity to present, and amend proposals.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Inclusion_(value_and_practice)" class="mw-redirect" title="Inclusion (value and practice)">Inclusive</a>:</b> As many <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/stakeholder" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:stakeholder">stakeholders</a> as possible should be involved in the consensus decision-making process.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Participation_(decision_making)" class="mw-redirect" title="Participation (decision making)">Participatory</a>:</b> The consensus process should actively solicit the input and <a href="/wiki/Participation_(decision_making)" class="mw-redirect" title="Participation (decision making)">participation</a> of all decision-makers.<sup id="cite_ref-Sandelin_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sandelin-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_to_common_decision-making_practices">Alternative to common decision-making practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Alternative to common decision-making practices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Consensus decision-making is an alternative to commonly practiced <a href="/wiki/Group_decision-making" title="Group decision-making">group decision-making</a> processes.<sup id="cite_ref-Basics_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Basics-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Robert%27s_Rules_of_Order" title="Robert&#39;s Rules of Order">Robert's Rules of Order</a></i>, for instance, is a guide book used by many organizations. This book allows the structuring of debate and passage of proposals that can be approved through <a href="/wiki/Majority" title="Majority">majority</a> vote. It does not emphasize the goal of full agreement. Critics of such a process believe that it can involve adversarial debate and the formation of competing factions. These dynamics may harm group member relationships and undermine the ability of a group to cooperatively implement a contentious decision. Consensus decision-making attempts to address the beliefs of such problems. Proponents claim that outcomes of the consensus process include:<sup id="cite_ref-CODM_book_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CODM_book-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Better decisions: Through including the input of all stakeholders the resulting proposals may better address all potential concerns.</li>
<li>Better implementation: A process that includes and respects all parties, and generates as much agreement as possible sets the stage for greater cooperation in implementing the resulting decisions.</li>
<li>Better group relationships: A cooperative, collaborative group atmosphere can foster greater group cohesion and interpersonal connection.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decision_rules">Decision rules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Decision rules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Consensus is not synonymous with "unanimity"&#8211; though that may be a rule agreed to in a decision making process.  The level of agreement necessary to finalize a decision is known as a "decision rule".<sup id="cite_ref-CODM_book_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CODM_book-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Blocking_and_other_forms_of_dissent">Blocking and other forms of dissent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Blocking and other forms of dissent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To ensure the agreement or consent of all participants is valued, many groups choose unanimity or near-unanimity as their decision rule. Groups that require unanimity allow individual participants the option of blocking a group decision. This provision motivates a group to make sure that all group members consent to any new proposal before it is adopted. Proper guidelines for the use of this option, however, are important. The ethics of consensus decision-making encourage participants to place the good of the whole group above their own individual preferences. When there is potential for a block to a group decision, both the group and dissenters in the group are encouraged to collaborate until agreement can be reached. Simply <a href="/wiki/Vetoing" class="mw-redirect" title="Vetoing">vetoing</a> a decision is not considered a responsible use of consensus blocking. Some common guidelines for the use of consensus blocking include:<sup id="cite_ref-CODM_book_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CODM_book-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Providing an option for those who do not support a proposal to “stand aside” rather than block.</li>
<li>Requiring a block from two or more people to put a proposal aside.</li>
<li>Requiring the blocking party to supply an alternative proposal or a process for generating one.<sup id="cite_ref-Canadian_Cohousing_Network_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canadian_Cohousing_Network-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Limiting each person's option to block consensus to a handful of times in one's life.</li>
<li>Limiting the option of blocking to decisions that are substantial to the mission or operation of the group and not allowing blocking on routine decisions.</li>
<li>Limiting the allowable rationale for blocking to issues that are fundamental to the group's mission or potentially disastrous to the group.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dissent_options">Dissent options</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dissent options">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A participant who does not support a proposal may have alternatives to simply blocking it. Some common options may include the ability to:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Declare reservations:</b> Group members who are willing to let a motion pass but desire to register their concerns with the group may choose "declare reservations." If there are significant reservations about a motion, the decision-making body may choose to modify or re-word the proposal.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Stand aside:</b> A "stand aside" may be registered by a group member who has a "serious personal disagreement" with a proposal, but is willing to let the motion pass. Although stand asides do not halt a motion, it is often regarded as a strong "nay vote" and the concerns of group members standing aside are usually addressed by modifications to the proposal. Stand asides may also be registered by users who feel they are incapable of adequately understanding or participating in the proposal.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Object:</b> Any group member may "object" to a proposal. In groups with a unanimity decision rule, a single block is sufficient to stop a proposal. Other decision rules may require more than one objection for a proposal to be blocked or not pass (see previous section, <i><a href="/wiki/Consensus_decision-making#Decision_rules" title="Consensus decision-making">Decision rules</a></i>).</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Process_models">Process models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Process models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The basic model for achieving consensus as defined by any decision rule involves:
</p>
<ul><li>Collaboratively generating a proposal</li>
<li>Identifying unsatisfied concerns</li>
<li>Modifying the proposal to generate as much agreement as possible</li></ul>
<p>All attempts at achieving consensus begin with a good faith attempt at generating full-agreement, regardless of decision rule threshold.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Specific_models">Specific models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Specific models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <b><span id="Spokescouncil"></span><span id="spokescouncil">spokescouncil</span></b> model, <a href="/wiki/Affinity_groups" class="mw-redirect" title="Affinity groups">affinity groups</a> make joint decisions by each designating a speaker and sitting behind that circle of spokespeople, akin to the <a href="/wiki/Spoke" title="Spoke">spokes</a> of a wheel. While speaking rights might be limited to each group's designee, the meeting may allot breakout time for the constituent groups to discuss an issue and return to the circle via their spokesperson. In the case of an activist spokescouncil preparing for the <a href="/wiki/Washington_A16,_2000" title="Washington A16, 2000">A16 Washington D.C. protests in 2000</a>, affinity groups disputed their spokescouncil's imposition of nonviolence in their action guidelines. They received the reprieve of letting groups self-organize their protests, and as the city's protest was subsequently divided into pie slices, each blockaded by an affinity group's choice of protest. Many of the participants learned about the spokescouncil model on the fly by participating in it directly, and came to better understand their planned action by hearing others' concerns and voicing their own.<sup id="cite_ref-HaworthElmore2017_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HaworthElmore2017-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Consensus_voting">Consensus voting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Consensus voting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The group first elects, say, three referees or consensors.
The debate on the chosen problem is initiated by the facilitator calling for proposals.
Every proposed option is accepted if the referees decide it is relevant and conforms with the UN Charter on Human Rights.
The referees produce and display a list of these options.
The debate proceeds, with queries, comments, criticisms and/or even new options.
If the debate fails to come to a verbal consensus, the referees draw up a final list of options - usually between 4 and 6 - to represent the debate.
When all agree, the chair calls for a preferential vote, as per the rules for a <a href="/wiki/Modified_Borda_Count" class="mw-redirect" title="Modified Borda Count">Modified Borda Count</a>, MBC.
The referees decide which option, or which composite of the two leading options, is the outcome. If its level of support surpasses a minimum consensus coefficient, it may be adopted.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Blocking">Blocking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Blocking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Consensus-flowchart.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Consensus-flowchart.png/250px-Consensus-flowchart.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="314" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ed/Consensus-flowchart.png 1.5x" data-file-width="307" data-file-height="385" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Consensus-flowchart.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flowchart of basic consensus decision-making process.</div></div></div>
<p>Groups that require unanimity commonly use a core set of procedures depicted in this flow chart.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Once an agenda for discussion has been set and, optionally, the ground rules for the meeting have been agreed upon, each item of the agenda is addressed in turn. Typically, each decision arising from an agenda item follows through a simple structure:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Discussion of the item:</b> The item is discussed with the goal of identifying opinions and information on the topic at hand. The general direction of the group and potential proposals for action are often identified during the discussion.</li>
<li><b>Formation of a proposal:</b> Based on the discussion a formal decision proposal on the issue is presented to the group.</li>
<li><b>Call for consensus:</b> The facilitator of the decision-making body calls for consensus on the proposal. Each member of the group usually must actively state whether they agree or consent, stand aside, or object, often by using a hand gesture or raising a colored card, to avoid the group <a href="/wiki/Warnock%27s_dilemma" title="Warnock&#39;s dilemma">interpreting silence or inaction as agreement</a>.  The number of objections is counted to determine if this step's consent threshold is satisfied.  If it is, dissenters are asked to share their concerns with proceeding with the agreement, so that any potential harms can be addressed/minimized.  <i>This can happen even if the consent threshold is unanimity, especially if many voters stand aside.</i></li>
<li><b>Identification and addressing of concerns:</b> If consensus is not achieved, each dissenter presents his or her concerns on the proposal, potentially starting another round of discussion to address or clarify the concern.</li>
<li><b>Modification of the proposal:</b> The proposal is amended, re-phrased or <a href="/wiki/Rider_(legislation)" title="Rider (legislation)">ridered</a> in an attempt to address the concerns of the decision-makers. The process then returns to the call for consensus and the cycle is repeated until a satisfactory decision passes the consent threshold for the group.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Quaker-based_model">Quaker-based model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Quaker-based model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Quaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Quaker">Quaker</a>-based consensus<sup id="cite_ref-Quaker_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Quaker-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> is said to be effective because it puts in place a simple, time-tested structure that moves a group towards unity.  The Quaker model is intended to allow hearing individual voices while providing a mechanism for dealing with disagreements.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Quaker_group_facilitator_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Quaker_group_facilitator-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Quaker model has been adapted by <a href="/wiki/Earlham_College" title="Earlham College">Earlham College</a> for application to secular settings, and can be effectively applied in any consensus decision-making process.
</p><p>Its process includes:
</p>
<ul><li>Multiple concerns and information are shared until the sense of the group is clear.</li>
<li>Discussion involves <a href="/wiki/Active_listening" title="Active listening">active listening</a> and sharing information.</li>
<li>Norms limit number of times one asks to speak to ensure that each speaker is fully heard.</li>
<li>Ideas and solutions belong to the group; no names are recorded.</li>
<li>Ideally, differences are resolved by discussion. The <a href="/wiki/Facilitator" title="Facilitator">facilitator</a> ("clerk" or "convenor" in the Quaker model) identifies areas of agreement and names disagreements to push discussion deeper.</li>
<li>The facilitator articulates the sense of the discussion, asks if there are other concerns, and proposes a "<a href="/wiki/Minutes" title="Minutes">minute</a>" of the decision.</li>
<li>The group as a whole is responsible for the decision and the decision belongs to the group.</li>
<li>The facilitator can discern if one who is not uniting with the decision is acting without concern for the group or in selfish interest.</li>
<li>Ideally, all dissenters' perspectives are synthesized into the final outcome for a whole that is greater than the sum of its parts.<sup id="cite_ref-Quaker_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Quaker-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Should some dissenter's perspective not harmonize with the others, that dissenter may "stand aside" to allow the group to proceed, or may opt to "block".  "Standing aside" implies a certain form of silent consent.  Some groups allow "blocking" by even a single individual to halt or postpone the entire process.<sup id="cite_ref-Quaker_group_facilitator_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Quaker_group_facilitator-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Key components of Quaker-based consensus include a belief in a common <a href="/wiki/Human_condition" title="Human condition">humanity</a> and the ability to decide together. The goal is "unity, not unanimity."  Ensuring that group members speak only once until others are heard encourages a diversity of thought. The facilitator is understood as serving the group rather than acting as person-in-charge.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In the Quaker model, as with other consensus decision-making processes, articulating the emerging consensus allows members to be clear on the decision in front of them.  As members' views are taken into account they are likely to support it.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roles">Roles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Roles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The consensus decision-making process often has several roles designed to make the process run more effectively. Although the name and nature of these roles varies from group to group, the most common are the <a href="/wiki/Facilitator" title="Facilitator">facilitator</a>, <a href="/wiki/Consensor" class="mw-redirect" title="Consensor">consensor</a>, a timekeeper, an empath and a secretary or notes taker. Not all decision-making bodies use all of these roles, although the facilitator position is almost always filled, and some groups use supplementary roles, such as a <a href="/wiki/Devil%27s_advocate" title="Devil&#39;s advocate">Devil's advocate</a> or greeter. Some decision-making bodies rotate these roles through the group members in order to build the experience and skills of the participants, and prevent any perceived concentration of power.<sup id="cite_ref-FNB_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FNB-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The common roles in a consensus meeting are:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Facilitator:</b> As the name implies, the role of the facilitator is to help make the process of reaching a consensus decision easier. Facilitators accept responsibility for moving through the agenda on time; ensuring the group adheres to the mutually agreed-upon mechanics of the consensus process; and, if necessary, suggesting alternate or additional discussion or decision-making techniques, such as go-arounds, break-out groups or role-playing.<sup id="cite_ref-Kerrigan_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kerrigan-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Guides_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guides-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Some consensus groups use two co-facilitators. Shared <a href="/wiki/Facilitation_(business)" title="Facilitation (business)">facilitation</a> is often adopted to diffuse the perceived power of the facilitator and create a system whereby a co-facilitator can pass off facilitation duties if he or she becomes more personally engaged in a debate.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Consensor:</b> The team of consensors is responsible for accepting those relevant proposals which conform with the UN Charter on Human Rights; for displaying an initial list of these options; for drawing up a balanced list of options to represent the entire debate; to analyse the preferences cast in any subsequent MBC ballot; and, if need be, to determine the composite decision from the two most popular options.</li>
<li><b>Timekeeper:</b> The purpose of the timekeeper is to ensure the decision-making body keeps to the schedule set in the agenda. Effective timekeepers use a variety of techniques to ensure the meeting runs on time including: giving frequent time updates, ample warning of short time, and keeping individual speakers from taking an excessive amount of time.<sup id="cite_ref-FNB_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FNB-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Empath or 'Vibe Watch':</b> The empath, or 'vibe watch' as the position is sometimes called, is charged with monitoring the 'emotional climate' of the meeting, taking note of the body language and other <a href="/wiki/Nonverbal_communication" title="Nonverbal communication">non-verbal cues</a> of the participants. Defusing potential emotional conflicts, maintaining a climate free of intimidation and being aware of potentially destructive power dynamics, such as sexism or racism within the decision-making body, are the primary responsibilities of the empath.<sup id="cite_ref-Kerrigan_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kerrigan-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Note taker:</b> The role of the notes taker or secretary is to document the decisions, discussion and action points of the decision-making body.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tools_and_methods">Tools and methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Tools and methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Consensus_Cards.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Consensus_Cards.svg/200px-Consensus_Cards.svg.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="174" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Consensus_Cards.svg/300px-Consensus_Cards.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Consensus_Cards.svg/400px-Consensus_Cards.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1023" data-file-height="888" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Consensus_Cards.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Front face, back face and embossing mask for colored consensus cards</div></div></div>
<ul><li>Some consensus decision-making bodies use a system of colored cards to indicate speaker priority. For instance, red cards to indicate feedback on a breach in rules or decorum, yellow cards for clarifying questions, and green cards for desire to speak.<sup id="cite_ref-Canadian_Cohousing_Network_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canadian_Cohousing_Network-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><span id="Hand_signals"></span><a href="/wiki/Hand_gestures" class="mw-redirect" title="Hand gestures">Hand signals</a> are another method for reading a room's positions nonverbally. They work well with groups of fewer than 250 people and especially with multi-lingual groups.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The nature and meaning of individual gestures varies between groups, but a widely adopted core set of hand signals include: wiggling of the fingers on both hands, a gesture sometimes referred to as "twinkling", to indicate agreement; raising a fist or crossing both forearms with hands in fists to indicate a block or strong disagreement; and making a "T" shape with both hands, the "time out" gesture, to call attention to a point of process or order.<sup id="cite_ref-Guides_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guides-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> One common set of hand signals is called the "Fist-to-Five" or "Fist-of-Five". In this method each member of the group can hold up a fist to indicate blocking consensus, one finger to suggest changes, two fingers to discuss minor issues, three fingers to indicate willingness to let issue pass without further discussion, four fingers to affirm the decision as a good idea, and five fingers to volunteer to take a lead in implementing the decision.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Occupy_hand_signals" class="mw-redirect" title="Occupy hand signals">A similar set of hand signals</a> are used by the <a href="/wiki/Occupy_Wall_Street" title="Occupy Wall Street">Occupy Wall Street</a> protesters in their group negotiations.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/First-past-the-post" class="mw-redirect" title="First-past-the-post">First-past-the-post</a> is used as a <i>fall-back method</i> when consensus cannot be reached within a given time frame.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> If the potential outcome of the fall-back method can be anticipated, then those who support that outcome have incentives to block consensus so that the fall-back method gets applied. Special fall-back methods have been developed that reduce this incentive.<sup id="cite_ref-SCFC_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCFC-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_blocking">Criticism of blocking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Criticism of blocking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Critics of consensus blocking often observe that the option, while potentially effective for small groups of motivated or trained individuals with a sufficiently high degree of <a href="/wiki/Affinity_(sociology)" title="Affinity (sociology)">affinity</a>, has a number of possible shortcomings, notably
</p>
<ul><li><b>Preservation of the status quo:</b> In decision-making bodies that use formal consensus, the ability of individuals or small minorities to block agreement gives an enormous advantage to anyone who supports the existing state of affairs. This can mean that a specific state of affairs can continue to exist in an organization long after a majority of members would like it to change.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The incentive to block can however be removed by using a special kind of voting process.<sup id="cite_ref-SCFC_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCFC-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Susceptibility to widespread disagreement:</b> Giving the right to block proposals to all group members may result in the group becoming hostage to an inflexible minority or individual. When a popular proposal is blocked the group actually experiences widespread disagreement, the opposite of the consensus process's goal. Furthermore, "opposing such obstructive behavior [can be] construed as an attack on freedom of speech and in turn [harden] resolve on the part of the individual to defend his or her position."<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> As a result, consensus decision-making has the potential to reward the least accommodating group members while punishing the most accommodating.</li>
<li><b>Stagnation and group dysfunction:</b> When groups cannot make the decisions necessary to function (because they cannot resolve blocks), they may lose effectiveness in accomplishing their mission.</li>
<li><b>Susceptibility to splitting and excluding members:</b> When high levels of group member frustration result from blocked decisions or inordinately long meetings, members may leave the group, try to get to others to leave, or limit who has entry to the group.</li>
<li><b>Channeling decisions away from an inclusive group process:</b> When group members view the status quo as unjustly difficult to change through a whole group process, they may begin to delegate decision-making to smaller committees or to an executive committee. In some cases members begin to act unilaterally because they are frustrated with a stagnated group process.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Groupthink">Groupthink</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Groupthink">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Consensus seeks to improve <a href="/wiki/Solidarity" title="Solidarity">solidarity</a> in the long run.  Accordingly, it should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/Unanimity" title="Unanimity">unanimity</a> in the immediate situation, which is often a symptom of <a href="/wiki/Groupthink" title="Groupthink">groupthink</a>.  Studies of effective consensus process usually indicate a shunning of unanimity or "illusion of unanimity"<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> that does not hold up as a group comes under real-world pressure (when dissent reappears). <a href="/wiki/Cory_Doctorow" title="Cory Doctorow">Cory Doctorow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a> and other proponents of <a href="/wiki/Deliberative_democracy" title="Deliberative democracy">deliberative democracy</a> or judicial-like methods view the explicit dissent as a symbol of strength.
</p><p>In his book about Wikipedia, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Reagle" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph Reagle">Joseph Reagle</a> considers the merits and challenges of consensus in open and online communities.<sup id="cite_ref-Reagle2010_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reagle2010-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Randy Schutt,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Starhawk<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> and other practitioners of <a href="/wiki/Direct_action" title="Direct action">direct action</a> focus on the hazards of apparent agreement followed by action in which group splits become dangerously obvious.
</p><p>Unanimous, or apparently unanimous, decisions can have drawbacks.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>  They may be symptoms of a <a href="/wiki/Systemic_bias" title="Systemic bias">systemic bias</a>, a rigged process (where an <a href="/wiki/Agenda_(meeting)" title="Agenda (meeting)">agenda</a> is not published in advance or changed when it becomes clear who is present to consent), fear of speaking one's mind, a lack of creativity (to suggest alternatives) or even a lack of courage (to go further along the same road to a more extreme solution that would not achieve unanimous consent).
</p><p>Unanimity is achieved when the full group apparently consents to a decision.  It has disadvantages insofar as further disagreement, improvements or better ideas then remain hidden, but effectively ends the debate moving it to an implementation phase.  Some consider all unanimity a form of groupthink, and some experts propose "coding systems...for detecting the illusion of unanimity symptom."<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>  In <i>Consensus is not Unanimity</i>, consensus practitioner and activist leader <a href="/wiki/Starhawk" title="Starhawk">Starhawk</a> wrote:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>Many people think of consensus as simply an extended voting method in which every one must cast their votes the same way. Since unanimity of this kind only rarely occurs in groups with more than one member, groups that try to use this kind of process usually end up being either extremely frustrated or coercive. Either decisions are never made (leading to the demise of the group, its conversion into a social group that does not accomplish any tasks), they are made covertly, or some group or individual dominates the rest. Sometimes a majority dominates, sometimes a minority, sometimes an individual who employs "the block". But no matter how it is done, it is NOT consensus.</i><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Confusion between unanimity and consensus, in other words, usually causes consensus decision-making to fail, and the group then either reverts to majority or supermajority rule or disbands.
</p><p>Most robust models of consensus exclude uniformly unanimous decisions and require at least documentation of minority concerns.  Some state clearly that unanimity is not consensus but rather evidence of intimidation, lack of imagination, lack of courage, failure to include all voices, or deliberate exclusion of the contrary views.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_majority_voting_processes">Criticism of majority voting processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Criticism of majority voting processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some proponents of consensus decision-making view procedures that use <a href="/wiki/Majority_rule" title="Majority rule">majority rule</a> as undesirable for several reasons. Majority <a href="/wiki/Voting" title="Voting">voting</a> is regarded as <a href="/wiki/Competition" title="Competition">competitive</a>, rather than <a href="/wiki/Cooperation" title="Cooperation">cooperative</a>, framing decision-making in a win/lose dichotomy that ignores the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Compromise" title="Compromise">compromise</a> or other mutually beneficial solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Carlos Santiago Nino, on the other hand, has argued that majority rule leads to better deliberation practice than the alternatives, because it requires each member of the group to make arguments that appeal to at least half the participants.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> A. Lijphart reaches the same conclusion about majority rule, noting that majority rule encourages coalition-building.<sup id="cite_ref-tyranny_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tyranny-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, opponents of majority rule claim that it can lead to a '<a href="/wiki/Tyranny_of_the_majority" title="Tyranny of the majority">tyranny of the majority</a>', a scenario in which a majority places its interests so far above those of an individual or minority group as to constitute active oppression.  Some voting theorists, however, argue that majority rule may actually prevent tyranny of the majority, in part because it maximizes the potential for a minority to form a coalition that can overturn an unsatisfactory decision.<sup id="cite_ref-tyranny_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tyranny-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some advocates of consensus would assert that a majority decision reduces the commitment of each individual decision-maker to the decision. Members of a minority position may feel less commitment to a majority decision, and even majority voters who may have taken their positions along party or bloc lines may have a sense of reduced responsibility for the ultimate decision. The result of this reduced commitment, according to many consensus proponents, is potentially less willingness to defend or act upon the decision.
</p><p>Majority voting cannot measure consensus. Indeed,&#8212;so many 'for' and so many 'against'&#8212;it measures the very opposite, the degree of dissent. Consensus voting, in contrast, the Modified Borda Count, MBC, can identify the consensus of any electorate, whenever such a consensus exists. Furthermore, the rules laid down for this procedure can be the very catalyst of consensus.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Outside of Western culture, multiple other cultures have used consensus decision-making. Perhaps the oldest example is the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Council_of_the_Six_Nations" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Council of the Six Nations">Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy Grand Council</a>, which has used consensus in decision-making using a 75% super majority to finalize decisions,<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> potentially as early as 1142.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Zulu_people" title="Zulu people">Xulu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xhosa_people" title="Xhosa people">Xhosa</a> (South African) process of <a href="/wiki/Indaba" title="Indaba">indaba</a>, community leaders gather to listen to the public and negotiate <a href="/wiki/Red_line_(phrase)" title="Red line (phrase)">figurative thresholds</a> towards an acceptable compromise. The technique was also used during the <a href="/wiki/2015_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Aceh" title="Aceh">Aceh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nias" title="Nias">Nias</a> cultures (Indonesian), family and regional disputes, from playground fights to estate inheritance, are handled through a <i>musyawarah</i> consensus-building process in which parties mediate to find peace and avoid future hostility and revenge. The resulting agreements are expected to be followed, and range from advice and warnings to compensation and exile.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Consensus-building and direct democracy experimentation was a feature of voter registration projects by the <a href="/wiki/Student_Nonviolent_Coordinating_Committee" title="Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee">Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee</a> (SNCC) in the <a href="/wiki/American_South" class="mw-redirect" title="American South">American South</a>; the Economic Research and Action Project (ERAP) of <a href="/wiki/Students_for_a_Democratic_Society" title="Students for a Democratic Society">Students for a Democratic Society</a> (mid-1960s), some <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_liberation" class="mw-redirect" title="Women&#39;s liberation">women's liberation</a> groups (late 1960s to early 1970s) and <a href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear_movement" title="Anti-nuclear movement">anti-nuclear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peace_movement" title="Peace movement">peace movement</a> groups (late 1970s and early 1980s).<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> For example, the anti-nuclear <a href="/wiki/Clamshell_Alliance" title="Clamshell Alliance">Clamshell Alliance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Movement_for_a_New_Society" title="Movement for a New Society">Movement for a New Society</a> engaged in consensus decision-making processes.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The origins of formal consensus-making can be traced significantly further back, to the <a href="/wiki/Religious_Society_of_Friends" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious Society of Friends">Religious Society of Friends</a>, or Quakers, who adopted the technique as early as the 17th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Mitchell_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Anabaptists" class="mw-redirect" title="Anabaptists">Anabaptists</a>, including some <a href="/wiki/Mennonite" class="mw-redirect" title="Mennonite">Mennonites</a>, have a history of using consensus decision-making<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> and some believe Anabaptists practiced consensus as early as the <a href="/wiki/Martyrs%27_Synod" title="Martyrs&#39; Synod">Martyrs' Synod</a> of 1527.<sup id="cite_ref-Mitchell_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Some Christians trace consensus decision-making back to the Bible. The Global Anabaptist Mennonite Encyclopedia references, in particular, Acts 15<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> as an example of consensus in the New Testament.  The lack of legitimate consensus process in the unanimous conviction of Jesus by corrupt priests<sup id="cite_ref-Pagels1996_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pagels1996-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> in an illegally held <a href="/wiki/Sanhedrin" title="Sanhedrin">Sanhedrin</a> court (which had rules preventing unanimous conviction in a hurried process) strongly influenced the views of pacifist Protestants, including the Anabaptists (Mennonites/Amish), Quakers and Shakers. In particular it influenced their distrust of expert-led courtrooms and to "be clear about process" and convene in a way that assures that "everyone must be heard".<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Consensus voting was first advocated by (a) Ramón Llull in 1199. Next by (b) Nicholas Cusanus in 1435. Then by (c) Jean-Charles de Borda in 1784. Later by (d) Hother Hage in 1860 and (e) Charles Dodgson (Lewis Carroll) in 1884. And finally by (f) Peter Emerson in 1986. With the possible exception of (e), none of these 'inventors' knew anything about the inventions of any of their predecessors.
</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> credits <a href="/wiki/Mollie_Hunter" title="Mollie Hunter">Mollie Hunter</a> (1922–2012) with following quotation regarding <a href="/wiki/Consensus" class="mw-redirect" title="Consensus">consensus</a>: "No single group has the right to ignore a consensus of thoughtful opinion"<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese_business">Japanese business</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Japanese business">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Japanese companies normally use consensus decision-making, meaning that unanimous support on the board of directors is sought for any decision.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> A ringi-sho is a circulation document used to obtain agreement. It must first be signed by the lowest level manager, and then upwards, and may need to be revised and the process started over.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IETF_rough_consensus_model">IETF rough consensus model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: IETF rough consensus model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Engineering_Task_Force" title="Internet Engineering Task Force">Internet Engineering Task Force</a> (IETF), decisions are assumed to be taken by <i><a href="/wiki/Rough_consensus" title="Rough consensus">rough consensus</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> The IETF has studiously refrained from defining a mechanical method for verifying such consensus, apparently in the belief that any such codification leads to attempts to "<a href="/wiki/Game_the_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Game the system">game the system</a>." Instead, a <a href="/wiki/IETF_Working_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="IETF Working Group">working group</a> (WG) chair or <a href="/wiki/Birds_of_a_Feather_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Birds of a Feather (computing)">BoF</a> chair is supposed to articulate the "sense of the group."
</p><p>One tradition in support of rough consensus is the tradition of humming rather than (countable) hand-raising; this allows a group to quickly tell the difference between "one or two objectors" or a "sharply divided community", without making it easy to slip into "<a href="/wiki/Majority_rule" title="Majority rule">majority rule</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Much of the business of the IETF is carried out on <a href="/wiki/Mailing_list" title="Mailing list">mailing lists</a>, where all parties can speak their view at all times.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_constructivism_model">Social constructivism model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Social constructivism model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2001, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rocco_Cottone" title="Robert Rocco Cottone">Robert Rocco Cottone</a> published a consensus-based model of professional decision-making for counselors and psychologists.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Based on <a href="/wiki/Social_constructivist" class="mw-redirect" title="Social constructivist">social constructivist</a> philosophy, the model operates as a consensus-building model, as the clinician addresses ethical conflicts through a process of negotiating to consensus. Conflicts are resolved by consensually agreed on arbitrators who are defined early in the negotiation process.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="US_Bureau_of_Land_Management_collaborative_stakeholder_engagement">US Bureau of Land Management collaborative stakeholder engagement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: US Bureau of Land Management collaborative stakeholder engagement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The United States <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Land_Management" title="Bureau of Land Management">Bureau of Land Management</a>'s policy is to seek to use collaborative stakeholder engagement as standard operating practice for natural resources projects, plans, and decision-making except under unusual conditions such as when constrained by law, regulation, or other mandates or when conventional processes are important for establishing new, or reaffirming existing, precedent.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Additional_criticism_from_biblical_and_philosophical_perspectives">Additional criticism from biblical and philosophical perspectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consensus_decision-making&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Additional criticism from biblical and philosophical perspectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a notable example of the failure of unanimity in the <a href="/wiki/Western_canon" title="Western canon">Western canon</a>, New Testament historian <a href="/wiki/Elaine_Pagels" title="Elaine Pagels">Elaine Pagels</a> cites the <a href="/wiki/Sanhedrin" title="Sanhedrin">Sanhedrin</a>'s unanimous vote to convict <a href="/wiki/Jesus_of_Nazareth" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesus of Nazareth">Jesus of Nazareth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pagels1996_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pagels1996-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> To a Jewish audience familiar with that court's requirement to set free any person unanimously convicted as not having a proper defense, Pagels proposes that the story is intended to signal the injustice of unanimous rush to agreement and Jesus' lack of a defender.<sup id="cite_ref-Pagels1996_59-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pagels1996-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> She cites the shift away from this view and towards preference for visible unanimity as a factor in later "demonization" of Jews, pagans, heretics (notably <a href="/wiki/Gnostic" class="mw-redirect" title="Gnostic">Gnostics</a>) and others who disagreed with orthodox views in later Christianity. Unanimity, in other words, became a priority where it had been an <a href="/wiki/Anathema" title="Anathema">anathema</a>.
</p><p>Some formal models based on <a href="/wiki/Graph_theory" title="Graph theory">graph theory</a> attempt to explore the implications of suppressed <a href="/wiki/Dissent" title="Dissent">dissent</a> and subsequent sabotage of the group as it takes action.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>High-stakes decision-making, such as judicial decisions of appeals courts, always require some such explicit documentation. Consent however is still observed that defies factional explanations. Nearly 40% of Supreme Court of US decisions, for example, are unanimous, though often for widely varying reasons. "Consensus in Supreme Court voting, particularly the extreme consensus of unanimity, has often puzzled Court observers who adhere to ideological accounts of judicial decision making."<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Historical evidence is mixed on whether particular Justices' views were suppressed in favour of public unity.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another method to achieve more agreement to satisfy a strict threshold a voting process under which all members of the group have a strategic incentive to agree rather than block.<sup id="cite_ref-SCFC_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCFC-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> However, this makes it very difficult to tell the difference between those who support the decision and those who merely tactically tolerate it for the incentive. Once they receive that incentive, they may undermine or refuse to implement the agreement in various and non-obvious ways. In general <a href="/wiki/Voting_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Voting system">voting systems</a> avoid allowing offering incentives (or "bribes") to change a heartfelt vote.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Abilene_paradox" title="Abilene paradox">Abilene paradox</a>: Consensus decision-making is susceptible to all forms of <a href="/wiki/Groupthink" title="Groupthink">groupthink</a>, the most dramatic being the <a href="/wiki/Abilene_paradox" title="Abilene paradox">Abilene paradox</a>. In the Abilene paradox, a group can unanimously agree on a course of action that no individual member of the group desires because no one individual is willing to go against the perceived will of the decision-making body.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Time Consuming: Since consensus decision-making focuses on discussion and seeks the input of all participants, it can be a time-consuming process. This is a potential liability in situations where decisions must be made speedily, or where it is not possible to canvass opinions of all delegates in a reasonable time. Additionally, the time commitment required to engage in the consensus decision-making process can sometimes act as a barrier to participation for individuals unable or unwilling to make the commitment.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> However, once a decision has been reached it can be acted on more quickly than a decision handed down. American businessmen complained that in negotiations with a Japanese company, they had to discuss the idea with everyone even the janitor, yet once a decision was made the Americans found the Japanese were able to act much quicker because everyone was on board, while the Americans had to struggle with internal opposition.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
